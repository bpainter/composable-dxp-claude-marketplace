# Research Design Framework: From Question to Insights

## Starting Point: The Research Question

Before choosing methodology, clarify what you need to know and why.

### Defining the Research Question

**Start with the decision**: What business decision does this research inform?
- "Should we enter this market?" (Market sizing, competitive analysis)
- "Why are customers churning?" (Diagnostic research)
- "Which positioning resonates?" (Messaging testing)
- "Is this feature viable?" (Concept testing)

**Translate to research objectives**:
- Exploratory: "What are barriers to adoption?"
- Descriptive: "What % of customers face these barriers?"
- Explanatory: "Why do some overcome barriers while others don't?"
- Predictive: "Which customers are most likely to churn?"

**Good research questions are**:
- Answerable: Feasible to research with available resources
- Relevant: Answers a real business question
- Clear: Specific enough to guide methodology choice
- Bounded: Narrow enough to address thoroughly

### Example Research Question Development

**Poor**: "How do customers feel about our product?"
- Too broad; unclear what "feel" means; no decision tie

**Better**: "Why do customers initially adopt our product but discontinue within 3 months?"
- Specific decision problem
- Clear target population (discontinuers)
- Bounded scope (3-month window)
- Implies diagnostic research (qualitative)

## Choosing Methodology

### Qualitative vs. Quantitative Decision Matrix

**Choose Qualitative when**:
- Goal is understanding "why" or exploring new topics
- Small sample is acceptable
- Topic is complex or sensitive
- You want rich, detailed data
- Budget is limited (lower per-person cost for small samples)
- Examples: Customer motivations, barriers, user experience

**Choose Quantitative when**:
- Goal is measuring prevalence, testing hypotheses
- Need large representative sample
- Topic is straightforward
- You need statistical rigor and generalizability
- Budget allows for larger sample
- Examples: Market size, customer satisfaction, segment characteristics

**Choose Mixed Methods when**:
- Need both breadth and depth
- Want to explain "what" with "why"
- Want convergence/triangulation across methods
- Can afford time and cost for both approaches
- Examples: User adoption barriers (survey prevalence + interviews for understanding)

## Sample Design

### Sample Size Considerations

**Qualitative research**:
- Smaller samples (8-20 for interviews, 3-5 groups for focus groups)
- Goal: Saturation (new data isn't adding new themes)
- Diversity of sample matters more than size

**Quantitative research**:
- Larger samples needed for statistical power
- Calculation depends on:
  - **Effect size**: How big is the difference you want to detect?
  - **Confidence level**: Usually 95% (means 5% chance of error)
  - **Power**: Usually 80% (means 20% chance of missing real effect)
  - **Population variance**: How much variability in the population?

**Rule of thumb**:
- Small effect + high confidence + high power = large sample (500+)
- Large effect + moderate confidence + moderate power = smaller sample (100-200)

**Sampling strategy**:
- **Random sampling**: Everyone in population has equal chance; best for generalizability
- **Stratified sampling**: Population divided into groups (strata); random sample within each
- **Quota sampling**: Set targets for different groups; convenience within quotas (faster, lower cost; may introduce bias)
- **Convenience sampling**: Easiest to reach people (fast, cheap; high bias risk)

### Representativeness and Generalizability

**Representativeness**: Sample matches population on key characteristics
- Age, gender, income, location, behavior patterns
- Non-representative sample = Limited generalizability
- Check: Compare sample to population on available data (census, customer base)

**Generalizability**: Findings apply beyond the sample
- Random sampling, representative sample → Higher generalizability
- Convenience sample, unrepresentative → Lower generalizability; findings apply only to sample
- Be clear about limitations: "Findings represent our customers who completed survey, not all customers"

## Instrument Design

### Survey Questionnaire Quality

**Item design principles**:
- **Single concept**: Each item measures one thing ("I like the product's features and price" is two items)
- **Clarity**: Simple language; avoid jargon and double negatives
- **Neutral wording**: Avoid leading language ("Isn't this great?" vs. "How would you rate this?")
- **Appropriate scale**: Match scale to construct (Likert for attitudes, frequency for behavior)
- **Avoid response biases**: Don't use double-barrel, double-negative, or overly complex items

**Common scale types**:
- **Likert (1-5 or 1-7)**: "Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree"; for attitudes
- **Semantic differential**: Paired opposites (e.g., "Good...Bad"); for perceptions
- **Frequency scales**: "Never" to "Always"; for behavior
- **Ranking/rating**: "Which is most important?" or "Rate importance 1-5"
- **Open-ended**: "What barriers do you face?"; for exploratory, qualitative data

**Survey structure**:
- Demographic/screening questions first (optional, no burden on respondent)
- Easier items before harder ones
- Related items grouped together
- Most important items in early-middle section (attention drops)
- Logical flow; avoid jumping between topics
- End with open-ended if needed (longer survey)

### Interview/Focus Group Guide Design

**Interview guide elements**:
- **Opening**: Build rapport, explain purpose, confidentiality, time frame
- **Warm-up questions**: Easy, non-threatening start
- **Key questions**: Main research questions; 5-7 key questions maximum
- **Probes**: "Tell me more," "Can you give an example?" to deepen responses
- **Transition questions**: Move between topics smoothly
- **Closing**: Summary, opportunity to add thoughts, thank you

**Guide design**:
- Semi-structured: Questions are open-ended, flexible in order
- Conversational: Not a script; natural dialogue
- Balanced**: Guide keeps discussion on track but allows exploration
- Time-boxed: Estimate time for each section

**Facilitation guidelines**:
- Listen more than talk (80/20 split)
- Use silence: Let respondents think and elaborate
- Probe respectfully: "What do you mean by...?" not "Why did you...?" (less defensive)
- Avoid leading: Don't inject own ideas or opinions
- Manage group dynamics: Balance talkative and quiet participants

## Bias and Validity Assessment

### Common Sources of Error

**Sampling bias**:
- Non-random sample (volunteers, convenience)
- Non-response bias (who doesn't respond? Often different from respondents)
- Selection bias (some groups systematically excluded)
- Mitigation: Use random sampling; track response rate; compare sample to population

**Measurement bias**:
- Leading questions that influence responses
- Social desirability bias (people answer how they think they should)
- Recall bias (can't remember accurately)
- Mitigation: Use neutral wording; anonymous surveys for sensitive topics; recent behavior focus

**Validity threats**:
- **Construct validity**: Does item measure what you think it measures?
- **Internal validity**: Are findings due to your intervention or other factors?
- **External validity**: Do findings generalize beyond study sample?
- **Statistical conclusion validity**: Is your statistical test appropriate?

### Quality Checks

**Qualitative**:
- **Credibility**: Do findings feel true to those you studied? (member checking)
- **Dependability**: Would another researcher reach similar conclusions? (codebook clarity)
- **Transferability**: Are findings applicable elsewhere? (rich description)
- **Confirmability**: Are findings from data or researcher bias? (data-driven coding)

**Quantitative**:
- **Reliability**: Consistent measurement (internal consistency: Cronbach's alpha > 0.70)
- **Validity**: Measure what you intend (factor analysis, convergent validity)
- **Generalizability**: Sample representative of population
- **Statistical power**: Sample large enough to detect effects

## Implementation Checklist

- [ ] Define research question tied to business decision
- [ ] Identify target population and how to access them
- [ ] Choose methodology (qual, quant, mixed) with justification
- [ ] Calculate sample size (quantitative) or saturation target (qualitative)
- [ ] Design instrument (survey, interview guide, protocol)
- [ ] Assess bias and validity threats
- [ ] Plan for data quality (training, checking, documentation)
- [ ] Recruit and screen participants
- [ ] Conduct research with fidelity to design
- [ ] Analyze data using planned approach
- [ ] Assess findings' limitations and generalizability
- [ ] Translate findings to insights and recommendations
