# Team Dynamics, Conflict, and Psychological Safety

## Team Development and Maturity

Teams don't start effective; they develop through stages. Understanding where your team is helps you intervene appropriately.

### Tuckman's Five Stages

**Forming** (Initial orientation):
- Team is new or newly composed
- Uncertainty about roles, norms, and goals
- Members are on their best behavior, somewhat guarded
- Leadership: Provide structure, clarity on goals and roles

**Storming** (Conflict and competition):
- Interpersonal conflicts emerge; members challenge each other
- Resistance to established norms
- Competition for status or leadership
- This stage is normal and healthy; suppressing it delays maturity
- Leadership: Acknowledge conflict, establish psychological safety, facilitate dialogue

**Norming** (Cohesion and cooperation):
- Team develops shared norms and expectations
- Cohesion and trust increase
- Collaboration improves; conflicts are managed constructively
- Leadership: Reinforce positive norms, maintain engagement

**Performing** (Productivity and autonomy):
- Team is highly effective and autonomous
- Minimal oversight needed; members self-manage
- Focus is on goals and delivery
- Leadership: Remove obstacles, develop team members, focus on strategic issues

**Adjourning** (Dissolution or transition):
- Project completion or team dissolution
- Process closure, recognize accomplishments, transition members
- Leadership: Celebrate success, facilitate transitions, preserve relationships

**Key insights:**
- Storming is not failure; suppressing it delays development
- Teams can regress if composition changes or major disruption occurs
- Effectiveness depends on stage-appropriate leadership

## Psychological Safety

Psychological safety is the belief that you can take interpersonal risks (speak up, ask questions, make mistakes) without negative consequences. It's foundational for team effectiveness.

**Impact of psychological safety:**
- High safety: Open communication, innovation, learning from mistakes, diverse perspectives
- Low safety: Silence, conformity, risk avoidance, poor decision-making, turnover

**Building psychological safety:**
1. **Model vulnerability**: Leaders who admit mistakes and ask for help signal that it's safe
2. **Respond productively to mistakes**: Treat mistakes as learning opportunities, not failures
3. **Invite input**: Ask for opinions, especially from quieter team members
4. **Listen actively**: Signal that you hear and value contributions
5. **Reward transparency**: Praise honesty, even when message is critical
6. **Avoid blame**: Blame reduces safety; focus on systems and improvement instead

**Leadership actions that destroy safety:**
- Punishing mistakes or honest disagreement
- Dismissing concerns or questions
- Playing favorites or showing partiality
- Communicating through anger or sarcasm
- Taking personal credit for team work

## Managing Conflict Productively

Conflict is normal and can be healthy if managed well.

### Types of Conflict

**Task conflict** (disagreement about goals, approach, priorities):
- Can improve decisions if managed well
- Healthy expression: "I see the technical risks differently"
- Leadership: Encourage diverse perspectives, focus on best approach

**Process conflict** (disagreement about how to work together):
- Usually resolvable through clarified roles, processes, norms
- Healthy expression: "We need clearer handoffs"
- Leadership: Establish explicit processes and norms

**Relationship conflict** (interpersonal tension, dislike, personality clashes):
- Damages team effectiveness and satisfaction
- Unhealthy expressions: Gossip, exclusion, passive-aggressive behavior
- Leadership: Address early; redirect to respect and professional focus

### Conflict Resolution Approaches

**Avoiding**: Ignore the conflict (usually a mistake unless truly trivial)
- Risks: Conflict festers; resentment builds

**Accommodating**: One person yields to preserve harmony
- Use when: Relationship is more important than issue outcome
- Risks: Accommodater feels resentful; patterns develop

**Competing**: One person wins, other loses
- Use when: Decision is critical and time is short
- Risks: Creates resentment; damages relationships

**Compromising**: Both parties give up something
- Use when: Parties are equal in power and issue is moderate importance
- Risks: Both parties dissatisfied; mediocre solutions

**Collaborating**: Both parties work to find solution satisfying both needs
- Use when: Time allows, relationship is important, both perspectives matter
- Process: Understand each person's underlying needs, brainstorm solutions, find mutual gain

**Leadership's role in conflict:**
- Address early (don't let conflict fester)
- Understand root causes (is this about task, process, or relationship?)
- Use appropriate conflict style for context
- Help parties understand each other's perspectives
- Facilitate collaborative problem-solving when possible

## Team Norms and Psychological Contracts

**Norms** are shared expectations about how team members should behave. Effective teams have norms that support their goals.

**Examples:**
- Communication: "We speak up in meetings; silence isn't agreement"
- Accountability: "We deliver on commitments; if we can't, we say so early"
- Feedback: "We give and receive feedback regularly; it's how we improve"
- Inclusion: "Everyone contributes; we welcome diverse perspectives"

**How norms form:**
- Explicit: Leader or team explicitly states expected behavior
- Implicit: Patterns emerge from initial behavior and leadership reinforcement
- Most powerful norms form implicitly through repeated behavior and consequences

**Changing unhealthy norms:**
- Make the norm explicit: Name what behavior you're observing
- Explain why it's problematic: "This norm is limiting our innovation"
- Model new behavior: Show what you want to see
- Reinforce new behavior: Acknowledge and reward when people follow new norm
- Address violations: Gently redirect when people fall back to old norm

**Psychological contracts** are implicit agreements between employee and organization about what each owes the other.

**Employee expectations:** Job security, fair pay, development, meaningful work, respect
**Organization expectations:** Effort, loyalty, performance, reliability

**Breaches occur when:**
- Organization: Downsizes without warning, changes role unexpectedly, ignores performance issues
- Employee: Disengages, reduces effort, looks for other jobs

**Rebuilding after breach:**
- Acknowledge the breach and impact
- Explain decision rationale (not excuses)
- Clarify new expectations
- Demonstrate consistent follow-through
- Rebuild through small, reliable interactions over time

## Implementation Checklist

- [ ] Assess team stage (forming, storming, norming, performing, adjourning)
- [ ] Diagnose psychological safety: Can people speak up? Are they heard?
- [ ] Identify team norms: What behaviors are reinforced? What's discouraged?
- [ ] Assess conflicts: Are they task, process, or relationship conflicts?
- [ ] Evaluate roles and clarity: Does everyone know their role and how they contribute?
- [ ] Check psychological contracts: Are expectations aligned?
- [ ] Identify points of leverage: Where can small changes create big impact?
- [ ] Plan interventions: Team charter, norms conversation, explicit role clarity, conflict resolution
