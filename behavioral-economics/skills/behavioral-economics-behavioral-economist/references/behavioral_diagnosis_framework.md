# Behavioral Diagnosis Framework

## Structured Approach to Understanding Decision Problems

When faced with a behavioral challenge, use this framework to diagnose root causes and identify intervention points.

### Step 1: Define the Current Behavior

**What people actually do:**
- Describe the behavior precisely (not the desired behavior, the actual behavior)
- Quantify the behavior (what % participate, what's the adoption rate, conversion rate?)
- Understand the status quo (what are people currently doing instead?)

**Examples:**
- Only 25% of employees save for retirement (actual) vs. 80% should participate (desired)
- 60% of customers abandon shopping carts at checkout (actual) vs. intended 20% abandonment
- 40% of patients skip follow-up appointments (actual) vs. intended 95% attendance

### Step 2: Identify the Decision Point

What must the person actively choose or do?

**Active choices:**
- Click a button, fill out a form, sign up for something
- Make a trade-off (price vs. features, cost vs. risk)
- Change status quo behavior

**Passive choices (default behaviors):**
- Accept a default (enrollment status, privacy settings, treatment)
- Do nothing (inertia)
- Stick with current provider/approach

**Is it an active choice or passive default?**
- Active choices have low participation by design; defaults have high participation by inertia
- Different interventions apply

### Step 3: Map Behavioral Barriers

Using prospect theory and bounded rationality, identify what's preventing the desired behavior:

**Cognitive barriers:**
- Is the information easy to understand? Do people comprehend the choice?
- Is there too much information (hyperchoice)?
- Are trade-offs clear (risk/reward, cost/benefit)?
- Is the decision salient? Do people think about it?

**Emotional/motivational barriers:**
- Is there loss aversion at play? ("I don't want to lose what I have")
- Is present bias blocking future benefits? ("I'll do it later")
- Is there status quo bias or inertia? ("The current situation is safest")
- Is there psychological reactance? ("I don't want to be told what to do")

**Social barriers:**
- Do they understand social norms? ("What do similar others do?")
- Is there social proof or peer influence? ("Others like me do X")
- Are there fairness concerns? ("Is this deal fair to me?")
- Is there trust or credibility issues?

**Structural barriers:**
- How much effort does the behavior require? (Application forms, waiting times, costs)
- Are there time or availability constraints?
- Is the behavior inconvenient or painful?
- Are there financial or resource constraints?

### Step 4: Apply Behavioral Theory

Match barriers to behavioral concepts:

**Status quo bias + defaults**: Are defaults creating inertia? Can changing defaults improve outcomes?
**Loss aversion**: Are people overweighting losses? Can reframing (gain vs. loss) help?
**Present bias**: Is immediate cost blocking future benefit? Can commitment devices help?
**Mental accounting**: Is framing creating perceived inequity? Can restructuring improve perception?
**Hyperchoice**: Are too many options causing paralysis? Can simplifying help?
**Availability heuristic**: Are recent examples skewing judgment? Can providing base rates help?
**Confirmation bias**: Are people filtering information through existing beliefs? Do they resist change?

### Step 5: Review Evidence

What does behavioral research say about similar problems?

- Is there an RCT or strong evidence for an intervention?
- How strong is the effect? (Small nudge vs. major behavior change)
- Does the effect depend on context? (Will it work in your specific setting?)
- What are potential unintended consequences?
- Are there implementation barriers?

### Step 6: Design Interventions

Based on diagnosis and evidence, design specific interventions:

**Nudges (low-cost, low-friction):**
- Change defaults (auto-enrollment, pre-selected options)
- Add reminders or prompts (emails, notifications)
- Reframe information (loss vs. gain, absolute vs. relative)
- Use social proof ("Most like you do X")
- Simplify choices (reduce options, use decision trees)

**Structural changes:**
- Redesign choice architecture (presentation, sequence, options)
- Reduce friction (fewer steps, easier enrollment)
- Change incentives (rewards, penalties, loss aversion)
- Implement commitment devices (advance pledges, deposits)

**Information and education:**
- Clarify trade-offs (side-by-side comparison)
- Provide decision support (recommender systems, choice tools)
- Build credibility and trust (expert endorsement, social proof)
- Use narratives (stories more persuasive than statistics)

### Step 7: Anticipate Unintended Consequences

- **Adaptation**: Will people find ways around the intervention?
- **Backlash**: Could the intervention trigger reactance or resistance?
- **Equity**: Does the intervention affect groups differently?
- **Moral hazard**: Does it create perverse incentives?
- **Crowding out**: Does extrinsic incentive undermine intrinsic motivation?

### Step 8: Design Tests

Before scaling, test the intervention:

- **A/B test**: Compare old vs. new design with randomized users
- **Pilot program**: Test with small group before full rollout
- **Metrics**: What will you measure to know if it worked?
- **Control group**: Who doesn't get the intervention for comparison?
- **Sample size**: How many users needed to detect effect?
- **Time horizon**: How long to measure impact?

## Example: Retirement Savings Problem

**Current behavior**: 25% of employees save for retirement (enrollment rate)

**Decision point**: Active enrollment in retirement plan

**Behavioral barriers:**
- *Cognitive*: Complex plan comparison, unclear tax implications, overwhelming options
- *Emotional*: Present bias (more immediate expenses feel more important than future retirement)
- *Social*: Uncertainty about adequacy ("Is this amount enough?")
- *Structural*: Time required to enroll, form complexity, need for decision

**Behavioral theory**: Status quo bias + present bias + hyperchoice

**Evidence**: Research shows auto-enrollment increases participation to 80%+

**Intervention**: Change from opt-in to auto-enrollment with automatic escalation

**Unintended consequences**: Some employees don't want to save; ensure easy opt-out

**Test**: A/B test auto-enrollment with 10% of new employees, measure 6-month enrollment, satisfaction, and retention
