# Framing Effects, Priming, and Message Architecture

## Core Framing Effects

Framing is how you present the same information in different ways. The frame—not just the facts—determines what people believe and do.

### Gain vs. Loss Frames

**Loss frames** ("If you don't do X, you'll lose Y"):
- More persuasive for prevention, risk, and cautious audiences
- Activate loss aversion and avoidance motivation
- Examples: "Delaying vaccination increases your risk," "This disease kills X people annually"
- Best for: Health behavior, financial risk, safety messaging

**Gain frames** ("Do X and you'll gain Y"):
- More persuasive for innovation, opportunity, and optimistic audiences
- Activate approach motivation and hope
- Examples: "Vaccination protects you and your community," "Early savings mean more retirement security"
- Best for: Technology adoption, investment, positive change

**Context matters:**
- Loss frames work better when the audience is analytical and risk-averse
- Gain frames work better when the audience is intuitive and optimistic
- The best approach often combines both: address fear (loss) with solutions (gain)

### Identity and Values Framing

People respond strongly to messages aligned with their identity and values:

**Value-based framing:**
- **Fairness/justice**: "This policy ensures equal opportunity"
- **Loyalty/community**: "We take care of our own"
- **Care/compassion**: "This helps vulnerable people"
- **Liberty/autonomy**: "You have the right to choose"
- **Authority/respect**: "Expert consensus supports this"

Match the frame to your audience's values. Testing shows:
- Conservative audiences respond to liberty and authority frames
- Progressive audiences respond to fairness and care frames
- Diverse audiences need multiple frames or value-neutral language

**In-group/out-group framing:**
- Positioning your audience as the "in-group" ("We are," "People like us")
- Positioning opponents as "out-group" ("They are," "Unlike us")
- Example: "We believe in science" vs. "They deny expert consensus"
- Warning: Creates tribal thinking; use ethically

## Priming and Salience

**Priming** is the subtle activation of concepts or associations in memory. A prime (words, images, ideas encountered before a decision) influences subsequent judgments.

**Examples:**
- Priming words related to wealth → more selfish behavior
- Priming medical terms before health decision → more cautious choices
- Priming competition → more aggressive negotiation
- Priming fairness → more cooperative behavior

**Salience** is how top-of-mind something is. Salient information seems more important and true.

**Making information salient:**
- **Recency**: Recent examples are more salient (recent deaths from disease seem more common)
- **Vividity**: Concrete, emotional, visual examples are more salient (video more than statistics)
- **Repetition**: Repeated exposure increases salience (and perceived truth)
- **Emotional resonance**: Information that triggers emotion is more salient

**In messaging:**
- Open with salient, emotional examples
- Repeat key phrases and ideas
- Use concrete, specific numbers over abstractions
- Pair statistics with human stories

## Message Architecture: The Persuasion Sequence

**Classic persuasion structure:**
1. **Attention**: Open with something salient and emotionally relevant
2. **Interest**: Frame the issue as relevant to audience values
3. **Credibility**: Establish expertise and trustworthiness
4. **Argument**: Present main claim with supporting evidence
5. **Social proof**: Show that similar others believe or do this
6. **Call to action**: Make the desired behavior clear and easy
7. **Closing**: Reinforce core message

**Example: Vaccine messaging**
1. Attention: "Last year, X people in our community got [disease]"
2. Interest: "Do you want to protect your family?"
3. Credibility: "Leading health organizations recommend vaccination"
4. Argument: "Vaccination provides X% protection against serious illness"
5. Social proof: "Most people in our community are vaccinated"
6. Call to action: "Schedule your appointment today at [link]"
7. Closing: "Vaccination is how we protect each other"

## Avoiding Backfire and Reactance

**Backfire effects** occur when a message has the opposite effect of intent:
- Contradicting strong existing beliefs can trigger psychological reactance (defensive rejection)
- Acknowledging counterarguments and legitimate concerns reduces backfire risk
- Avoiding overselling and absolute claims reduces skepticism

**Risk factors:**
- Messaging that feels like an attack on identity
- Extreme claims without evidence
- Ignoring legitimate concerns or counterarguments
- Talking down to or appearing to manipulate audience

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Acknowledge legitimate concerns: "Some worry about side effects..."
- Use two-sided arguments: Present your view and address counterargument
- Avoid extreme language and absolute claims
- Signal respect for audience intelligence
- Focus on shared values before introducing disagreement

## Language and Clarity

**Effective messaging uses:**
- **Simple, familiar language** (8th-grade reading level for general audience)
- **Active voice** ("We did X" not "X was done")
- **Concrete examples** (specific stories, not abstractions)
- **Repeated key phrases** (the most important idea three times minimum)
- **Short sentences** (average 15 words for complex topics)
- **Analogies and metaphors** (connect new ideas to familiar concepts)

**Avoid:**
- Jargon and technical language (unless audience expects it)
- Passive voice and nominalization (convert back to verbs)
- Hedge words ("might," "possibly," "arguably") that signal uncertainty
- Lists and bullet points (harder to remember than narrative)
- Acronyms (spell out first use; consider whether necessary)

## Implementation Checklist

- [ ] Know your audience's current beliefs and values
- [ ] Choose framing (gain vs. loss; values-based; identity-based)
- [ ] Identify salient examples and stories
- [ ] Test with target audience before scaling
- [ ] Design message architecture with attention → action sequence
- [ ] Use plain language and concrete examples
- [ ] Include social proof and credibility signals
- [ ] Anticipate and address counterarguments
- [ ] Monitor for unintended effects or backlash
