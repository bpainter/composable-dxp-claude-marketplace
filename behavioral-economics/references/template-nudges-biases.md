# Template — Nudges (Common Cognitive Biases)

**Source:** Slalom Behavioral Design Model, p. 33. Synthesizes biases from `book-thinking-fast-slow.md`, `book-nudge.md`, and `book-predictably-irrational.md`. Cross-reference [The Decision Lab biases index](https://thedecisionlab.com/biases) for the comprehensive catalog (~100+ biases).

A nudge is a subtle change through positive reinforcement or indirect suggestion that influences how decisions are made without restricting freedom of choice. Nudges work by leveraging predictable cognitive biases. This template lists the **eight biases Slalom flags as most consequential for choice-architecture work** (from p. 33), plus a deeper catalog and the rules for choosing the right one.

## When to use it

- During Stage 3 (Explore Choice Architectures), when you have a problematic decision flow and need to pick the lever
- As a checklist for "what could be in play here?" — most behavior problems involve 2–3 biases interacting
- As a teaching aid in workshops where the team needs shared vocabulary

## The eight common biases (Slalom p. 33)

### 1. Status Quo Bias

**Definition:** The preference to keep things the same or maintain a previous decision (Kahneman, *TFS* pp. 343–344, 411).

**How it shows up:** Customers stay enrolled in suboptimal plans, keep their default settings, don't switch even when switching is cheap.

**Nudge with it:** Switch the default to the better option (organ donation opt-out, 401(k) auto-enrollment). Make the right answer the path of least resistance.

**Nudge against it:** Add an active-choice prompt at a natural transition point (DMV organ-donor question; new-hire benefits selection).

### 2. Loss Aversion

**Definition:** The tendency to prefer avoiding losses over acquiring equivalent gains; losses loom 1.5–2.5× larger than equivalent gains (Kahneman, *TFS* pp. 333–336).

**How it shows up:** Customers won't change because the change feels like a loss; sellers overprice; users hoard unused options.

**Nudge with it:** Frame benefits as "avoid losing $X" rather than "save $X" (plastic-bag fees > rewards). Wellth's medication-adherence model uses upfront cash + daily forfeit.

**Nudge against it:** Reduce the perceived loss — guarantee no paycheck cut (Save More Tomorrow); offer money-back guarantees.

### 3. Anchoring

**Definition:** Reliance on the first offer presented as a reference for subsequent judgments. Anchoring index typically 30–55% of the gap between anchors (Kahneman, *TFS* pp. 143–154).

**How it shows up:** Customers' WTP tracks whatever number was on the page first. Taxi-tip defaults of 15/20/25% vs. 20/25/30% measurably shift earnings (*Nudge* pp. 28–30).

**Nudge with it:** Set the anchor where you want the decision — for tips, donations, savings rates, or pricing-page comparisons.

**Cautions:** Excessive anchors trigger reactance; check the bimodal-response trap (some customers anchor, others rebel and zero out).

### 4. Optimism Bias

**Definition:** Belief that negative events are less likely to happen to oneself than to others. Underlies founder optimism and the planning fallacy (Kahneman, *TFS* pp. 304–310).

**How it shows up:** Patients underestimate disease progression risk; founders underestimate failure base rates; teams underestimate project timelines.

**Nudge with it:** Hard to ethically nudge *with* (encourages risky behavior). Don't.

**Nudge against it:** Reference-class forecasting (TFS pp. 296–299); the premortem (Klein, TFS pp. 313–315); concrete base-rate displays ("X% of similar customers had this outcome").

### 5. Confirmation Bias

**Definition:** The tendency to favor information that confirms preexisting beliefs.

**How it shows up:** Customers ignore disconfirming product reviews; teams over-index on data supporting their preferred decision.

**Nudge against it:** Surface dissenting evidence by design (e.g., "considerations" panel next to "benefits"); structured pre-decision dissent (red team / devil's advocate).

### 6. Endowment Effect

**Definition:** People ascribe more value to things they own. WTA roughly 2× WTP (Kahneman, *TFS* pp. 344–352; Ariely Duke ticket study, *PI* pp. 127–138, 14× gap).

**How it shows up:** Free trials convert at high rates; sellers hold prices high; customers overvalue what they've configured/customized (IKEA effect).

**Nudge with it:** Free trials, "30-day money back," "drive it home for a week," configurable products that customers invest effort in.

**Nudge against it:** When you need customers to let go (e.g., un-subscribe to upgrade), reduce the perceived ownership signals.

### 7. Sunk-Cost Fallacy

**Definition:** Continued investment in a losing course because of prior commitment (Kahneman, *TFS* pp. 408–409).

**How it shows up:** Customers stick with bad plans because they've already paid for the year; teams escalate failing projects.

**Nudge against it:** Make exit costs visible upfront ("you're locked in for 12 months"); offer no-penalty switching windows; reference-class forecasting for project decisions.

### 8. Availability Heuristic

**Definition:** Events more easily remembered are believed to be more common or likely (Kahneman, *TFS* pp. 155–162).

**How it shows up:** Customers overestimate vivid risks (data breaches after a news cycle); under-estimate base-rate risks (chronic disease).

**Nudge with it:** Make the relevant statistic vivid (Lake Shore Drive curve stripes simulating acceleration; cigarette graphic warnings).

**Nudge against it:** When availability is over-pulling (panic, overreaction), surface base-rate data prominently.

## Pitfalls

- **Naming a bias without testing whether it's actually in play.** Bias hypotheses are hypotheses. Confirm before designing — qualitative interviews + small experiments before large-scale rollout.
- **Stacking biases that conflict.** Anchoring high *and* loss-aversion framing on the same screen can cancel out or trigger reactance. Pick one primary lever per step.
- **Treating biases as universally bad.** Many biases are functional shortcuts that fail in specific contexts. The design job isn't to "eliminate biases" — it's to align them with customer welfare.
- **Forgetting that biases are cultural.** Many findings replicate weakly outside WEIRD samples. Hong Kong replication of Ariely's beer-uniqueness study (PI Ch. 13) showed conformity instead of differentiation.
- **Ethics drift.** A nudge that exploits a bias for business gain at customer expense is sludge (Nudge Ch. 8). Run the ethics gate (`template-use-case-validation.md`).
