# Foundational Behavioral Economics Concepts

Shared reference for choice-architect, behavioral-economist, and behavioral-economist-social-cognition skills. Covers core theories, frameworks, and biases that apply across all three specializations.

---

## 1. Dual-Process Theory (System 1 / System 2)

**Kahneman's Framework**

- **System 1 (Fast, Intuitive)**: Automatic, emotion-driven, heuristic-based. Operates with minimal effort. Processes identity, social cues, vivid examples, pattern recognition.
- **System 2 (Slow, Deliberative)**: Effortful, logic-driven, evidence-based. Requires attention. Engaged for complex reasoning, contradiction resolution, careful evaluation.

**When Each Dominates**

- System 1 dominates under time pressure, cognitive load, low stakes, and routine decisions
- System 2 engages when stakes are high, information is complex, or System 1 triggers doubt
- Most real-world decisions rely on System 1; System 2 is resource-constrained

**Implications for Intervention Design**

- **Activating System 1**: Use emotion, identity, social proof, vivid examples, simple messaging. Effective for quick decisions or low-engagement audiences.
- **Engaging System 2**: Provide evidence, expert credibility, logical structure, time for consideration. Needed for complex choices or skeptical audiences.
- **Hybrid approach**: Effective interventions layer System 1 activation (emotional resonance, identity alignment) with System 2 reasoning (clear logic, evidence).

## 2. Prospect Theory & Loss Aversion

**Reference Points & Loss Aversion**

- People evaluate outcomes relative to a **reference point** (current state), not absolute value
- **Loss aversion ratio**: Losses feel ~2x stronger than equivalent gains
- A loss of $100 is more painful than the joy of gaining $100
- People are more risk-averse when facing losses; more risk-seeking when facing equivalent gains

**Endowment Effect**

- Once something is "yours," you demand more to sell it than you'd pay to buy it
- Applies to physical goods, time, autonomy, and even abstract concepts (beliefs, identities)
- Drives inertia and resistance to change

**Status Quo Bias**

- Default options are chosen at rates 15-50% higher than alternatives, even identical ones
- Roots: inertia, perceived safety of current state, cognitive burden of switching costs
- Most powerful tool in choice design; also primary source of suboptimal outcomes

## 3. Bounded Rationality

**Satisficing vs. Maximizing**

- People don't optimize; they **satisfice** (choose "good enough" and stop searching)
- Maximizers search exhaustively; satisficers stop once they meet a threshold
- Satisficing is adaptive in complex environments; maximizing leads to decision fatigue and regret

**Cognitive Load & Decision Fatigue**

- Limited working memory: 3-5 pieces of information held simultaneously
- Decision fatigue: Each choice depletes mental resources; later choices suffer
- Too many options (hyperchoice) triggers paralysis and default-choosing

**Heuristics (Mental Shortcuts)**

- **Availability**: Judge likelihood by how easily examples come to mind (recent, vivid, repeated)
- **Representativeness**: Judge probability by similarity to a stereotype or prototype
- **Anchoring**: Initial information disproportionately influences estimates (first price, first number)
- All are fast and often accurate; all can systematically mislead

## 4. Core Biases Taxonomy

Brief definitions of the 10-15 most commonly applied biases:

| Bias | Definition | Application |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Anchoring** | First information disproportionately influences judgments | Pricing, negotiation, policy framing |
| **Framing Effect** | Same choice presented differently triggers different decisions | Gain vs. loss messaging, attention direction |
| **Default Effect** | Chosen options selected at much higher rates | Choice architecture, auto-enrollment, presets |
| **Social Proof** | People follow others' behavior; assume popular = correct | Descriptive/injunctive norms, peer messaging |
| **Present Bias** | Immediate rewards weighted heavily; future discounted steeply | Commitment devices, timing of choices |
| **Sunk Cost Fallacy** | Past investments influence future decisions (irrationally) | Quitting, switching, abandonment |
| **Confirmation Bias** | People seek information confirming existing beliefs | Message exposure, interpretation, polarization |
| **Optimism Bias** | Overestimate personal positive outcomes; underestimate risk | Planning fallacy, overconfidence, risk-taking |
| **Hyperbolic Discounting** | Preference shifts as time horizon changes; steep near-term discount | Savings, health behavior, procrastination |
| **Decoy Effect** | Adding a dominated option shifts preference toward target | Product bundling, option architecture |
| **Peak-End Rule** | Memory of experience driven by peak moment + ending, not duration | Interaction design, service recovery |
| **IKEA Effect** | Value increases when people invest effort in creating it | Customization, participation, ownership |
| **Availability Bias** | Overweight recent/vivid/repeated examples; underweight statistics | Media influence, narrative power, recall |
| **Recency Bias** | Recent information disproportionately influences judgment | Timing of messaging, information order |
| **Hindsight Bias** | Overestimate how predictable past events were; justify after-the-fact | Causal reasoning, accountability, learning |

## 5. Ethical Framework

**Libertarian Paternalism**

- **Paternalistic**: Nudges aim to improve outcomes (beneficiary's stated goals, not designer's values)
- **Libertarian**: Always preserves choice; no option is eliminated or made impossible
- **Principle**: Design choice architecture to guide toward better outcomes *while maintaining exit options*
- **Example**: Auto-enrollment in retirement savings with easy opt-out; organ donation defaults with clear opt-in/out options

**Transparency Requirements**

- Nudge effectiveness depends partly on *not being noticed*, but lack of transparency undermines autonomy
- **Ethical threshold**: People should be able to discover the nudge (explanation available; not hidden in dark patterns)
- When nudges involve defaults or defaults that disadvantage some groups, explicit disclosure increases legitimacy
- Test for: "Would informed consent be likely? Would discovery of the nudge trigger resentment?"

**When Nudges Cross into Manipulation**

- **Dark patterns**: Designs that trick, pressure, or exploit rather than inform and guide
- **Examples**: Hidden costs, difficult opt-outs, misdirection, false urgency
- **Red flag**: Nudge *prevents informed choice* rather than simplifying it
- **Ethical test**: Would you defend this choice architecture to regulators or the public? Would your audience agree they were nudged toward their own goals?

**Equity & Unequal Impacts**

- Behavioral interventions often affect different groups unequally (education, culture, income, language)
- **Ethical requirement**: Assess and mitigate disparate impacts. What works for one group may exclude or harm another
- When designing for vulnerable populations: involve community representatives, ethics boards, and continuous monitoring
