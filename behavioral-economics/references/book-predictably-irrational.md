# Predictably Irrational — Dan Ariely (2008/2009)

**Source digest** for the behavioral-economics plugin. Cite as "(Ariely, *PI*, p. X)". Page numbers refer to the 308-page Harper edition.

## Thesis

Human decision-making deviates from the rational-actor model not randomly but **systematically and predictably**. Through dozens of field and lab experiments, Ariely shows that we consistently overpay, underestimate, procrastinate, and overvalue what we own — and that we make the *same kinds* of mistakes again and again because of how our brains are wired. Unlike pure noise, these patterns can be **anticipated, designed for, and engineered around** — by choice architects, marketers, employers, and ourselves. The book is the popular-press foundation of behavioral economics' "free lunches" idea: if biases are predictable, well-designed defaults, framings, and commitment devices can produce net welfare gains for everyone (pp. xi–xx, 239–244).

## The decoy effect / asymmetric dominance — full treatment

Ariely's most-cited contribution to pricing strategy.

The *Economist* subscription page offered three options:
1. Web only — $59
2. Print only — $125
3. Print + Web — $125

Run on 100 MIT Sloan MBAs:
- Web only: 16
- Print only: 0
- Print + Web: 84

Then dropped the print-only "decoy" so only options 1 and 3 remained:
- Web only: 68
- Print + Web: 32

**Lesson.** Adding option (-A) — clearly worse than A but not comparable to B — made A *also* look better than B. The decoy doesn't have to be chosen; it just has to be there. In two-attribute space (quality × beauty), introducing -A creates an easy A > -A comparison and hijacks the harder A vs. B comparison (pp. 9–10).

**Replications:** colonial-with-bad-roof house (p. 8); Rome / -Rome / Paris travel package (p. 10); Williams-Sonoma bread-maker (sales rose only after a 50%-bigger, 50%-pricier model was added — p. 14); MIT face-attractiveness Photoshop experiment (75% picked the regular-A face when a slightly-disfigured -A version was present, pp. 11–14).

**How a `pricing-strategist` should use it:** Design **three-tier menus with a deliberate "asymmetric dominance" decoy** that is dominated by the target on every dimension. Pair with restaurant-menu pricing (high-anchor entrée → second-most-expensive becomes the "value" pick — Gregg Rapp study, p. 4) and TV-display anchoring (middle option captures the most volume, pp. 3–4). When introducing a premium tier, add a decoy dominated by it on at least two attributes, even if no one will buy the decoy.

## Anchoring & arbitrary coherence (Ariely's twist on Kahneman)

Kahneman & Tversky established anchoring as a number-priming bias. Ariely's twist is **arbitrary coherence** (pp. 26–30):

- The *initial* anchor is genuinely arbitrary — random numbers, MSRPs, or imprinted first-encounter prices.
- But *once an anchor is set*, subsequent valuations are perfectly coherent with it. People who anchor high on wine A also anchor high on wine B, and the relative spread between A and B is rational.

**The Social-Security-Number experiment (pp. 26–29).** Prelec, Loewenstein, and Ariely had 55 MIT MBAs write the last two digits of their SSN, then bid (Vickrey 2nd-price auction) on six items: two wines, a Logitech trackball, a Logitech keyboard, a design book, and Neuhaus chocolates. Students with SSNs ending 80–99 bid **216–346% higher** than those ending 00–19. Correlations 0.32–0.52. Yet within each subject, WTP for the keyboard exceeded the trackball, and Hermitage exceeded Côtes du Rhône — coherent ordering on top of arbitrary level.

**Implication for `pricing-strategist`:** First exposure to a category sets the anchor. New product launches should manage first-encounter price aggressively. Real-world replication: Simonsohn & Loewenstein's study of relocators who keep paying their *previous* city's housing prices in the new city (pp. 30–31).

## Social norms vs. market norms

Two non-fungible exchange systems:

- **Social norms** — favors, reciprocity unmetered, warmth, communal identity. ("Could you help me move this couch?")
- **Market norms** — wages, prices, contracts, instant accounting. ("$50 to move the couch.")

Once money is named, the system flips to market — and **flipping back is hard or impossible**.

**Key experiments:**
- **Drag-circle (Heyman & Ariely, pp. 70–73).** $5 → 159 circles. 50¢ → 101. Favor (no pay) → 168. Effort under social norms beat both monetary conditions.
- **Gift variant (pp. 72–73).** Snickers (~50¢) → 162. Godiva (~$5) → 169. Favor → 168. Gifts stay social.
- **Priced-gift collapse (p. 73).** "A 50¢ Snickers bar" → effort matched 50¢ cash, not Snickers. *Mentioning the price destroys the social frame.*
- **AARP attorney study (p. 71).** Lawyers said no to $30/hr for needy retirees, yes to free pro-bono. Once $30 is on the table, market norms apply and $30 < market wage.

**Why mixing backfires:** The market signal evicts the social signal — and social norms are slow and costly to rebuild (pp. 74–77). Vohs/Mead/Goode money-priming work shows even *thinking* about money makes people more self-reliant, less helpful, and less collaborative.

**For `organizational-behavior-specialist`:** This is the canonical argument against monetizing internal favors, against framing employee discretionary effort as paid labor, and against pay-for-performance schemes that displace pride/mission/identity. **For `pricing-strategist`:** be careful when a service has both transactional and relationship components — do not casually introduce price into the relationship side.

## Endowment / IKEA effect

Ariely builds on Knetsch, Thaler, and Kahneman's endowment work and extends it.

**The Duke basketball ticket experiment (Carmon & Ariely 1994; pp. 127–133).** Duke rations Final Four tickets via a multi-week tent campout (Krzyzewskiville) followed by a lottery — so winners and losers were behaviorally identical until the moment of the draw. Ariely and Carmon then phoned 100+ students:

- Non-winners' average **WTP**: ~$170
- Winners' average **WTA**: ~$2,400
- **Ratio: ~14×**
- **Trades closed: zero**

**Three irrational quirks of ownership** (pp. 133–138):
1. We fall in love with what we already have (memory & nostalgia attach instantly)
2. We focus on what we'd *lose*, not what we'd gain (loss aversion)
3. We assume the other side sees the transaction as we do

Plus a fourth, the **IKEA effect**: the more effort sunk into an object — the longer the campout, the assembly time, the design choices — the more we value it.

## Cross-references

**vs. Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow*:** Anchoring is in both books; cite Kahneman for the canonical mechanism (pp. 143–154 of TFS), Ariely for the SSN-bidding experiment. Endowment is in both; cite Kahneman for Thaler's mug experiments (p. 344), Ariely for Duke tickets (p. 127). Decoy effect is Ariely's territory; Kahneman covers the related but distinct **single-vs-joint-evaluation reversals** (pp. 417–426). The hot/cold empathy gap (Ariely Ch. 5) overlaps with Kahneman's experiencing/remembering self framing.

**vs. Thaler & Sunstein, *Nudge*:** Ariely's anchoring → *Nudge*'s anchoring chapter (pp. 28–30). Ariely on procrastination/self-control → *Nudge*'s Planner/Doer framing and commitment-device chapter. Ariely on social vs. market norms → *Nudge*'s social-influence chapter. Ariely cites Save More Tomorrow (p. 242) directly. Where T&S focus on *defaults*, Ariely focuses on *primes, frames, and contextual cues*.

**Quick rule for which to cite:** the kindergarten/late-pickup-fine study (Gneezy & Rustichini), placebo pricing experiments (Veladone, SoBe), the cash-vs-tokens cheating findings, the *Economist* decoy, and the SSN auction are **Ariely's territory** — cite *PI*. Linda the bank teller, cold-pressor, colonoscopy, the Asian disease problem, and the canonical anchoring index are **Kahneman's**. Defaults, NUDGES mnemonic, libertarian paternalism, and Save More Tomorrow's design are **T&S's**.
