# Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman (2011)

**Source digest** for the behavioral-economics plugin. Page numbers refer to the 609-page Doubleday Canada edition. Cite as "(Kahneman, *TFS*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

Kahneman synthesizes four decades of research (much with Amos Tversky) on how human judgment systematically departs from rational-agent models. The mind operates via two metaphorical agents — fast, automatic, associative **System 1** and slow, effortful, deliberate **System 2** — and the heuristics, biases, and framing effects that follow are not random errors of fatigued thinkers but predictable, replicable features of normal cognition. The practical payoff: a richer vocabulary for diagnosing judgment errors, plus the foundation (with prospect theory) for behavioral economics as an empirical alternative to expected utility theory (pp. 10–24, 316–322, 489–492).

## The five frameworks every skill should know

### 1. System 1 / System 2 (Part I, pp. 25–128)

Two metaphorical agents. **System 1** runs automatically — perception, association, intuition, emotion. **System 2** allocates attention to effortful activity — complex computation, self-control, monitoring (pp. 27–28). Almost every bias in the book traces to a System 1 shortcut that a lazy or inattentive System 2 fails to override (p. 33). The "law of least effort" (p. 45) is why de-biasing is hard.

### 2. Prospect Theory (Ch. 26, pp. 327–340)

Replaces expected utility's *states of wealth* with *gains and losses relative to a reference point*. Three principles: **reference dependence**, **diminishing sensitivity** (concave for gains, convex for losses), and **loss aversion** — losses loom roughly **1.5× to 2.5×** larger than equivalent gains (pp. 332–335). The single most-cited finding in behavioral economics; explains the endowment effect, status quo bias, the disposition effect, and most asymmetric reactions to change.

### 3. Anchoring (Ch. 11, pp. 143–154)

Estimates of an unknown quantity stay close to any number considered just before — even an obviously random one. Two mechanisms: System 2 adjustment that ends prematurely, and System 1 priming that activates compatible information (pp. 144–148). The **anchoring index** typically runs 30–55%: real-estate appraisals (41%), German judges sentencing on dice rolls (50%), willingness-to-pay studies, negotiation. "Any number that is on the table has had an anchoring effect on you" (p. 154).

### 4. Availability Heuristic (Ch. 12–13, pp. 155–170)

Judging the frequency or probability of a category by the ease with which instances come to mind. Salience, recency, vividness, personal relevance, and media coverage all increase ease of retrieval independent of true frequency. Underlies risk perception (terrorism vs. car accidents), media-driven public-policy salience, and the spousal-contribution overestimate where each partner adds up to >100% of housework (p. 157).

### 5. Two Selves: Experiencing vs. Remembering (Ch. 35–38, pp. 445–492)

The **experiencing self** lives moments; the **remembering self** keeps score and makes choices. They diverge because memory follows the **peak-end rule** (a memory is the average of the worst moment and the end) and **duration neglect** (length of an episode barely registers retrospectively) (pp. 448–453). Demonstrated in the colonoscopy and cold-pressor studies. "What we learn from the past is to maximize the qualities of our future memories, not necessarily of our future experience" (p. 450).

### Bonus: WYSIATI — "What You See Is All There Is" (pp. 85–88, 308–311)

System 1 builds the most coherent story from whatever information is currently available and ignores what it doesn't know. Underlies overconfidence, the planning fallacy, competition neglect, the illusion of validity, and the halo effect.

## Three concrete experiments worth quoting

### The Linda Problem (pp. 187–197)

Linda is described as 31, single, outspoken, a former philosophy major active in anti-nuclear demonstrations. Subjects rank "Linda is a bank teller" vs. "Linda is a bank teller and active in the feminist movement." A large majority (including ~85% of doctoral students in decision science, p. 188) rank the conjunction as more probable — a logical impossibility.

**Demonstrates:** Representativeness overrides logic; plausibility is confused with probability; "less is more" — adding detail makes a story more persuasive but less likely.

**Cite when:** explaining why detailed scenarios persuade audiences against the evidence (social-cognition); arguing that survey items must distinguish plausibility from likelihood (research-methods); showing why intuition fails Bayesian reasoning (statistician).

### The Asian Disease Problem — Framing (pp. 435–436)

A disease will kill 600 people. Frame 1 (lives saved): A saves 200 for sure; B has 1/3 chance of saving 600, 2/3 chance of saving none. Frame 2 (lives lost): A' kills 400 for sure; B' has 1/3 chance no one dies, 2/3 chance 600 die. Mathematically identical. Subjects pick A under "saved" (risk-averse) and B' under "die" (risk-seeking). Public-health professionals showed the same bias.

**Demonstrates:** Risk preferences are properties of *framings*, not options. Loss aversion plus diminishing sensitivity produces the flip from risk-averse-for-gains to risk-seeking-for-losses.

**Cite when:** designing message frames for desired behavior (choice-architect, social-cognition); contrasting "cash discount" vs. "credit surcharge" (p. 430) or "90% fat-free" vs. "10% fat" (p. 492) (pricing-strategist).

### The Cold-Pressor Experiment (pp. 450–452)

Subjects immerse a hand in painfully cold (14°C) water for 60 seconds. Then a second trial — 90 seconds, with the last 30 seconds slightly warmer (still painful). Given a free choice of which to repeat, **80% chose the longer, objectively-more-painful trial** because their remembering self favored its better ending.

**Demonstrates:** Peak-end rule and duration neglect produce systematic divergence between *experienced utility* and *decision utility*. Confirmed in the colonoscopy studies (pp. 447–449).

**Cite when:** designing service experiences with strong endings — "the tyranny of the remembering self" (choice-architect); distinguishing moment-by-moment data from retrospective surveys (research-methods); for any well-being measurement (behavioral-economist).

## Cross-references

**vs. Nudge (Thaler & Sunstein):** *Nudge* operationalizes Kahneman's catalog. Where Kahneman is descriptive, Thaler & Sunstein are prescriptive. Use *Nudge* for "what to do," Kahneman for "why it works." Kahneman explicitly endorses *Nudge* as "the basic manual for applying behavioral economics to policy" (p. 440).

**vs. Predictably Irrational (Ariely):** Ariely is breezier and experiment-by-experiment; Kahneman embeds every finding in System 1/2 + prospect theory. The Linda problem, cold-pressor, colonoscopy, anchoring index numbers, regression to the mean, and the framing-effect experiments are Kahneman's. The decoy effect, social-vs-market norms, IKEA effect, "free!" zero-price effect, and arbitrary coherence are Ariely's. Both treat anchoring; cite Kahneman for the canonical mechanism, Ariely for the SSN-bidding experiment.
