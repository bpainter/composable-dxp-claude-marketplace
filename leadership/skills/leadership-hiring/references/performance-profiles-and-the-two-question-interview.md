# Performance Profiles and the two-question performance-based interview

The two ideas at the core of Adler's system. Define success before you define the candidate; ask two questions deeply instead of twenty questions shallowly.

## Performance Profile — define success first

A Performance Profile replaces the traditional job description. It contains 5–8 bullets describing what the person will *accomplish* in the role, not what they need to *be*.

### Skills shopping list (don't do this)

> **Senior Engineering Lead, Composable DXP**
> - 8+ years software engineering, 4+ in leadership
> - Strong knowledge of headless CMS, React, Node, GraphQL
> - Excellent communication and stakeholder management
> - Experience leading distributed teams
> - MS/CS preferred

This attracts skill-checkers. It tells you nothing about whether the person can do the job. Every interviewer makes up their own definition of success.

### Performance Profile (do this)

> **Senior Engineering Lead, Composable DXP — first 12 months**
> 1. By month 3: own the architecture review for two active engagements; ship a documented reference architecture for headless commerce that the team can reuse on the next pursuit.
> 2. By month 6: hire and onboard 2 senior engineers against the practice's growth plan; both shipping production code by week 6.
> 3. By month 9: lead the technical discovery for at least one $1M+ pursuit, partnered with the Solution Director; convert at least one pursuit to closed-won.
> 4. By month 12: stand up a quarterly reusable-asset cadence (3–4 patterns or accelerators per quarter) that the broader practice consumes.
> 5. Ongoing: directly manage 4–6 engineers across engagements; run weekly 1:1s; deliver one substantive growth conversation per quarter per direct report.
> 6. Ongoing: participate in 2 conference talks or whitepapers per year representing the practice externally.

Now every interviewer can ask: *what evidence is there this person can do bullet 1, bullet 2, etc.* The conversation in the debrief is about evidence, not vibes.

### Writing a good Performance Profile — the test

For each bullet, ask: *if the person did exactly this and nothing else for 12 months, would I be thrilled?* If yes, it earns the bullet. If you'd want more, the bullet is incomplete. If you'd be fine with less, it's bloat.

Also ask: *can I tell from a 1-hour interview whether this person can do this?* If no, you can't probe it. Move it to the 90-day plan, not the Profile.

## The two-question performance-based interview

The premise: most interview questions are noise. Two questions, asked with depth and fact-finding follow-ups, produce more signal than fifteen behavioral prompts.

### Question 1 — Most Significant Accomplishment (MSA)

> "Of everything you've done in the past few years, what's the most significant accomplishment you've had that's similar to what you'd be doing here?"

Then **peel the onion** with fact-finding follow-ups. Spend 20–30 minutes on this, easily.

**Fact-finding sequence:**
1. **When did this happen?** (Get specific dates. Recent matters more.)
2. **Where did this happen?** (Company, team, role at the time.)
3. **What was the situation when you arrived / when this started?** (Baseline.)
4. **What was your specific role?** (Owner, contributor, supporter? Press hard. "We" is suspicious — clarify what *they* did.)
5. **Walk me through what you actually did, day by day or week by week.** (Specifics force them out of pitch mode.)
6. **Who else was involved?** (Their manager, peers, team. Names are nice.)
7. **What was the result, in numbers if possible?** (Quantify. "It went well" is not a result.)
8. **How does the team know this was your accomplishment vs. someone else's?** (Tests ownership.)
9. **What would you do differently if you did it again?** (Reflective capacity.)
10. **What did you learn?** (Growth orientation.)
11. **Compare this to your second-most-significant accomplishment.** (Calibrates depth.)
12. **What feedback did you get on this work — positive and critical?** (Gets at how others saw it.)

**Listen for:**
- Comparable scope to your role's outcomes
- Specifics that line up with the Performance Profile bullets
- Signs of ownership vs. participation
- A coherent story (not a polished pitch)
- Reflective capacity — they can name what they'd change
- Names, dates, results — concrete evidence rather than glossy generalities

**Red flags:**
- Cannot describe specifics
- "We" without ever saying "I"
- Cannot articulate what they learned or would change
- Result is vague or unmeasured
- The accomplishment doesn't actually match the role's level

### Question 2 — Visualization / Problem-Solving (PSQ)

> "Here's a real problem this role will face: [describe a specific, real problem from the Performance Profile]. Walk me through how you'd approach it."

Pick a problem that actually appears in the role. For the Senior Engineering Lead example: *"We just won a Sitecore-to-headless replatform for a Fortune-500 retailer. They have six in-house engineers and want us to lead the architecture. You're 30 days in. Walk me through the first conversation with their CTO."*

**Listen for:**
- They ask clarifying questions before diving in (do they understand the problem space?)
- They propose a method or framework, not just a punchline
- They surface trade-offs and unknowns explicitly
- They name what they'd need (people, time, decisions, context)
- They move at the right altitude — not too tactical, not too hand-wavy
- Realism — have they done this kind of thing before, or are they making it up?

**Red flags:**
- Jumps to solution without scoping
- Cannot name what they don't know
- All assertion, no method
- The reasoning doesn't match their MSA — claims they'd do something they've never actually done

### Why two questions and not twenty

Adler's claim: depth produces evidence; breadth produces impressions. A 60-minute interview with 12 questions averages 5 minutes per question — barely enough to hear a polished story. A 60-minute interview with 2 questions and fact-finding produces real evidence about whether the person can do the work.

This works at every level. Junior candidates have smaller MSAs and simpler PSQs; senior candidates have bigger ones. The structure is the same.

## Wiring the loop together

Each interviewer in the loop owns one or two bullets from the Performance Profile. They run their own version of the two-question interview anchored to their bullet(s). No two interviewers cover the same ground. The hiring manager runs the deepest version and does the close.

In debrief, every interviewer reports evidence per Performance Profile bullet — not "I liked them" or "good fit." See `evidence-based-debrief.md`.

## Common pitfalls

**The Performance Profile got watered down to be "fair to all candidates."** This is the death of the profile. Outcomes attract specific people; vagueness attracts everyone (and so signals nothing).

**The MSA turns into a chronological resume walkthrough.** Stop them. "Of all of that, what's the *most significant* accomplishment that's similar to what you'd do here?" Pick one and go deep.

**The PSQ becomes a brainteaser.** It shouldn't. It's a real problem, presented as a real problem, asked because the answer tells you whether they can do the work. Brainteasers test interview skill, not job skill.

**Interviewers wing it.** Adler's whole system collapses if interviewers don't prepare. 15 minutes of prep — re-read the Performance Profile, pick the two bullets you own, draft your MSA and PSQ — is the price of entry.
