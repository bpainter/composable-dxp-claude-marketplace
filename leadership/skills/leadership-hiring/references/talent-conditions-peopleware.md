# Talent conditions — what makes great hires stay productive (Peopleware)

DeMarco and Lister's quiet, devastating point: most "people problems" are environment problems. You can hire the best person on earth, drop them into a team that fragments their attention, suppresses their judgment, and treats their time as fungible, and you will get mediocre output. Then you will say, "I guess they weren't as good as we thought."

This reference covers the hiring-relevant conditions: what to fix before you hire, what to be honest about with candidates, and what to watch for once they're in.

## "The major problems of our work are not so much technological as sociological"

DeMarco and Lister's opening claim. Engineers, designers, consultants — knowledge workers in general — solve technical problems all day. The reason most projects fail is that the social and environmental conditions around the work are broken: people can't think, can't communicate, can't disagree productively, can't trust the org, can't get their hands on the work without interruption.

For a hiring manager, this means: a candidate's productivity in your team is not a function of *their* capability alone. It's a function of capability times environment. Don't hire people you'll then waste.

## Quiet, uninterrupted thinking time as a hiring asset

Knowledge work requires "flow" — long blocks of uninterrupted, focused attention. DeMarco and Lister cite research showing it takes ~15 minutes to enter flow. Each interruption — the chat ping, the unscheduled drop-by, the "quick question," the meeting — resets the clock.

A team that interrupts constantly produces engineers who are perpetually warm but never hot. It also drives away the senior people you most want to keep, who notice they cannot do their best work in your environment.

**Hiring implication:** If the candidate asks "what's your team's working norm around meetings, async, and focus time?" they are probing whether they'll be able to do their work. A vague or defensive answer signals a no-flow environment, and the right candidates will weight that.

**Practice implication for Slalom Composable DXP:** consulting work is naturally interruption-heavy — clients ping, engagements have ceremonies, the firm has ceremonies. The leverage is in defending *some* deep-work time per week for senior practitioners. A team that has zero flow time is not a team where senior architects do their best work.

## "People are not interchangeable parts"

The book's anti-Frederick-Taylor stance: knowledge workers are not fungible. Two engineers of "the same level" produce wildly different output, often 10x apart, and the difference is not skill — it's fit, environment, motivation, judgment. You cannot solve a hiring problem by adding more bodies; sometimes you solve it by *removing* a misfit and giving the team back its conditions.

**Hiring implication:** size the team for actual throughput, not for body count. One excellent senior engineer in a healthy environment outperforms three mediocre ones in a bad one. If your hiring plan assumes interchangeability, it's wrong.

## "Teamicide" — the things that kill teams

DeMarco and Lister's list of practices that destroy teams. A hiring manager should audit their own practice for these *before* extending offers. Hiring into a teamicide environment guarantees attrition.

| Pattern | What it looks like | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| Defensive management | Manager hedges, micromanages, won't trust the team's judgment | Catch yourself. Trust by default; verify by exception. |
| Bureaucracy | Process-for-process-sake; sign-offs that no one reads | Cut at least one ceremony per quarter; ask whether each artifact actually informs a decision |
| Physical separation | Team members in different rooms, time zones with no overlap, no shared space | Co-locate working sessions deliberately; defend overlap windows |
| Fragmentation of people's time | Engineers split across 4 projects, each demanding 25% | Enforce single-project allocation for senior delivery roles; multi-project leadership is a different skill |
| The illusion that quality is negotiable | "We'll fix it later" as a recurring excuse | Quality decay drives away the engineers you most want to keep |
| Phony deadlines | Deadlines set by leadership for motivation, with no real consequence on the date | Real dates only; don't burn credibility |
| Clique control | Manager picks favorites, splits the team | Calibrate explicitly; rotate exposure; check who's invited to which meetings |

If your team has any of these patterns, the senior person you're about to hire will notice within 60 days. Either fix the pattern before hiring, or be honest about it and hire someone who's prepared to help fix it.

## Manager-caused turnover

Lister's point, repeated through the book: the most common cause of voluntary turnover is the manager, not the role, the comp, or the company. People quit managers, not jobs.

Specific patterns:
- Lack of recognition for good work
- Public correction or humiliation for mistakes
- Asymmetric feedback (only negative; or only "great job" with no specifics)
- Withheld information ("don't tell the team yet")
- Unfulfilled commitments around growth or scope

**Hiring implication:** the candidate's question "why is this role open?" deserves an honest answer. If the previous person quit because of management failures you've fixed, say so. If the previous person quit because of management failures you haven't fixed, hiring isn't your bottleneck — your management is.

## Hiring honesty — what to disclose

Senior candidates do diligence. They will figure out the reality of your team within their first 30 days regardless of what you tell them. Two consequences:

1. **The candidate who accepts under false pretenses leaves at month 4.** You burned 4 months and an open req.
2. **The candidate who hears the honest version and accepts anyway is committed.** They're choosing the role with eyes open.

Disclose:
- Real meeting load (hours per week of standing meetings; meeting:making ratio)
- Realistic flow time per week
- Project allocation reality (single-threaded vs. fragmented)
- Recent attrition and the reasons (without naming specific people)
- Current organizational pressure points (a layoff cycle, a re-org rumor, a struggling engagement)
- The 1–2 worst things about working here, in your honest opinion

The pitch for the role still works — it just works on candidates who can handle the truth, who are exactly the candidates you want.

## What "fix the conditions" looks like in practice

Before opening a senior req, audit:

1. **Calendar audit on the existing team.** What's the meeting load? What's the longest unbroken focus block? Is there one?
2. **Allocation audit.** Are senior engineers single-threaded or split? If split, is that a deliberate choice or drift?
3. **Bureaucracy audit.** What artifacts do you produce that no one reads? What sign-offs slow work without changing decisions?
4. **Recognition audit.** When did you last specifically thank someone for excellent work in writing or in front of others?
5. **Attrition audit.** Who left in the last 18 months and why? Are the reasons addressable?

If three or more come back broken, fix at least one before hiring. The new hire is not the lever — the conditions are.

## What candidates should ask (and what your answers reveal)

You should be ready for these. They're tells:

| Candidate question | What it tells you they care about | Your answer reveals |
|---|---|---|
| "What's a typical week look like for someone in this role?" | Flow time, fragmentation | Whether you've thought about it |
| "How many meetings per week is normal?" | Meeting load | Honest baseline |
| "How does the team handle disagreement?" | Psychological safety | Whether you can give a real example |
| "What's the worst thing about working here right now?" | Honesty signal | Your willingness to be truthful |
| "Why is this role open?" | Stability, attrition | Whether you'll be vague |
| "Who left this team in the last year, and why?" | Attrition pattern | Whether you can speak honestly without disparaging people |
| "How do decisions actually get made?" | Power and process | Whether you understand your own org |

If you're hedging on these, the candidate is reading you correctly.
