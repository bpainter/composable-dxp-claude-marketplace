# Sourcing and closing

The bookends of the hiring process. Most teams underinvest in both — they post a job and wait for applicants, then scramble through the close because nobody planned it. Adler's claim: the best people are not on the open market, and "selling" them late doesn't work. You have to source like a recruiter and recruit through the entire loop.

## Sourcing — the best people aren't applying

### The active vs. passive split

| | Active | Passive |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | Currently job-hunting | Not job-hunting; would consider for the right opportunity |
| Volume | Smaller pool | Much larger pool — most of the market |
| Quality | Mixed — includes both top performers in transition and struggling candidates | Includes the best people; bar is higher because they're not desperate |
| How to reach | Job postings, applicant tracking | Outreach, network, referrals, targeted research |

If you only fish in the active pool, you're competing with every other employer for the same small set of candidates. The senior hires that move the practice forward are usually passive.

### Multilevel sourcing — Adler's principle

Don't rely on one channel. Run several in parallel:
1. **Internal referrals.** Your team's network. Highest-yield channel by far.
2. **Direct outreach.** You (or recruiter) identify named targets and reach out personally.
3. **Past candidates.** People who interviewed well in the last 18 months but didn't get hired or didn't accept.
4. **Job postings.** Yes, still. But know it's the lowest-yield channel for senior roles.
5. **Industry presence.** Conference talks, blog posts, and the open-source / community work your team does are sourcing assets.

### Internal referrals — how to actually generate them

Most teams treat referrals as "we have a referral bonus, please send us names." That doesn't work. What works:

1. **Bring the open Performance Profile to a team meeting** and walk through what kind of person you're looking for, in plain language.
2. **Ask each person to scan their contacts** with a specific prompt: "Who's the best [role] you've ever worked with? Who would you want to work with again?"
3. **Make the intro low-friction.** Send a draft email the team member can forward.
4. **Follow up personally.** When someone makes an intro, treat it as a gift — respond fast, be respectful, close the loop.

### Outreach that converts (passive candidates)

The cold outreach message that works on senior people is:
- **Specific** about why this person, not a mass blast
- **Brief** — 4 short paragraphs, not a wall of text
- **Honest** about what's on offer (and what's not)
- **Low-friction** — proposes a 20-min conversation, not a full application

Template:

> Subject: [practice / company name] — your [specific work] caught my eye
>
> [Name],
>
> I run the Composable DXP practice at Slalom. I came across your [specific thing — talk, post, project] on [where] and was struck by [specific observation]. We're growing the practice and I think there might be an interesting role here for someone with your background.
>
> The short version: [1–2 sentence pitch — what the person would do, what makes it interesting]. The longer version is in the attached one-pager.
>
> No pressure either way — but if you're open to a 20-min conversation, I'd love to find a time. I'm happy to share what we're working on regardless.
>
> [Sign-off]

### "Offer careers, not jobs"

When you describe the role, lead with what the person will accomplish and learn over the next 2–3 years, not the title and the perks. Senior people aren't moving for incrementally better comp — they're moving for scope, growth, and the work itself.

Reframe each Performance Profile bullet as a learning / scope opportunity:
- "By month 6, you'll have hired and onboarded 2 senior engineers" → "You'll build the senior bench for one of the practice's strategic accounts"
- "By month 9, lead technical discovery for a $1M+ pursuit" → "You'll shape and close at least one large enterprise deal in your first year — the kind of work most engineers never get to do"

## Closing — recruiting through the entire loop

The close starts at the recruiter screen, not at offer. Every interaction either builds the candidate's belief that this is the right next move, or chips away at it.

### What "recruiting" means

Adler's distinction: recruiting is *not* selling. Selling pushes; recruiting helps the candidate decide. The best closes ask the candidate good questions and let their own answers persuade them.

### The motivation map

By the end of the loop, you should be able to fill in for any candidate you'd hire:

| Question | Their answer |
|---|---|
| Why are they considering leaving their current role? | |
| What do they specifically want more of in their next role? | |
| What do they specifically want less of? | |
| What's the comp expectation, and how flexible? | |
| What's the timeline pressure (current employer, family, etc.)? | |
| What other companies are they talking to? At what stage? | |
| Who are the deciders in their personal life (spouse, mentor)? | |
| What would make this role a "no" for them? | |
| What would make it a "yes"? | |

Most of this comes out organically across the loop if interviewers ask. The recruiter and the closer are explicitly responsible for the missing pieces.

### Creating the opportunity gap

Adler's "opportunity gap" technique: ask a question that helps the candidate see the gap between their current role and what this role would offer.

> "If you took this role, what's the biggest thing you'd be doing here that you can't do where you are now?"

Sit in the silence. Their answer is your closing material. The candidate is now making the case to themselves.

### The close conversation

In the last interview slot (typically the senior leader), explicitly:

1. **Ask the candidate what questions remain.** Real questions, not polite ones.
2. **Address each one fully.** If you don't know the answer, say so and commit to coming back with it within 24 hours.
3. **Probe for hidden concerns.** "If you ended up turning this down, what would the reason most likely be?"
4. **Reinforce the opportunity gap.** Reflect back what they said they wanted that this role offers.
5. **Set expectation on next steps.** "We'll have a decision by [date]. If we make you an offer, we'd want to hear back by [date]." Specific timeline.

### Common candidate concerns and how to handle them

| Concern | Response |
|---|---|
| "I just got promoted / a raise — feels like bad timing." | Reframe as a sign they're at a peak — better moves come from strength, not desperation. |
| "I'm worried about [specific risk]." | Acknowledge directly. Don't dismiss. Address with specifics. |
| "My spouse/family has concerns." | Ask what specifically. Offer to talk to them or send specific information. Don't push. |
| "I have a competing offer." | Ask what's appealing about it. Don't compete on comp alone. Reinforce the opportunity gap. |
| "I'm not sure I can leave my team." | Acknowledge. Ask whether the *role* is right; deal with the *transition* separately. |

### The offer itself

By the time the offer goes out, none of it should be a surprise. You've discussed comp range, scope, start date, and concerns through the loop.

The offer should:
- Match what was discussed (no rug-pulls on level, comp, or scope)
- Come with a clear, fast timeline ("decision by Friday")
- Be delivered verbally first, then in writing
- Be paired with a re-recruitment call — not a wait-and-see

### Counteroffers

Adler is direct on counteroffers: most people who accept counteroffers from their current employer leave within 12 months. The counteroffer is a short-term retention play, not a real change. If your candidate is wavering on a counteroffer:

- Acknowledge the financial pressure is real
- Reframe: "What was true before this counteroffer that made you want this role? Is that still true?"
- Reinforce the opportunity gap — what is the new role offering that the current employer can't, even with the comp bump?
- Don't engage in a bidding war. If the role isn't worth taking on its merits, raising the offer doesn't fix that.

If you lose to a counteroffer: leave the door open with a clean follow-up in 6 months. Counteroffer-takers often do leave their employer eventually.

## Sourcing for diversity

A pipeline you don't actively shape is a pipeline that reflects existing networks. To broaden:
- Source from communities, conferences, and networks beyond your team's default
- Use blind initial screens for candidates whose backgrounds make implicit bias likely
- Check the demographic spread of the slate, not just the hire — if every late-stage candidate looks like the team, the slate has failed
- Audit your Performance Profile for unnecessarily narrow signals (specific schools, specific companies, specific tech stacks where adjacent ones would do)
