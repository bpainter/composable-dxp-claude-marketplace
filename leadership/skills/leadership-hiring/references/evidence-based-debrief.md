# Evidence-based debrief

The debrief is where most loops fall apart. People walk in with impressions, vote, and the loudest opinion wins. Adler's evidence-based assessment turns the debrief into a structured discussion of what each interviewer actually heard.

## The hiring manager's job in the debrief

Surface disagreement, force evidence, prevent groupthink, make the call.

You are not running a democracy. You are running a structured assessment. You decide.

## The 45-minute debrief structure

| Time | Step |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Each interviewer states their lean (hire / no-hire / unsure) — *no rationale yet* |
| 5–25 min | Walk the Performance Profile bullet by bullet. For each bullet, the assigned interviewer cites evidence. Others contribute corroborating or contradicting evidence. |
| 25–35 min | Discuss disagreements. The hiring manager probes the strongest no-hire and the strongest hire to see whether the gap survives evidence. |
| 35–40 min | Identify any unprobed bullets or unresolved evidence gaps. Decide whether to do a follow-up. |
| 40–45 min | Hiring manager makes the call (or commits to a follow-up). Decision recorded. |

## "State your lean first" — why

When interviewers explain rationale before stating their lean, they back into a verdict that matches their narrative. When they state a lean first, they're committed to it and will defend with evidence. The hiring manager sees the loop's spread up front.

You also avoid one strong voice anchoring the room. If 4 interviewers say "hire" first, the 5th who was leaning no will hedge to "unsure."

## The evidence card per interviewer

Every interviewer fills this in within 24 hours of their interview, before the debrief:

```
Interviewer: [name]
Candidate: [name]
Role: [role]
Bullets I owned: [1, 3]

Bullet 1 — [text]
- Evidence FOR: [specific quotes, claims, examples from the interview]
- Evidence AGAINST: [specific quotes, gaps, hesitations]
- Conclusion: [strong yes / lean yes / unsure / lean no / strong no] on this bullet

Bullet 3 — [text]
- Evidence FOR: ...
- Evidence AGAINST: ...
- Conclusion: ...

Overall lean: [hire / no-hire / unsure]
Confidence (1–5): [n]
The one thing I'd want another interviewer to probe: [open question]
```

If an interviewer can't fill in evidence per bullet, they didn't run a real interview. Note it; coach them; don't weight their input.

## How to facilitate the bullet-by-bullet walk

For each Performance Profile bullet, the hiring manager:

1. **Reads the bullet aloud.** "Bullet 2: hire and onboard 2 senior engineers, both shipping by week 6."
2. **Names the bullet's owner.** "Marcus, you owned this one. What did you hear?"
3. **Marcus cites evidence.** Specific. Quote-level. "She told me about hiring 3 senior engineers at [prior company] in 4 months; she ran the loop herself; she described how she onboarded them with a structured first-30 plan; she could name what worked and what she'd change."
4. **Open the floor.** "Anyone else hear something on this bullet?" Sometimes a peer interviewer caught something Marcus missed.
5. **Conclude the bullet.** "On bullet 2, we've got strong evidence she's done this. Move on."

This is *not* a discussion of whether the candidate seems good. It's a discussion of whether the evidence supports the bullet.

## Handling disagreement

When the loop is split, don't take the average. Probe the gap.

**The hiring manager asks the strongest no-hire:** "What specifically would have to be true for you to change your mind?"

If the answer is "different evidence" — go get it. Schedule a 30-minute follow-up with the candidate to probe the unanswered bullet.

If the answer is "I just don't think they can do this" — push back. "What evidence are you citing for that?" Force them to ground it. If they can't, the lean is impression, not evidence, and shouldn't outweigh the rest of the loop.

**The hiring manager asks the strongest hire:** "What's your biggest concern about this person, if any?" Force the same level of honesty in the other direction.

## The "fatal flaw" check

Adler distinguishes minor concerns from fatal flaws. A fatal flaw is a deal-breaker — a missing capability that the role requires and that can't be coached up in the first 6 months.

Examples of fatal flaws:
- Cannot demonstrate ownership of similar-scope accomplishment (the role is too big a step)
- Cannot articulate technical reasoning at the required depth
- Repeatedly demonstrates contempt for stakeholders, peers, or past employers
- Cannot describe specifics in their own work (suggests they didn't do it)

Examples of *not* fatal flaws (these are coachable):
- Hasn't used your specific tech stack but has shipped equivalents
- Hasn't done a $1M+ deal but has done several $300K ones
- Quieter style than your existing team

A hire decision means: zero fatal flaws + strong evidence on most bullets + coachable gaps on the rest.

## When the answer is "unsure"

If the loop's lean is unsure on more than 1–2 bullets, you don't have enough evidence to hire. Options:

1. **Targeted follow-up.** 45-min interview with one interviewer, focused on the unprobed bullets. Don't drag the whole loop back.
2. **Reference checks early.** Adler argues references can resolve specific bullets if you ask the right questions (see below).
3. **Pass with the door open.** "We don't have enough evidence right now. Let's revisit if X."

Do *not* hire on hope. The cost of a no-hire is an open req. The cost of a wrong hire is 6 months of mismatch and a hard conversation.

## Reference checks as evidence

Adler treats references as a structured probe of remaining unknowns, not a formality. Ask the reference:
- "Tell me about a project where [candidate] [did the thing in bullet X]. What was their actual role?"
- "On a scale of 1–10, how would you rate them on [the unresolved bullet]?"
- "What's the one thing you'd want a future manager of theirs to know?"
- "Would you hire them again? For what kind of role?"

You're after evidence that fills the gap, not character endorsements.

## The decision

The hiring manager makes the call at the end of the debrief. Not "we'll think about it" — that's how loops drift to no-decision. Either:

1. **Hire.** Move to offer.
2. **Hire pending follow-up.** Schedule the targeted follow-up; commit to a decision date.
3. **No-hire.** Communicate respectfully; close the loop with the candidate within 48 hours.

Whatever the call, document the rationale per bullet in the candidate file. This is how you build a learning loop and defend the decision later.

## What to write down

After the debrief, the hiring manager files:
- The decision
- The Performance Profile bullets that drove it
- The evidence cited (paraphrased)
- Any dissent and how it was resolved
- The follow-up plan (if any)

## Common debrief failures

**The loudest voice wins.** Mitigation: each interviewer states lean first; written evidence cards before debrief.

**"Cultural fit" stands in for evidence.** Cultural fit is unfalsifiable and a frequent vector for bias. Translate any "fit" claim into a Profile bullet ("you mean she didn't show evidence of cross-functional collaboration?") or set it aside.

**The hiring manager doesn't push back on a strong vote.** A unanimous-hire vote in a 45-min debrief should make you nervous, not happy. Probe at least one bullet hard. "What would change my mind on this?"

**The debrief slides past unprobed bullets.** Mitigation: the bullet-by-bullet walk surfaces these.

**Nobody owns the close, so a strong candidate evaporates.** That's a loop-design failure, not a debrief failure — see `structured-interview-loop-design.md`.
