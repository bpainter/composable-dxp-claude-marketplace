# Motivation diagnosis with AMP (Pink's *Drive*)

Pink's central synthesis: extrinsic motivators (carrots, sticks, contingent rewards) work for routine, algorithmic work. They suppress intrinsic motivation for complex, creative, judgment-heavy work — the kind senior consultants do every day. For knowledge work, the three drivers that produce sustained engagement are autonomy, mastery, and purpose (AMP).

This reference uses AMP diagnostically: when motivation is flagging, ask which of A, M, or P is constrained, then design the fix.

## Type I and Type X

Pink's distinction:
- **Type I** (intrinsic) — driven by interest, growth, contribution to something larger
- **Type X** (extrinsic) — driven by external rewards, status, compensation

Most people aren't innately one or the other. They're Type I in conditions that allow it, and Type X in conditions that suppress autonomy / mastery / purpose. The leader's job is to design conditions that bring out Type I behavior.

This is a stronger claim than it sounds. It implies that "lack of motivation" in a previously high-performing person is usually a *condition* failure, not a *person* failure. Diagnose the conditions before concluding the person changed.

## The three drivers

### Autonomy

The desire to direct your own life. Four dimensions:

- **What** you work on (which problems, which projects)
- **When** you work (schedule autonomy)
- **How** you work (method autonomy)
- **With whom** you work (team selection)

Senior professionals especially need autonomy in *what* and *how*. Constraining a senior consultant to follow a prescribed methodology on every engagement, while paying them senior wages, is a Type-X-inducing setup.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "Do you feel like you choose what you work on, or does it choose you?"
- "When you have an opinion about how to do something, does it land?"
- "How much of your week is yours to design vs. defined by others?"
- "What's something you'd love to spend time on that you're not allowed to?"

**Common autonomy constraints in consulting:**
- Tightly-defined engagement scope (low *what* autonomy)
- Client-driven schedule (low *when* autonomy)
- Methodology mandates (low *how* autonomy)
- Staffing decisions made above (low *with whom* autonomy)

**Interventions:**
- Give senior people latitude on *how* even when *what* and *when* are client-defined
- Carve out a 10–20% time block for self-directed practice work (asset development, learning, mentoring) — Pink cites Google's 20% time and Atlassian's ShipIt days as examples
- Push staffing input to senior practitioners, not just up the chain
- Give autonomy on *whom* to mentor, *what* topics to write on externally, *which* practice rituals to lead

### Mastery

The desire to get better at something that matters. Three properties:

- **Mastery is a mindset.** People who believe their abilities can grow (Dweck's "growth mindset") engage with challenge differently than those who believe abilities are fixed.
- **Mastery is pain.** Real growth requires deliberate practice — hard, focused, often unpleasant work at the edge of capability.
- **Mastery is asymptotic.** You never fully arrive. The pursuit itself is the reward.

For senior professionals, mastery often means *new* mastery — the techniques that made them senior are no longer growth edges. They need novel challenges. The senior architect who's been doing the same kind of work for 5 years is bored regardless of how good they are at it.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "What's something you're getting visibly better at right now?"
- "Where are you stretched? Where are you under-stretched?"
- "When you finish a piece of work, do you typically feel you've grown?"
- "Is there a skill you wish you were building that you're not?"

**Common mastery constraints:**
- Same kind of work for years (boredom)
- No challenge stretch (under-stretch)
- Challenge way beyond capability (frustration; gives up)
- No feedback on whether they're actually improving
- No deliberate development structure (training, mentoring, reading, conferences)

**Interventions:**
- Stretch assignments — projects at the edge of current capability
- Explicit feedback on growth (not just performance)
- Investment in deliberate development (conference budget, learning time)
- Mentoring (both directions — being mentored and mentoring others)
- Public craft — talks, writing, teaching forces a deeper articulation of expertise

### Purpose

The yearning to be part of something larger than oneself.

For consulting work specifically, purpose can erode when work feels transactional ("we ship the deck and move on") versus when work feels mission-driven ("we made this client's customers' lives genuinely better"). Same hours, same comp, dramatically different motivation.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "What does your best week look like? What did you do? Why did it matter?"
- "What's the work that makes you proud?"
- "When you describe what you do to a friend, what do you say? Does it animate you?"
- "What's something we do here that you wish we did less of? More of?"
- "Does the practice's mission resonate with you, or does it feel performative?"

**Common purpose constraints:**
- Work disconnected from real outcomes (deck-and-leave consulting)
- Misalignment between personal values and the firm's actual behavior
- Lack of visibility into how the work lands with end users
- Pressure on commercial metrics in ways that crowd out the craft and the mission

**Interventions:**
- Connect daily work to outcome (when did a past client succeed because of this team's work? what changed for them?)
- Explicit mission articulation (not corporate-comms platitudes; what does this practice actually stand for?)
- Choose engagements with mission-fit when possible (and acknowledge when commercial pressure overrides this)
- Senior leader behavior matches stated mission (the highest-leverage purpose intervention)

## AMP audit — the diagnostic

For an individual or team showing flagging motivation, run this audit:

| Driver | Score 1–5 | Specific evidence | Specific intervention |
|---|---|---|---|
| Autonomy | | | |
| Mastery | | | |
| Purpose | | | |

Don't average. Look at the lowest score. That's the constrained driver.

If autonomy is 1 and the others are 4, the intervention is autonomy-focused (more *what/how* latitude, time block for self-direction). Mastery interventions won't fix an autonomy problem.

If all three are 2, the situation is structural — you're looking at the conditions of the role itself, not an individual fix.

## When the person actually has changed

Sometimes motivation flags not because of the conditions but because the person's life has changed (health, family, burnout, identity shift). The AMP audit will show *high* scores on the design factors but the person is still disengaged. In that case, the conversation is about what they're going through, not about role redesign — see `leadership-people-leader` for that conversation.

But — and this is Pink's load-bearing point — most "the person changed" diagnoses are wrong. The conditions changed. Check the conditions first.

## Pink's seven reasons carrots and sticks (often) don't work

1. They can extinguish intrinsic motivation
2. They can diminish performance (on creative tasks)
3. They can crush creativity
4. They can crowd out good behavior (gaming for the reward)
5. They can encourage cheating, shortcuts, unethical behavior
6. They can become addictive (need bigger reward each time)
7. They can foster short-term thinking

Implications for leaders:
- Be very careful with contingent rewards on creative / judgment work
- Comp matters as a *baseline of fairness* (otherwise it's a demotivator); it doesn't motivate above that baseline
- Don't tie OKR completion to bonus (creates sandbagging incentives)
- Recognition (qualitative, specific, public) often beats reward (quantitative, generic)

## When extrinsic motivators do work

Pink's "Chapter 2A" caveat: for routine, algorithmic, well-defined tasks where the path to the answer is clear, contingent rewards can work. The risk is misapplying that to creative or judgment work, where they backfire.

Most senior consulting work is not routine. AMP applies.

## For Bermon's specific context

Senior consultants in Composable DXP have generally:
- High autonomy when client-side allows it (engagements vary widely on this)
- High mastery requirement (Composable DXP is a fast-moving field; complacency is real risk)
- Variable purpose — the highest performers tend to find purpose in client outcomes; the disengaged often describe work as "transactional"

The practice-level interventions:
- Defend autonomy in *how* even when client / scope constrains *what* and *when*
- Build deliberate mastery investment (asset development time, conference budget, internal teach-back rituals)
- Make purpose explicit in practice rituals (regular client-outcome reviews; not just metrics)
