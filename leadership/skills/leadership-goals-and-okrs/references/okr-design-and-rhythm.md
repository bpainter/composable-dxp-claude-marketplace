# OKR design and rhythm

Wodtke's central claim: OKRs fail not because the format is wrong but because teams treat them as quarterly paperwork. The format works when it's wrapped in an operating rhythm that *uses* the OKRs every week.

This reference covers the mechanics: how to write OKRs, how to size them, what the weekly rhythm looks like, and the anti-patterns that kill them.

## The format

**One Objective. Three Key Results.** Per quarter. Per team.

| | Objective | Key Result |
|---|---|---|
| Form | Qualitative, motivating | Quantitative, measurable |
| Question | "What do we want to do?" | "How will we know if we did it?" |
| Length | One sentence | One sentence with a number |
| Test | Does it make people lean in? | Could a stranger tell from the data whether you hit it? |

### A good Objective

- Inspiring (someone reading it leans in)
- Aligned with strategy
- Specific enough to constrain action
- Time-bounded (quarterly)
- Memorable (you can recite it without looking)

Examples:
- "Become the most-trusted Composable DXP partner in mid-market retail"
- "Make the engineering team a place where senior architects do their best work of the year"
- "Land the practice's first $2M+ enterprise pursuit"

Not good:
- "Improve sales" (vague, not motivating)
- "Drive customer satisfaction across all touchpoints" (consultant boilerplate)
- "Be excellent" (meaningless)

### Good Key Results

- Quantitative
- Measurable in a way both you and the team agree on
- Outcome-focused (not activity)
- 50% confidence at quarter start

Examples (under "Become the most-trusted Composable DXP partner in mid-market retail"):
- "Close 4 new logos at $500K+ ACV in mid-market retail"
- "Publish 6 referenceable case studies in mid-market retail"
- "Sustain NPS 60+ across active mid-market retail engagements"

Not good:
- "Have lots of customer conversations" (activity, not outcome)
- "Customer satisfaction improves" (unmeasurable as written)
- "Hit revenue targets" (not specific to the Objective)

### The 50% confidence calibration

Wodtke's specific calibration: **at the start of the quarter, you should be ~50% confident you'll hit each KR.** Not 90% (too easy; you'll coast). Not 10% (fantasy; team will give up).

This is uncomfortable. Teams will want to commit to KRs they're confident on. Push back. The goal is to *stretch* — and stretch by definition means uncertainty.

Track confidence weekly:
- Each KR owner reports current confidence (1–10) at Monday commit
- If confidence drops below 30% by week 6, the team needs help (resources, scope reduction, escalation)
- If confidence is at 90% by week 4, the KR was too easy — note for next quarter

## Sizing — how many OKRs

| Level | OKRs |
|---|---|
| Team (per quarter) | 1 Objective with 3 KRs (sometimes 2 Os with 3 KRs each, max) |
| Individual | 1–2 KRs they own toward the team's OKRs (not separate personal OKRs) |
| Practice-level (annual) | 2–3 Objectives with 3 KRs each |
| Firm-level | Beyond this skill's scope |

**The most common mistake: too many OKRs.** Teams that have 5 Objectives have no priorities. Teams that have 1 Objective and 3 KRs have priorities.

Killing OKRs is harder than adding them. The discipline is: which one matters most this quarter, and which 4 things will we do less of to focus on it?

## The weekly cadence — Monday commit / Friday celebrate

This is the operating system. Without it, OKRs become quarterly paperwork.

### Monday commit (30 min)

Each team member presents:
- **My P1, P2, P3 for this week** (3 priorities, ranked)
- **Which KR each priority advances**
- **Confidence check** on each KR they own

The team:
- Notices misalignment ("nothing on the list advances KR2 — is that right?")
- Identifies dependencies and offers help
- Re-allocates if needed (someone overloaded; someone with slack)

By end of meeting: the team has a public commitment to the week's work. P1/P2/P3 are visible and tied to OKRs.

### Friday celebrate (30 min)

Each team member shares:
- **What I shipped this week** (specific, named)
- **What blocked me / didn't get done**
- **A win I want to recognize from someone else**

The team:
- Celebrates publicly (named, specific, by other team members not just the manager)
- Surfaces patterns of blockage
- Updates KR progress

### Why both halves matter

Monday alone produces commitment without recognition. Friday alone produces ritual gratitude without accountability. The pair creates a rhythm that compounds.

Wodtke's stronger claim: the Friday celebration is the leadership move that retains people. Recognition of specific work, in front of peers, weekly, is the highest-ROI motivation practice. It costs 30 minutes; it produces engagement that no comp adjustment can buy.

### Quarterly cadence

| Cadence | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Pre-quarter (1 week before) | Draft OKRs; the leader proposes, team contributes; revise; commit |
| Week 1 | Kickoff: re-share OKRs; first Monday commit |
| Mid-quarter (week 6 or 7) | Mid-quarter review: KR confidence, course correction |
| Final 2 weeks | Push hard on what's achievable; honestly grade what's not |
| Post-quarter (1 week after) | Retro: what worked, what didn't, what to change for next quarter |

## Mid-quarter review

At week 6 or 7:
- Each KR owner reports current state and confidence
- Team discusses: what's at risk, what's on track, what needs help
- Decisions:
  - **Push** (we'll find a way to hit it)
  - **Descope** (we'll redefine the KR — explicitly, with reasoning)
  - **Escalate** (we need help we can't provide ourselves)
  - **Accept** (we'll miss this one; what do we learn)

The discipline of explicit descoping — versus quiet drift — is what makes the team comfortable being honest the next quarter. Hidden failure damages trust; openly-named descoping doesn't.

## End-of-quarter retro

Honest scoring of each KR. Not for blame; for learning.

- 100% (we hit it): probably too easy; calibrate up next quarter
- 70%: in the right zone; the KR was real and stretching
- 50%: stretchy but missed; what got in the way?
- 30%: missed badly; what was wrong (the KR, the resources, the situation)?

Then forward-looking:
- What conditions enabled the wins?
- What conditions prevented them?
- What changes for next quarter — in OKRs, in process, in resources?

Document the retro. The compounding effect of quarterly retros is what makes a team systematically better at OKRs over years.

## Anti-patterns (the executioner's tale)

Wodtke's fable shows several common failure modes. The biggest:

### Golden apples

Distractions that show up mid-quarter and pull the team off the OKR. A speaking slot. A new client opportunity. A "small" feature request from leadership. Each is plausible; cumulatively they kill the OKR.

The discipline: when something new lands, ask "does this advance our OKR? If not, can it wait until next quarter? If it can't, what are we trading off?" Make the trade-off visible. Don't silently absorb.

### Yes to everything

The team commits to the OKR but also to every other piece of work that lands. Result: nothing gets the focus it needs. The "stop doing" list is critical.

### OKRs unchanged from previous quarter

If your Q2 OKRs are mostly the same as Q1, either Q1 succeeded fully (you should celebrate and move on) or Q1 failed and you're hoping repetition fixes it (it won't). Either way, write new OKRs that reflect what you actually learned.

### OKRs as performance review

OKRs are about team / org goals, not individual performance assessment. Tying them too tightly to performance reviews creates the gaming behaviors that destroy them (sandbagging KRs, fighting over credit, hiding failures).

### Cascading too literally

Avoid making everyone's individual OKRs a sub-set of their manager's. The team-level OKR is real; individual contributors hold *parts* of the team's KRs, not their own personal mini-OKRs.

### Too many OKRs

Already covered. The single biggest mistake. Cut.

## OKRs at multiple levels

For Bermon's role specifically:

- **Practice-level (Composable DXP):** 2–3 Objectives, 3 KRs each. Annual horizon, with quarterly check-ins.
- **Team-level (per engagement, per function):** 1 Objective, 3 KRs. Quarterly.
- **Individual:** held by team-level KRs they own, not separate personal OKRs.

The discipline of practice-level OKRs makes Bermon's "what are we optimizing for?" question concrete and shareable upward (with VP / GM) and downward (with EMs and senior practitioners).

## The "stop doing" list

Wodtke (and Collins, in *Good to Great*) make this point: the list of things you'll *stop doing* this quarter is as important as the list you'll start. Without it, the OKR is additive on top of an already-full plate, and the team will choke.

For each new OKR, name: what existing work are we deprioritizing or killing to make room? If "nothing," the OKR isn't real.
