# Saying no and killing priorities

The discipline that distinguishes teams with priorities from teams with wishlists. Adding to the list is easy; subtracting is the senior leader's actual work.

## The premise

A practice / team has finite capacity. New priorities don't *add* to the existing list — they *displace* something. If you don't choose what they displace, the team chooses for you, and the choice is usually what's hardest, what's longest-arc, or what's most strategically important (because the urgent fights its way back in).

Every yes is a no to something else. The senior leader's job is to make the no explicit instead of letting it happen by drift.

## The "stop doing" list

Collins's *Good to Great* concept: alongside your "to do" list, maintain a "stop doing" list. Things that were once a priority and aren't anymore. Things you used to invest in that no longer earn the investment. Initiatives that limp on past their usefulness.

For a senior consulting practice leader:

- Engagements outside the hedgehog you'll stop pursuing
- Speaking/conference slots that don't compound (the conference is fine; the speaking-investment ROI is low)
- Internal initiatives that no longer drive outcomes
- Recurring meetings you're attending out of habit
- Reports you're producing that no one acts on
- Time spent on tactical work that should have been delegated months ago
- Relationships you're maintaining out of nostalgia rather than mutual value

Updated quarterly. Visible to your team (signals you take saying-no seriously).

## The trade-off conversation

When something new lands, the discipline is:

> "Yes — and here's what we're going to deprioritize to make room."

If you can't name what you're deprioritizing, the team is overcommitted by definition.

The conversation with leadership when they ask for a new initiative:

> "We can do this. Given our quarterly OKRs and capacity, taking it on means we'd need to deprioritize [X] or [Y]. Which of those is your preference, or do you want to pick a third?"

This forces the asker (often above you in the hierarchy) to confront the trade-off. Sometimes they'll say "yeah, deprioritize X." Sometimes they'll realize the new initiative isn't worth the trade and withdraw the ask. Either is a good outcome.

The bad outcome: silently absorb the new ask on top of the existing OKRs. The team stretches further; quality drops; the OKRs slip; everyone is exhausted; nothing was explicitly chosen.

## The "no" patterns

### The clean no

> "Not this quarter. Here's why: [specific OKR + capacity]. Let's revisit at Q4 planning."

### The conditional no

> "Yes if [condition]. Without [condition], we can't take this on without breaking [other commitment]."

### The proxy no

> "I'm not the right person — [other team] is closer to this. Let me make the introduction."

### The pre-mortem no

> "Let me pre-mortem this with you. If we did this and it succeeded, what would we get? If it failed, what would we lose? Given our other commitments, is the expected value worth the displacement?"

This often surfaces the answer "actually, never mind" without you having to be the heavy.

### The hedgehog no

> "This is outside what we're choosing to be best in the world at. We could do it; we wouldn't be world-class at it. Better to refer to [partner / other team] and stay focused."

## What's hardest to say no to

The patterns that consistently break senior leaders' priority discipline:

- **Asks from above.** The CEO / VP / GM asks; you say yes reflexively. Practice the trade-off conversation instead.
- **Plausible-sounding new opportunities.** A new vertical, a new partnership, a new offering. They sound smart; they're often outside the hedgehog. Apply the lens.
- **Emergencies that turn out to be non-emergencies.** You drop everything; later it turns out the emergency was perception, not reality. Build a 30-min "is this real?" buffer before fully committing.
- **Loyal asks from peer leaders.** A peer asks for help; you say yes; the help eats your week. Sometimes right; often a quiet displacement of your own priorities.
- **Hobby horses.** Things you personally find interesting but that don't earn the practice's time. Hardest because the resistance comes from you.

## How to actually do it

### Audit current commitments

List everything the practice is doing this quarter:
- The OKRs explicitly committed
- The recurring meetings, reports, ceremonies
- The active engagements (with their resource intensity)
- The pursuits in flight
- The hiring loops open
- The internal initiatives and side-projects
- The personal commitments you've made that the team is on the hook for

Most senior leaders, doing this audit honestly, are shocked by the length.

### For each, ask:

1. Does this advance our OKRs / hedgehog?
2. If we stopped doing it, what would be lost?
3. Who actually cares if we keep doing it?
4. Is the alternative use of this time more valuable?

### Draft a "stop doing" list

3–5 items. For each:
- What you're stopping
- Why
- What the alternative use of the freed time will be
- Who needs to be told
- When it stops

### Communicate

Share the "stop doing" list with your boss, your team, and any affected stakeholders. The transparency is the discipline. Stopping in secret invites silent re-litigation.

### Hold the line

The first time you stop something visible, people will push back. ("But we've always…" "But [stakeholder] expects…") The first stop is the hardest; subsequent ones get easier as the team sees you actually mean it.

## Saying no inside the team

Senior leaders also have to enforce no for their team. When a direct report comes with "we should also do X this quarter," the conversation:

> "Help me understand — what do we displace? You're already at capacity on [the OKR work]. Adding X means moving something off the plate. Which?"

If they can't name it, the answer is no. If they can name it, you might say yes — but you've forced the trade-off into the open.

This is the discipline that prevents OKR drift inside the team.

## When yes is the right call

Saying no consistently doesn't mean reflexively rejecting everything. Sometimes:
- A new opportunity is genuinely better than what's on the plate (replace, don't add)
- A short emergency genuinely demands the team
- A strategic shift means the OKRs themselves should change

The discipline is *deliberateness*, not refusal. Each yes is a chosen trade-off, not a default reflex.

## The cost of not doing this

Without priority discipline:
- The team is busy but not making strategic progress
- Quality drops on the things that matter
- Senior people leave (their time gets fragmented; nothing they care about gets the focus it needs)
- The flywheel doesn't build (every quarter pulls in a different direction)
- You spend the year fighting fires instead of building the practice

The cost compounds over years. The cost of saying no is felt in the moment (someone is disappointed); the cost of not saying no is felt across the year (nothing meaningful gets built).
