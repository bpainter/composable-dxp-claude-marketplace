# Q2 prioritization and Big Rocks (Covey)

The framework most leaders nominally know and most leaders ignore in practice. Covey's quadrants for time management:

| | Urgent | Not urgent |
|---|---|---|
| **Important** | **Q1** — crises, deadline-driven crises, pressing problems | **Q2** — strategic work, prevention, planning, relationships, learning, recreation |
| **Not important** | **Q3** — interruptions, some calls/emails, "urgent" requests from others, popular activities | **Q4** — busywork, time-wasters, trivia, some calls/emails |

Most senior leaders live in Q1 + Q3 and never do real Q2 work. The compounding cost: more crises (because Q2 prevention was skipped), worse strategy (Q2 thinking skipped), worse relationships (Q2 investment skipped), worse recovery (Q2 renewal skipped).

The discipline: place Q2 priorities in the calendar *first*, then let Q1 and Q3 fit around them. Reverse the default.

## What Q2 actually contains for a senior leader

The list of things you know matter but never seem to get to:

- **Strategy thinking time.** Where is the practice going? What are we optimizing for? What should we stop doing? *Almost always Q2.*
- **People investment beyond 1:1s.** Career conversations, sponsorship, succession planning, intentional development.
- **Relationship-building.** Peer leaders, cross-capability partners, key clients (when there's no fire). Coffees and check-ins with no immediate purpose.
- **Learning.** Reading, conferences, courses, conversations with peers in adjacent fields.
- **Writing.** External thought leadership, internal frameworks, decisions you want to be deliberate about.
- **Personal renewal.** Exercise, sleep, family, hobbies. The "sharpen the saw" Habit 7.
- **Process improvement.** The system change that would prevent the crisis from recurring (instead of just fighting today's crisis).
- **Hiring ahead of need.** Sourcing senior people you'll need in 6 months but don't yet feel the pain for.

These all share a property: they don't have urgent deadlines. They're easy to defer. And they compound — every quarter you do them, you reap benefits next year. Every quarter you skip them, you push the cost into next year, plus a small recurring penalty.

## What Q1 + Q3 look like

| Q1 | Q3 |
|---|---|
| Client escalation — fix today | Slack ping — needs a response |
| Pursuit deadline this week | "Quick question" walk-up |
| Direct report's project on fire | Meeting that someone else cares about |
| Major engagement risk | Minor stakeholder concern |
| Calibration deadline | Standing meeting drift |

Q1 is real and unavoidable; the goal is to *minimize* Q1 by doing Q2 prevention. You'll never eliminate Q1 entirely.

Q3 is the trap. It feels productive (you're responding! you're available!) but it's mostly low-value work that the urgency-language disguises.

## The Big Rocks practice

Covey's metaphor: a jar, sand, gravel, big rocks. If you fill the jar with sand and gravel first, the big rocks don't fit. If you put the big rocks in first, the sand and gravel fill in around them.

Operationalized:

1. **Identify your Q2 Big Rocks for the week** (3–5; not more)
2. **Block them in the calendar Sunday or Monday morning, first**
3. **Let other meetings and asks fit around them**
4. **At end of week: review.** How many Big Rocks landed?

For a senior leader, typical weekly Big Rocks:
- 2–4 hours of strategy thinking time (one block, defended)
- 1 career-development conversation (with a direct or skip-level)
- 1 peer-leader coffee or call (no agenda; relationship)
- 1 hour of writing or learning
- 1 hour of personal renewal (exercise, family, hobby)

That's 7–10 hours of Big Rocks per week. The rest of the week (30+ hours) absorbs the Q1 and necessary Q3.

## The audit

Print or screenshot your last week. For each block, label:
- Q1 (important, urgent)
- Q2 (important, not urgent)
- Q3 (urgent, not important)
- Q4 (busywork)

Senior leaders' typical reads:
- Q1: 30–50%
- Q2: 5–15% (often much less)
- Q3: 30–50%
- Q4: 5–15%

The goal is to push Q2 to 25%+ over a quarter, primarily by killing or async-ing Q3.

## Common Q2 dodges

The reasons leaders give for skipping Q2, and what's really happening:

**"I don't have time."**
You have the same 168 hours as anyone. The question is what fills them. If Q3 fills first, Q2 doesn't fit.

**"It's not as visible."**
True. Q2 doesn't generate quick recognition. It compounds slowly. You're choosing slower visibility now for outsized leverage later.

**"There's always something more urgent."**
Yes. There always will be. Q2 only happens if you defend it against the always-something-more-urgent.

**"I'll do Q2 next week."**
Next week will look the same. The leaders who do Q2 do it this week.

**"I'm doing Q2 by working extra hours after the urgent stuff."**
That's not Q2 — it's Q1 fatigue. Q2 done when you're already depleted produces low-quality output. Schedule Q2 in your best hours, not your residual ones.

## Defending Q2 from interruption

Specific tactics:

- **Block as "thinking" or "strategic time" — unavailable, not tentative**
- **Out-of-office in the morning** (so people don't expect responsiveness)
- **No-meeting day** (one full day a week with no internal meetings)
- **Pair with personal disciplines** — Q2 thinking happens better with rest, exercise, and reflection (see `manage-yourself.md`)
- **Have an EA / scheduling discipline** that protects the slots
- **Communicate to the team** that you're protecting these hours and what they're for

The team will respect protected Q2 time once they see it produces results (better strategy, fewer crises). The first quarter is the hardest; the team will test whether you'll really hold the line.

## Q2 over a year

The compounding effect: 7–10 hours/week of Q2 across a year is 350–500 hours. That's enough for:

- The strategic plan that matters
- The 4 senior hires that change the practice
- The published thought leadership that lifts the practice's brand
- The peer relationships that produce deal flow
- The 3–4 capabilities you build that will be the practice's hedgehog in 3 years

Without it, the same year produces: a lot of urgent fires fought, no compounding capabilities built, and the same set of structural issues you started with.

## For Bermon specifically

Q2 candidates that are easy to defer in this role:

- Quarterly practice retrospective (where is the practice actually going? not GTM ceremonies, real strategy)
- 1:1 coffees with peer Senior Directors in adjacent capabilities (the relationships that produce cross-cap deals)
- Composable DXP industry tracking (vendor moves, competitor moves, technology shifts) — easy to defer, expensive to skip
- Senior practitioner sponsorship (specific people you're betting on; investing time in their growth)
- Personal writing / external thought leadership
- The asset / accelerator backlog (Composable DXP capabilities that compound across engagements)

If these aren't on the calendar this week, they're already at risk for the quarter.
