# Team priority rituals — Monday commit / Friday celebrate

The operating cadence Wodtke prescribes around OKRs. Without it, OKRs become quarterly paperwork. With it, OKRs become the team's actual operating system.

## The two rituals

### Monday commit (30 min)

Each team member presents:
- **My P1, P2, P3 for this week** — three priorities, ranked
- **Which Key Result each priority advances** — explicit tie to the OKR
- **Confidence on the KR I own this quarter** — current 1–10

The team:
- Notices misalignment ("nothing on the list advances KR2 — is that intentional?")
- Identifies dependencies and offers help
- Re-allocates if someone is overloaded and someone has slack

By end: the team has a shared, visible commitment to the week's work, with explicit OKR tie.

### Friday celebrate (30 min)

Each team member shares:
- **What I shipped or substantially advanced this week** — specific, named
- **What blocked me / didn't get done**
- **A win I want to recognize from someone else** — by name, with what they did

The team:
- Celebrates publicly, with peer recognition (not just manager-down)
- Surfaces patterns of blockage to address next week
- Updates KR progress

By end: the team has closed the week with explicit recognition and an honest read of progress.

## Why this works

- **Public commitment.** Saying P1/P2/P3 to peers is harder to break than saying it to yourself. Social accountability is real.
- **OKR pressure-testing.** When the priorities don't tie to KRs, you notice. When they do, the OKR feels alive.
- **Pattern detection.** Same person blocked on the same kind of thing 3 weeks running? You can act.
- **Recognition compounds.** Specific, peer-driven, public recognition is the highest-ROI motivation practice. Costs 30 min/week; produces engagement that no comp adjustment matches.
- **Calibration.** The team learns who systematically over-commits, who sandbags, who is reliable. Useful info for the manager and for peers.

## Format details

### The P1/P2/P3 worksheet

Simple. Each person fills in before Monday commit:

```
This week's priorities

P1 — [specific outcome] | KR# [advances which KR] | Confidence after this week: __
P2 — [specific outcome] | KR# | Confidence: __
P3 — [specific outcome] | KR# | Confidence: __

What I'd want help on this week:

What I'm worried about:
```

Posted in shared doc / Slack / wherever the team works. Visible to peers, not just manager.

### The Friday wins worksheet

```
This week

Shipped / advanced:
- [specific]
- [specific]
- [specific]

Didn't ship that I committed to:
- [specific] — what got in the way

KR progress this week:
- KR1: [previous] → [current]
- KR2: [previous] → [current]
- KR3: [previous] → [current]

I want to recognize:
- [Name] — for [specific thing]
```

The recognition piece is critical. The manager calls on each person; everyone names someone (rotating across weeks; ideally everyone gets named over a quarter).

## Common failures

### "We don't have time for two meetings."

It's 60 min total. The team's existing standing meetings probably absorb 4+ hours/week. Convert one of them to commit/celebrate; don't add on top.

### Monday commit becomes a status read-out

It can. The discipline is to keep it tight (30 min hard cap) and focus on the *next* week's commitments, not the past week's status.

### Friday celebrate becomes a complaint session

Some weeks are bad weeks. People legitimately need to vent about blockers. But if every Friday is a vent, you have a different problem (likely a structural one), and the celebrate ritual is a symptom.

The fix: explicitly structure the meeting so wins come first, blockers come second, recognition comes third. Don't let the order flip.

### Manager monopolizes recognition

If the manager is the only one giving recognition, the ritual is top-down. Push it to peers. Each person names someone. Manager goes last and brief.

### Confidence numbers are theater

If everyone reports "8" on every KR every week, the numbers aren't honest. Two fixes:
- Lead by example — the manager shares their own confidence, including downward shifts
- Reward honest downward updates ("I shifted from 7 to 4 because I learned X" is more useful than "still at 7")

### People skip Monday or Friday

Same rule as 1:1s — defend it. If people skip, the ritual loses force fast. If circumstances genuinely require flex, move it; don't cancel.

### Drift over the quarter

Around week 6–8, the rituals start feeling routine. The fix: change the format slightly (open with a different question; rotate facilitator; do a "what do we want to keep doing / change" check). A small refresh resets attention.

## When to add a mid-week check

For larger or more distributed teams (6+ people, or split time zones), a 15-min mid-week check (Wednesday) can be useful:

- Quick standup-style: what's on, what's blocked, who needs help
- 90 seconds per person, max
- Async-acceptable (written check-in instead of meeting)

This catches blockers earlier than waiting until Friday.

For small co-located teams, Monday + Friday is enough. Don't add mid-week unless real.

## Rolling out the ritual

If the team has never done this:

1. **Brief the team** in advance: what we're trying, why, format, duration. 5-min explainer.
2. **Do a trial month** (4 cycles of Monday/Friday). Don't dilute the format the first month.
3. **At week 4: retro the ritual itself.** What's working, what to adjust. Keep going for 4 more weeks.
4. **At week 12: real review.** Did it improve OKR completion? Did it improve team engagement? If yes, sustain. If not, redesign or kill — don't drift.

Most teams that try the ritual honestly for 3 months adopt it permanently. The teams that drop it usually never gave it the discipline.

## For Bermon's specific context

For a Senior Director with 5 EMs reporting in: the ritual works at *team* level, not at *Senior Director's direct reports* level. Each EM should run Monday/Friday with their team. The Senior Director participates in their own peer-leadership Monday/Friday with the EMs.

At the EM-team level, P1/P2/P3 ties to the team's quarterly OKRs.
At the Senior Director level, P1/P2/P3 ties to the practice-level OKRs.

The two rhythms reinforce each other. The practice leader sees what the teams are committing to (via the EMs in the Monday commit), and the teams see what the practice leader is committing to (via the leader's own commitments at the EM-staff level).
