# Hedgehog, Level 5, Flywheel (Collins)

The mental models from *Good to Great* that hold up two decades later. Less specific than OKRs, but they shape *what the OKRs should be focused on* — the longer-arc thinking that prevents the practice from drifting from quarter to quarter without compounding.

## The Hedgehog Concept

From Isaiah Berlin's essay: "The fox knows many things, but the hedgehog knows one big thing."

Collins's claim: companies that go from good to great develop a deep, simple understanding of one big thing — the intersection of three circles:

1. **What you can be the best in the world at** (and equally important: what you cannot)
2. **What you are deeply passionate about**
3. **What drives your economic engine** (your "denominator" — profit per X)

Sustained greatness comes from disciplined focus at the intersection. Most organizations stay scattered because they refuse to confront circles 1 or 3 honestly: "We could be best in the world at lots of things if we just tried." No, you couldn't.

### Worked example for the Composable DXP practice

| Circle | Question | Possible answers |
|---|---|---|
| Best in the world at | Where can this practice genuinely be world-class — not just competent? | Composable headless commerce architecture for mid-market retail? Composable DXP transformation for legacy CMS replatforms? End-to-end Composable DXP partner from strategy through run? |
| Passionate about | What does this team get fired up about? | The craft of headless / composable architecture? The transformation impact for clients? Building reusable patterns and accelerators? |
| Drives the economics | What unit economics does the practice run on? Revenue per architect? Per engagement? Average deal size × number of deals? | E.g., "revenue per Senior Architect per quarter," with a target that requires high-margin work and senior staffing |

The intersection might be: *world-class at composable headless commerce for mid-market retail; passionate about transforming legacy customer experience; economics driven by senior-architect revenue × repeat-engagement multiplier.*

That's a hedgehog. It tells you what to say yes to (mid-market retail composable engagements with strong senior-architect leverage) and what to say no to (one-off small projects, large-enterprise commodity replatforms with junior staffing, vertical adjacencies where you have no real edge).

### How to develop the hedgehog

Collins is clear: this is not an away-day exercise. It takes 2–4 years of disciplined thinking, with intentional re-examination. Start now; revisit every 6 months; expect the answer to sharpen over time.

Start with:
- Honest read of where the practice has actually won (and why) vs. where it has competed but not won
- Where senior practitioners are most engaged and producing best work
- What the unit economics look like across engagement types
- Where the market is heading (so you don't optimize for a hedgehog that the market is leaving behind)

The hedgehog is not the practice's tagline. It's the underlying truth that the tagline (and the OKRs) should reflect.

### Hedgehog discipline

Once the hedgehog clarifies, the disciplined leader says no to opportunities outside it — even tempting ones. Collins's data: companies that broke their hedgehog discipline (acquired adjacent businesses, expanded into adjacent markets, took on big clients outside the focus) underperformed companies that stayed disciplined.

For Bermon: the temptation will be to say yes to every plausible engagement. The hedgehog discipline says: only engagements at the intersection. The rest are distractions, even when revenue-positive in the short term.

## Level 5 Leadership

Collins's research surprise: every company that made the good-to-great leap had a Level 5 leader at the helm. The Level 5 leader is characterized by a combination of two traits often considered opposite:

- **Personal humility.** Quiet. Doesn't seek the limelight. Credits the team for success. Takes responsibility for failure.
- **Professional will.** Fierce resolve to do whatever it takes to make the company great. Sets high standards relentlessly. Makes hard calls.

Level 5 leaders are different from Level 4 (charismatic visionaries who often produce short-term spikes followed by decline once they leave). The defining characteristic of Level 5 is that the institution is built to outlive them.

### What this looks like in practice

- They credit the team publicly and specifically; they take blame personally and visibly
- They make the hard call no one else will make — and don't milk the credit
- They focus on the long arc; they're suspicious of their own personal brand
- They build successors deliberately; the institution is the point
- They look in the mirror to assign responsibility and out the window to assign credit (the inverse of most leaders)

### Level 5 self-assessment

For Bermon (or any senior leader), honest questions:

- When the practice succeeds, who do I credit publicly? Specifically?
- When something fails, do I take responsibility or look for the explanation that protects me?
- Am I building a successor? Could the practice run without me? Is that my goal?
- Do I make the hard calls others avoid?
- Is my external presence about the practice, or about me?
- When I leave the role someday, what do I want the institution to look like?

These aren't softball questions. The Level 5 mindset is genuinely uncommon. Aspirational for most senior leaders, including the high-performing ones.

## First Who, Then What

Collins's claim: great companies got the right people on the bus, the wrong people off, and the right people in the right seats — *before* they figured out where to drive. Strategy followed team.

This inverts the usual pattern (set strategy, then hire to execute). Collins's argument: in a world of change, you can't pick a strategy that holds for years. So you don't hire to a fixed strategy; you build a team that can navigate whatever comes, then figure out where to go.

For a consulting practice, this means:
- Hiring decisions are *strategic* decisions, not staffing decisions
- The bar on senior hires is "would this person make the practice better forever, even if I don't yet know exactly what they'd do?"
- The "wrong person" call is just as critical — keeping a wrong-fit person on the bus damages the right people

Connection to the hedgehog: the hedgehog tells you what kind of team you need. The team then sharpens the hedgehog. The two evolve together.

## The Flywheel and the Doom Loop

Collins's metaphor for sustained greatness: a massive flywheel. Each push moves it slightly. No single push is the breakthrough. But pushed consistently in the same direction over years, the flywheel builds momentum that becomes nearly impossible to stop.

The Doom Loop is the opposite pattern: each year a new strategy, a new direction, a new "transformation initiative." Each push goes in a different direction; no momentum compounds. The organization works hard and goes nowhere.

### What this means for OKRs

OKRs that pull in a *consistent* direction over multiple quarters compound. OKRs that swing wildly each quarter don't.

For the Composable DXP practice, this implies:
- Quarterly OKRs should mostly be in service of the same multi-year arc (the hedgehog)
- A new direction every quarter is a Doom Loop signal
- The flywheel takes years to build; the impatience to "show progress this quarter" can break it

### The Stockdale Paradox

Within the flywheel discussion, Collins introduces Admiral Stockdale's paradox: maintain unwavering faith that you will prevail in the end (regardless of the difficulties), and at the same time confront the most brutal facts of your current reality (whatever they may be).

The combination is the point. Optimism without honest reality is delusion (the people in the POW camps who said "we'll be home by Christmas" died first). Reality without faith is despair.

For a practice leader: hold the long-arc conviction (the hedgehog will compound, the practice will grow into what it should be) while honestly confronting current data (where pipeline is, where attrition is, where the work is mediocre).

## Confront the brutal facts

Adjacent to the Stockdale Paradox: great companies create cultures where the brutal facts surface up the org. People bring bad news without fear. Leaders ask hard questions and listen to the answers.

For Bermon: the practical practices are:
- Ask "what's the brutal fact you're not telling me?" in 1:1s
- Reward people who bring bad news early
- Penalize the surfacing of bad news late, not the news itself
- Run honest retros (60–70% completion is honest; 100% is sandbagged or low-set)
- Skip-levels to test whether the brutal facts are reaching you (see `skip-level-meetings.md`)

## How these shape OKR-setting

When setting OKRs for a quarter, run them through the Collins lens:

- **Hedgehog:** is this OKR at the intersection of our three circles? If not, why are we doing it?
- **Level 5:** does this OKR build the institution, or my personal brand?
- **First Who:** is the team aligned with this OKR? Are the right people in the right seats to execute it?
- **Flywheel:** is this OKR another push in the same long-arc direction, or a swerve to something new?
- **Brutal facts:** what's the data we're avoiding? What would change this OKR if we faced it?

The OKR survives all five lenses → write it. Fails one or more → reconsider.
