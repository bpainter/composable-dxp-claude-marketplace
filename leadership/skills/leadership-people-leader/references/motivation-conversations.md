# Motivation conversations

When a direct report is disengaged, going through the motions, or producing flatter work than usual — the conversation about it. Most managers handle this badly: either they don't notice, or they ask "is everything okay?" and accept "yeah, fine" as the end of it.

This reference is the playbook for the motivation conversation, grounded in Pink's AMP framework (see `motivation-amp-diagnosis.md` in `leadership-goals-and-okrs` for the deeper theory).

## The signal that prompts the conversation

You notice in your direct:
- Quieter than usual in meetings
- Output quality slightly degraded (or unchanged but the engagement evident in it has diminished)
- Side conversations / extracurriculars increasing
- Slower response times on async work
- Vague unhappiness in 1:1s
- A sense of "going through the motions"
- Cynicism creeping into their language about the work
- Loss of curiosity (they used to ask questions; they don't anymore)

One signal is data. Three or more sustained over weeks is the prompt.

## Don't open with "is everything okay?"

It's the worst question. It invites "yeah, fine" and ends the conversation. Most people, when asked that, don't have the language ready to say what's actually going on.

Better openers:

> "I want to check in on something. I've noticed [specific observation: you've been quieter in staff for the last few weeks; your engagement on the [project] feels different than it did at the start; you've stopped raising the architecture questions you used to bring]. Walk me through what's going on for you?"

The specificity does the work. The person knows you actually noticed something specific; they can't deflect with "yeah, all good" because you've named the observation.

## The conversation structure

### Open with observation (3 min)

Specific. Behavior-based. Not interpretation.

> "I've noticed [X] in the past [Y weeks]. I might be reading it wrong, but it feels different from where you were [earlier reference point]. I want to understand what's going on."

### Listen (10–15 min)

Then *actually listen*. The Stanier / coaching habit applies (see `coaching-habit-7-questions.md`):
- "What's on your mind right now?"
- "And what else?"
- "What's the real challenge here for you?"

Sit with silence. Don't rescue. Don't problem-solve mid-listening.

The honest answer often takes 5–10 minutes to surface. The first answer is the polite one. The second adds context. The third or fourth, often after silence, is the real thing.

### Diagnose using AMP (5 min)

Once you have the real answer, sort it (silently or together) using Pink's AMP framework:

- **Autonomy constrained?** They don't choose what they work on, when, how, or with whom — and used to.
- **Mastery flat?** They're not stretching, not learning, not building anything new. The work has become routine.
- **Purpose disconnected?** The work has become transactional; they don't feel connected to outcomes that matter to them.

Often it's two of three. The conversation focuses on the most-constrained driver.

### Co-design the next step (5–10 min)

Don't unilaterally announce the fix. Co-create:

> "Given what you've said, it sounds like the biggest gap is [autonomy / mastery / purpose]. What would help?"

Their answer often surprises you. They've been thinking about this longer than you have. Take their input seriously even if it requires you to work to make it happen.

Possible interventions for each:

**Autonomy gaps:**
- Give them latitude on a specific decision they'd been having to ask permission for
- Carve out a self-directed time block weekly
- Move them to a project where they have more *how* control
- Stop micromanaging a specific area (and tell them you're stopping)

**Mastery gaps:**
- Stretch assignment outside their current comfort zone
- Coaching plan around a specific skill they want to build (see `delegation-and-feedback-cascade.md`)
- Conference / training investment
- Mentor relationship with someone outside your team
- Change of scope to a problem they don't yet know how to solve

**Purpose gaps:**
- Connect them visibly to a client outcome that matters
- Move them onto an engagement with mission-fit
- Surface the impact of their work in ways they can see
- A practice-level conversation about what the team is for, and how their work fits

### Close with commitment (2 min)

Specific. Time-bound. Both sides own a commitment.

> "Here's what I'm hearing: [recap]. Here's what I'm going to do: [your commitment]. Here's what you're going to do: [their commitment]. Let's check in on this in [timeframe]."

Document. Track. Follow up.

## When the answer is "I'm thinking about leaving"

Sometimes the motivation conversation surfaces that the person is considering leaving. Two options:

### Option A — They're early-stage thinking; the conversation might shift it

Engage. Genuinely. What would have to change for them to stay? Some answers are within your power; some aren't. Be honest about which.

If you can address the real driver (autonomy gap, mastery flat, scope mismatch), do it visibly and quickly. The person needs to see you take it seriously.

If you can't address the real driver (they want to leave the firm; they want a different city; they've outgrown the ladder), don't pretend you can. Honor their thinking. Sometimes the role isn't the right next step for them, and the kind move is to acknowledge it.

### Option B — They've decided; the conversation is informational

Don't try to undo a real decision. Honor it. Help them transition well. Maintain the relationship for the long arc — these are often people who become valuable parts of your network later.

The mistake to avoid: getting defensive or punishing them for the honesty. The next person who's thinking about leaving will hide it from you, which is much worse.

## When the answer is "I don't know what's wrong"

Sometimes people genuinely don't know. They feel off and can't articulate why.

Useful follow-ups:
- "When did this start, even roughly?"
- "What's something at work that used to energize you that doesn't now?"
- "If we changed one thing, what would have the biggest effect?"
- "Has anything changed outside work that might be playing into this?"

Sometimes the honest answer is "I'm not sure; let me think about it." Schedule a follow-up. Don't force resolution in one conversation.

## When the issue is outside work

Sometimes the answer is genuinely outside the work — health, family, life event. The role of the manager:

- Listen and acknowledge
- Don't try to fix what isn't yours to fix
- Offer flexibility if appropriate (workload reduction, time off, schedule flexibility)
- Refer to EAP / benefits resources if appropriate
- Don't probe for personal details they haven't volunteered

The line: you care about them as a whole person, but you're not their therapist. Support, refer, give them space, hold the role's accountability lightly during a hard period.

## When you're the cause

Sometimes the motivation conversation surfaces that *you* are part of the problem — micromanaging, withholding scope, unclear feedback, bad cadence.

The discipline:
- Listen without defending
- Acknowledge ("you're right, I have been doing X")
- Commit to a specific change ("starting next week, I'm going to do Y")
- Follow through (the commitment without follow-through is worse than not asking)

This is harder than it sounds. The instinct is to defend. The Radical Candor / Stoic discipline is to take the feedback as data, not attack.

## Cadence and follow-up

After the initial conversation:
- Quick check-in within a week ("how are you feeling about the change we discussed?")
- Substantive check-in at 30 days ("is the [intervention] making the difference we hoped?")
- Recalibrate at 60 days if needed

The motivation conversation is rarely one event. It's a thread you pick up across weeks. The cadence is the value.

## What if motivation doesn't recover

Sometimes you make the change and motivation doesn't return. Possibilities:
- The change wasn't the right one (try another)
- The role itself is wrong for the person right now (different scope, different team, transition out)
- The person is dealing with something outside the role you can't see
- Mismatch between the person and the firm at this stage

The honest conversation might be: "We've tried [X], it hasn't moved the needle. What's your read?" Sometimes the person knows the answer is that this isn't the right role for them, and the conversation surfaces it.

Don't drag chronic disengagement indefinitely. Either the conditions can change or the role can change or the person can change roles. Drift damages everyone.
