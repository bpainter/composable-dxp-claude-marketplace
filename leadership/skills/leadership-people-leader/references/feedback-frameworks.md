# Feedback Frameworks: Giving Feedback That Lands

## The SBI Model: Situation-Behavior-Impact

The most effective feedback follows this structure: describe what you observed (Situation), the specific action (Behavior), and why it mattered (Impact).

### Structure
1. **Situation**: Context. When did this happen? What was the setting?
2. **Behavior**: The specific action you observed (not interpretation, not impact yet)
3. **Impact**: How did this affect the team, project, or person?

### Example (Positive)
- **Situation**: "In the architecture review meeting yesterday..."
- **Behavior**: "You asked clarifying questions about the team's concerns before proposing a solution."
- **Impact**: "That approach let people feel heard, and we got better buy-in on the final decision. It's a great model for how to lead."

### Example (Developmental)
- **Situation**: "In our 1:1 last Tuesday, you mentioned you're overwhelmed with project scope."
- **Behavior**: "Rather than asking what constraints we could work with, you said 'I'll just work harder.'"
- **Impact**: "That worried me, because it suggests you might be taking on too much without talking to me first. I want you to know it's okay to say no or ask for help."

### Why SBI Works
- **Removes defensiveness**: You're describing what you saw, not judging character
- **Makes it actionable**: The person knows exactly what to change
- **Connects action to outcome**: They understand why this matters

## Radical Candor: Care Personally + Challenge Directly

Radical Candor is being both direct (not softening the truth) and caring (invested in the person's growth).

### The 2x2 Matrix

| | **Challenge Directly** | **Don't Challenge Directly** |
|---|---|---|
| **Care Personally** | **Radical Candor** (Great feedback) | **Ruinous Empathy** (Overly kind, unhelpful) |
| **Don't Care Personally** | **Obnoxious Aggression** (Harsh, demoralizing) | **Manipulative Insincerity** (Fake, undermining) |

### Radical Candor in Practice

**The frame**:
- "I care about you and your growth"
- "I want to give you feedback because I see your potential"
- "This is about helping you succeed, not criticizing you"

**The feedback**:
- Be direct about the issue (no softening or hedging)
- Be specific (not general complaints)
- Be focused on growth (how to improve, not judgment)

**Example (Caring Directness)**:
- "I want to talk about your communication in meetings. You're often quiet during debates, and then you seem frustrated afterward. I think you have strong opinions, and I want to hear them *in* the conversation, not after. What would help you speak up?"

## Feedback Timing & Frequency

### Immediate vs. Delayed
- **Best**: Within 24 hours of the behavior (while it's fresh)
- **Acceptable**: Within a week for less urgent feedback
- **Avoid**: Months later (feels like ambush; person has moved on)

### Public vs. Private
- **Positive feedback**: Can be public (celebrates achievement, builds confidence)
- **Developmental feedback**: Always private (respects dignity, allows vulnerability)
- **Exception**: If the issue is about team dynamics (how they behaved in public), you can reference the context ("In the meeting yesterday..."), but still address privately

### Frequency
- **Weekly or biweekly check-ins**: Create space for informal feedback (not formal)
- **Quarterly formal reviews**: Synthesize patterns; no surprises
- **In real-time**: When behavior is critical or moment is ripe for learning

## Three Types of Feedback Conversations

### Type 1: Appreciation (What They're Doing Right)
Purpose: Reinforce strength; build confidence; show you're paying attention

**Structure**:
- "I noticed [specific action]"
- "Here's why that matters / impressed me"
- "I want to see more of that"

**Example**: "In the kickoff meeting, you drew a diagram that helped everyone visualize the system. That kind of clarity is valuable. Keep doing that."

### Type 2: Coaching (Growth Opportunity)
Purpose: Help someone develop a skill or change approach

**Structure**:
- "I've noticed [behavior/pattern]"
- "Here's what I think is happening: [your interpretation, open to correction]"
- "What would change if you [alternative approach]?"
- "How can I support you in trying that?"

**Example**: "I've noticed you often wait until the last minute to start projects, then pull all-nighters. I wonder if it's a deadline-driven focus thing? What if you set an earlier internal deadline and used that momentum differently? What would help?"

### Type 3: Course Correction (This Needs to Change)
Purpose: Address behavior that's unacceptable or significantly impacting performance

**Structure**:
- **Frame it clearly**: "I need to give you feedback about something important"
- **Situation-Behavior-Impact**: Describe specifically what happened and why it matters
- **Your expectation**: "Going forward, I need you to [specific behavior]"
- **Support**: "Here's how I'll support you. Here's what I'll monitor. Here are the consequences if this doesn't change."
- **Curiosity**: "What do you need from me to make this shift?"

**Example**: "I need to talk about the sprint retro yesterday. When Sarah raised a concern, you dismissed it without asking questions, and she shut down. That kind of response prevents us from getting real feedback as a team. I need you to listen to concerns fully before responding. I'll help by coaching you in real-time if I see this pattern. Let's check in weekly. Does this make sense?"

## Receiving Feedback: How to Help People Hear You

People are more likely to hear feedback if:

1. **You've established trust**: You've shown you care about them as a person
2. **They understand the context**: Why this matters, what success looks like
3. **You're curious, not certain**: "I noticed..." "I wonder if..." (not "You always...")
4. **You follow up**: Feedback isn't a one-time event; check in on progress

## Common Feedback Mistakes to Avoid

| **Mistake** | **Why It Fails** | **Better Approach** |
|---|---|---|
| Being vague: "Your communication needs work" | Person doesn't know what to change | "In meetings, you often don't finish your thoughts. Say what you think fully, even if it's not perfect" |
| Delayed feedback: "You did X three months ago..." | Feels ambushed; they can't connect to the moment | Address immediately or let it go |
| Mixing praise + criticism: "You're great, but..." | Person only hears the "but"; cancels out the praise | Separate conversations |
| Feedback without curiosity: "You're not a team player" | Defensive, judgmental, not actionable | "I noticed you didn't volunteer to help with project X. What was going on?" |
| Assuming intent: "You don't care about quality" | Attacks character, not behavior | "The code you submitted had three bugs. Let's talk about QA processes" |

## Reference: Feedback Phrases That Work

### Opening frames (builds safety)
- "I want to give you feedback because I care about your growth"
- "I noticed something I want to discuss—are you open to that?"
- "This might be tough to hear, and I think it's important"

### SBI structure (specific, not vague)
- "In [meeting/situation], you [specific action]. That mattered because [impact]"
- "I observed [behavior]. Here's why I'm bringing it up: [impact]"

### Curiosity (invites dialogue, not defensiveness)
- "Help me understand what was happening when..."
- "What was your thinking there?"
- "What would it look like if you tried [alternative]?"

### Compassion (shows you care)
- "I'm bringing this up because I see your potential"
- "I want you to succeed, and I think this is important"
- "I know this might be hard to hear, and I trust you can do this"

### Next steps (makes it actionable)
- "Here's what I'd like to see going forward: [specific behavior]"
- "Let's check in weekly on this and see how it's progressing"
- "What support do you need from me to make this shift?"
