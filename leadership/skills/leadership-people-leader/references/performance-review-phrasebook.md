# Performance review phrase book

A library of specific phrases for performance reviews — both written and verbal. The book's value isn't its philosophy (which is standard); it's the catalog of specific language calibrated to specific behaviors and rating levels.

This reference distills the most useful phrasing patterns and adapts them for senior consulting / practice context.

## The principle

Vague feedback ("good communicator") is unactionable. Specific, calibrated language ("articulates complex technical trade-offs in ways non-technical stakeholders can act on") tells the person what's being assessed and what excellent looks like.

The phrase book gives you a calibrated vocabulary so you don't have to invent the language under time pressure during review season.

## How to use this reference

For each rating level on a competency, you have:
- 2–3 example phrases
- The pattern (what makes the phrasing work)
- Adapted phrasing for senior consulting context

Don't copy-paste. Use the phrases as templates, then customize with specific examples from the person's actual work.

## Quantitative attributes

### Productivity / output

| Level | Example phrase | What it signals |
|---|---|---|
| Exceeds | "Consistently delivers ahead of commitments without sacrificing quality; takes on stretch scope in addition to baseline" | Output + reliability + capacity |
| Meets | "Reliably delivers committed work on the agreed timeline" | Solid; trustworthy |
| Below | "Delivers work, but timelines often slip without proactive communication" | Delivery is happening but reliability is the issue |

Customizing for senior consulting:
- Specify the work units (engagements led; pursuits shaped; pages of whitepaper shipped; reusable assets produced)
- Note the leverage (work done personally vs. through team)
- Reference the specific commitments tracked (engagement charters, pursuit milestones, OKRs)

## Avoiding common phrasing pitfalls

### Generic vs. specific

| Generic (don't) | Specific (do) |
|---|---|
| "Good communicator" | "Translates complex Composable DXP architectural trade-offs into language non-technical client stakeholders can act on (e.g., the [client] discovery readout in March)" |
| "Strong leader" | "Direct reports rate engagement and growth highly; promoted 2 reports in the past 18 months; team retention 100% under their leadership" |
| "Team player" | "Stays late to help peers in adjacent practices when their pursuits have technical needs; cross-team partnership on [example] is widely cited" |

The specific version takes more time to write. It also actually helps the person.

### Loaded language

Avoid words that sound positive but signal demotion:
- "Has potential" (often code for "not yet ready")
- "Working on" (often code for "deficient in")
- "Comfortable with [easy thing]" (often code for "limited to easy things")

If you mean these things, say them more directly: "ready for next-level scope in 6 months with the following development"; "needs to develop [specific skill]"; "plays at level X but not yet at level Y."

### Unintended consequences

Be careful with phrases like:
- "Strong individual contributor" — can imply "limited as a leader"
- "Reliable executor" — can imply "follows direction but doesn't shape"
- "Detail-oriented" — can imply "loses the big picture"

Each of these is a real strength when intended; each can be heard as a backhanded limit. Pair with the inverse strength when applicable: "Strong individual contributor *and* increasingly effective leader."

## The structure of a good written review

For each major competency:
1. **Specific behavior or outcome observed** (with examples)
2. **Pattern over the review period** (consistent? variable? trajectory?)
3. **Impact** (on team, client, practice, firm)
4. **Calibration** (how this compares to the bar for level)
5. **Forward-looking** (what's the development edge for next period)

Shorter is fine if specific. Longer doesn't help if generic.
