# Delegation, feedback, and coaching — the Manager Tools cascade

Mark Horstman's signature contribution beyond the One-On-One: a sequenced approach to manager mechanics where each practice builds on the previous one. You don't start with delegation; you build the cadence (1:1s) first, then add feedback, then coaching, then delegation. Trying delegation without the prior cascade fails predictably.

## The cascade

```
1. One-On-Ones (the relationship foundation)
   ↓
2. Feedback (the calibration mechanism)
   ↓
3. Coaching (the growth investment)
   ↓
4. Delegation (the leverage multiplier)
```

Each step requires the previous one to be working. Delegation without feedback fails because the person doesn't know if they're doing it well. Feedback without 1:1s fails because there's no relationship to land it. Coaching without delegation is theoretical (no real work to coach against).

## Feedback (Horstman's model)

Horstman's feedback model is more prescriptive than SBI. Format:

> **"Can I give you some feedback?"** [Wait for yes.]
> **"When you [did specific behavior]..."**
> **"...the impact / what happens is [specific consequence]..."**
> **"Could you [do specific behavior next time]?"** [for corrective]
>   or
> **"Thank you / keep doing that."** [for positive]

Note the elements:
- **Permission asked.** Sometimes seems formal; lands better than launching in.
- **Future-focused.** Not "you did wrong"; "could you do this differently next time"
- **Behavior-specific.** Not character; not vague
- **Brief.** 30–60 seconds total. Long feedback dilutes.
- **Frequent.** Both positive and corrective. Not annual; not even weekly. Real-time.

### The 8-week positive ramp

Horstman's specific advice for managers introducing the model: the first 8 weeks, give *only positive feedback*. Specific, frequent, calibrated.

Why: most teams have no recent experience of receiving frequent, specific positive feedback. Building the muscle (and the trust) before introducing corrective feedback prevents the team from feeling they're being set up for criticism.

After 8 weeks of positive: introduce corrective feedback gradually, maintaining the positive at 3–4x the rate of corrective.

### The frequency calibration

Horstman's stronger claim: most managers give feedback far too infrequently. Daily small feedback (positive most days) outperforms weekly substantial feedback. The compounding effect is significant — a team operating on real-time feedback course-corrects before drift becomes performance issue.

The cost of not doing this: feedback gets bottled up for the quarterly or annual review, the recipient feels blindsided, the trust is broken.

## Coaching (the 4-step model)

Horstman distinguishes feedback (reactive — to behavior that happened) from coaching (proactive — building skill that doesn't yet exist or is underdeveloped).

The 4-step model:

### Step 1 — Collaborate to set a goal

Specific, measurable, time-bound. Coaching goal examples:
- "Run a discovery workshop solo for the next pursuit, by end of Q2"
- "Build the technical pre-read for the [client] readout, with one round of revision before delivery"
- "Lead a teach-back on Composable DXP architecture for the practice in October"

The goal must be co-owned. Imposed goals fail.

### Step 2 — Collaborate to brainstorm resources

What's needed to develop the capability? Could include:
- Reading / training
- Shadowing a senior person doing the thing
- Practice opportunities (a small-stakes version first)
- Specific mentoring sessions
- Books / courses / conferences

### Step 3 — Collaborate to create a plan

Specific milestones. Often weekly check-in points within the broader goal. Visible to both you and the direct.

### Step 4 — The direct acts and reports on the plan

The direct executes; you check in regularly (often in the existing 1:1 cadence); they report progress. Adjust as needed.

The role of the manager in step 4 is mostly: ask, listen, support, hold accountable. Not do.

### Coaching cadence

A coaching plan typically runs 6–12 weeks. After completion, retro: what worked, what didn't, what's the next coaching focus. Most senior people benefit from 1 active coaching focus at a time, not 5 simultaneous.

## Delegation (the cascade payoff)

Delegation is what feedback and coaching enable. Without them, delegation is either over-controlled (the manager re-does the work) or abandoned (the manager hopes for the best).

### The Manager Tools delegation model

Horstman's specific approach:
1. **Identify what to delegate.** Not the whole role — specific decisions, recurring tasks, or ownership of outcomes.
2. **Identify who.** Match the delegation to the person's growth edge (not too easy; not unreachable).
3. **Specify the level of autonomy.** What decisions are theirs? What requires consult? What requires escalation?
4. **Set the cadence of accountability.** Weekly check-in, status report, milestone review — not micromanagement, not abandonment.
5. **Coach through the work.** As blockers emerge, coach (don't take it back).
6. **Course-correct via feedback** when the work isn't landing right.
7. **Acknowledge transfer of ownership** when the delegation is mature.

### Levels of delegation (Tannenbaum / Horstman variant)

Delegation isn't binary. Levels:

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 | "Look into this and report back; I'll decide." |
| 2 | "Look into this and recommend; I'll decide." |
| 3 | "Decide, but check with me before acting." |
| 4 | "Decide and act; report what you did after." |
| 5 | "Decide and act; report only by exception." |
| 6 | "Own this entirely; I won't ask about it unless you raise it." |

A common failure: the manager *thinks* they delegated at level 5, the direct *thinks* they're at level 2 — confusion ensues, and both are frustrated.

The fix: name the level explicitly when delegating. "I'm asking you to decide and act; report after — that's level 4." Or: "I want you to recommend; I'll decide — that's level 2."

### What to delegate

Not the meaningful work you enjoy. Not just the busywork.

Delegate:
- Recurring decisions that don't need your judgment
- Tasks at the growth edge of someone on your team
- Work that someone else could do at 80% of your quality (because at scale, 80% × 5 people > 100% × you)
- Decisions where the person has more context than you

Don't delegate:
- Decisions about the team's structure, hires, exits (these are yours)
- Strategy-shaping decisions that require senior context
- Conversations that are yours to have (hard performance conversations, close relationships)

### Common delegation failures

- **Taking it back.** The first time the delegated person does it imperfectly, you swoop in. Result: they learn you don't trust them. Don't take it back unless it's a real emergency.
- **Vague handoff.** "Just handle this." With no scope, no autonomy level, no cadence. Fails predictably.
- **No follow-through.** Delegate, then never check in. Person doesn't know if they're doing well or doing wrong.
- **Delegating only the bad parts.** If you keep the interesting work and delegate the drudgery, the team notices and disengages.

## How the cascade works for senior leaders specifically

For Bermon's role — Senior Director with 5 EM directs — the cascade plays at two altitudes:

**Direct level (with EMs):**
- Weekly 1:1s
- Real-time feedback (positive and corrective; both routine)
- Coaching focus per quarter (one growth area each)
- Delegation: significant scope (engagements, hiring, asset development)

**Practice level (Bermon's coaching the EMs to coach their teams):**
- The EMs run their own version of the cascade with their teams
- Bermon's job is to coach the EMs on running it (meta-coaching)
- Visible delegation modeling — when Bermon delegates well to EMs, they learn to delegate to their reports

The cascade compounds across two levels. A practice where the cascade is running at both levels has very different team health than one where it's missing at either layer.

## When to add to the cascade

Don't try to install all four practices at once with a new direct. Sequence:

- **Week 1 onward:** Set up weekly 1:1s. Run them well. Build the relationship.
- **Week 4–8:** Begin giving real-time positive feedback. Establish the rhythm.
- **Week 8+:** Add corrective feedback as needed.
- **Quarter 2:** Identify a coaching focus. Co-create a plan.
- **Quarter 3+:** Material delegation — based on what you've learned about the person through the prior layers.

Trying to leap to delegation in week 2 fails because you haven't earned the trust, calibrated expectations, or coached the capability yet.
