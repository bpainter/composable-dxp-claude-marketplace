# 1:1 Mastery: Running Effective Regular Checkpoints

## Why 1:1s Matter

Effective 1:1s are where trust is built, expectations are clarified, and performance is actually managed. They're not status meetings; they're coaching conversations.

**Good 1:1s result in:**
- Early detection of problems (misalignment, overload, conflict)
- Continuous feedback (not annual surprises)
- Psychological safety (people know they can bring real issues)
- Career growth clarity (person knows how they're progressing)

## The Standard 1:1 Structure (30 min)

### Opening (2 min)
- **Check-in**: "How are you doing? How's [personal thing they mentioned last time]?"
- **Quick context**: "Anything urgent we should tackle first, or should we go through the agenda?"

### Topic 1: Wins & Challenges (10 min)
**Their agenda first** (not yours)
- "What's going well? What are you proud of from this week?"
- "What's frustrating or blocking you?"
- **Your role**: Listen, validate, help them think through obstacles (don't solve immediately)

### Topic 2: Work Progress (8 min)
**Update on goals/priorities**
- "How are you tracking on [current goals]?"
- "What's the next milestone? Are we on track?"
- **Your role**: Remove obstacles, clarify priorities if things have shifted

### Topic 3: Growth & Development (5 min)
**Rotate focus** (not every week, but regularly):
- Skills they want to develop
- Stretch opportunities
- Feedback on their performance (if you have something to discuss)
- Career progression

### Closing (5 min)
- **Recap**: "Here's what I heard you saying..."
- **Your asks**: Anything they need from you?
- **Next steps**: "Next week, let's [topic]. Come ready to discuss [thing]."

## Cadence & Consistency

**Minimum**: Biweekly (every two weeks)
**Better**: Weekly (30 min) or twice-weekly for very new hires
**Frequency varies**: More frequent early on, can space out as trust builds

**Consistency matters more than duration**: Regular 30-min meetings beat irregular hour-long meetings

## The 1:1 Agenda: You vs. Them

**Split the time**:
- **First half: Their agenda** (wins, challenges, blockers, things they want to discuss)
- **Second half: Your feedback or topics** (growth, performance, expectations)

**Why this order**: They're more likely to hear your feedback if they feel heard first

## Common 1:1 Conversations

### The High Performer Who's Disengaged
**What you notice**: Good work, but less energy; maybe looking externally

**Your approach**:
- "I've noticed you seem less engaged lately. What's going on?"
- **Listen without assuming**: Could be boredom, external life stress, pay, lack of growth, bad team dynamics
- **Be specific about what you've noticed**: Not "You're not as engaged," but "You used to jump into design reviews; now you're quiet"
- **Ask about growth**: "What would energize you? What's missing?"
- **Offer options**: "Let's think about what would make this more interesting for you"

**Possible solutions**: Stretch project, mentor role, new responsibility, conversation about comp, or acknowledging they might be ready to move on

### The Struggling Performer
**What you notice**: Missed deadlines, quality issues, or interpersonal friction

**Your approach**:
- **Assume good intent**: Start with curiosity, not judgment
- **Get the full picture**: "Tell me what's happening with [project]. How are you feeling about your progress?"
- **Identify the real blocker**: Is it skill, clarity, overload, personal situation, or motivation?
- **Co-create a plan**: "Here's what I'm seeing. Here's what I need. What support do you need to get there?"
- **Set clear expectations**: "By [date], I need to see [specific improvement]. We'll check in weekly."
- **Follow through**: Document, check in regularly, be fair but firm

### The Burned-Out Performer
**What you notice**: Good work historically, but exhaustion, errors, or expressing overwhelm

**Your approach**:
- **Name what you see**: "I'm concerned. You seem exhausted."
- **Validate**: "This has been an intense stretch. That's real."
- **Diagnose**: "What's pushing you hardest? Is it workload, scope, or something else?"
- **Take action**:
  - Reduce scope (defer or delegate work)
  - Adjust timeline (slow down to avoid crisis)
  - Increase support (pair them with someone, increase check-ins)
- **Prevent future burnout**: "Let's be clearer about what you can't take on. Tell me when you're approaching capacity."

### The New Hire (First 90 Days)
**Week 1-2: Onboarding**
- "How's the onboarding going? What's confusing? Who's been most helpful?"
- Share org context, introduce key people
- Set clear expectations for first month

**Week 3-6: Early learning**
- "What are you learning? What surprises you?"
- "Here's feedback on your first work [project/meeting/etc]"
- Check understanding of role and expectations

**Week 7-12: Performance ramp**
- "You're hitting [X] well. [Y] needs work. Here's how we can improve [Y]"
- Expand responsibilities gradually
- Introduce them to broader team/org

## 1:1 Tips for Better Conversations

### Listen More Than You Talk
**Target**: 70% them talking, 30% you
- If you're talking more than half the time, you're not learning about them
- Resist the urge to solve immediately; ask questions first

### Ask Better Questions
**Weak questions**:
- "How's everything going?" (too vague)
- "You're doing great, right?" (leading, not open)
- "Are you happy?" (yes/no, no depth)

**Strong questions**:
- "What was the most interesting thing you worked on this week?"
- "Where are you stuck or blocked?"
- "What would make this role more fulfilling for you?"
- "What do you need from me to be successful?"
- "What's one thing you want to improve?"

### Manage Silence
Silence is often useful; don't fill it immediately.
- Ask a question
- Wait 10 seconds (feels long; it's not)
- Let them think; they'll usually say something meaningful

### Take Notes
- Shows you care about what they're saying
- Helps you remember details for follow-up
- Lets you track patterns over time

### Create Psychological Safety
- **Confidentiality**: What they say in 1:1 stays there (unless it's a serious concern)
- **Vulnerability**: Share about your own challenges, not just advice
- **No surprises**: If you have concerns, you've mentioned them before the performance review
- **Follow through**: If they ask for something, come back with an answer

## 1:1 Conversation Starters by Focus

**Performance & Goals**:
- "How are you tracking on [goal]? What's the next step?"
- "I want to make sure I understand what success looks like for [project]."

**Growth & Development**:
- "What skill would you like to develop?"
- "What kind of work energizes you?"
- "What would the next step in your career look like?"

**Well-being & Engagement**:
- "How are you doing? Really?"
- "What's one thing that's going well? One thing that's hard?"
- "What would make this role better for you?"

**Feedback & Course Correction**:
- "I want to give you some feedback. Are you open to that?"
- "I noticed [specific behavior]. Help me understand what happened."
- "Here's what I'm seeing. How do you see it?"

## 1:1 Anti-Patterns to Avoid

| **Anti-Pattern** | **Why It Fails** | **Better Approach** |
|---|---|---|
| Status meeting only | You're not building relationship; not learning about them | Split agenda: their topics + yours |
| You dominate | They don't practice thinking; you miss what they care about | Listen 70%, talk 30% |
| Skipped or rescheduled | Signals they're not a priority | Protect the time; reschedule only if necessary |
| All positive, no feedback | They don't know where to improve | Balance appreciation + developmental feedback |
| You solve their problems | They become dependent; don't develop ownership | Coach them to find solutions |
| No follow-up | Promises mean nothing; trust erodes | Write down asks and commitments; deliver |

## Reference: The Coaching Habit Framework

For coaching conversations within your 1:1s, use this progression:

1. **Ask an opening question**: "What's on your mind?"
2. **Listen and clarify**: "Tell me more" / "Help me understand"
3. **Ask the coaching question**: "What do you think is happening?" / "What are your options?"
4. **Use "and" not "but"**: "You want X, and you're worried about Y—how do we handle both?"
5. **Check understanding**: "Does this feel right? What will you do?"
