# Firing with dignity

The conversation most managers handle worst — partly because most managers don't get many reps, partly because the discomfort drives the worst patterns (delay, surprise, ambiguity, harshness, evasion).

This reference is the playbook for doing the hard thing in a way that honors the person, the team, and the integrity of the role.

## The premise

Firing is sometimes the right call. A wrong-fit hire, sustained underperformance, irreparable culture damage, integrity violations — these are real situations and managing them poorly damages everyone:
- The person being managed out (lingering uncertainty, lost time, professional damage)
- The team (low morale; sense that no one is held accountable; their best people leave)
- The org (the cost compounds across roles)
- You (your credibility, your team's trust, your own integrity)

The discipline is not avoiding firing — it's doing it well when it's needed.

## The slow version of "we should let them go"

The Nightingales' framing: by the time most managers are willing to fire, they've spent 6+ months knowing it's the right call and not acting. The cost of those months is significant.

Common reasons for delay:
- "I've just invested in coaching them" (sunk cost; the question is forward-looking)
- "If I let them go, who will do the work" (the work-going-undone is rarely worse than the wrong-person-doing-it)
- "It feels personal" (it is personal, for them too — and lingering is worse than acting)
- "Maybe one more quarter" (you said that last quarter)
- "I haven't given them every chance" (you have; you're soft-talking yourself out of the call)

The discipline: when the data is clear, act. The kind action is the clear action.

## The progression to firing — the conversations that should have happened first

A fire shouldn't be a surprise. By the time you're firing, the person should have had:
- Clear, specific feedback over months
- A formal performance conversation (30–60 days before the fire) naming what's at stake
- A clear improvement path with specific outcomes and a date
- Regular check-ins on progress against the path
- Direct, honest assessments at each check-in

If they're surprised by the fire, you didn't do the prior work. The fire is on you, not them.

## The "performance improvement plan" question

PIPs (Performance Improvement Plans) are controversial. Some patterns:

- **The genuine PIP.** The person has potential to recover; the plan is real; success rate ~30%. Use when the diagnosis honestly is "they could make this work" with structure.
- **The CYA PIP.** The decision is made; the PIP is paperwork; the person is being moved out. The 30 days are mostly a formality with severance prep. Less honorable but sometimes legally / process-required.
- **The drift PIP.** Manager doesn't really know whether they want to fire; PIP becomes a way to defer the decision. Worst pattern. Either commit to recovery or commit to exit.

Be honest with yourself about which you're running. The person can usually tell, regardless of what you say.

## The firing conversation itself

When the call is made and the day arrives:

### Pre-meeting
- HR briefed; severance / agreements ready
- Logistics planned (laptop return, access removal, communications)
- Successor or coverage planned
- The team's communications drafted
- Your own composure prepared (use the Stoic gap; this conversation will be hard)

### The meeting
- 15–20 minutes, no more
- 1:1 with you (occasionally HR present; depends on context)
- In-person if possible; video if remote
- Quiet space, not where the team can see

### The script

Brief, direct, dignified. Don't soften so much they don't understand. Don't elaborate so much they feel attacked.

> "[Name], I asked for this meeting because I have hard news. We're ending your employment with [team / firm], effective [date]. I want to be clear about why, walk you through the logistics, and answer your questions."

Pause. Let them absorb.

> "The decision is final. I want to honor you by being straight: this isn't working for the team or for you, and continuing isn't going to change that. The reasons we've discussed in our conversations — [brief, specific] — have led us here."

Don't re-litigate. Don't justify excessively. The decision is made; the conversation is to communicate it, not to defend it.

> "Here are the details: [last day, severance terms, transition expectations, references policy, communications plan, what to do today]."

Hand over a written summary.

> "I'm going to listen now if you want to talk. There's no need to make decisions today; you can call HR with questions through the rest of this week."

Listen. Don't argue. Don't try to make them feel better with platitudes. Just be present.

End with respect:

> "I appreciate the work you've done. I'm sorry it's ending this way. Take whatever time you need."

### After the meeting

- Walk them out respectfully (don't have security escort unless there's a specific safety concern)
- Communicate to the team within 24 hours (specific announcement; clear that the person is no longer with the team; brief; respectful)
- Communicate to broader stakeholders as appropriate
- Be available to the team for questions
- Don't gossip about the person; don't disparage; honor the decision and move forward

## What not to do

### Don't:
- Surprise them (the firing should be at most a 60-day-out arrival)
- Schedule it on a Friday before a long weekend (cruel; gives them no support window)
- Schedule it via Slack or email (in person, even if uncomfortable)
- Have HR do it without you (you owed them this conversation)
- Drag the meeting beyond 20 minutes
- Argue about the reasons
- Apologize so much it becomes about you
- Talk to the team about it before the meeting happens
- Walk them out humiliatingly unless there's a specific safety concern
- Disparage them after they're gone

### Do:
- Tell them yourself, in person
- Be brief and clear
- Have logistics ready
- Listen if they want to talk
- Be respectful in everything that follows
- Communicate to the team honestly (without details that aren't yours to share)

## Communicating to the team

Within 24 hours. Honest, brief, respectful. Template:

> Team —
>
> I want to share that [name] is no longer with [team / firm], effective [date]. We've made this decision because [brief, professional rationale — don't air grievances; don't be vague to the point of suspicious]. I'm happy to discuss further in 1:1s.
>
> [Coverage / transition plan: who picks up what, what's interim]
>
> I appreciate everyone's professionalism and care during this transition. Please be respectful in any communication about [name]; I'm asking that any specifics stay confidential.
>
> [Your name]

Anticipate questions in 1:1s for the next two weeks. The team is processing.

## When firing is for misconduct (different protocol)

For integrity violations, harassment, safety issues — the protocol is different:
- Move fast (the cost of delay is to others, not just you)
- Work with HR / legal from the start
- Document carefully
- Don't engage in the long performance-management arc
- Communicate to the team carefully (often without disclosing specifics)

This skill is not a substitute for HR / legal expertise on misconduct cases. Escalate appropriately.

## Layoffs (different from individual firing)

Layoffs are firings for business reasons, often affecting multiple people who didn't underperform. The conversation is different — the person isn't being told they failed; they're being told the role is being eliminated.

Specific differences:
- Lead with the business reason ("we're reducing scope on [X]; this role is being eliminated")
- Be clear it's not performance ("you've done good work; this isn't about you specifically")
- Severance is usually more generous
- The communication to the team is different (acknowledge the loss; speak to the survivors' anxieties)
- Often handled in a wave with HR coordination

The Stoic frame is important here too — you may be communicating decisions you didn't make. Hold the role; do the conversation well; honor the people; don't perform agreement with decisions you privately disagree with.

## After a firing — the team's needs

The team experiences:
- Anxiety about whether they're next
- Sympathy for the person fired
- Curiosity about what happened (which you mostly can't satisfy)
- Re-evaluation of their relationship with you
- Sometimes survivor's guilt

Address by:
- Being visible, available, calm in the days after
- Holding the team's regular 1:1s without skipping (the cadence is reassurance)
- Not over-explaining (signals defensiveness)
- Modeling forward focus while acknowledging the human moment
- Following through on whatever transition / coverage commitments you made

## The Lopp note on firing high performers

Some of the hardest fires are high performers who are toxic to the team. The math usually favors the team: keep the asshole and you lose the people they damage; lose the asshole and the team's potential rises.

The discipline:
- Have the explicit conversation about behavior (not output)
- Make clear that high output doesn't immunize from culture violations
- Document the pattern
- Be willing to lose the output if the behavior doesn't change

This is one of the senior leader's higher-stakes calls. It tests whether you actually mean what you say about culture.

## Your own emotional preparation

The firing conversation will be emotionally costly. Prepare:
- Sleep well the night before
- Don't schedule emotionally heavy work the same day
- Have your own debrief person (mentor, peer, partner) for after
- Use the Stoic gap (see `insults-anger-and-the-reactivity-gap.md` in `leadership-stoic-perspective`) — you may receive an emotional response; meet it with calm
- Don't over-process publicly the next day; the team needs you steady
