# The Coaching Habit — seven questions

Michael Bungay Stanier's seven questions, plus the question masterclass principles. The book's central claim: managers default to advising, but coaching produces more lasting growth — and good coaching is mostly about asking better questions.

A coaching conversation can happen in 10 minutes. It doesn't require a separate meeting. The seven questions fit naturally into 1:1s, hallway conversations, and the moments when a direct comes to you with a problem.

## The seven questions

### 1. The Kickstart Question

**"What's on your mind?"**

The opener. Used in any conversation that might benefit from coaching. Doesn't presuppose what the conversation is about; lets the other person bring what's actually live for them.

Better than: "How's the project going?" (specific; constrains)
Better than: "What did you want to talk about?" (procedural; not inviting)

### 2. The AWE Question

**"And what else?"**

After the first answer to any question, ask "and what else?" The first answer is often the polite or top-of-mind one. The second, third, fourth answers go deeper.

Stanier's claim: the AWE question is the most powerful follow-up in the book. The first thing they say is rarely the most important thing. Keep going.

Use up to 3–5 times. After that, move on.

### 3. The Focus Question

**"What's the real challenge here for you?"**

The "for you" is essential. It surfaces what the person is actually wrestling with, as opposed to the abstract problem in the world. Often the abstract problem is a stand-in for a personal challenge (capability, anxiety, conflict, role) the person hasn't named.

When someone presents a problem, this question often reveals that the surface problem isn't the real one.

### 4. The Foundation Question

**"What do you want?"**

Most conversations don't surface what the person actually wants. They surface what they need to do, what they're worried about, what others want from them — but not the underlying desire.

This question is uncomfortable. Most people aren't used to being asked it directly at work. Sit with the silence; don't rush to fill it. The answer often unlocks the rest of the conversation.

### 5. The Lazy Question

**"How can I help?"**

Stanier calls it "lazy" because it's a counter to the default manager move (jumping in to solve). The question forces the person to articulate what kind of help they actually want — coaching, advice, sponsorship, removal of a blocker, just to be heard.

The answer often is "you don't need to do anything — I just needed to think out loud." That's a successful coaching outcome.

### 6. The Strategic Question

**"If you're saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?"**

The trade-off question. New commitments displace existing ones; rarely do people think through the displacement until asked.

Useful in any conversation about scope, priority, or new initiative. Surfaces the implicit choice the person is making.

### 7. The Learning Question

**"What was most useful for you?"**

The closer. Asked at the end of the conversation. Two functions:
- Forces the person to articulate what they're taking away (which cements the learning)
- Tells you what was actually valuable (which calibrates your next conversation)

Often surprising — what landed for the person isn't always what you thought you contributed.

## The question masterclass principles

Throughout the book, Stanier interleaves these:

### Ask one question at a time

Multi-part questions confuse and produce shallow answers. Pick one. Ask it. Listen. Ask another.

Wrong: "What's the challenge here, and what do you want, and how can I help?"
Right: "What's the real challenge for you?" [pause; listen; respond] then "What do you want?"

### Cut the intro and just ask

The setup before the question often softens the question into ineffectiveness:
- "I was just thinking that maybe, you know, it might be worth considering, what do you want?"
- vs.
- "What do you want?"

The direct question respects the person's intelligence and lands harder.

### Should you ask rhetorical questions?

No. Rhetorical questions are advice in disguise. "Don't you think you should…" — that's not a question; it's pressure. If you have a recommendation, give it directly.

### Stick to questions starting with "What"

"What" questions are open. "Why" questions trigger defensiveness ("why did you do that?" sounds like accusation). "Why" reframed as "what" is usually better:

- "Why didn't you escalate?" → "What kept you from escalating?"
- "Why did you make that call?" → "What was driving the decision?"
- "Why are you frustrated?" → "What's driving the frustration?"

### Get comfortable with silence

The hardest discipline. After you ask a question, *let it sit*. Don't rescue the silence. The space is where the deep answer comes.

Most managers can't tolerate more than 2–3 seconds of silence. Coaches can hold 10–20. The difference shows up in the quality of the answer.

### Actually listen to the answer

Sounds obvious. It isn't. Most "active listening" is the listener planning their next move while the other person talks.

Real listening: when they finish, you can paraphrase what they said back to them. If you can't, you weren't listening.

### Acknowledge the answer

Brief, specific acknowledgment lands. "That's a real tension." "I hadn't thought of it that way." "That makes sense given what you said earlier." Validates without taking over.

Generic acknowledgment ("interesting!") is hollow. Specific lands.

### Use every channel

The seven questions work in person, on Slack, in email, in writing. Async coaching is real coaching. Don't reserve the questions for a "coaching conversation."

## Putting it together — a 10-minute coaching conversation

Direct report comes to you with a problem.

You: "What's on your mind?" (Q1)

They: [describes situation in 2 minutes]

You: "And what else?" (Q2)

They: [adds context, often gets closer to the real issue]

You: "What's the real challenge here for you?" (Q3)

They: [pauses, articulates the underlying issue]

You: [silence; let them sit]

You: "What do you want?" (Q4)

They: [pauses, sometimes for a long time, then names what they want]

You: "How can I help?" (Q5)

They: "Honestly, I think I just needed to think this through out loud. I know what I need to do."

You: "What was most useful for you?" (Q7)

They: [names the takeaway]

End of conversation. 10 minutes. Direct report has done their own thinking; you helped them get there.

## Where Stanier's framework fits in

**In 1:1s**: the seven questions can structure or punctuate any 1:1 (see `manager-tools-one-on-one.md` in `leadership-meetings-and-cadences`).

**In hallway / Slack moments**: when someone comes to you with a problem, default to coaching mode (questions) before switching to advising mode.

**In feedback conversations**: pair with SBI-style feedback — give the feedback, then use coaching questions ("What's your read on this? What do you want to do about it? How can I help?") to convert feedback into action.

**For senior people specifically**: senior reports often need less advising and more coaching. Their problem-solving capability is high; what they need is space to think and good questions to focus the thinking.

## Common mistakes

### Asking but not actually being curious

If you've already decided what they should do, don't pretend to coach. Coaching as theater is worse than direct advice. Ask only if you're genuinely open to where the answer goes.

### Asking too many questions

Coaching is not interrogation. Two or three good questions in a 15-minute conversation, with real listening between, beats seven questions rattled off.

### Defaulting back to advice mid-conversation

Common pattern: you ask Q3 ("what's the real challenge"), they answer, and you immediately offer your read on what they should do. The discipline is to keep asking; the advice can wait until they ask for it (Q5).

### Using the questions on someone in distress

Coaching questions assume cognitive bandwidth. A direct report in acute crisis (personal, professional, emotional) doesn't need "what do you want?" — they need to be heard and supported. Different mode.

### Skipping the closer (Q7)

The Learning Question is the one most managers skip ("we ran out of time"). Don't. The 30 seconds it takes is what cements the conversation as useful.

## When to *not* use coaching mode

Coaching is one mode among several. Other modes:

- **Tell mode** (when there's an answer they need to know — policy, decision, fact)
- **Direct feedback mode** (when behavior change is needed — see SBI / Radical Candor references)
- **Crisis mode** (when speed matters — coach later)
- **Sponsor mode** (when they need you to advocate or open doors, not to think with them)

The coaching habit is a default to reach for *more often* — not the only mode.
