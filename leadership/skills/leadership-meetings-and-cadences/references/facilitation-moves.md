# Facilitation moves

Specific in-meeting moves. Use these as a tactical menu — the right one depends on the moment.

## Drawing out the quiet

The quiet person in the room often has the most useful read; they've been listening while others talked.

**Direct invitation, not pressure:**
> "[Name], you've been listening — what are you thinking?"

If they pass:
> "Want to come back to you in a minute, or want me not to call on you again? Either's fine."

This keeps the door open without pinning them.

**Round the table for one specific question:**
> "I want to hear from each person on this — what's the biggest risk you see?"

Going person-by-person flushes out the people who weren't going to interject voluntarily.

**Async invitation:**
> "I want to hear from each of you on this. Type your read in the chat in the next 90 seconds."

Async written input often surfaces what verbal-dominant rooms miss.

## Managing the loud

The loud person isn't usually trying to dominate — they're filling silence and don't notice they're crowding others. Sometimes they are. Either way, the move is the same: redirect airtime.

**Acknowledge then redirect:**
> "Good point — let me come back to you. [Name], what's your read?"

**Use a structure that limits airtime:**
> "Round the table — 90 seconds each."

A timer makes this real. Without it, "round the table" becomes "the loud person talks for 5 minutes per turn."

**Name the pattern privately:**
After the meeting, in 1:1: "You had really useful things to say in staff today. I noticed you were the first voice on most items — could you try holding back on the first three items next week and let others land first? I'm curious whether [other person] has perspective we haven't heard."

If the loud person is your peer or above you, this private feedback move is the only one that works long-term.

## Parking off-topic items

The "good question, wrong meeting" move.

**Park visibly:**
> "Great point — that's a different decision. Let me park it. [Captures in a parking lot, by name, with a follow-up owner.] Let's stay on [current topic]."

The visibility matters. If items disappear into the void, people lose trust in the parking move and start re-raising them.

**Schedule the parked item:**
End of meeting, take 60 seconds to assign the parked items: who owns following up, by when. If nothing happens with parked items, the meeting becomes a venue for things to disappear.

## Surfacing dissent

Most rooms have dissent that doesn't get voiced because the cost feels too high. A facilitator's job is to make voicing dissent low-cost.

**Direct invitation:**
> "Before we close — what's the dissent? Who thinks I'm about to make a mistake, and why?"

**Steel-man the opposition:**
> "Let's spend 5 minutes on the strongest argument *against* this option. [Person], you've been quiet — give me the no-hire case."

People will voice dissent as a thought exercise that they wouldn't voice as their own opinion.

**Anonymous fast-poll:**
> "Open the poll — thumbs up if you'd commit to this decision today, thumbs sideways if hesitant, thumbs down if you'd block. Vote anonymously."

Cheap to do, and a sideways spread tells you to slow down before deciding.

## Managing the derail

When someone takes the meeting somewhere unproductive (a tangent, a re-litigation of an earlier decision, a personal grievance):

**Acknowledge then redirect:**
> "I hear that. Let's not re-open [decision X] in this meeting — happy to talk after. Back to [current topic]."

**Time-box:**
> "Five minutes on this; if we can't resolve, we move on."

**If the derail is interpersonal (one person taking aim at another):**
> "Let me pause. [Name A] and [Name B], it sounds like there's a real friction here — let's set this aside and the three of us can talk after. Right now, back to [current topic]."

## Decision-forcing

When a meeting is about to dissolve without a decision:

**Name what's happening:**
> "We've been on this 20 minutes and we're circling. We need to decide. Three options on the table — [A, B, C]. [Decider], what's your call?"

**Force the call or escalate:**
> "If we can't decide today, this goes to [escalation owner] by [date]. Which?"

Don't let meetings end ambiguously. Either decide or explicitly defer with a path.

## Recovery from a bad moment

Sometimes the meeting goes wrong — an attack, a flaring conflict, a moment of someone losing their cool.

**Pause the room:**
> "Let's take a 2-minute break."

Real pause. Stand up, water, the clock resets.

**Name what happened, briefly:**
> "That got heated. Let's reset and make sure we hear each side."

**Redirect to substance:**
> "[Name A], reframe the concern as concretely as you can. [Name B], your response — what's the substance, not the heat?"

If the moment is too hot to recover, end the meeting:
> "Let's not finish this today. I'll talk to each of you separately and we'll pick this up [date]."

## Closing well

The last 5 minutes determine whether the meeting was useful.

**Decisions and owners:**
> "Decisions made: [list]. Owners: [list]. Follow-ups: [list]. Anything missing?"

**The handoff:**
> "Next steps: [name] sends [thing] by [date]. We'll review at [next meeting]."

**The short capture:**
A one-paragraph summary, sent within 24 hours. Decisions, owners, follow-ups, dates. That's it.

Without this, the meeting evaporates.

## The pre-meeting move

The most powerful facilitation move happens before the meeting:

- Confirm the agenda with the decider 24 hrs in advance
- Send the pre-read 48 hrs in advance
- Talk to 1–2 people likely to dissent before the meeting (so the dissent is shaped, not surprising)
- Plan the question that opens the room ("what's our biggest risk?" vs. "let's start with status updates" — these produce different meetings)

Most "bad meetings" are bad because of pre-meeting failures, not in-meeting failures. Pre-meeting prep is the highest-leverage facilitation work.
