# The Manager Tools One-On-One (O3)

Horstman's most copied — and most ignored-by-people-who-think-they're-too-senior-for-it — practice. The weekly 30-minute conversation with each direct report, structured the same way every time, run with discipline.

If you do nothing else as a manager, do this. The compounding effect of weekly 30-min conversations over a year (~24 hours per direct) outperforms every "leadership offsite" combined.

## The format

**Weekly. 30 minutes. With every direct report. Not optional.**

| Time | Owner | Content |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00–0:10 | Direct report | Whatever they want to bring. Their agenda. |
| 0:10–0:20 | Manager | What you need to ask, share, redirect. |
| 0:20–0:30 | Future | Career, growth, longer-arc topics. |

The structure is simple, unbreakable, and intentional.

## Why each piece matters

### First 10 minutes — the direct report's

This is the heart of the cadence. The direct report sets the agenda. Whatever's on their mind: a stuck task, a frustration, a question, something personal, an idea, a celebration.

The manager's job in this 10 minutes:
- Listen
- Ask clarifying questions (not redirect)
- Capture commitments (theirs and yours)
- Resist the urge to "solve" — coach instead

The reason this comes first: if the manager goes first, the direct report's content gets squeezed out. Reverse the flow and you'll find the direct report stops bringing things up because there's no time. Then you wonder why your reports don't surface problems.

### Middle 10 minutes — the manager's

Your asks, your updates, your redirects. This is where you bring:
- Things you need them to do
- Information they need
- Specific feedback (positive and corrective; see Horstman's feedback model in `references` for `leadership-people-leader`)
- Context they're missing

Keep it crisp. If you have more than 10 minutes of manager content, you're either undercommunicating between 1:1s or over-loading this one.

### Last 10 minutes — future

The growth conversation. Career, longer-arc skill development, ambitions, reflections.

This is the slot most managers skip ("we ran out of time"). Skipping it consistently is how direct reports start feeling like they're being managed but not developed.

Topics that fit here:
- "Where do you want to be in 18 months?"
- "What skill do you most want to build this quarter?"
- "What's frustrating you about your trajectory?"
- "I noticed [thing about your growth] — what's your read?"
- "What feedback do you have for me?"

Not every week is a deep conversation. Some weeks "future" is 5 minutes of "you're crushing it, no questions for me." That's fine. The slot exists so the conversation *can* happen.

## The cadence is the value

Horstman's strongest claim: the cadence is the value. Not the depth of any one conversation. Eight 30-min weekly conversations beat one 4-hour deep-dive every quarter.

Why?
- Trust accumulates over reps, not in single big meetings
- Small problems surface early, before they become big problems
- The direct report knows there's always a slot soon, so they don't accumulate concerns
- You build a longitudinal read on the person, not just snapshots

This is also why **canceling 1:1s when you're busy is malpractice**. That's exactly when they're most needed. If you have to move it, move it — don't cancel it.

## Common failures and the fix

### "We have nothing to talk about."

Almost never true. You have a topic bank — see below. Also: silence is not the enemy. If you have 5 quiet minutes after the report's agenda, that's fine. The slot exists.

### Manager talks 80% of the time.

Watch yourself. Ask: "Of the last 30 minutes, how much was them vs. me?" If it's lopsided toward you, restructure: shut up for the first 10 minutes, even if it means silence.

### Topics swing wildly week-to-week with no continuity.

Keep a running 1:1 log per direct report — see `T_1on1_Log.md` in `70_Templates/`. Open it at the start of each 1:1. Reference what they raised last week. "Last week you mentioned X — where did that land?"

### 1:1s get bumped for "more important" things.

This is a leadership values statement. If the 1:1 is bumpable, the report knows it. Defend the slot.

### "I'm too senior for weekly 30-min 1:1s."

You're not. Senior leaders do this with their direct reports. The format scales — 45 min for senior reports, but the cadence and structure hold.

### Public conversation about the report's performance happens elsewhere first.

Bad. Every direct should hear performance feedback from you in 1:1 *before* they hear it in any other forum. If the report learns about a concern in a calibration or staff meeting first, you've broken trust.

## Topic bank — when 1:1s feel stale

Use these as starters when the report doesn't bring an agenda:

**On current work:**
- What's going well right now?
- What's stuck?
- What decision do you need from me?
- What would you do differently if you started over?
- What's something you wish someone would tell you about [their current project]?

**On the team:**
- How's the team feeling, in your read?
- Who on the team is doing especially well?
- Who's struggling?
- What's a cross-team friction you've noticed?

**On growth:**
- What did you learn this week?
- What skill are you most actively building?
- Who do you most learn from on the team?
- Where do you feel stretched? Where do you feel under-stretched?

**On the manager (you):**
- What's something I could do differently to help you?
- What's a recent decision of mine you'd push back on?
- What's something I do well? (calibrate gratitude into your reps)
- Is there a meeting I run that's not working for you?

**On longer arc:**
- What does success in 12 months look like for you in this role?
- What about in 3 years?
- What kind of work makes you lose track of time?
- What kind of work drains you?
- What part of your job would you happily delegate?

Pick 2 per 1:1, max. The slot is a conversation, not an interview.

## Setting up O3s with a new team (Horstman's start protocol)

If you've never run O3s with this team:

1. **Announce in the next staff meeting.** Frame it as a recurring weekly 30-min. Not "I want to add more meetings" — "I want to make sure we have dedicated time."
2. **Send the calendar invite for 12 weeks out.** Block it.
3. **For the first 8 weeks: only positive feedback in the manager slot.** Build trust. Show this is not a punishment meeting.
4. **After 8 weeks, begin including corrective feedback** — see Horstman's feedback model.
5. **Don't skip even when nothing's wrong.** The cadence is the point.

## When something's actually wrong

If the report is struggling, the 1:1 is your highest-resolution instrument. Don't reserve hard conversations for special meetings — bring them into the weekly slot, *with the report knowing they're coming*. ("I want to talk about [X] in our 1:1 Thursday — wanted to flag now so you can think.")

## Per-report 1:1 log

Keep one running log per direct report — not one note per meeting. Dated entries at the top, scroll down for history. See `T_1on1_Log.md` in `70_Templates/` for the template.

This is also one of Bermon's working agreements (per project instructions): "One running 1:1 log per direct report, dated entries at the top, not one note per meeting."
