# Staff meeting design

The weekly staff meeting is one of the most-corrupted meetings on the calendar. It starts as alignment, drifts into status read-out, becomes the place where nothing gets decided, and eventually everyone phones it in.

This reference covers how to design a staff meeting that earns its slot — at two altitudes: with ICs and with managers.

## What a staff meeting is for

The synchronous time exists because some things require synchronous interaction:
1. **Cross-team alignment** — what each person needs to know about the others' work that wouldn't naturally land in their inbox
2. **Decisions that require the room** — see `decision-meeting-format.md`
3. **Surface conflict / friction** — easier to resolve in a room than over Slack
4. **Group sense-making** — when something happened (an incident, an org change, a client signal) and the team needs to interpret it together
5. **Recognition** — naming good work in front of peers

What a staff meeting is *not* for:
- Status read-outs that could be a doc
- Manager monologues
- Surprises (anything bigger than a small announcement should be telegraphed before)
- Hard 1:1 feedback (do that in 1:1)

## Staff meeting with ICs (frontline manager) — 60 min weekly

Format:

| Time | Item |
|---|---|
| 0:00–0:05 | Frame the week — biggest priority, biggest concern |
| 0:05–0:15 | Async-status check — everyone has filed brief written status; verbal only on what changed and what's blocked |
| 0:15–0:35 | Topic of the week — one item that benefits from group discussion (a decision, a design, a friction) |
| 0:35–0:50 | Cross-cutting alignment — what each person needs to know about the others' work; what dependencies exist |
| 0:50–0:55 | Recognition — name 1–2 specific things people did well |
| 0:55–1:00 | Wrap, follow-ups, next-week preview |

Variations:
- Some weeks the "topic of the week" is technical (a design review)
- Some weeks it's process (a retro, a cadence revision)
- Some weeks it's planning (the upcoming month / quarter)

What makes this work: the async-status before the meeting. People file 3–5 bullets:
- What I shipped / progressed this week
- What I'm working on next week
- What I'm blocked on or need help with
- One thing I want the room to know

If everyone files this Sunday night, Monday's meeting can skip the "go around the room" portion and spend real time on the topic of the week.

## Staff meeting with managers (manager of managers) — 90 min weekly

The cadence shifts because the conversation is broader.

| Time | Item |
|---|---|
| 0:00–0:05 | Frame — biggest cross-team priority, biggest concern |
| 0:05–0:25 | Cross-team status — managers share what's happening in each team that the others need to know (more substantive than IC status) |
| 0:25–0:55 | Strategic topic — one substantial item: a planning cycle, a hiring decision, a process change, a calibration discussion |
| 0:55–1:15 | Surface frictions — cross-team dependencies that aren't working; people who are stretched across teams; client escalations |
| 1:15–1:25 | Recognition + people calls — who's doing well, who's struggling (no detail; this surfaces patterns that warrant 1:1 follow-ups between managers) |
| 1:25–1:30 | Wrap, follow-ups |

The manager-of-managers staff is also where you build the cohesion between your direct reports as a leadership team. They should know each other's work, advocate for each other's people in calibration, and feel like a team — not a bunch of solo operators reporting to you.

## Common failures

### "Round-robin status updates"

Eats 70% of the meeting. Replace with async status filed before the meeting. Use the synchronous time for what only synchronous time can do.

### Manager monologue

If you talk for >30% of a 60-min staff meeting, you're broadcasting, not running a meeting. Convert your monologue into a doc and let the team read async.

### Same agenda every week, regardless of need

The "topic of the week" should be picked Friday or Monday morning based on what actually needs the room this week. Static agendas drift to status.

### Surprises

Big news (org changes, key departures, major decisions) should land via 1:1 or a separate forum *before* it hits staff. The staff meeting is for shared sense-making, not for breaking news.

### People are quiet and you don't know why

Lopp's read: silence in staff is a tell. It usually means one or more of:
- The people don't trust the room (psychological safety issue)
- The agenda doesn't engage them (you're talking about things they don't own)
- They're bored / phoning it in (the meeting has lost relevance)
- One person dominates and the others stopped trying

Diagnose, don't ignore.

### Recurring meeting that has lost its purpose

Quarterly: ask the team, "what do you actually get out of staff meeting?" The answer is your audit.

## Lopp's "Update, Question, Decision" taxonomy

Lopp argues every item that surfaces in a staff meeting is one of three things:
- **An update** ("here's what's happening") — usually goes async
- **A question** ("I need help thinking through X") — needs the room
- **A decision** ("I need a call by end of meeting") — needs the room *and* the right people

When someone brings an item, ask: which of the three is this? If they can't say, send it back to be sharpened.

This single discipline cleans up most staff meetings. Updates leave the room (go async). Questions and decisions earn the synchronous time.

## What makes a great staff meeting

- People prepare. Status filed. Topic surfaced.
- The agenda is fresh — different topics, different intensities, week to week.
- Decisions get made.
- Conflict gets surfaced and resolved (or escalated).
- People leave with a clearer read on what each other's working on.
- Recognition is specific and public.
- The manager talks the least.
