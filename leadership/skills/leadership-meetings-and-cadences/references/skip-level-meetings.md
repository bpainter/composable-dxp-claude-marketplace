# Skip-level meetings

Skip-levels are the senior leader's instrument for ground-truth: what's actually happening in the org two levels below you, unfiltered by the layer in between.

Done well, they catch flight risks early, surface culture problems, validate (or contradict) what your direct reports are telling you, and signal to the broader team that you care about the work and the people. Done badly, they're awkward, performative, and produce no information either party can use.

## What skip-levels are for

- **Ground-truth.** Your manager-direct's read of their team is filtered through their lens. Skip-levels give you the IC's read.
- **Early-warning.** You hear about problems before they reach crisis (a flight risk, a project on fire, a brewing conflict).
- **Recognition.** ICs deeper in the org learn that senior leaders know their work exists.
- **Pattern-detection.** Across 8–12 skip-levels per quarter, themes emerge that you can't see from your direct reports.
- **Trust accrual.** When the org senses you actually listen, the next time something's wrong, someone tells you.

## What skip-levels are *not* for

- **End-running their manager.** The IC's manager remains the IC's manager. You're not adopting their issues.
- **Performance feedback to the IC.** That's their manager's job.
- **Performance feedback about the manager to the IC.** Don't. (You can ask about the manager's leadership patterns, but not in a "what should I tell their manager" way.)
- **Status updates on projects.** That's what status meetings are for.

## Cadence

For a Senior Director with 5 EMs and ~25–40 second-line ICs:

- **Quarterly rotation** through every IC: 1 per week, 30 min each, hits ~12/quarter
- **Annual coverage** of every IC at least once
- **Higher cadence** for new EMs' teams (more skip-levels in the first 6 months as the EM beds in)
- **Higher cadence** for senior ICs you want to develop a longer-arc relationship with

Don't promise "monthly skip-levels with everyone" if you can't sustain it. Quarterly with intent beats monthly that you cancel.

## The format — 30 min

| Time | Item |
|---|---|
| 0:00–0:05 | Reframe what this meeting is and isn't |
| 0:05–0:20 | Their agenda — what's on their mind, what they want you to know |
| 0:20–0:25 | Your asks — usually patterns / themes you want to test |
| 0:25–0:30 | Their feedback for you / the leadership team |

### Opening — set expectations

Most ICs have never been in a skip-level. Frame it explicitly:

> "Quick context — these are 30 min I do quarterly with everyone in the org. The point is for me to hear directly what's going on in the work and the team, and for you to ask me anything. This isn't going around your manager — I'm not going to take notes back to [EM] about you. It's also not a performance conversation. The agenda is mostly yours; I might bring 1–2 questions at the end."

If you can't honestly say this, don't have the meeting.

### The IC's agenda — 15 min

Open with their agenda first. They've thought about it, they're nervous, they want to use the slot.

Useful prompts when they don't have a strong agenda:
- "What's something going well right now you're proud of?"
- "What's a recent decision that affected your work — what's your read on it?"
- "What's something about the team / the work you wish leadership knew?"
- "What's blocking your ability to do your best work?"

The skill is *listening*, not *advising*. Reflect back what you hear. "What I'm hearing is X — am I getting that right?" Don't try to solve their problems in real time; that bypasses their manager.

### Your asks — 5 min

Ask 1–2 questions about patterns you're testing, not specific people:
- "How does the team handle [recent process change]?"
- "What's the team's read on [recent strategy decision]?"
- "If you could change one thing about how the team works, what would it be?"
- "What's something I'd be wrong to assume?"

Don't:
- Ask about specific peers ("how is [name] doing?")
- Ask about their manager directly ("what do you think of [EM]?") — too charged
- Probe for gossip

You can ask: "What's something you wish [EM] did more / less of?" — but only if you're explicitly going to talk to the EM about it (and tell the IC you will).

### Their feedback — 5 min

> "Last thing — feedback for me or the leadership team. What should we do differently? What's a decision we're getting wrong?"

This will sometimes get you blank stares. That's fine. Sometimes it'll get you something gold. Either way, ask.

## After the skip-level

- **Capture themes, not specifics.** A note on patterns is useful; a verbatim transcript creates risk and erodes trust.
- **Follow up on commitments.** If you said you'd come back with something, do it.
- **Loop the EM in on patterns.** "Across the skip-levels this quarter, I'm hearing X. Let's talk about it." Never: "[IC] said Y, what's going on?"
- **If something serious surfaced, handle it.** If an IC tells you something concerning (harassment, unsafe behavior, integrity issue), you have an obligation to act regardless of the "no end-runs" frame.

## Common failures

### "I don't have time for skip-levels."

You're underweighting an early-warning instrument. Cancel a less-leveraged meeting first.

### "My EMs feel threatened when I do skip-levels."

Frame it explicitly with them: this is part of the senior leader's job, it's not a check on them, and you'll loop them on patterns. If the EM continues to feel threatened, that's a different conversation about trust between you two.

### Skip-levels become performative.

The IC doesn't share anything real because they don't trust the format. Symptoms: short answers, no substantive feedback, scripted-feeling responses. Fixes: longer arc — you have to demonstrate over multiple skip-levels that nothing they say comes back to them through the wrong channel. Trust takes 2–3 reps.

### You take action on a single IC's concern without checking the pattern.

One IC's frustration with a process is data; a hasty change based on one IC's frustration burns the EM's authority and may not match the rest of the team. Check the pattern across multiple skip-levels before acting.

### You skip-level only the people you like.

Watch your own bias. The skip-levels with people you find harder are usually the most informative. Rotate through everyone.

## Group skip-levels

A variant: 60 min with 4–6 ICs from across teams, 1–2x per year. Useful for:
- Surfacing cross-team friction
- Letting ICs hear each other's perspective
- Showing visibility to ICs further down

Format:
- 5 min frame
- 35 min open discussion (a topic — current strategy, a recent change, a culture question)
- 15 min individual asks (each IC names one thing they'd want from leadership)
- 5 min wrap

Group skip-levels don't replace 1:1 skip-levels. Different signal, different purpose.
