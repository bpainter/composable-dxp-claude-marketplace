# Calendar pruning — quarterly audit

A team's calendar accretes meetings the way a hard drive accretes files. Nobody notices. Nothing dramatic happens. Then one day a senior person quits because "I'm in meetings all day every day and I never get to do real work." That's a leadership failure that compounded for months.

Prune quarterly. Defend the slots that earn it. Kill the rest.

## The audit ritual

90 minutes per quarter. Solo (you, with your calendar) or with your manager-direct-reports (if you want them auditing theirs at the same time).

### Step 1 — Print the week

Print or screenshot a representative week of your calendar. Color-code by category:
- 1:1s
- Staff / standing team meetings
- Project syncs
- External (clients, partners)
- Working blocks (focused alone time, deliberate)
- Open / discretionary

You're looking at the visual shape. Most senior leaders are shocked when they see it on paper.

### Step 2 — For each recurring meeting, the four questions

For every recurring meeting, ask:

1. **What decision does this meeting drive?** If none, it's a status meeting. Most status meetings can be async.
2. **Who must be in the room?** Cut anyone not directly contributing to the output.
3. **What would happen if it just didn't exist for a month?** If "nothing" — kill it. If "I'd lose visibility on X" — replace with an async report or a less frequent meeting.
4. **Has this meeting drifted from its original purpose?** If yes, redesign or split.

### Step 3 — Categorize each meeting

| Status | Action |
|---|---|
| Earns its slot | Keep as-is |
| Earns its slot but bloated | Trim attendees, shorten, or sharpen agenda |
| Could be async | Convert to written status / report |
| Could be less frequent | Move biweekly → monthly, weekly → biweekly |
| Drifted from purpose | Redesign with explicit purpose |
| Doesn't earn its slot | Kill |

### Step 4 — Defend the cadence

After cuts, look at what's left. The cadence (1:1s, staff, skip-levels, focus blocks) should be visible and intact. If the cadence is buried under project syncs and ad-hoc meetings, the org has a leadership problem.

### Step 5 — Add a no-meeting block (if you don't have one)

One full day per week, or two half-days, with no internal meetings. This is non-negotiable space for thinking, writing, deep work. Senior leaders without it produce worse decisions.

### Step 6 — Re-block the kept slots first, then accept new requests

After the audit, re-block your defended cadence in the calendar for the next 13 weeks. Then any new meeting request fits around the cadence, not in front of it.

## Common patterns to look for

### The recurring "status sync" with 8+ people

Nearly always async-able. A weekly written report from the team lead to the group covers the same content in 15 min of writing instead of 60 min × 8 people = 8 hrs of meeting.

### Two recurring meetings with 70%+ attendee overlap

Merge or pick one. The team is being asked to cover similar ground in two slots and resenting it.

### The "let me know if you have time" 1:1 with someone you used to manage

If they're no longer in your org, this is a relationship slot, not a manager slot. Move it to monthly or quarterly. Be honest about that.

### Meetings you "have to" attend that you don't actually contribute to

If you're a passive attendee and the meeting wouldn't change without you, you don't need to be there. Either decline or ask to be removed from the recurring invite.

### Pre-meetings for meetings

A "pre-discussion before the steering committee" almost always means the steering committee doesn't actually decide anything (it ratifies what was decided in pre-meetings). Kill the steering committee or kill the pre-meeting; one is real, one is theater.

### "Office hours" that no one comes to

Two options: (a) it's actually fine that no one comes — the option is the value, keep it; or (b) you've signaled it's not real and people stopped using it — relaunch with intent or kill.

### Meetings that exist because someone leadership-adjacent attends them

These accrete around senior people. Audit honestly: does *your* presence add value, or are you decorative? If decorative, decline. The org will adjust.

## Killing meetings — how to do it without drama

Not every kill needs an announcement. Many meetings can quietly disappear from the calendar and no one notices for two weeks. That's the test — if no one notices, you killed correctly.

For meetings with stronger inertia:
- Send a 1-paragraph note: "I've been auditing recurring meetings. I'd like to convert [meeting X] to async — here's the report format we'll use instead. Trying it for 6 weeks; if it doesn't work, we'll bring it back."
- For high-attendance meetings, give attendees a chance to push back before the kill ("any concerns?")
- Watch for the meeting to come back in another form — sometimes the team reinvents it because there was a real need. If so, design the new version intentionally.

## Adding meetings — gate the request

The flip side of pruning. Every new recurring meeting request should pass:

1. **What decision does it drive?**
2. **Who must be in the room?**
3. **Could the work be done async?**
4. **What's it replacing on the calendar?** (You should be net-zero or net-negative, not just adding.)
5. **What's the kill criteria?** (After 6 / 12 weeks, what would tell us this isn't working?)

If the proposer can't answer all five, the meeting isn't ready.

## Audit the team's calendar, not just yours

If you're managing managers, ask each one to do the same audit on their own calendar and on their team's calendar. The compounding effect of pruning across the org is dramatic.

For the Composable DXP practice specifically: pursuit ceremonies, engagement ceremonies, and practice rituals all accrete. Quarterly pruning is the only thing that keeps senior practitioners' time recoverable.

## What to expect

The first audit usually finds 3–5 recurring meetings to kill or convert. The second finds 1–2. By the third quarterly audit, the calendar is mostly defended cadence + intentional working time + a small set of high-leverage standing meetings + flex space. That's the goal.
