# Three meeting types — beginning, middle, end (Hoffman)

Hoffman's central organizing claim: every synchronous gathering has a primary purpose that fits one of three types. Naming the type before scheduling forces you to design the meeting for what it actually has to accomplish — and prevents the most common failure, which is meetings that try to do everything badly.

## The three types

### Beginning meetings — start something

**Purpose.** Reduce ambiguity. Define the problem. Establish working agreements. Get everyone aligned enough to begin.

**Outputs.**
- Shared problem statement
- Scope boundaries (in scope / out of scope)
- Named owners and decision rights
- Working agreements (cadence, channels, escalation)
- A concrete next step with an owner and date

**Anti-patterns.**
- Leaping into solutions before the problem is shared
- Skipping working agreements ("we'll figure it out as we go")
- No named decider, so the project later stalls on permission

**Examples in your context.** Pursuit kickoffs, engagement kickoffs, new-team formation, project initiation, factory-run starts.

**Format suggestion (90 min):**
- 0:00–0:10 — Frame the meeting (purpose, what we'll leave with)
- 0:10–0:30 — Problem statement: what are we actually trying to do? Why now? Whose problem is it?
- 0:30–0:50 — Scope boundaries: what's in, what's out, what's deferred
- 0:50–1:05 — Named owners and decision rights (RACI-lite: who's accountable, who decides, who consulted, who informed)
- 1:05–1:20 — Working agreements (cadence, comms, where artifacts live)
- 1:20–1:30 — Next steps and dates

### Middle meetings — chart the course

**Purpose.** Make progress on work in flight. Decisions, course corrections, working sessions, alignment checkpoints.

**Outputs.**
- Decisions made (and recorded)
- Blockers cleared or escalated
- Work shaped (next chunk defined)
- Alignment confirmed (or surfaced as misalignment)

**Anti-patterns.**
- Status read-out that could have been a doc
- Discussion without decision ("we'll think about it")
- "Updates from each person" that take 90% of the meeting
- Same agenda every time, regardless of what's actually needed this week

**Examples in your context.** Weekly staff, engagement check-ins, working sessions, decision meetings, retros mid-engagement.

**Format suggestion (60 min middle meeting):**
- 0:00–0:05 — Frame: what's the one decision or alignment we need by end?
- 0:05–0:15 — Async-status surfaced (people read the doc; verbal updates only on what changed and what's blocked)
- 0:15–0:45 — The actual work: discuss the decision, surface the disagreement, design the next chunk
- 0:45–0:55 — Decisions and owners captured
- 0:55–1:00 — Next meeting agenda preview

### End meetings — find closure

**Purpose.** Close the loop. Capture what was learned. Hand off cleanly. Mark the work as done.

**Outputs.**
- Lessons captured (what worked, what didn't, what to change)
- Next-stage owners briefed (if work continues elsewhere)
- Recognition (what people did well; named)
- Archived artifacts

**Anti-patterns.**
- Skipping the closure entirely (work just dribbles to a stop)
- Retro that produces the same complaints quarter after quarter (no one acts on them)
- "Lessons learned" that get filed and never read
- No recognition (the team feels invisible)

**Examples in your context.** Engagement closeout, factory-run wrap, project retros, end-of-quarter review, role transitions.

**Format suggestion (90 min retro):**
- 0:00–0:10 — Frame: what we set out to do, what we delivered
- 0:10–0:30 — What worked? Specific. Named. (3–5 things)
- 0:30–0:50 — What didn't work? Specific. Named. (3–5 things)
- 0:50–1:10 — What do we change next time? Named owners on each commitment.
- 1:10–1:25 — Recognition. Specific gratitude.
- 1:25–1:30 — Archive plan and any handoffs

## Naming the type when scheduling

In the calendar invite, name the type:
- "Pursuit kickoff (beginning meeting) — [client] DXP discovery"
- "Engagement check-in (middle meeting) — [client] week 8"
- "Engagement retro (end meeting) — [client] closeout"

This sets the agenda style and the participant expectation. People who attend a "beginning meeting" expect to leave with a problem definition, not a status update. People who attend an "end meeting" expect closure work, not new initiation.

## Mixed-purpose meetings — the common failure

The all-purpose "team meeting" tries to be all three types every week. Result: it's bad at all of them.

If a recurring meeting is doing beginning work *and* middle work *and* end work, split it:
- Mondays — middle meeting (decisions, blockers)
- End of project — end meeting (retro)
- New project starts — beginning meeting (kickoff)

The cadence stays sane and each meeting can be designed for its actual job.

## The "do we need this meeting" test

For any proposed meeting, run Hoffman's test:

1. **What type is it?** Beginning, middle, or end. If you can't say, you don't know what the meeting is for.
2. **What's the output?** A specific artifact, decision, or shared understanding. If "we'll talk about it," it's a status meeting in disguise.
3. **Could the output be produced asynchronously?** If yes, do that instead. Save the synchronous time for what *requires* synchronous interaction.
4. **Who must be there?** Cut anyone not directly contributing to the output.
5. **How long?** Apply the ideas × people calculation (see `agendas-from-constraints` below).

If the meeting passes, design it for its type. If not, kill it or do it async.

## Agendas from constraints — ideas, people, time

Hoffman's design constraint: every agenda is shaped by three numbers.

| Constraint | Reality |
|---|---|
| Ideas | A focused brain handles ~7 distinct items in 60 minutes; fewer if discussion is required per item |
| People | Each additional person adds coordination cost. Above 8, the meeting becomes a presentation, not a working session. |
| Time | Ideas × discussion time per item × people-coordination factor = required time |

If the three don't fit:
- **Cut ideas** (defer some to the next meeting)
- **Cut people** (smaller group; brief others async)
- **Split the meeting** (two 45-min sessions instead of one 90-min)

The most common failure: trying to do 12 things in 60 min with 10 people. Result: no item gets real treatment, everyone walks out frustrated, and the meeting recurs to "finish what we didn't get to."
