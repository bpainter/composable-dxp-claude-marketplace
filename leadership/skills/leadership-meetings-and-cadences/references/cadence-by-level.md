# Cadence by management level

The right meeting cadence depends on what level you're managing at. The same calendar that works for a manager of 4 ICs will overwhelm a Senior Director with 5 EMs reporting in. Conversely, the cadence that suits a Senior Director will starve a frontline manager.

This reference maps cadence to level and gives concrete weekly templates. Built on Fournier's *Manager's Path* (which walks the levels) and Lopp's *Managing Humans* (which is opinionated about what each level requires).

## The principle

At every level, the manager's calendar is the team's operating system. As you move up, the cadence shifts:
- **Frontline managers** spend most of their meeting time *with their team* (1:1s, staff)
- **Managers of managers** spend more time *across teams* (peer syncs, skip-levels)
- **Senior leaders** spend most of their meeting time *outside the org* (peers, executives, key clients) and depend on the cadence to surface what's happening inside

Designing your cadence is a leadership act — what you put on the calendar tells the team what matters.

## Manager of ICs (frontline manager) — 4–8 direct reports

| Cadence | Format | Time/week |
|---|---|---|
| 1:1s with each direct | Weekly, 30 min, MTOM format | 2.0–4.0 hrs |
| Team staff meeting | Weekly, 45–60 min | 0.75–1.0 hrs |
| Skip-level | Quarterly, 30 min each (rotation) | ~0.5 hrs/week amortized |
| Your 1:1 with your manager | Weekly, 30 min | 0.5 hrs |
| Office hours / open slot | Weekly, 60 min (drop-in) | 1.0 hr |
| Peer syncs (other frontline managers) | Biweekly, 30 min | ~0.25 hrs/week |

**Total: ~5–7 hours/week of structured leadership time.** The rest of your week is delivery work, prep, ad-hoc problem-solving, and (importantly) thinking time.

**Common failure:** managers of ICs schedule too many other meetings (project syncs, status calls) and crowd out the 1:1s. The team feels under-managed. Defend the cadence above first.

## Manager of managers — 4–6 manager direct reports

| Cadence | Format | Time/week |
|---|---|---|
| 1:1s with each manager | Weekly, 45 min (more breadth than IC 1:1) | 3.0–4.5 hrs |
| Manager staff meeting | Weekly, 60 min | 1.0 hr |
| Skip-level with 2nd-line ICs | Rotating, 30 min, ~2 per week | 1.0 hr |
| Your 1:1 with your manager | Weekly, 45 min | 0.75 hrs |
| Cross-team peer syncs | Biweekly | ~0.5 hrs/week |
| Open office hours for the org | Weekly, 60 min | 1.0 hr |
| Strategy / planning slot | Weekly, 90 min protected | 1.5 hrs |

**Total: ~8–10 hours/week.** Your 1:1s are longer because the conversations cover the manager's people, the manager's growth, and cross-cutting org issues.

**Common failure:** managers-of-managers stop doing skip-levels because they "don't have time." Result: you lose the unfiltered read on what's happening at the IC level. The cost: you find out about a flight risk after they've resigned. Defend skip-levels.

## Senior Director / Director — 4–6 manager-or-IC reports, broader scope

| Cadence | Format | Time/week |
|---|---|---|
| 1:1s with each direct | Weekly, 45–60 min | 3.0–6.0 hrs |
| Leadership staff (your reports) | Weekly, 60–90 min | 1.0–1.5 hrs |
| Skip-levels | Quarterly rotation through 2nd-line | 1.0 hr/week amortized |
| Your 1:1 with your manager | Weekly, 45–60 min | 0.75–1.0 hr |
| Cross-capability peer Directors | Weekly or biweekly | 1.0 hr/week |
| Senior leadership reviews | Monthly + ad hoc | ~1.0 hr/week |
| Client / external slots | Variable — protected time for senior client conversations | 2–4 hrs |
| Strategy / thinking slot | Weekly, 2–3 hours protected | 2–3 hrs |
| Practice rituals (depending on role) | Variable | 1–2 hrs |

**Total: ~12–18 hours/week.** A Senior Director with this cadence has 22+ hours of discretionary / making time per week if they defend it. Without defense, the calendar absorbs everything and "thinking" disappears.

**Common failures at this level (Lopp is sharp on these):**
- "Calendar tetris" — every 30 min of every day booked, no buffer, no recovery
- "Strategy theater" — protected slots used for status meetings instead of strategy
- 1:1s with reports skipped to take "more important" meetings; report relationships erode
- All meeting time, no thinking time; decisions get worse over the quarter
- Skip-levels stop happening; the senior leader loses ground-truth

## Defending the cadence

The senior the leader, the more the calendar is a target for everyone else's priorities. Defenses that work:

1. **Block the recurring slots first.** 1:1s, staff, skip-levels, thinking time. Then let the rest of the week absorb other meetings.
2. **Mark thinking blocks as unavailable.** Not "tentative." Not "free if you really need me." Unavailable.
3. **Have an EA / scheduling discipline.** Either a person or a rule that says: nothing breaks the cadence without your sign-off.
4. **No-meeting day(s).** One full day per week with no internal meetings is a 20% productivity gain at this level.
5. **Quarterly cadence audit.** What's in your calendar that doesn't earn its slot? See `calendar-pruning.md`.

## When to revise the cadence

Quarterly. Not more often (gives the cadence time to work) and not less (org changes, scope shifts, new direct reports).

Triggers for an immediate revision:
- New direct report
- Change in number of directs (>1 in a quarter)
- Scope change (new practice, new region, new accountability)
- Repeated 1:1 cancellations — figure out why
- Repeated "we don't have time" excuses for skip-levels — same

## Cadence and Bermon's role specifically

For a Senior Director in Market Solutions running the Composable DXP practice in Enterprise:

- Direct reports likely include both EMs/Solution Directors and senior IC architects
- Practice is matrixed across geos and engagements, so 1:1s have to absorb practice strategy *and* per-person growth
- Pursuit reviews are recurring and high-stakes — they earn their slot but should be tightly designed (decision meetings, not status)
- External cadence (clients, partners, conferences) is non-trivial and grows with seniority
- Slalom rituals (capability syncs, GTM check-ins, calibration) eat predictable chunks

A reasonable defended cadence for this role:
- 5 directs at 60 min weekly = 5 hrs
- Leadership staff weekly = 1.5 hrs
- Skip-levels — 1 per week rotating through 8–12 senior practitioners = 0.75 hrs
- Pursuit reviews (only when there's a real decision; otherwise async) = 1 hr/week typical
- Cross-capability peer cadence = 1 hr/week
- Practice ops / strategy block = 2 hrs/week protected
- 1:1 with VP = 1 hr/week
- Client time (variable, defended) = 4 hrs/week typical
- Plus all the unscheduled stuff that consumes the rest

Total: ~16 hrs/week of structured time. This leaves real thinking time IF the rest of the week is defended from drift.
