# The five conversations with your boss

Watkins's framework for the relationship that determines whether you have authority and air cover in your new role. Have these explicitly, in roughly this sequence, in the first 60 days. Then revisit them quarterly — situations evolve and so do expectations.

The mistake most new leaders make: they let these conversations happen by accident, or assume the answers, and then discover six months later that they were operating on different assumptions than their boss.

## 1. The situation-diagnosis conversation

**When:** Week 2–4, after you've had time to form an early read.

**Purpose:** Align on what kind of situation you're walking into. STARS, the diagnosis areas, and the relative priorities.

**Why first:** Everything downstream — expectations, resources, style — hangs on the diagnosis. If you and your boss have different reads on whether this is Sustaining Success or Realignment, every other conversation will misfire.

**Agenda:**
- "Here's what I'm seeing so far in [area 1, area 2, area 3]. STARS read: [X, Y, Z]."
- "Where's my read consistent with yours?"
- "Where do you see something I'm missing?"
- "Anything you'd weight as a higher priority than I am?"

**Failure modes:**
- Assuming you have the right diagnosis without testing it
- Letting your boss's diagnosis become yours without examining it
- Avoiding the conversation because you "haven't seen enough yet" — by week 4 you have enough to start; the diagnosis evolves

## 2. The expectations conversation

**When:** Week 3–6, after the diagnosis has stabilized.

**Purpose:** Define what success looks like in 6 and 12 months — concretely.

**Agenda:**
- "Given the situation, what does success in 6 months look like to you? Specifically?"
- "12 months?"
- "What's the bar — minimum acceptable vs. excellent vs. above-and-beyond?"
- "What are the 2–3 outcomes you'd most want to see?"
- "What's something you'd consider a clear failure?"
- "Where might we disagree about priorities, and how do we want to handle it?"

**Failure modes:**
- "Use your judgment" answers — push for specifics; vague expectations let your boss redefine success after the fact
- Setting expectations that ignore the situation diagnosis (you can't deliver turnaround results in a sustaining-success time frame, or vice versa)
- Not surfacing your own version of success — your boss may be aiming at a different target

## 3. The style conversation

**When:** Week 4–8, after you've had real interactions.

**Purpose:** Define how you'll work together — cadence, decision rights, communication preferences.

**Agenda:**
- "How often do you want to meet 1:1? Length?"
- "What do you want to know about, day-to-day or week-to-week? What can I just handle?"
- "What decisions are mine, what's yours, what's shared?"
- "How do you want bad news? How fast, what channel?"
- "How do you prefer to be challenged? In private? In writing? Public dissent okay?"
- "What's a recent experience with a previous direct report that you'd want to repeat? Avoid?"

**Failure modes:**
- Assuming your boss's style matches your previous boss's
- Not naming the asymmetries — if your boss likes constant updates and you like autonomy, surface it now, not at the moment of friction
- Conflating "style" with "process" — style is preference, process is what you'll actually do

## 4. The resources conversation

**When:** Week 6–10, after the plan is taking shape.

**Purpose:** Get clear about what you have to work with and what you can ask for.

**Agenda:**
- "Given the expectations we've set, here's what I think I need: [budget, people, time, authority, air cover for X]."
- "What's available?"
- "What's not available, and what's the path to it?"
- "What's something I should *not* ask for that would damage credibility?"
- "When I need air cover for [X], how do I ask for it?"

**Failure modes:**
- Asking for everything (signals you don't understand constraints)
- Asking for nothing (you'll later resent the lack of resources you didn't request)
- Missing the air-cover question — you'll need it, and asking for it cold under pressure is harder than agreeing on the protocol now

## 5. The personal-development conversation

**When:** Month 3–4, after you've started delivering.

**Purpose:** Align on your growth in the role; start building toward what's next.

**Agenda:**
- "What's the growth opportunity for me in this role, beyond delivering the outcome?"
- "What skills do you see me needing to build?"
- "Where am I weakest right now, in your read?"
- "What would put me on the path to [next level / next role]?"
- "What feedback would you give me that I haven't asked for?"

**Failure modes:**
- Treating your manager as a passive observer of your growth rather than a sponsor and coach
- Avoiding the weakness question — you'll learn nothing unless you ask
- Letting this conversation get crowded out — it almost always is, because the situation is busy. Defend the slot.

## How to actually run them

These don't have to be 60-minute capital-letter Conversations. Often they fit in your 1:1s, with explicit framing: "I want to spend 20 min today on the situation diagnosis."

Some teams prefer a written version — you send a 1-page note ahead with your read, your boss responds with their read, and you discuss the deltas in the meeting. This is highly effective for senior leaders who think in writing.

## Cadence

| Conversation | First | Revisit |
|---|---|---|
| Situation diagnosis | Week 2–4 | Quarterly + when major events change the situation |
| Expectations | Week 3–6 | Quarterly |
| Style | Week 4–8 | Annually + when friction emerges |
| Resources | Week 6–10 | Quarterly + at planning cycles |
| Personal development | Month 3–4 | Annually + at performance review cycles |

## What if your boss doesn't engage?

Sometimes your boss is too busy, too senior, or too disinterested to have these conversations. Options:

1. **Shorter, more frequent.** If they won't do a 60-min conversation, get 10 min on each over multiple 1:1s.
2. **Written.** Send your version of each as a doc; ask for response in writing.
3. **Bring specific decisions to surface preferences.** "I'm planning to handle [X] this way — does that match how you'd want me to?" — surfaces style and expectations without naming them.
4. **Find a senior peer or mentor as proxy.** Not a full substitute, but useful when your direct boss isn't available.
5. **Be explicit about the cost.** "I want to make sure I'm aligned with you on [diagnosis]. If we're not, I'll spend 6 months pulling in the wrong direction." Sometimes naming the cost gets the time.

## What if your boss is wrong?

Sometimes the diagnosis conversation surfaces that your boss has the wrong read. Options:

1. **Bring more data.** Often the issue is they have less ground-truth than you now do. Share what you're seeing.
2. **Propose a specific test.** "If I'm right that this is a turnaround, we'd see [X, Y, Z] in 60 days. If we don't, I'm wrong and we adjust."
3. **Defer.** If you're early in the role and don't have enough credibility yet, sometimes you defer publicly while continuing to validate internally. This isn't ideal but is sometimes necessary.
4. **Escalate.** If the diagnosis difference is foundational and affects whether the role can succeed, you may need to surface it to your boss's boss. Use carefully — this can blow up the relationship.
