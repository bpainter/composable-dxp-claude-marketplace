# Manage yourself

The chapter most leaders skim and most regret skimming. Watkins's argument: leaders fail in transitions not (mostly) because they're not capable, but because they get caught in vicious cycles of overcommitment, under-rest, and isolation that compound until they can't think clearly.

## The vicious cycles

Watkins names several cycles new leaders fall into:

1. **The over-commit cycle.** New leader wants to demonstrate value, says yes to everything, becomes overloaded, quality drops, more anxiety, more compensatory work, less rest, more anxiety…
2. **The isolation cycle.** New leader doesn't yet have peers in the new role, doesn't know who to talk to, holds problems alone, decisions get worse, less sleep, more isolation…
3. **The energy-depletion cycle.** New leader stops the things that recharged them (exercise, family time, hobbies) "until things settle down," energy depletes, judgment degrades, more time required to compensate, less margin for the things that recharge…
4. **The defensive cycle.** New leader, under pressure, becomes defensive, peers and team perceive defensiveness, they stop bringing problems early, problems land later and bigger, more defensiveness…

Each cycle, once started, is hard to break. Prevention is much easier than repair.

## The three pillars (Watkins)

Three personal disciplines that prevent the cycles:

### Sleep, exercise, and recovery

Boring. Non-negotiable. Without these, every other piece of leadership advice is irrelevant because the brain doing the work is degraded.

- 7+ hours sleep, defended like a meeting
- Exercise 4+ times/week, blocked in calendar
- One full day per week with no work email / no meetings (the "no-meeting day" extended to actual rest)
- Vacation taken (and protected from creep)

Senior leaders who deprioritize this for a quarter recover slowly. Two quarters is dangerous. A year is career-altering.

### Personal disciplines (the rituals)

Specific recurring practices:

- **Daily reflection.** 10 min at end of day: what happened, what did I learn, what am I worried about, what's the one thing for tomorrow?
- **Weekly review.** 30 min Friday: what worked, what didn't, what changes for next week, longer-arc note for the quarter.
- **Monthly read.** 60 min: how am I doing against the 90-day plan? What patterns am I noticing? Where's drift?
- **Quarterly retro.** Half-day: full review against the original plan, recalibrate forward.

These rituals create the meta-cognitive layer that catches the cycles before they take hold.

### Advice and counsel network

You need people you can talk to who:
- Aren't in your reporting chain (no power asymmetry)
- Have enough context to give useful advice
- You trust to keep what you share confidential

Three types Watkins identifies:
- **Technical advisors.** People deep in the work / domain.
- **Cultural advisors.** People who know the org, its norms, its land mines.
- **Political advisors.** People who can read power and help you navigate it.

Most new leaders build one or two and miss the third. Map your network in week 4 of any new role; identify gaps; close them deliberately.

For Bermon's specific context: Slalom-internal cultural advisors (other Senior Directors / GMs), industry-external technical advisors (peers in DXP / digital strategy), and political advisors (cross-capability peers, Slalom executives who've watched the practice trajectory).

## Specific patterns to watch

### "I'll exercise once things settle down"

Things will not settle down. The role is the role. If you don't build exercise into the structure of the role, you won't get to it. Block calendar; defend it the way you'd defend a 1:1 with your CEO.

### "I don't have time to reflect; I need to act"

The leaders who say this consistently are the ones who make the most preventable mistakes. 10 min/day of reflection saves multiple hours/week of repair work.

### "I'll see my family on the weekends"

The senior leader who works through every weekend has stopped recovering. The compounding cost is loss of judgment, then loss of relationships, then loss of perspective.

### "I'll figure this out alone"

Isolation is the single biggest predictor of bad senior decisions. The advice-and-counsel network is not optional.

### Skipping vacation

Vacation is not optional. The brain genuinely requires it. "I'll take it next quarter" is a lie. Take the vacation.

## What this looks like in a senior leader's calendar

A protected version:

- 7 hours sleep target (in by 11, up by 6)
- 4x/week 60-min exercise blocks (calendar-blocked)
- One no-meeting day (typically Friday) for thinking and recovery
- 10-min end-of-day reflection (last block of each day)
- 30-min Friday afternoon weekly review
- 60-min first-Friday-of-month strategic read
- Half-day quarterly retro
- 3 weeks of vacation per year, taken (not banked)
- 1:1s with 3–5 advice-and-counsel relationships, monthly cadence

This looks like a lot. It is. It's also what makes the rest of the role sustainable.

## When you're already in the cycle

If you're reading this and recognizing you're in one of the vicious cycles right now, the first move is *not* to immediately rebuild every discipline at once.

Order of operations:

1. **Sleep first.** Protect 7 hours for two weeks. Everything else gets clearer when sleep is fixed.
2. **One exercise block per week.** Build from one to two to four over six weeks; don't try to leap.
3. **End-of-day reflection.** 5 min, not 30. Build the habit before scaling.
4. **One advice-and-counsel relationship.** Reach out to one person you trust this week.
5. **Weekly review.** Add this in week 3 or 4.

If you try to fix everything at once, you'll fail at all of it and reinforce the belief that "I don't have time for this stuff." Build incrementally.

## The cost of not doing this

Watkins is direct: the leaders who don't manage themselves through transitions are the ones who:
- Make preventable strategic mistakes (judgment degraded)
- Burn out at month 6 of an 18-month transition
- Damage the relationships they need to succeed
- Become the person their team starts to fear bringing things to
- End up in a hard place where the recovery requires leaving the role

Self-management is not a personal indulgence. It's the operational discipline that lets the role work.

## For Bermon specifically

Two practical adjacent considerations:

1. **Neurodivergent context.** ADHD and autism (per `leadership-executive-coach`) make the personal-discipline work both more important and more difficult. The rituals (reflection, weekly review) help compensate for executive function challenges; missing them costs more than for a neurotypical leader. Build them into the structure (calendar, environment, accountability partner) rather than relying on willpower.

2. **Slalom-context cadence.** The firm's natural rhythms (quarterly business reviews, semi-annual calibration, fiscal cycle) align with the quarterly retro discipline. Use them as forcing functions — quarterly retro the week before QBR; weekly review on Friday afternoon when client load is lighter.
