# STARS — match strategy to situation

The most important diagnostic in *The First 90 Days*. Most transition failures happen because the leader brought a strategy that worked in their *last* situation and doesn't fit the *new* one. STARS forces the diagnosis before the strategy.

## The five situations

### S — Start-up

**Definition:** Building something new — a new team, a new practice, a new business line, a new function. There may be no existing structure, process, or team to inherit.

**Characteristics:**
- High ambiguity, high autonomy
- Resources are usually constrained
- No bureaucratic inertia (good) but also no playbook (bad)
- Hiring is the primary activity
- Direction-setting is wide open
- The team you build determines the outcome

**Strategy:**
- Define the mission and scope crisply (clarity is the first leadership act)
- Hire ahead of need (you'll always feel under-resourced)
- Build the operating cadence early (it scales with the team)
- Accept that you will work harder for less recognition than in established roles
- Don't import process from larger orgs prematurely (it'll suffocate the start-up)
- Wins look like: shipped first version, hired first 5, secured first customer/engagement

**Common failures:**
- Trying to build the perfect team before shipping anything
- Importing "real company" process before the team needs it
- Underhiring at the senior level because "we can't afford it" — then drowning at month 6

### T — Turnaround

**Definition:** Saving something that's failing. The team or business is broken and everyone knows it.

**Characteristics:**
- Low morale, high churn risk
- Crisis mentality
- Often accompanied by leadership change (you may be the second or third leader in 18 months)
- Permission to act decisively (people are looking for someone to do *something*)
- Time pressure — situation degrades if you wait
- Often requires hard cuts (people, products, accounts)

**Strategy:**
- Make the hard cuts fast (within 30–60 days)
- Restore credibility through visible early wins
- Be willing to be unpopular
- Communicate the reality bluntly; don't sugar-coat
- Stabilize first; growth comes later
- Remove people who are part of the problem; protect the people who are part of the solution
- Wins look like: stopped the bleeding, made the painful call others avoided, key talent stayed

**Common failures:**
- Waiting too long to make hard cuts (the situation degrades in the meantime)
- Trying to be liked instead of effective
- Confusing "turnaround" with "realignment" — the team will *not* convince itself a problem exists; you have to act on the problem you can see

### A — Accelerated growth

**Definition:** Scaling something that's winning. The fundamental business is sound; the challenge is throughput, hiring, and structure as volume grows.

**Characteristics:**
- High morale (people are winning)
- Constant hiring
- Process and systems are bottlenecks (they were sized for the smaller version)
- Burnout risk (people are heroically holding it together)
- Senior leaders are stretched across too many things
- The "everything's on fire because we're successful" mode

**Strategy:**
- Hire ahead of demand (always — by the time the gap is felt, you're already too late)
- Build systems and process before they're desperately needed
- Watch for senior burnout (they're the ones holding it together)
- Add layers of management proactively (a flat 30-person team works; a flat 50-person team doesn't)
- Defend the things that made the win possible (they erode under scaling pressure)
- Wins look like: kept the win going, added structure without losing speed, no key departures

**Common failures:**
- Letting senior people burn out because "we're killing it, why would we change anything"
- Underbuilding management layer (everything routes to one or two senior people who melt down)
- Letting culture drift unchecked (the things that made the team great get diluted by hiring volume)

### R — Realignment

**Definition:** Fixing a team that's drifted but doesn't know it. Performance is mediocre; the team thinks it's fine.

**Characteristics:**
- The team is comfortable
- Past success masks current decline
- Internal narratives explain away the problems ("the market is hard right now")
- Strong cultural resistance to change
- The single hardest situation Watkins identifies
- Often inherited from a leader who was too tenured / too liked to challenge it

**Strategy:**
- Convince the team a problem exists (this is most of the work)
- Build coalition for change before announcing change
- Use external data (benchmarks, customer signals, competitor moves) to make the case
- Identify the people who already agree there's a problem and amplify them
- Move slower than turnaround, faster than sustaining-success — change is needed but trust isn't yet built
- Wins look like: shared diagnosis across the team, first changes embraced not resisted

**Common failures:**
- Announcing changes before the team accepts there's a problem (gets resisted, you burn credibility)
- Underestimating how invested the team is in the current narrative
- Treating it like turnaround (heavy-handed) — the team isn't in crisis and won't accept turnaround tactics

### S — Sustaining success

**Definition:** Inheriting something great. The team is winning, the work is solid, the culture is strong.

**Characteristics:**
- High morale
- Established patterns that work
- High expectations (don't break it)
- Predecessor often beloved
- The most dangerous situation for new leaders' egos

**Strategy:**
- Don't break it (the first commandment)
- Spend longer in listen-mode (4–6 months before material changes)
- Honor what's working (publicly; people need to know you see it)
- Identify the modest improvements (every system has them) — and only those
- Build relationships with the team's culture-carriers
- Wins look like: nothing notable — the system kept winning under your tenure
- The temptation to "make your mark" is the failure mode — resist it

**Common failures:**
- Changing things to demonstrate you're now in charge (signals insecurity)
- Disrespecting the predecessor (the team will hear it)
- Adding process that wasn't needed
- Reorganizing the team (a near-universal new-leader tic; almost always a mistake here)

## Mixed situations

Most real situations are mixes. A practice might be Sustaining Success at the practice level but need Realignment in one engagement. A new region is a Start-up but inside an Accelerated Growth firm. A team you inherit might be Sustaining Success in delivery but need Turnaround in sales pipeline.

The diagnosis is per-area, not per-team. List the major areas of accountability; diagnose each. Apply the matching strategy to each.

A worked example for a Senior Director taking over a hypothetical Composable DXP practice:

| Area | STARS read | Strategy implication |
|---|---|---|
| Active engagements (3 healthy) | Sustaining success | Don't change. Show up to steering committees; don't add process. |
| Active engagements (1 in trouble) | Turnaround | Day-30 deep dive; hard call on intervention vs. exit; visible wins by day 60 |
| Sales pipeline (declining) | Realignment | Convince the practice the pipeline is the leading indicator; build coalition for go-to-market change before announcing change |
| Reusable assets (none yet) | Start-up | Define what assets, hire/identify owners, ship first asset by day 90 |
| Team morale | Mixed — strong delivery, anxious sales | Listen-tour first; address the anxiety with honesty; protect the morale that's working |

This level of granularity prevents the new leader from applying one strategy across the whole portfolio (which is always wrong) and forces honest diagnosis area by area.

## How to diagnose

For each major area of accountability:
1. Look at hard data first (pipeline, performance, attrition, NPS, retention)
2. Get the leader's read (your boss; your peers in adjacent areas)
3. Get the team's read (your direct reports, in 1:1s)
4. Get external read (clients, partners, cross-functional peers)
5. Look for narratives — what story is the team telling itself? Is it true?

The diagnosis takes 30–45 days for a senior role. Don't pressure yourself to have it done day 7. But by day 45, you should have a clear STARS read for each major area and the strategy implications written down.

## Sharing the diagnosis

Watkins is direct: share your diagnosis with your boss in the situation-diagnosis conversation (one of the five conversations). Get alignment on the read before you act. If you and your boss have different STARS reads on the same area, you have a conversation to have *before* you start changing things.

Share the diagnosis with the team eventually too (often around day 60–90), framed as "here's what I'm hearing, here's what I'm planning, here's what I'm asking for."
