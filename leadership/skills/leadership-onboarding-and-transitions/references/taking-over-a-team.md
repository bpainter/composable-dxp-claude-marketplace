# Taking over an inherited team

You didn't pick this team. They didn't pick you. The dynamics are different from a fresh hire and different from a continuation under the same leader. The team is sizing you up; you're sizing them up; everyone is operating under uncertainty.

The first 90 days here is the difference between an inherited team that consolidates around you and one that resents you for years.

## Day 1 — what the team is feeling

Before you do anything, understand the room. The team is feeling some mix of:
- **Anxiety.** Their previous boss is gone (or is moving). What does that mean for them?
- **Curiosity.** Who is this person? How are they different?
- **Self-protection.** Will they make changes? Will my role / project / team survive?
- **Loyalty.** To the previous boss; to the team they had; to the way things were.
- **Hope.** Maybe this person fixes [thing the previous boss never fixed].

Whatever you do day 1, the team will read it through these lenses. Be deliberate. Don't over-perform; don't under-show-up.

A reasonable day 1:
- 15–30 min team intro (intro yourself, frame what you're doing in the first 90, listen for questions, don't promise change)
- Schedule 1:1s with each direct (within first 2 weeks)
- Schedule skip-levels with select 2nd-line ICs (within first 4 weeks)
- Do not announce changes
- Do not reorganize anything
- Do not say anything negative about the previous leader, even if invited to

## The first 30 days — listening tour

The dominant mode is *listen*. Watkins is unequivocal: a new leader who starts changing things in the first 30 days has not done the listening required to know what to change.

### 1:1 with every direct report (60 min each)

Same agenda for everyone (signals fairness; produces comparable data):

1. **Who you are** (5 min). Brief. They've heard most of it; this is permission to ask follow-ups.
2. **What you're hearing about their work** (10 min). What's their current scope? What's going well? What's stuck?
3. **Their read on the team** (15 min). What's working at team level? What's not? Where would they want change?
4. **Their read on you** (10 min). What do they hope for from you? What's their concern? What style works for them?
5. **What they need from you in the first 90 days** (10 min). Specific.
6. **Open questions to you** (10 min). Yours.

Keep notes. They'll be useful when you're synthesizing.

### Skip-level with key 2nd-line ICs

Rotate through 4–6 second-line ICs in the first month. Same goal: ground-truth the read your direct reports gave you. Where's the convergence and where's the divergence?

### Listen-tour with cross-functional partners

5–8 partners in your first month: peer leaders who work with this team, key clients/stakeholders, internal customers. Same questions: what works, what doesn't, what would you want from this team?

### Listen-tour with your boss and select peers

Test your emerging diagnosis. STARS read. What are you missing?

## End of day 30 — synthesize

You should have:
- A draft STARS diagnosis per major area
- A read of each direct report (strengths, concerns, fit with the team's needs)
- A list of patterns you're hearing across multiple sources
- A list of unknowns to investigate next month
- 1–2 candidate early wins

Test your synthesis with your boss in the situation-diagnosis conversation (see `the-five-conversations.md`).

## Days 30–60 — share the read, design the plan

Around day 45–60, you start sharing your read with the team. Frame:

> "I've spent the last month listening. Here's what I'm hearing. Here's what I think the situation is. Here's what I'm thinking about doing. I want your input before I commit."

This positions changes as informed by the team's input, not imposed top-down. The team will push back on parts of your read; that's good — incorporate it.

Don't share the read prematurely (week 2) or too late (week 12). Sweet spot is around day 45.

By day 60, you should have:
- A diagnosis the team understands and largely agrees with (mostly)
- A draft 90-day plan with named outcomes
- Identified the early win you'll deliver
- Begun any people changes that the situation requires (see below)

## Days 60–90 — execute, deliver the early win, visible movement

Time to deliver. The early win lands by day 90. The 90-day plan starts producing visible artifacts. The team starts to feel direction.

By day 90:
- Early win delivered (or in clear flight)
- Cadence (1:1, staff, skip-levels) established and humming
- The team has a coherent read on what you stand for
- Initial people calls made (who's growing, who's at risk, who needs to move)
- Plan for the next 90 days drafted

## People decisions — the hardest part

Inherited teams often have one or two people who are wrong for the role / wrong for the team / actively destructive. New leaders are tempted to either (a) move too fast (make a change without enough information) or (b) move too slow (avoid a hard call indefinitely).

The right cadence:
- **Days 1–30:** observe. Don't decide. The person who looks bad in week 2 may look good in week 6.
- **Days 30–60:** form a tentative read. Test with your boss and 1–2 peers (without naming the person to anyone who shouldn't know).
- **Days 60–90:** if there's a real issue, begin the conversation. Performance feedback first, in 1:1, with specifics. See `leadership-people-leader` for the conversation craft.
- **Days 90+:** the actual call (improvement plan, role change, exit) happens after you've given real performance feedback, not before.

Exception: if someone is doing active harm (harassment, integrity issues, severe culture damage), act faster. The cost of not acting is too high.

Avoid: hasty firing in the first 30 days as a "show you mean business" move. It signals impulsiveness, and it damages the trust of everyone else watching.

## Honoring what came before

Whatever your situation diagnosis, your predecessor built the team you inherited. Honoring their contribution publicly buys you trust:
- Acknowledge specific things the team did well under the previous leader
- Don't trash the predecessor (even if invited; even if the team trash-talks them)
- Carry forward what was working (don't change for the sake of change)

Even in turnaround — where you may need to undo many of the previous leader's choices — you can honor the *effort* without endorsing every *decision*.

## The "new sheriff" failure mode

Lopp names this in *Managing Humans*: the new boss who wants to demonstrate they're now in charge. Symptoms:
- Reorg in week 2
- New rituals announced before listening
- Public correction of someone's work in front of the team
- "We're going to do things differently around here" speeches
- Visible changes to previous-leader artifacts (renaming meetings, changing channels)

Each of these costs trust. New leaders trade away years of relationship-building for a brief feeling of control. Don't.

## Keeping the existing team's momentum

Inherited teams often have momentum on specific work. Your job in the first 90 days is to *not* disrupt that momentum unless absolutely necessary. Ride along; absorb context; only intervene when the trajectory is wrong and the team can hear you.

Practical: in 1:1s during the first 30 days, ask "what should I make sure *not* to disrupt right now?" Direct reports usually have a clear answer. Listen to it.

## When to deviate from this playbook

The patient listening-tour playbook is right for most situations. Deviate when:

- **Active crisis.** A client is leaving, a major project is failing, an integrity issue surfaced. Act on the crisis; do the listening tour around it.
- **Active toxic actor.** Someone on the team is doing real harm. Act decisively.
- **Clear turnaround mandate.** Your boss told you "this team needs to be cut by 30%, you have 60 days." Act, but communicate transparently with the team about what's happening.

Otherwise: listen first.
