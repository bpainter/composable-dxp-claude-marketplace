# Leaving a role cleanly

The exit playbook. Watkins is mostly silent on this side of transition, but the discipline matters as much. A bad exit damages your reputation, harms the team you spent years building, and can foreclose options you'd want later.

This applies to:
- Leaving the firm
- Internal moves (different role, different practice)
- Sponsor's exit (you've been the practice lead; new role pulls you elsewhere)
- Partial exits (reducing scope, planning for succession)

## The principles

1. **Tell your boss first. Always.** Before anyone else. Before peers. Before your team. Before clients.
2. **Negotiate the timeline collaboratively.** Long enough for responsible transition; short enough that you don't become a lame duck.
3. **Build a real transition plan.** What does your successor inherit? Document it.
4. **Communicate in the right order.** Boss → directs → broader team → cross-functional → external.
5. **Don't trash-talk on the way out.** Your reputation outlasts the moment.
6. **Maintain ties post-exit.** The relationships you built are durable assets.

## The conversation with your boss

When you've decided. Not when you're considering. Bringing a maybe to your boss is different from bringing a decision; both have a place, but be honest about which it is.

Agenda:
- "I've decided to leave the role. Here's why." Brief. Honest. Don't over-explain.
- "Here's the timeline I'm thinking — open to discussing." (Usually 30, 60, or 90 days, depending on role and situation.)
- "Here's how I'd want to transition the team and the work." (Have a draft.)
- "How do you want to handle the announcement?" (Their preference matters; align before broadcasting.)
- "What do you need from me in this period?" (Surface their priorities.)

Don't:
- Negotiate as if you're open to staying (unless you actually are; in which case have that different conversation)
- Make it personal (even if your reasons are personal)
- Disparage the firm, the role, or specific people

If you're leaving for a reason your boss should know about (a structural problem, a person issue, a strategic disagreement), the exit conversation is the moment to surface it. They can't do anything about it now, but you owe them the candid feedback.

## The transition plan

A real one. Not "I'll write some docs."

For each major area of accountability:

| Area | What's in flight | Who knows what | What needs to happen before I leave | What the successor needs to know |
|---|---|---|---|---|

Concretely, for a Senior Director leaving Composable DXP:

- **Active engagements:** state of each, key client relationships, risks, what the successor needs to know about each. Specific names and contacts.
- **Pursuit pipeline:** what's in flight, where each pursuit stands (PE stage), who owns each, what's blocked.
- **Direct reports:** state of each (performance, growth needs, in-flight conversations), what's pending in each 1:1, where each one is in their growth arc.
- **Cross-capability relationships:** who you partner with, what's been built, who the successor should reach out to first.
- **Practice rituals:** what's running (cadence, ceremonies, content), what's planned.
- **Open commitments:** what you've promised to whom, by when.
- **In-flight strategic initiatives:** the asset cadence, the GTM play, the partnership work — state, owner, next step.
- **Knowledge that's in your head:** the political map, the historical context, the client-specific quirks. Write it down.

## The successor handoff

Ideally, the successor is named before you leave and you have 2–4 weeks of overlap.

In the overlap:
- Daily 1:1 (you and successor)
- Joint 1:1s with each direct report (you, successor, direct)
- Joint client meetings for key relationships
- Joint pursuit reviews
- Your decision authority gradually transfers to the successor
- By the last week, the successor is leading and you're observing

If there's no successor named before you leave: the handoff goes to interim leadership. You document more heavily, identify a "knowledge contact" the team can reach out to (often you, for the first 60 days post-exit), and accept the cost of the gap.

## Communicating to the team

After your boss, before the broader org. In this order:

1. **Each direct report individually, in 1:1.** Tell them why, when you'll leave, what's happening to them. Acknowledge what you've built together. Listen to their reaction.
2. **Skip-level: 2nd-line ICs you've worked with closely.** Brief, in person or 1:1. Same content.
3. **The broader team / practice in a forum.** Email or all-hands, depending on culture and scale.
4. **Cross-functional partners.** A note to peer leaders, key partners.
5. **External: clients, vendors, network.** When the news is public internally.

Match the channel to the relationship. A peer Director gets a personal note. A 200-person practice gets an email. Don't make people hear about your exit from the wrong channel — it damages trust.

## What to actually say

Honest. Brief. Forward-looking. Don't over-explain. Don't justify.

Template for the team email:

> Team,
>
> I want to share that I've decided to [leave Slalom / move to a different role]. My last day in the [practice / role] will be [date].
>
> [1–2 sentences on what's next, brief — even "I'm taking some time" is fine]
>
> [1–2 sentences on what this team has built and what you've valued about working together]
>
> [Logistics: who's interim / successor; how the transition will work; what happens to in-flight work]
>
> [Personal: I'm grateful, available for questions, will continue to be reachable at [contact]]
>
> [Sign-off]

Don't:
- Overdo the gratitude (reads as performative)
- List grievances
- Promise things you can't keep ("I'll always be available" — be realistic)
- Surprise the team with information they should have heard from their direct manager first

## Last 90 days as a leader

Don't become a lame duck. Specific:
- Keep doing 1:1s on cadence
- Make the decisions that need making (don't punt to your successor)
- Don't ghost responsibilities ("that's a successor problem")
- Don't take on new commitments you can't fulfill in the time remaining
- Stay engaged externally (clients, partners) until the handoff
- Document as you go, not in a frantic last week

## What to leave behind

Beyond the transition plan:
- A clear-eyed assessment of the role's reality (the next person needs to know)
- Your read on each direct report (delivered to your successor confidentially)
- The political map (how to navigate)
- The decisions you wish you'd made differently (lessons; not blame)
- The wins you're proudest of (so the team feels seen)

## What not to do

- **Don't trash the firm publicly.** Even if you're leaving for cause. The internet remembers, your network remembers, the firm's reputation outlasts your particular grievance.
- **Don't poach your team.** If people want to follow you, that's their call later, not your sales pitch in the exit window.
- **Don't violate the spirit of confidentiality.** Even if no NDA, certain information is the firm's, not yours.
- **Don't tell people things they shouldn't hear from you.** Calibration outcomes, comp specifics, performance assessments — those belong with the successor and the firm.
- **Don't lose your composure.** The last 90 days test patience; people will ask awkward questions and act in ways that frustrate you. Keep equanimity.

## The relationship arc post-exit

The team you leave is part of your network for the rest of your career. Maintain it deliberately:
- Reach out to former direct reports periodically (no agenda)
- Show up to former-team milestones when invited
- Be available for advice and references
- Don't pull the team toward your next thing in the first 12 months (gives the new leader space to land)

Some of the highest-leverage relationships in your career will be with people you used to manage who later become peers, sponsors, or clients. Treat the exit as the start of the next phase of those relationships, not the end.

## Internal moves vs. external exits

Internal moves are similar but compressed. The principles hold; the timeline shortens; the political stakes change (you'll see these people in the halls).

For internal moves:
- Tell your boss first
- Don't tell your team until your boss has agreed and the new role is confirmed
- Be especially careful about poaching (your old team and your new team both watch this)
- The transition plan is shorter but should still be real
- Maintain ties; you may need to partner with your old team
