# Secure early wins

Early wins build credibility, generate momentum, and signal to the team and the org that you understand the situation. Wrong early wins burn credibility because they tell everyone you don't.

The discipline is picking the *right* wins, not just any visible ones.

## What an early win does

- Builds personal credibility (proves you can deliver, not just talk)
- Generates momentum (small win → bigger win → bigger win)
- Tests your read (the win has to be matched to the situation; if it doesn't land, your diagnosis was off)
- Energizes the team (first 90 days has a different psychological texture; people are watching)
- Buys you time and political capital for harder, longer plays

## The win matrix

Watkins's framework — two axes:

| | Visible | Less visible |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategic / high-impact** | Foundation wins (the real plays) | Stealth wins (build internal capacity; emerge later) |
| **Cosmetic / low-impact** | Vanity wins (avoid — they signal misjudgment) | Time wasters (don't bother) |

The dangerous quadrant is top-right (visible-but-cosmetic). New leaders are most tempted by these because they're easy and visible. They also reveal you don't understand what matters.

## Quick wins (90 days)

Pick 1–2 early wins that:
- Land within 90 days
- Are visible to the team and your boss
- Are clearly strategic (connect to a real outcome you'll be measured on)
- Are low-risk to execute (you don't have full system context yet)
- Position you for the foundation win

Examples:
- A specific decision the previous leader was avoiding (people noticed; making the call earns credibility)
- A visible improvement to a broken process (people felt the pain daily; fixing it shows you listened)
- Closing a particular open issue or risk (clears a long-running irritation)
- Hiring a key role the team has been begging for (the hire itself is the early win)

## Foundation wins (3–9 months)

The real play. Less visible early but compounds over time.

Examples:
- Standing up a new operating cadence (invisible the first month; clearly working by month 6)
- Restructuring around a real strategic priority (controversial in the moment; obviously right in retrospect)
- Building a key relationship (with a major client, partner, peer leader) that pays off over years
- Fixing a structural issue that everyone tolerated (it was "just how things were"; you proved it didn't have to be)

## What to avoid

### Vanity wins (visible-but-cosmetic)

The classic: a new leader reorganizes the team within 30 days. It's visible (everyone notices the new org chart), but it's cosmetic (it doesn't change outcomes) and often counterproductive (it disrupts work that was fine). It signals you didn't take time to understand the team.

Other examples:
- Renaming a function or initiative
- "Updated" branding or visual identity that doesn't change customer value
- New process that adds bureaucracy without solving a real pain
- Hiring a role to "build out the team" without a clear outcome the role will own

### Misaligned wins

Wins that succeed but in the wrong area for the situation. A turnaround leader who delivers a beautiful sustaining-success quick win has missed the situation. The team thinks: "Why aren't they making the hard cuts?"

### Overcommitted wins

The early win that requires resources, alignment, and execution beyond what you can muster in 90 days. When it slips or fails, your credibility is worse than if you'd picked something smaller.

### Wins that step on the predecessor

A win that frames "the previous leader was wrong" can land badly — especially in Sustaining Success situations where the predecessor was beloved. Frame your win as building on what came before, not correcting it.

## How to pick

For each candidate early win, run the test:

1. **Does it match the situation?** (STARS — see `stars-diagnosis-and-matched-strategy.md`)
2. **Is it strategic, or just visible?** (Top-right of the matrix is the trap)
3. **Can you actually deliver it in 90 days?** (Pre-mortem honestly)
4. **Does it position you for the foundation win?** (The early win should set up something bigger)
5. **What's the cost if it fails?** (Some wins that succeed are great; if they fail they damage credibility more than the upside justified)

Pick 1–2 quick wins and 1 foundation win. Communicate them to your boss in the expectations conversation.

## Pre-mortem the early win

Before committing publicly:
- "If this win succeeds spectacularly, what does it actually buy me?"
- "If this fails, what does it cost me?"
- "What would have to be true for it to fail? Is any of that already true?"
- "Who has to be on board for it to land? Are they?"
- "Am I pre-empting a play that was already in motion under my predecessor?"

If the answers are weak, pick a different win.

## Communicate it

The early win is partly about delivering and partly about being seen to deliver. The communication matters:

- Tell your boss about it before pushing it (don't surprise them)
- Tell your team about it as part of the listen-tour wrap (frame it as your read of what the team needs)
- After the win lands, communicate the result clearly (publicly to the team; to your boss; to peer leaders if relevant)
- Credit the team specifically (the win happened because [people], not because of you alone)
- Don't milk it (if you spend 6 weeks crowing about a 30-day win, the next 30 days look quieter than they are)

## After the early win

Don't immediately swing for the fences. The pattern that works:
- Quick win lands at day 60–90
- 30 days of consolidation (don't burn the credibility on something else immediately)
- Then start setting up the foundation win

The temptation is to immediately follow up with another visible win. Resist. The credibility from the first win is more useful as latent capital than as fuel for the next round of attention-seeking.

## Common patterns across STARS

| Situation | Typical early win |
|---|---|
| Start-up | First customer / first ship; first 5 hires; first revenue |
| Turnaround | Made the hard cut others avoided; visible cost reduction; saved a key client relationship |
| Accelerated growth | Hired the senior role that was bottlenecking; built the system that was needed but no one had time to build |
| Realignment | Surfaced the unspoken problem with data; built coalition; small change everyone felt good about |
| Sustaining success | Modest improvement people had wanted; a small honoring of the predecessor; visible recognition of team excellence |

## What if you can't find an early win?

Sometimes the situation doesn't offer an obvious one in 90 days. Options:

1. **Smaller win.** A quick win can be small as long as it's strategic and visible. "Decided X by day 45" is enough.
2. **Process win.** Setting up a new cadence (1:1s, staff, retro) can itself be the early win — it signals operating discipline.
3. **Communicate the early phase explicitly.** "I'm spending the first 90 days on diagnosis. The early wins will come in the next 90." Some bosses will accept this; some won't.
4. **Reconsider the situation.** If the situation truly offers no early-win path, you may have a harder situation than you thought (or your read is wrong).
