# The seven seismic shifts

Watkins's most underrated piece. The shift from senior functional leader (running a department, a domain, a function) to general management (running a business, a practice, a P&L, a multi-function org) requires seven distinct mindset and behavior changes. Most leaders make some; few make all seven.

These compound. Each shift, alone, is hard. Together they reshape what you do every day.

For Bermon's career trajectory — Senior Director in Market Solutions running a consulting practice — these are directly load-bearing. The role is general management of a practice (not just leadership of a function), and the seismic shifts are the work.

## 1. Specialist → Generalist

**The shift.** You can't be the deepest expert in everything anymore. You have to be deep enough to make good calls across many areas, but you'll be less expert in each than the people you depend on.

**What it feels like.** Discomfort. Specialists become senior because they're really good at their thing. As a general manager, you're often the person in the room who is *not* the expert on the topic at hand. You have to lead anyway.

**What goes wrong.** The specialist who refuses to let go — they keep being the deepest engineer / designer / consultant on every project, while neglecting the manager-of-managers and strategy work that's actually their job now.

**What to do.**
- Learn enough about each domain to ask good questions
- Trust the experts (your direct reports and senior ICs) for depth
- Hire experts you can trust
- Let go of doing the deep work yourself; learn to *enable* it instead
- Find a way to keep your specialty alive in small doses (write, teach, mentor) so it doesn't atrophy and you don't resent the role

For a Composable DXP practice lead: you can't be the deepest architect on every engagement anymore. You can be the deepest *practice strategist* and a strong *technical thought partner*, but the per-engagement architecture goes to senior architects.

## 2. Analyst → Integrator

**The shift.** Earlier in your career, success came from analyzing one thing deeply and producing a great answer. As a general manager, success comes from integrating across functions, weighing trade-offs, and making decisions that no single analysis would surface.

**What it feels like.** Comfortable analysis becomes incomplete. You can no longer perfect the answer in one domain because the answer interacts with five others.

**What goes wrong.** The analyst who keeps trying to perfect each piece — and stalls because they refuse to integrate while uncertainties remain.

**What to do.**
- Get comfortable making calls with incomplete information
- Develop the synthesis muscle: "given finance says X, ops says Y, and sales says Z, here's the call"
- Trust the analysts in each function; your job is the synthesis, not the underlying analysis
- Build the tools (frameworks, criteria, decision logs) that make repeated synthesis easier

## 3. Tactician → Strategist

**The shift.** Earlier, success was tactical execution — shipping things, closing deals, delivering quality. Now, success is strategic — picking the right things to ship, the right markets to enter, the right capabilities to build.

**What it feels like.** Time horizons stretch. Today's emergency matters less; the 12-month decision matters more. The work is less visible (strategy doesn't ship in a sprint).

**What goes wrong.** The tactician who keeps fighting today's fires and never makes the strategic calls. By the time they look up, the strategic decisions have been made by default.

**What to do.**
- Defend protected thinking time (see `cadence-by-level.md` in `leadership-meetings-and-cadences`)
- Develop a strategy cadence (annual, quarterly) that forces the longer-arc questions
- Read widely outside your domain (industry, adjacent domains, history)
- Resist the pull of the urgent tactical thing your old self would have jumped on

## 4. Bricklayer → Architect

**The shift.** Earlier, you built things — features, designs, documents, decks. As a general manager, you design organizations and systems that build things. The output is the *org*, not the deliverable.

**What it feels like.** You stop producing tangible work. Your "output" becomes the team's output, mediated by your design choices.

**What goes wrong.** The bricklayer who keeps grabbing the trowel — writing the deck themselves, redesigning the website themselves, doing the analyst work themselves. Their team is under-developed because they never get to do their own work.

**What to do.**
- Design the operating system: the cadence, the rituals, the rubrics, the structure
- Hire and develop the bricklayers; let them do the laying
- Measure your impact in the team's output, not your own
- Keep small specialty practice (writing, occasional contribution) so you don't lose touch — but as exception, not norm

## 5. Problem-solver → Agenda-setter

**The shift.** Earlier, you were rewarded for solving problems. As a general manager, you're rewarded for picking which problems to solve — and which to ignore.

**What it feels like.** A larger fraction of your day is choosing what to spend time on, not doing the time itself. You'll feel less productive even as you become more impactful.

**What goes wrong.** The problem-solver who keeps reflexively grabbing the loudest problem in front of them. They never pause to ask whether it's the *right* problem.

**What to do.**
- Build a quarterly agenda. What are the 3–5 things you're focused on this quarter? Defend the list against incoming distractions.
- Learn to say "not now" or "not me" to plausible-but-not-priority asks
- Audit your time vs. your stated priorities monthly. Where you actually spend time *is* your real agenda.
- Use the OKR or quarterly priorities skill (`leadership-goals-and-okrs`) to make the agenda explicit and shared.

## 6. Warrior → Diplomat

**The shift.** Earlier, success often came from competing — beating the timeline, the competitor, the deadline. As a general manager, success comes from influence, coalition, alliance — diplomacy, not combat.

**What it feels like.** You can't just out-execute the problem. You have to bring people along. Decisions you used to make alone now require buy-in.

**What goes wrong.** The warrior who keeps trying to win every meeting, push every decision, beat every adversary. They burn relationships and find that their next move requires the goodwill they spent.

**What to do.**
- Invest in peer relationships proactively (not just when you need something)
- Build coalitions before you need them
- Master "yes, and" instead of "no, but"
- Learn to lose small battles to win larger ones
- Develop the diplomatic vocabulary (see `leadership-executive-coach` references on Voss/negotiation)

## 7. Supporting cast → Lead

**The shift.** Earlier, you were on a team led by someone else. You contributed; you weren't the protagonist. As a general manager, you *are* the protagonist. The team takes cues from you. Your tone, your pace, your priorities, your equanimity propagate through the org.

**What it feels like.** Visible. Watched. Lonelier. The previous coping mechanism of "I'll just do my work and let the boss handle it" is gone — you are the boss.

**What goes wrong.** The supporting-cast leader who never fully takes the lead. They wait for permission they no longer need. They defer to peers when they should call it. They continue to be a great #2 in a #1 role.

**What to do.**
- Take up the space the role asks for — visibly own decisions, set direction, model behavior
- Accept the visibility (your reactions in a meeting matter; your energy on a tough day matters)
- Build a small set of trusted advisors / coaches you can be unguarded with — the role is lonelier than the previous one
- Practice the equanimity work (see `leadership-stoic-perspective`) — your steady state is the team's steady state

## How to use this framework

Self-assessment: which of the seven have you mostly made? Which are partial? Which have you not really started?

For Bermon at Senior Director / Market Solutions:
- The shifts that are typically hardest at this band are 4 (architect), 5 (agenda-setter), 7 (lead)
- 1 (specialist→generalist) is usually mostly done by Senior Director — but the temptation to grab the trowel persists
- 6 (warrior→diplomat) is critical at this band because the work is increasingly cross-capability and partnership-driven

A useful exercise: ask three people who know your work well to rate you 1–5 on each of the seven. Average their reads. The lowest scores are your growth areas; the highest are your strengths to leverage.
