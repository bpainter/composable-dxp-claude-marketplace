# Identity vs. role

The leader who *is* the role is fragile. The leader who *holds* the role is anti-fragile.

This is not detachment. It's the discipline of doing the work seriously without becoming the work — so that when the role inevitably ends or changes, you remain whole.

## The distinction

| | Identity-fused leader | Role-holding leader |
|---|---|---|
| Statement | "I am a Senior Director" | "I am currently serving as a Senior Director" |
| Vacation | Difficult; feels wrong | Necessary; protected |
| Off-time | Mostly checking phone | Genuinely off |
| Bad day at work | Pervades the evening | Stays at the day |
| End of role | Existential crisis | Significant transition |
| Relationship to feedback | Personal attack | Information about the role |
| Relationship to peers | Competitive comparison | Collegial collaboration |

These are two ends of a continuum. Most leaders are somewhere in the middle and don't notice when they're drifting toward identity-fusion until something shocks them awake.

## Why identity-fusion happens

It's not character weakness. It's structural:

- **The role rewards attention.** The more you put in, the better you do. The slope is real.
- **The role reflects status.** "Senior Director at Slalom" is identity-coded in social settings.
- **The role takes time.** As a fraction of waking life, it's the dominant activity.
- **The role provides feedback loops.** Every interaction signals worth or its lack.
- **The role's culture often reinforces it.** Modern professional culture is built around identity-fusion (LinkedIn bios, "what do you do," industry awards).

Without active counter-discipline, identity drifts into role over years. The Stoic practice is one way to reverse the drift.

## The signals of over-fusion

You're drifting if:
- You can't take vacation, or vacation feels like punishment
- A bad day at work derails the whole evening
- You introduce yourself by your role almost exclusively
- Your closest non-family relationships are all role-related
- You can't picture life without the role
- A peer's promotion or recognition produces visceral resentment
- Your hobbies / interests outside work have atrophied
- Your physical health has declined and you tell yourself "after this quarter…"
- You spend off-time mentally rehearsing work conversations
- You'd describe yourself as "addicted to the work"

Three or more, especially over a sustained period, is a yellow signal. Five or more is red.

## Why this matters operationally

Identity-fused leaders make worse decisions. Specifically:

- **Risk-aversion.** Decisions that might cost the role feel like existential threats. They're not.
- **Defensiveness.** Feedback is felt as personal, and rejected.
- **Hard calls deferred.** Anything that risks your standing in the role is hard to do.
- **Burnout trajectory.** No recovery time → degraded judgment → more crises → more time → less recovery.
- **Lower team trust.** Teams sense the fusion. They protect the leader from bad news (because the leader can't take it). The leader gets a worse picture of reality.
- **Narrower thinking.** You stop reading widely, talking to people outside the role. The role is your whole world, so it's the whole world you have to think with.

A role-holding leader does the work fully — often better — without these failure modes.

## The Stoic frame

Two complementary techniques:

### Negative visualization on the role

Periodically — maybe quarterly — imagine the role ending tomorrow. Layoff, exit, re-org, you choose to leave. Sit with it.

The first time you do this it'll be uncomfortable. Good — that's the diagnostic. The discomfort is the measure of fusion.

After a few rounds, the discomfort fades. You realize you'd survive. You'd have skills, relationships, a life. The role is a meaningful slice of you, not your totality.

This is not a path to leaving the role. Often the opposite: leaders who do this work often re-engage with the role from a healthier place. They're choosing it, not being chained to it.

### The view from above on identity

Take the long view. In 5 years, will you still be in this role? Probably not — most senior roles have a 3–7 year arc. In 25 years? Almost certainly not. In 50 years? You'll likely be retired, possibly gone.

The role is a chapter, not the book. Treat it as a chapter. Engage fully; don't mistake it for the whole.

## Practical disciplines

### Hobbies and interests outside the role

Maintain at least 2–3 substantive activities completely unrelated to your work:
- A physical practice (sport, training, hiking, climbing)
- A creative practice (writing, music, art, cooking, woodworking)
- A community / family / faith practice (volunteering, parenting investment, religious community, friendship circle)

These are not "balance" — they're identity diversification. The leader whose only identity is the role is brittle. The leader with multiple identities is durable.

### Friends and relationships outside the role

Some of your closest people should not work with you. They should care about you regardless of whether you're successful at the role. They should be willing to challenge you when the role is making you worse. They should be the people you call when something goes wrong.

If your social circle is entirely colleagues and clients, that's a fusion signal.

### Off-time that's actually off

Not "I checked email lightly all weekend." Genuine off-time:
- Vacation actually taken (and protected from work creep)
- Phone in another room during family time
- One full day per week that's genuinely unplugged from work
- An evening per week where the laptop stays closed

The leader who can't do this is signaling something to themselves. The signal is information.

### A clean transition exit plan

Have a draft mental plan for the role ending. What's next? What's your trajectory? Where would you want to land? Not because you're planning to leave; because having the plan reduces the existential weight of staying.

## When the role does end

When the role eventually changes (promotion, exit, re-org, retirement), identity-fused leaders crash. Role-holding leaders transition.

The crash often surprises everyone, including the person experiencing it. They were "fine" while doing the role; the role ending reveals how much of them was the role.

The transition for a role-holder is also significant — the work was meaningful. But there's something on the other side of it: the person who held the role is still there, with skills, relationships, and other parts of life intact.

## Specific work for Bermon

For a Senior Director in a consulting practice, the role-fusion risks are:
- Role status is industry-visible (LinkedIn, conferences, network)
- Travel and client demands compress non-role identity
- Compensation and recognition are tightly tied to role performance
- Peers are mostly role-related
- Career-ladder pressure (next promotion, next move) keeps identity oriented forward

Counter-disciplines:
- Maintain non-Slalom relationships and identities deliberately
- Have hobbies that aren't compatible with always-on phone (lifting, climbing, sailing, woodworking)
- Take real vacation and make it a leadership statement to the team
- Build the perspective practice that holds the role at the right distance
- Cultivate one or two truly close friends who don't work in tech / consulting at all

For a neurodivergent leader specifically (per `leadership-executive-coach`), the fusion risk can be compounded by hyperfocus and the way ADHD/autism brains can lock onto a single demanding domain. The deliberate identity-diversification is more important, not less.

## The end-of-life test

A useful long-arc question: imagine yourself at 80, looking back. What do you most want to be true about how you spent these years? About what you built? About who you became as a person, not just a leader?

The honest answer rarely is "I was a really good Senior Director." More often it's about people, contribution, character, relationships, what the role *enabled* you to do for others.

The role is a chapter. Be a great character in the chapter. Don't confuse the character for the book.
