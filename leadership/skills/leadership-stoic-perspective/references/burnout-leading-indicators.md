# Burnout — leading indicators and what to do

Burnout is rarely sudden. It builds across months, often invisible to the person experiencing it. By the time it's named, the recovery is long.

The leader's discipline is to catch it early — when the signals are still leading indicators, not crisis.

## What burnout actually is

Christina Maslach's research-based definition (used in the WHO's official classification): a syndrome from chronic workplace stress that hasn't been successfully managed. Three dimensions:

1. **Exhaustion** — physical, cognitive, emotional depletion
2. **Cynicism / depersonalization** — emotional distance from the work and the people in it
3. **Reduced sense of professional efficacy** — the feeling that you're not effective anymore, even if you objectively are

Note: it's not just "tired." Tired is recoverable with rest. Burnout requires structural intervention.

## The leading indicators

Watch for these in yourself and your team. Three or more sustained over weeks is a signal; five or more is yellow; seven or more is red.

### Physical signals

- Sleep degradation (taking longer to fall asleep, waking at 3am, not feeling rested even after 8 hrs)
- Frequent minor illness (recurring colds; the immune system is degraded)
- Weight changes (either direction)
- Decreased exercise frequency (or compulsive exercise as escape)
- Increased alcohol or other substances
- Persistent low-grade physical complaints (headaches, back pain, GI issues)
- Tension in shoulders / jaw that doesn't release

### Cognitive signals

- Difficulty making decisions you used to make easily
- Re-reading the same email/document multiple times without absorbing it
- Working longer to produce less
- Procrastination on the things that matter most (Q2 work)
- Unable to settle into deep work; constantly checking trivial things
- Catastrophizing — small problems feel large
- Black-and-white thinking — nuance has disappeared

### Emotional signals

- Cynicism toward the work, the team, the firm, the clients
- Irritability — short fuse with people you used to be patient with
- Feeling distant from people you care about
- Loss of pleasure in things that used to be enjoyable
- Sense of being trapped — "I can't leave because…"
- Resentment that surfaces at inappropriate moments
- Numbness instead of either positive or negative emotion
- Persistent feeling of "going through the motions"

### Behavioral signals

- Skipping or shortening 1:1s with directs
- Avoiding the team's office hours / async channels
- Not taking lunch / meals at desk
- Cancelling personal plans for work that wasn't actually urgent
- Vacation deferred indefinitely
- Email-checking compulsively even when off
- Isolating from peers and colleagues
- Avoiding direct conversations (preferring slack to face-to-face for hard topics)

### Relational signals

- Closest people noticing you're "not yourself"
- Direct reports tiptoeing around you
- Spouse / partner asking if you're okay (often the leading edge)
- Friends commenting on your absence
- Kids asking why you're stressed

## The cost of not catching it

If burnout proceeds:
- Decisions get worse, often visibly so
- Team trust erodes (they see the pattern even if you don't)
- Health consequences (some serious; cardiovascular, mental health)
- Relationships damaged (often the lasting ones)
- Career consequences (a senior leader can't burn out for long without it showing)
- Recovery takes 6–18+ months once established

The cost of catching it at the leading-indicator stage is much lower. A few weeks of conscious adjustment vs. months of recovery.

## What to do — the recovery sequence

If you're seeing 3+ signals sustained over weeks:

### 1. Sleep first

7–8 hours, defended. No exceptions for two weeks. Almost everything else clarifies when sleep is fixed. The compounded sleep deprivation is often the underlying multiplier.

### 2. One non-negotiable recovery practice

Pick one and protect it absolutely. Not three; one.
- Daily walk (45 min, no phone)
- Exercise 4x/week (calendar-blocked)
- Phone in another room for evening hours
- Weekly screen-free day

The single practice for 4 weeks. Then add a second.

### 3. Reduce inputs

For 30 days:
- Cut 25% of your meetings (audit the calendar; kill or async)
- Defer non-essential commitments ("not this quarter")
- Reduce information consumption (less news, less Twitter, less Slack scroll)
- Say no to new asks ruthlessly

The system has to come down from the load before recovery is possible.

### 4. Have one honest conversation

Pick someone you trust deeply (spouse, mentor, trusted friend, coach, therapist). Tell them honestly what's happening. Not to solve it; to name it.

The naming is itself recovery work. Burnout thrives in isolation.

### 5. Take real time off

If you've been deferring vacation, take it. Even a long weekend with phone in a drawer. Build to a longer break (a week minimum) within the next 8 weeks.

If "I can't possibly take time off" — that's the signal. The leader who genuinely can't take a week off has a structural problem (over-centralized on themselves) that the time off would help diagnose.

### 6. Consider professional support

For sustained burnout, especially with depression / anxiety symptoms:
- Therapist (especially CBT-trained, or specialized in high-performer burnout)
- Coach with mental-health credentials
- EAP (employee assistance program)
- Psychiatric evaluation if mood symptoms persist

These aren't "weakness" or "for other people." They're the same kind of professional help a leader would seek for a physical condition.

### 7. Address the conditions

Recovery isn't permanent if you return to the same conditions. Once stabilized:
- What in the role / system contributed to the burnout?
- What can change? (Cadence, scope, delegation, hiring, structural choices)
- What can't change in this role? (And does that make this the right role?)

The structural conversation is uncomfortable. It's also where durable recovery lives.

## When you can't catch it in yourself

The cruel feature of burnout: the cognitive degradation makes it harder to notice. You're the worst person to assess your own state.

So: build the external instruments.
- The honest conversation partner who'll tell you when you're not yourself
- The coach / therapist who has standing visibility
- The annual physical with someone who'll catch the markers
- The peer leader who's permission-slipped to call you out
- The spouse / partner who'll say "you're not okay"

Listen when these people speak. The burned-out brain will dismiss them; that dismissal is itself a signal.

## When it's the team, not you

The same indicators apply at team scale. If multiple team members show 3+ signals sustained, the team is in collective burnout territory. Common causes:
- Unsustainable workload (capacity ÷ commitments < 1)
- Hero culture (over-rewarding the heroics; under-investing in sustainable rhythm)
- Poor priority discipline (everything is urgent)
- Inadequate staffing on a stretch project
- Performative urgency from leadership
- Loss of agency / autonomy

The team-burnout intervention is the same shape as individual: reduce inputs, restore sleep / recovery, fix structural conditions, address the conditions that allowed the burnout to develop.

## For Bermon's specific context

Senior consulting roles have particular burnout pathways:

- **Travel + client + always-on culture.** Compounds quickly without explicit defense.
- **The "responsive senior" trap.** The senior leader who responds to every Slack at all hours signals to the team that they should too, then burns out faster.
- **Client crisis pull.** A practice with multiple engagements always has someone in crisis. The senior leader who absorbs every crisis depletes faster.
- **Pursuit cycle pressure.** The deal-close moments are intense; recovery between them is often skipped.
- **Career-trajectory anxiety.** "If I don't push now, I won't make the next level" is a common driver, often unfounded.

The counter-disciplines for these specifically:
- Phone in another room overnight (no Slack on phone after 8pm)
- Vacation taken and protected (and used to model behavior to the team)
- Crisis triage — not every client crisis needs the senior leader; build the layer of crisis-handling below you
- Pursuit cycle recovery built in (a week of lighter cadence after each major close, win or lose)
- Long-arc career conversations (with mentors, not just with your boss) that calibrate against panic

## The Stoic frame on burnout

The dichotomy of control work specifically applies. Much of what feels burnout-inducing is energy spent on the uncontrollable:
- Other people's chaotic urgency
- Outcomes you can influence but not determine
- The market / firm conditions
- The pace of change

Redirecting energy to the controllable internal goals — your effort, your judgment, your boundaries, your response — reduces the burnout load even before the structural work happens.

Negative visualization on losing the role is also useful here. It reveals how much of the burnout-inducing pressure is self-imposed by the assumption that the role is permanent.
