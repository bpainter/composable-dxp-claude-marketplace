# Insults, anger, and the reactivity gap

The specific Stoic work on the moments leaders most often blow up — the slight, the disrespect, the public criticism, the email that cuts. Irvine devotes whole chapters to it because the ancient Stoics did, because reactivity to insult is universal and durable.

## Why this matters for senior leaders

Senior leadership creates a target. You'll get:
- Public criticism (sometimes fair, sometimes not)
- Backchannel disparagement
- Direct pushback, sometimes pointed
- Subordinates who think they could do your job better
- Peers who compete in zero-sum-feeling ways
- Clients who project their frustrations onto you
- Internet commentary if you have any external presence

How you respond shapes your effectiveness, your team's confidence in you, and your own perspective integrity.

The reactive leader retaliates, gets defensive, withdraws, or stews. Each costs more than the original insult.

The role-holding leader has a gap. The gap is where good response lives.

## The Stoic move on insults (Epictetus → Irvine)

The Stoic framework:

1. **Pause before reacting.** The reflexive response is rarely the wise one. Buy a moment.
2. **Assess the source.** Who is saying this? What's their context? What's their interest?
3. **Assess the content.** Is it true? Partially true? False?
4. **Choose the response based on the assessment, not the sting.**

If the insult is *true*: thank the source. They've given you information. Improve.

If the insult is *partially true*: extract the true part. Address it. Discard the rest.

If the insult is *false*: it doesn't actually touch you. Letting it provoke a reaction gives it power it doesn't otherwise have. Note the source's reasons (often more about them than you), and let it pass.

The hardest move is the third. The false insult feels like an attack on identity. The Stoic discipline says: only your own mistakes can damage your character. Other people's false claims are noise.

## The trichotomy applied to insults

- **What's in your control:** how you respond, what you let it mean about you, what you do with it (extract lessons, ignore, address)
- **What's outside your control:** that the insult was made, the source's character, what others believe about it
- **What's partly in your control:** your reputation in the longer arc (you influence by behavior over time, not by fighting each insult in the moment)

Most reactive responses are energy spent on the uncontrollable (trying to make the source un-say it; trying to win the moment; trying to control what others think). The Stoic redirect is to the controllable.

## Anger — Irvine's framing

Anger as "anti-joy." The Stoics treated anger as the most destructive emotion because it both:
- Damages the angry person (degrades judgment, harms health, costs relationships)
- Damages the people around the angry person (especially those with less power to push back)

For senior leaders specifically, anger is professionally costly. The leader who erupts in a meeting damages trust in every meeting after. The leader who emails angrily creates a written record. The leader who berates a direct report destroys the conditions for honest reporting.

This isn't to say anger isn't real or sometimes righteous. It's to say *acted-on anger* almost always costs more than it produces.

## Building the gap

The gap between stimulus and response is trainable. Specific practices:

### The breath

When you feel the spike (and you'll learn to recognize it — chest tightens, jaw clenches, heat in face): pause. One breath. Sometimes that's enough.

### The 24-hour rule for written response

For any email / Slack / text that triggers reactive response: do not send within 24 hours. Draft if you must. Sleep on it. Rewrite.

90% of the time, you'll send something different (or nothing) the next day. 10% of the time you'll send what you drafted, intentionally.

### The "what would I want a friend to do" question

In the moment: what would I want a friend or mentor to advise me to do here? Often the answer surfaces immediately — and is different from the reactive impulse.

### The view from above (in seconds)

Zoom out. Will this matter in a week? In a year? If the answer is "no," the proportional response is small.

### The pre-meeting anchor

For high-stakes / high-trigger meetings, take 60 seconds beforehand:
- Set your intention (patience, presence, clarity)
- Pre-commit to the gap ("if I feel the spike, I will pause")
- Negative-visualize the worst version of the meeting and your composed response

This pre-loads the discipline, so it's available when needed.

### The trusted-other check

For sustained reactive patterns with a specific person, talk to a trusted other before the next interaction. Sometimes the conversation reveals that the trigger is mostly in you (not them), or vice versa. Either way, the discussion shapes your response.

## When the source is more powerful than you

Insults from above are different from insults from below. The asymmetry matters.

If a senior leader (your boss, your boss's boss, an executive) criticizes you publicly or unfairly:
- The reactive response (publicly defending yourself, escalating, going quiet for weeks) usually backfires
- The Stoic response: assess privately, address calmly later if needed, don't let one incident reshape your work
- Often the senior person doesn't remember the incident the way you do; the disproportion is itself information

If the asymmetric criticism is part of a *pattern*, it's a different conversation (with a mentor, a coach, your boss directly). One incident is data; a pattern is a problem.

## When the source is less powerful than you

Insults from below (a direct report, a junior peer) are equally common but harder to navigate well. The reactive response (squashing the criticism, retaliating, becoming "the boss who can't be questioned") destroys psychological safety.

The Stoic response:
- Pause. Take it as data.
- Is there truth? Address. Acknowledge the truth even if the delivery was poor.
- Is there context the person doesn't have? Share it (without defensiveness).
- Is the criticism really about something else (a frustration with the org, a personal stress)? Address the real thing.
- Don't punish the surfacing. The person who surfaces uncomfortable truth is a gift, even when the delivery is rough.

## When it's truly slanderous / false / damaging

Sometimes the criticism is actually false and damaging — to your reputation, your standing, your work. The Stoic frame is not "absorb everything silently."

Address it:
- Calmly, with evidence, in the right forum
- After you've gained your composure, not in the heat
- With a goal (correction of the record; clarification; resolution) — not retaliation
- With the option of not responding if the cost of response exceeds the cost of the slander

Sometimes the wisest response is no response. The slander dissipates faster than the response would have.

## Recovery from the rare blow-up

You will sometimes lose your composure. Even with practice. When it happens:

1. **Notice.** Within the hour, with self-compassion.
2. **Acknowledge.** To the affected person, briefly: "I was reactive in that meeting; that wasn't my best self. I want to address [substance] more thoughtfully."
3. **Don't over-apologize.** A clean acknowledgment lands better than a flood of apology.
4. **Reflect.** What triggered it? What's the pattern? What gap-building would have helped?
5. **Resume practice.** A blow-up doesn't unmake the practice; it reveals where the practice needs deeper roots.

The leader who blows up rarely, owns it cleanly, and adjusts is more trustworthy than the leader who never blows up but is constantly stiff and controlled.

## The compounding effect

The gap is a muscle. Build it deliberately for 6 months and the reactivity load on you drops dramatically. People notice; the team feels it; your judgment in difficult moments improves; the cost of high-stakes interactions decreases.

The compounding works the other way too. A leader who lets reactivity run for years often gets to a point where the team has shaped itself around the volatility. The recovery is harder.
