# Management anti-patterns (the Nightingales)

The Nightingales' book is a counterpoint to the standard leadership canon: instead of "here's what great leaders do," it's "here's what most managers actually do, and why it's broken." It pairs naturally with the Stoic perspective work because most of the patterns trace to leaders who can't see themselves clearly.

This reference catalogs the patterns most relevant to senior consulting leadership and provides a self-audit format.

## The patterns

### 1. The startup superhero

**The pattern.** The leader as savior. Working insane hours. Becoming the single point of failure. "Only I can fix this." Modeling unsustainable behavior to the team. Conflating identity with the role's hardest moments.

**Why it shows up.** The leader's identity is fused with being the person who can solve anything (see `identity-vs-role.md`). The work-as-identity feels good in the moment.

**Why it breaks.** The team learns helplessness — they stop developing because the hero swoops in. The hero burns out. When the hero takes a break or leaves, everything falls apart. The org becomes brittle.

**The fix.** Visible delegation. Visible vulnerability ("I don't know — let's figure it out together"). Visible non-heroics. Take the vacation. Let the team handle the crisis without you. Build the layer of crisis-handling below you so you're not the only fire-fighter.

### 2. "You won't brilliant your way out of this"

**The pattern.** The smart leader who assumes individual brilliance can solve every problem. Skipping system design. Ignoring team capacity. Believing the next 10x decision will fix everything.

**Why it shows up.** Senior leaders got there by being smart. Brilliance worked at lower levels. The default keeps reaching for it.

**Why it breaks.** Most senior-leader problems are system problems, not individual ones. No amount of personal brilliance fixes a broken team structure or an unsustainable cadence.

**The fix.** Diagnose at system level. Ask: "What's in the system that's producing this outcome? What would I change in conditions, structure, hiring, cadence?" Resist the urge to be the smartest person solving the immediate puzzle.

### 3. Hiring superheroes

**The pattern.** Hiring for the hero pattern. "We need a 10x engineer." "Get me someone exceptional." Building a team of would-be saviors who don't collaborate.

**Why it shows up.** It's the manager-version of the superhero pattern — finding people who'll do what the manager believes they should.

**Why it breaks.** Hero-hiring produces individualists who don't build team capacity, create bottlenecks of their own, and often leave when they realize they're being asked to be the hero too.

**The fix.** Hire for the team's actual needs. Reward collaboration. Use Performance Profiles (see `leadership-hiring`). Reduce the hero premium in your decisions.

### 4. Failure to follow up on diversity

**The pattern.** Public commitments to diversity, no operational follow-through. Performative recruiting. No accountability for outcomes. Inclusive language without inclusive practice.

**Why it shows up.** Diversity is hard, slow, and politically charged. The performative version is easy.

**Why it breaks.** It's worse than not committing. People notice the gap between rhetoric and reality. The most-marginalized leave first. The pattern reinforces.

**The fix.** Specific, measurable commitments. Public progress. Hold yourself accountable in the same forums where you made the commitment. Don't make the commitment if you're not going to operationalize.

### 5. The "secret to better interviews"

**The pattern.** Interviews as personality auditions. "Cultural fit" as the deciding factor. Interview teams that hire people who look like them.

**Why it shows up.** Without structure, interviews regress to gut and pattern-match.

**Why it breaks.** Cultural-fit hiring produces monocultures. Pattern-match hiring entrenches existing biases. The team's ceiling is the team that hired it.

**The fix.** Structured, evidence-based hiring (see `leadership-hiring`). Evidence per Performance Profile bullet. Diverse interview panels. Calibration sessions. "Cultural add" as the alternative to "cultural fit."

### 6. Big lies, little lies (and the cheat code to a promotion)

**The pattern.** Leaders who play the politics game well — selectively share information, manage perceptions, optimize for the next promotion rather than for the work. Get promoted; the team underneath them suffers.

**Why it shows up.** The org rewards it. The promotion goes to the political player; the principled grinder watches.

**Why it breaks.** The team senses the politics. Trust erodes. The political player's career trajectory looks great on paper and produces real damage to the people they manage.

**The fix.** Work for an org that rewards substance over politics. Hire managers based on team outcomes, not on their personal upward optics. Be the leader who calls out the pattern rather than playing it. (And accept the cost.)

### 7. The open-faced shit sandwich

**The pattern.** The traditional "feedback sandwich" (positive — corrective — positive). Leaves the recipient confused, defensive, or unsure what was actually being said.

**Why it shows up.** Managers were taught it as a "compassionate" way to give feedback.

**Why it breaks.** It softens the criticism so much it doesn't land. It also corrupts the genuine positive feedback (recipients learn that praise is just packaging for criticism).

**The fix.** Direct, specific, behavior-based feedback. SBI model (Situation-Behavior-Impact). Praise and criticism *separately* and *frequently* — not bundled in the rare difficult conversation. (See `leadership-people-leader` references on feedback.)

### 8. How to talk to leaders about firing people

**The pattern.** Procrastinating the firing decision. Hoping it'll resolve itself. Telling yourself "give it one more quarter" indefinitely. The team and the underperformer both suffer for months.

**Why it shows up.** Firing is genuinely hard. Most leaders haven't done it well. The cost feels personal.

**Why it breaks.** Delayed firing damages the team more than the firing itself. The underperformer lives in dread for months. The team loses faith in management.

**The fix.** Make the call when the evidence is in. Do the firing humanely (notice, fairness, dignity). Most-improved aspect: when you decide, act within 30 days. (See `leadership-people-leader` on the actual conversation.)

### 9. Why your team is so angry

**The pattern.** The team is unhappy and the leader doesn't know why. Or: the leader knows but won't address the underlying causes.

**Why it shows up.** Direct surfacing of team frustration is uncomfortable. Easier to assume things are fine.

**Why it breaks.** Unaddressed anger doesn't dissipate. It compounds. It eventually shows up in attrition, performance decline, and explosions.

**The fix.** Skip-levels. Honest 1:1s. The "what's the brutal fact you're not telling me" question. Address what's surfaced, even if uncomfortable.

### 10. Leadership is not about your good intentions

**The pattern.** Leaders who excuse impact through intent. "I didn't mean to come across that way." "That wasn't my intention." "They're misreading me."

**Why it shows up.** Intent is real and often genuinely good. The leader feels misunderstood when impact diverges from intent.

**Why it breaks.** Impact is what people experience. Intent doesn't change impact. Repeated divergence between intent and impact produces a team that doesn't trust the leader's words because actions don't match.

**The fix.** Care about impact more than intent. When impact lands wrong, own the impact (not just the intent). Adjust behavior, not just explanation. ("I see how that came across; let me try again" lands better than "that wasn't my intention.")

### 11. Performative urgency / "go faster"

**The pattern.** Treating everything as urgent, always. "We need to move faster." "Why isn't this done?" "Sense of urgency!"

**Why it shows up.** Urgency feels like leadership. Pace is visible; thoughtfulness isn't.

**Why it breaks.** Constant urgency is exhausting and produces worse decisions. The team learns to perform urgency back rather than do good work. Real emergencies get lost in the noise.

**The fix.** Distinguish actual urgency from performative urgency. Most senior decisions benefit from a 24-hour pause. Move fast on the things that need it; resist the urge to manufacture urgency on the things that don't.

### 12. "Stop doing" exists for a reason

**The pattern.** Leaders who keep adding initiatives, never killing any. "We're going to also start [X]." The team's plate keeps growing; nothing comes off.

**Why it shows up.** Adding feels like leadership. Subtracting feels like giving up.

**Why it breaks.** The team eventually breaks. Quality drops. Senior people leave because nothing they care about gets the focus it needs.

**The fix.** Explicit "stop doing" decisions paired with every "start doing" decision. (See `saying-no-and-killing-priorities.md` in `leadership-goals-and-okrs`.)

## Why this pairs with the Stoic frame

The Nightingales' patterns are mostly perspective failures dressed up as management styles. The leader who can't see themselves clearly falls into them; the leader with active perspective practice catches them earlier.

Specific connections:

| Anti-pattern | Stoic counter |
|---|---|
| Superhero | Negative visualization on the role; identity-vs-role separation |
| Performative urgency | View from above; dichotomy of control |
| Procrastinating firing | Voluntary discomfort; the courage to do the hard thing |
| Excusing via intent | Honest self-examination in the end-of-day reflection |
| Promotion-optimization | Long-arc identity work |
| Feedback sandwich | The discipline to be direct (Stoic *parrhesia* — frank speech) |

## The honest cultural read for consulting

Slalom-context (and consulting more broadly):
- The startup superhero pattern shows up frequently in senior consultants who built their reputation on personal heroics
- Performative urgency is endemic in client-facing work (clients perform urgency at us, we absorb and amplify)
- Hiring for "cultural fit" is the field's most common hiring failure
- Promotion-optimization is structurally rewarded by the firm's career ladder
- Adding-without-subtracting is the practice-leader version of the always-yes-to-clients pattern

Naming these honestly is the first move. The Stoic discipline of seeing yourself clearly is the soil in which the management improvements grow.
