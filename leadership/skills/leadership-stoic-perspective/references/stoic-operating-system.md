# The Stoic operating system (Irvine)

William Irvine's translation of ancient Stoicism into a modern practical operating system. Five core techniques. Each is small; together they reshape how a leader holds their work over years.

## The grand goal — tranquility / equanimity

Irvine's adaptation of the Stoics' core aim. *Not* detachment, *not* indifference, *not* numbness. Equanimity: the inner stability that lets you respond to events with judgment rather than reaction. Joy is compatible; care is essential; equanimity is the substrate.

For a leader, this matters because reactive leaders make worse decisions, demoralize their teams, and burn out faster. Equanimity is the long-term competitive advantage.

## Technique 1 — Negative visualization (premeditatio malorum)

**The practice.** Periodically, briefly, contemplate losing the things you have. The role you hold could end. The team you've built could disperse. Your health could change. A loved one could be lost.

**The aim.** Not gloom — *gratitude calibration*. By imagining the loss, you re-experience the having. You also confront how much of your daily life consists of contingent gifts you've stopped noticing.

**How leaders use it:**
- Morning: 60 seconds. "If this role ended today, what would I miss most? What would I have left undone?" Often surfaces what you should be prioritizing.
- Before a difficult meeting with a peer: imagine they're not in your professional life next year. How do you want to have shown up?
- Before time with family: imagine this is the last week. How present do you want to be?
- Quarterly: imagine the practice you lead is being shut down. What would you be most proud of having built? What would you regret not having tried?

**The trap to avoid.** Negative visualization is not catastrophizing. Catastrophizing is "this will happen and it will be terrible." Negative visualization is "this *could* happen, and if it did, here's how I'd respond." The first is anxiety; the second is preparation.

**The Stoic source:** Seneca's letters; Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations*.

## Technique 2 — The dichotomy (and trichotomy) of control

**The practice.** Sort what arises into:
- **Entirely within your control:** your judgments, intentions, responses, effort
- **Entirely outside your control:** other people's actions, market conditions, weather, the past
- **Partly within your control:** outcomes that you influence but don't determine — your team's results, the deals you pursue, your kid's choices

**The aim.** Direct effort to what's controllable. Release attachment to outcomes that aren't fully yours. For partly-controllable things, distinguish the *outcome* (partly controllable) from the *internal goal* (entirely controllable — "did I bring my best effort and judgment?").

**The trichotomy.** This is Irvine's contribution. Epictetus's original dichotomy is binary; most leadership work lives in the partly-controllable middle. The trichotomy gives you a better frame for that middle: focus on *internal goals* (controllable) rather than *external goals* (partly so).

Example: closing a $2M deal.
- External goal: close the deal. Partly controllable.
- Internal goal: I bring my best preparation, my best judgment in the conversation, my best follow-through. Entirely controllable.

If you focus on the external goal, every loss feels like personal failure. If you focus on the internal goal, you can lose a deal you played perfectly and still know you did your work.

**How leaders use it:**
- When anxious about an outcome: explicitly sort. What's mine? What's not? Where am I expending energy on uncontrollables?
- When a deal / promotion / decision goes wrong: did I do *my* part well? If yes, the result is information about the world, not about me.
- For team performance: I influence; I don't control. My internal goal is to design conditions for the team to succeed; their actual success is partly theirs.

**The trap to avoid.** This is not "I'm not responsible for outcomes." It's "I'm responsible for *my* contribution; the outcome is co-determined by factors beyond me." Senior leaders are accountable for outcomes; the dichotomy is internal psychological work, not external excuse-making.

## Technique 3 — Voluntary discomfort

**The practice.** Periodically, deliberately do something hard or uncomfortable. A cold shower. A fast. A hard workout. A difficult conversation you're avoiding. A public push beyond your comfort zone.

**The aim.** Three benefits:
1. **Resilience.** You learn that discomfort is survivable; the next involuntary hard thing is less destabilizing.
2. **Calibration.** You realize most of what feels "hard" is actually mild. Reset your scale.
3. **Gratitude.** Voluntary discomfort makes the regular comforts of life feel like the gifts they are.

**How leaders use it:**
- Cold showers (genuinely useful even though it sounds gimmicky)
- Periodic fasting (24-hour, weekly or monthly — physical and psychological)
- Hard physical training (lifting, running, cycling at intensity)
- Doing the hard conversation you've been avoiding (the most leadership-relevant version)
- Volunteering to facilitate the meeting you'd rather hide in the back of
- Saying the unpopular thing in the room when it's true
- Pushing yourself to ask for the meeting / introduction / sponsorship you don't yet feel "ready" for

**The trap to avoid.** Voluntary discomfort is not asceticism for its own sake. It's calibration. Doing it constantly defeats the point (you're now living in discomfort and it's the new baseline). Episodic, deliberate, then return to comfort.

## Technique 4 — The last-time meditation

**The practice.** Imagine that the routine thing you're about to do is the last time you'll do it.

**The aim.** Presence. Most of life is treated as if there's an infinite supply, so we attend partially. The last-time framing makes presence the obvious choice.

**How leaders use it:**
- Before a 1:1 with a direct: "What if this is the last 1:1 I'll ever have with this person?"
- Before a meal with family: "If this is the last weekend dinner with my kids before something changes…"
- Before a hard conversation: "If this is the last conversation I have with this colleague…"
- At a milestone: "This may be the last time I lead this team through a planning cycle. What do I want to do with it?"

**The trap to avoid.** Performative gravitas. The point isn't to make every moment heavy; it's to bring fuller attention to the moments that you'd otherwise rush through. Some 1:1s are routine; some are unique; the last-time meditation helps you tell the difference.

## Technique 5 — The view from above

**The practice.** Periodically zoom out. From your role, take the view of a year. From your year, take the view of a decade. From your life, take the view of a century. From your generation, take the view of human history.

**The aim.** Calibrate the emotional charge of the current moment against larger scales. The crisis that feels life-defining usually doesn't, viewed from a year away.

**How leaders use it:**
- Mid-acute-stress: pause. Take the view from a year. Will this matter then?
- Quarterly: take the view from a decade. Am I building the leadership identity I want for the long arc?
- Before a high-emotion decision: zoom out. What would I tell a friend in this situation?
- After a perceived failure: view from above. Where does this rank against actual life setbacks?

**The trap to avoid.** Used as avoidance, the view from above can suppress legitimate response. Some things genuinely matter at the immediate scale. The view from above is an additional perspective, not a replacement.

## Putting it together — the operating rhythm

| When | Practice |
|---|---|
| Morning (5 min) | Brief negative visualization + intention setting + dichotomy check on today's anxieties |
| Before high-stakes moments (60 sec) | Trichotomy: my internal goal here is X; outcome is partly controllable |
| Mid-day reactivity (30 sec) | Pause. Breath. View from above. |
| End of day (10 min) | Review: where did I react vs. respond? What did I waste energy on that wasn't mine? |
| Weekly | Did I take any voluntary discomfort? Did I extend the last-time framing? Where am I drifting from the practice? |
| Quarterly | Identity-vs-role retro. Am I serving the role or serving the identity? |
| Annual | The big last-time: if I left this life arc this year, what would I most regret? |

## On the relationship to the work

The Stoic operating system is not separate from leadership — it *is* leadership for the long arc. Leaders without it burn out, become reactive, conflate identity with role, fail to make hard calls, lose perspective in the moments that matter most.

The 30 minutes per week that the operating system requires are not added on top of the work; they protect the quality of the work. The leader with perspective makes better decisions, models healthier behavior, builds more durable relationships, and lasts longer in the role.

## Reading

- Irvine, William B. *A Guide to the Good Life* — the canonical modern adaptation; this entire reference draws from it
- Epictetus, *The Enchiridion* — short, ancient, foundational; the original dichotomy
- Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* — the Roman emperor's personal journal; surprisingly modern
- Seneca, *Letters from a Stoic* (especially the letters on death, fortune, friendship)
- Modern: Pigliucci, *How to Be a Stoic*; Holiday, *The Daily Stoic* (daily reading format)
- Adjacent: Frankl, *Man's Search for Meaning* (different tradition; same emphasis on meaning under hardship)
