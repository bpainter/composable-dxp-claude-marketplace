# The dichotomy (and trichotomy) of control in leadership

The most operationally useful Stoic technique. The leadership version of "wisdom is knowing what you can change and what you cannot."

## The classical dichotomy (Epictetus)

> "Some things are up to us and others are not."

In your control:
- Your judgments
- Your intentions
- Your responses to events
- The effort you bring

Not in your control:
- Other people's actions and reactions
- Outcomes that depend on others
- Past events
- The world's response to you

## Irvine's trichotomy — the leadership-relevant addition

Irvine's contribution to the practical Stoic frame: most of what we care about lives in a *partly* controllable middle. The classic dichotomy is binary, but real leadership outcomes are co-determined.

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Entirely yours | Your effort, judgment, response, integrity, attention |
| Entirely not yours | Market conditions, what your competitors do, the past, weather, other people's character |
| Partly yours | Your team's outcomes, your deals' close rates, your kid's choices, your reputation, your health |

For partly-controllable things, distinguish:

- **External goal** (the outcome you want): partly controllable
- **Internal goal** (what *you* contribute toward it): entirely controllable

## Working examples for senior leadership

### A $2M deal you're trying to close

External goal: close the deal. Partly controllable.
Internal goal: bring my best preparation, deepest listening, clearest articulation of value, attentive follow-through.

If you focus on the external: every loss feels like personal failure. You might over-promise to win. You might burn out on dealsetters.

If you focus on the internal: you can lose a deal you played perfectly and know you did your work. The information ("this client wasn't ready" or "we weren't right for them") is data, not verdict.

### A direct report's promotion case

External goal: they get promoted. Partly controllable.
Internal goal: I prepared them honestly, I advocated effectively in calibration, I gave them the development they needed, I told them the truth about gaps.

If they get promoted: great. If they don't: the question is whether *you* did your part. If yes, the calibration outcome is data about the room, not about your work. If no, the work is on you for next cycle.

### Your team's quarterly OKRs

External goal: hit the OKRs. Partly controllable.
Internal goal: I set the right OKRs, I designed the operating cadence, I removed blockers, I had honest mid-quarter conversations.

If you hit them: good — and validate that the OKRs were well-set (not too easy).
If you miss: separately diagnose (a) was the OKR right, (b) did I do my part, (c) did the team do their part, (d) what conditions changed.

### A peer's competitive move that threatens the practice

External: their move and its impact. Almost entirely uncontrollable.
Internal: how you respond — calmly, strategically, without taking it personally. What you control: your response, not their move.

The senior leader who reacts to every competitive move with retaliation is being run by what they don't control. The senior leader who absorbs the move, takes the view from above, and chooses the response that serves the long-arc strategy is operating from inside the trichotomy.

## Where leaders waste energy

Common patterns of energy spent on the uncontrollable:

- **Replaying past mistakes.** The past is not yours to change. The lesson is yours; the rumination is wasted.
- **Resenting other people's character.** They are who they are. You can choose to engage or not, but rage at their nature changes nothing.
- **Anxiety about market conditions.** You don't control the market. You control your strategy in response to it.
- **Frustration at the org you can't fix today.** You may be able to influence over years. The current state is data, not personal grievance.
- **Worrying about reputation in the abstract.** Reputation is built by what you do; worrying about it doesn't build it.
- **Outcome-fixation on things others determine.** A direct's career, a client's renewal, a peer's call. Influence yes; control no.

Each of these is energy that could go to the controllable instead.

## The end-of-day question

A useful application: every evening, briefly review.

> "Where today did I expend energy on something that wasn't mine to control?"

Common answers:
- The way [person] reacted to my email
- The decision [other team] made
- A market headline
- A past conversation I'm replaying
- The calibration room dynamics I can't change

For each, the next-day discipline: redirect that energy to the controllable next time. What's *my* response, *my* effort, *my* judgment?

## When the dichotomy gets misused

This is the most-misused Stoic technique. Common misuses:

### As an excuse for inaction

> "Outcomes are partly outside my control, so I won't push hard."

Wrong. The trichotomy says: focus on the *internal goal* — your effort, your judgment. The internal goal *demands* you push. The external outcome is what you accept after you've done your part.

### As detachment from responsibility

> "I did my part; the team failed; not my problem."

Wrong. As a leader, the team's outcome is your accountability even when their execution is not your direct control. The dichotomy is internal psychological discipline, not external excuse-making. You influence the team through hiring, conditions, coaching, decisions — all controllable internal goals.

### As emotional suppression

> "I shouldn't feel anxious / angry / sad about this — Stoicism."

Wrong. The Stoic frame doesn't suppress emotion; it shapes the response to emotion. Feeling anxiety about a deal is normal. Letting the anxiety drive overcommitment, sleeplessness, or reactive decision-making is the cost. The dichotomy work doesn't remove the anxiety; it gives you a relationship with it.

### As detached from people

> "I won't let myself care about [person] because they're outside my control."

Wrong. Caring about people is not attachment to outcomes. You can love your team and still hold the dichotomy: you control how you treat them, not whether they choose to stay.

## Daily practice

Three moments to apply it:

**Morning (60 seconds).** What am I most anxious about today? Sort: what part is mine, what part isn't? My internal goal is X.

**In-meeting reactivity (10 seconds).** When you feel the spike of frustration / defensiveness / urgency: pause. Quick question — is this about something I control, or am I burning energy on what I don't?

**End-of-day (5 minutes).** Where did I waste energy on the uncontrollable today? Where did I focus well on the controllable? What's the pattern?

Over weeks, you notice your own patterns. The specific person, the specific kind of decision, the specific kind of news that pulls you out of the trichotomy. Naming the pattern is half the work; the other half is the redirection.

## For Bermon's specific context

Common partly-controllable middle for a Slalom Senior Director:
- Pursuit close rates
- Senior practitioner retention
- Practice growth metrics
- Promotion / non-promotion of direct reports
- Engagement health on accounts you don't lead day-to-day
- Cross-capability deal flow
- Speaking / external presence ROI

For each, the discipline: external outcome is partly yours; the internal goal is entirely yours. Where does your energy actually go? If you find yourself ruminating on outcomes, the redirection is to internal goals — what would *better effort* on your part look like?
