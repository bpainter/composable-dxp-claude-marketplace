# Negotiation — Voss's tactics

Chris Voss's FBI-hostage-negotiator framework, translated to non-life-or-death negotiations. The book argues that the standard "rational, win-win" school (Harvard Negotiation Project, Fisher & Ury's *Getting to Yes*) gets human psychology wrong — and that emotional intelligence, tactical empathy, and a specific set of conversational moves outperform position-based bargaining.

For senior leaders especially, the techniques apply broadly: client conversations, cross-capability negotiations, internal politics, hard 1:1s, hiring closes, partnership negotiations, even some 1:1 conversations with directs.

## The core stance — tactical empathy

Voss's foundation: the negotiation is about *understanding the other side's perspective so deeply that they feel heard*. Once they feel heard, defenses drop and the actual work becomes possible.

Tactical empathy is not agreement. It's not being "nice." It's the deliberate work of articulating what the other person feels and thinks accurately enough that they confirm "yes, that's right."

This is the unlock. Most negotiations stall because each side is defending; the moment one side feels truly heard, the dynamic changes.

## The seven techniques

### 1. Mirroring

**Repeat the last 1–3 words the other person said, with curious upward inflection.**

> Them: "We can't go above $300K on this scope."
> You: "Above $300K?"

Mirroring does several things:
- Makes the other person feel heard
- Buys you time to think
- Often prompts them to elaborate (the "additional information dump" — they fill the silence by saying more)
- Doesn't commit you to anything

Used 2–3 times per negotiation. Don't overdo (becomes parrot).

### 2. Labeling

**Name the emotion you observe in the other side, prefaced by "It seems like / sounds like / looks like…"**

> "It sounds like the budget pressure is the real concern here."
> "It seems like you're worried we won't deliver on time."
> "Looks like the decision-makers above you have already taken a position."

Labeling does several things:
- Makes the other person feel understood (the deepest emotional drive)
- Surfaces what they couldn't say directly
- Defuses negative emotions (named emotions lose intensity)
- Shifts the conversation to what's actually driving it

Two specific labels Voss highlights:
- The **accusation audit** — at the start of a hard conversation, name the worst things the other side might be feeling about you. "You're probably thinking we're going to lowball you on this." Defangs the unspoken accusations.
- **Negative labels** are more useful than positive ones for surfacing real concerns. "It sounds like this isn't going to work for you" is more diagnostic than "It sounds like you're excited about this."

### 3. "That's right" (the two transformative words)

The breakthrough moment in any negotiation. When you've labeled accurately enough that the other side says "that's right" — you've reached the underlying truth. From there, the negotiation can move.

Distinct from "you're right." "You're right" is dismissive — it's how people end an uncomfortable conversation. "That's right" is genuine — they're confirming you understand them.

The aim of mirroring + labeling is to get to "that's right." You'll feel the shift when it happens.

### 4. Master "no" instead of chasing "yes"

Counter-intuitive: "no" is more useful than "yes" early in a negotiation.

Three reasons:
- "No" makes the other side feel safe (they're in control)
- "No" is the start of the negotiation, not the end
- "Yes" can be a commitment they don't actually mean (the polite yes that doesn't deliver)

Voss recommends asking questions that invite "no" rather than questions that pressure "yes":

> Wrong: "Do you have time to talk now?" (pressures yes; might land badly)
> Right: "Is now a bad time to talk?" (invites no; lower-pressure; if "no it's fine," you're in)

> Wrong: "Have you given up on this project?" (forces a fake "no")
> Right: "Have you abandoned this project?" (invites real "no, we haven't, but…")

### 5. Calibrated questions

Open-ended questions that begin with "what" or "how" — designed to make the other side solve the problem with you rather than push back against you.

Voss's go-to questions:
- "How am I supposed to do that?"
- "What's the biggest challenge you're facing?"
- "How does that affect things?"
- "How do we know we're on the right track?"
- "What's the goal here?"
- "What about this is important to you?"

These do several things:
- Make the other side problem-solve (engages them)
- Buy time
- Surface constraints you didn't know about
- Don't commit you to anything
- Avoid "yes/no" questions which lock down

The "how am I supposed to do that?" move is particularly powerful when someone is making demands. It doesn't say "no"; it shifts the work of solving the demand to them.

### 6. The illusion of control

Use calibrated questions to give the other side the *feeling* of control while you actually shape the outcome. They're answering your questions; they feel agency; meanwhile you're directing the conversation.

This is the deeper move behind the calibrated-questions technique. Voss is explicit: people who feel they're in control are more cooperative; people who feel cornered are not.

### 7. The black swan

The piece of information that, if discovered, would change the entire negotiation. The asymmetric leverage. Often hidden — the other side has it but doesn't think it's relevant; you have it but haven't surfaced it.

Voss's claim: every negotiation has 1–3 black swans. Finding them changes everything. Look for them by:
- Listening for what the other side mentions in passing
- Asking what's not being discussed
- Asking who else cares about this and what they want
- Asking about the alternative (their walk-away)
- Asking the "why" behind the "what" (real motivations)

## Application to senior leadership conversations

### Client commercial negotiations

Standard pattern (split-the-difference): "Our budget is $300K, can you do it for that?" → "Our scope requires $400K, can we meet at $350K?" → frustrated middle.

Voss pattern:
- Mirror: "$300K?"
- Label: "It sounds like the budget got tighter than you'd hoped."
- Calibrated: "How did the budget land at $300K?"
- Surface the constraint, then problem-solve together: "Given that constraint, what's the most important outcome we can deliver?"

Often the conversation reveals: the $300K is for this fiscal year; there's another budget next quarter; the scope can be staged; the value perception was off. The real negotiation surfaces only after the labels and calibrated questions.

### Hard 1:1 conversations

When a direct comes to a 1:1 with frustration or a hard ask:
- Mirror to slow the conversation
- Label the emotion ("it sounds like you're frustrated with [X]")
- Calibrated question to surface the underlying concern
- "That's right" as the marker that you understand
- Then problem-solve

### Cross-capability negotiations

When negotiating scope, resources, or accountability with a peer leader:
- Accusation audit at the start ("you're probably thinking we're going to push the cost onto your team")
- Calibrated questions about their constraints
- Find the black swan (their real concern is often not the stated one — political pressure from above, prior bad experience with similar deals, etc.)

### Internal political dynamics

Voss's frame on bureaucracy and politics: "no" early is fine; "yes" early is suspicious. Get to the real underlying concerns before pushing for commitment.

### Hiring closes

When a candidate has a competing offer or is hesitating:
- Mirror what they say about the alternative
- Label the underlying concern ("it sounds like the timing isn't ideal")
- Calibrated questions ("what would have to be true for this to be the obvious choice?")
- Don't compete on comp alone — find the black swan (often it's not the comp; it's a relationship, a fear of leaving the team, a family consideration)

## Boundaries — when not to use these

Voss's tactics are powerful and can be misused:

- **In genuine collaborative work** with people who trust you, mirroring and labeling can feel manipulative. Use lightly.
- **In your closest relationships** (spouse, close family), use sparingly — these techniques work but can feel calculated, and trust is fragile.
- **When you're the one being negotiated against by someone using these tactics** — recognize the pattern; don't take the calibrated question as a real question; respond honestly anyway, because honesty often beats counter-tactics.

The deeper aim is genuine understanding, not manipulation. The techniques work because they're rooted in tactical empathy — actually understanding the other side. Used cynically, they feel hollow and sophisticated counterparts will sense it.

## For neurodivergent leaders specifically

Voss's tactics are particularly useful for ADHD / autistic leaders because:
- They give explicit scripts where the neurotypical "read the room" is harder
- Calibrated questions buy time (useful for processing differences)
- Labeling externalizes emotion-reading work that may be more deliberate
- The structure provides a fallback when the social ambiguity is overwhelming

Specific moves:
- The accusation audit is a script you can rehearse
- Mirroring requires no improv (just repeat what they said)
- "How am I supposed to do that?" is a magic phrase for buying processing time
- "That's right" gives a clear marker for whether your read is accurate

## Practice

Voss recommends practicing in low-stakes conversations:
- Coffee orders ("How am I supposed to fit a vanilla shot in there?")
- Casual disagreements
- Family negotiations about restaurant choice
- Internal team conversations with low stakes

The reps build the muscle. By the time you're in a high-stakes negotiation, the techniques are automatic.
