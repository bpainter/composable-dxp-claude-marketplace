# Conversation mechanics — what's actually happening in talk (Enfield)

N. J. Enfield's research-based account of how conversations actually work — turn-taking, repair, prosody, the gaps between turns, the universal mechanics of human talk. The book isn't aimed at leadership, but the insights are unusually load-bearing for senior leaders, and especially useful for neurodivergent leaders for whom the implicit conversation rules are more visible (and harder).

This reference distills the most useful mechanics for leadership conversations.

## Why this matters

Most leadership advice operates on what to *say*. Enfield operates one level deeper: how the *conversational substrate* works — the timing, the gaps, the repairs, the cues — that determines whether what you say lands.

Senior leaders who don't understand the substrate produce well-intentioned messages that don't land, generate friction without seeing why, and miss social signals that could have changed outcomes.

Neurodivergent leaders specifically often *intellectually* understand the substrate but find it more deliberate effort to navigate. Explicit knowledge helps.

## The 200-millisecond gap

One of the book's central findings: across every language and culture studied, the average gap between speakers' turns in conversation is ~200ms. Less than a quarter of a second.

This is shockingly fast. It implies:
- Listeners are predicting the end of your turn while you're still speaking
- They begin formulating their response before you finish
- The gap is so short that a longer pause is a *signal* — it means something

For leaders:
- Long pauses feel uncomfortable to others (they're waiting; their planned response is ready)
- A deliberate pause is a tool — it draws attention, signals importance, creates space
- The "I need to think before answering" approach is structurally disruptive to the conversational flow — manage it deliberately

## Turn construction units (TCUs)

Listeners predict turn endings via prosodic and grammatical signals. A turn "completes" at certain points; the listener jumps in then.

Implication for leaders: the way you end your sentences matters. Trailing off invites interruption. Ending with downward intonation signals "your turn." Ending with upward intonation signals "I'm continuing" (or a question).

If you frequently get interrupted, it's often because you're sending "your turn" signals you don't intend.

If you have trouble interrupting (and need to in some contexts — like decision meetings, dominant-talker situations), look for the natural completion points and jump in then.

## "Huh?" — the universal repair

Enfield's research finds "huh?" (or its phonetic equivalent) appears in every language as the primary signal for "I didn't understand — please repeat or clarify."

The repair sequence is universal:
1. Speaker says something
2. Listener doesn't understand
3. Listener signals (huh? what? sorry?)
4. Speaker repairs (clarifies, repeats, rephrases)
5. Conversation resumes

Implication for leaders:
- The repair is normal, not a failure. Don't avoid the moments where you need to ask for clarification.
- A team that *doesn't* repair (everyone nods even when they don't understand) is suppressing signal — usually a psychological-safety issue.
- Modeling repair openly ("wait, I lost the thread there — can you go back to [point]?") gives permission for others to do the same.

For neurodivergent leaders: the repair is your friend. Use it explicitly. "I'm processing what you said — can you say that again?" is a perfectly normal conversational move that some leaders treat as weakness.

## Prosody — pitch, pace, volume, emphasis

Beyond words, conversation carries information in prosody:
- Pitch (high, low, rising, falling)
- Pace (fast, slow, with pauses)
- Volume (loud, soft, varied)
- Emphasis (stress on specific words)

Same words in different prosody mean different things:
- "Sure, let's do that" (flat, low energy) vs. "Sure, let's do that!" (rising, energetic) — same words, opposite signal
- "We can ship by Friday." (downward, definite) vs. "We can ship by Friday?" (rising, doubtful)

Implication for leaders:
- Tone-deaf delivery undermines the words. The team hears the prosody more than the content.
- Listening to your own recordings (meeting recordings, voice memos) is uncomfortable but instructive
- For high-stakes communications, deliberate prosody choices matter (slow down for emphasis; vary pace to hold attention; downward intonation for finality)

For neurodivergent leaders: prosody can be more deliberate work. The autistic-leader stereotype of "monotone" is real for some; the ADHD-leader stereotype of "racing" is real for others. Recording yourself and adjusting deliberately is a useful practice.

## Eye gaze and turn allocation

In group conversation, the speaker's gaze allocates the next turn:
- Looking at someone at the end of your turn = "you're up next"
- Looking away = "anyone can go"
- Holding gaze through your own turn = "I'm not done"

Implication for leaders:
- In meetings, your gaze is allocating turns whether you mean to or not
- If you keep getting the same person speaking after you, check your gaze pattern
- To draw out a quiet person: end your turn with gaze on them

For video meetings: gaze is more ambiguous (camera placement varies). Compensating with explicit verbal turn allocation ("[Name], I'd love your read on this") is more important than in person.

## The "trouble" moments

Conversations have characteristic trouble points where they break down:
- **Self-repair** (you start a sentence, restart it differently — common, mostly invisible)
- **Other-repair** (someone else corrects you — feels social, can be a friction point)
- **Interruption** (someone takes the floor before you've signaled completion)
- **Overlap** (two people speak at once)
- **Long silence** (silence beyond the 200ms gap — anomalous, signal-bearing)

Each is normal in moderation. Each is a signal when frequent.

A meeting with constant interruption and overlap = high competition, low listening.
A meeting with long silences after the leader speaks = no one feels safe responding.
A meeting where the leader self-repairs constantly = nervousness or unclear thinking.

## "Mhm" and the back-channel

The small noises listeners make ("mhm," "yeah," "right," "uh-huh") aren't filler — they're explicit signals to the speaker that the listener is engaged and following. Without them, speakers feel they're talking to a void.

Implication for leaders:
- In 1:1s and small meetings, your back-channel signals matter. Quiet listening can feel disengaged.
- On video calls, back-channel cues are reduced (mics often muted) — compensate with visible nods, brief unmuted "mhm"s, or chat acknowledgments.
- Cultural variation matters — some cultures back-channel more, some less. Mismatch can cause misreads.

## The "next-turn proof procedure"

Conversation analysts use this principle: how the next speaker responds *demonstrates* what they understood from the previous turn. The response is the proof of comprehension.

Implication for leaders:
- The team's response to your direction is the evidence of how clearly you communicated
- If the response is off-target, the message wasn't clear (regardless of what you intended)
- "Tell me what you understood from that" surfaces the gap before action proceeds

This connects to the Nightingales' "leadership is not about your good intentions" — impact is what's communicated; intent is invisible.

## Interruption — gendered and cultural

Enfield notes that interruption patterns vary by gender and culture, and within mixed groups, the asymmetries are real:
- Men interrupt women more than the reverse in mixed groups (well-documented finding)
- Some cultures use overlap as engagement (high-overlap = high-interest); others use it as rudeness
- Rank asymmetries play in (junior people interrupted more than senior)

For leaders running meetings:
- Watch the interruption pattern. Who gets interrupted, by whom, how often?
- Intervene when patterns emerge ("hold on, [interrupted person] was finishing — go ahead")
- Don't model the pattern yourself — when you interrupt, the team learns it's okay

## Silence — what it means

Pauses beyond the 200ms gap carry information:
- ~500ms: thinking, calibrating
- 1–2s: significant — signals "this is hard" or "I'm choosing my words"
- >3s: high-tension — usually means refusal, hard decision, emotional moment, or breakdown of the conversation

Implication for leaders:
- Don't rescue every silence. The 1–2s pause is often where the deeper answer comes.
- Long silence after a hard question is information — the person is wrestling with something
- Performative quick-answers ("yes, sure, sounds good") can be cover for actual disagreement

For coaching specifically (see `coaching-habit-7-questions.md` in `leadership-people-leader`): the discipline of holding silence is the discipline of letting the other person think. Most managers can't tolerate >2s; coaches can hold 10–20.

## Cross-cultural conversation

Enfield notes that the universal substrate (200ms gap, repair sequences, turn-taking) is consistent across cultures, but the *style* layered on top varies:
- Some cultures use longer gaps as default (Scandinavian languages: ~500ms)
- Some cultures use overlap as engagement (Mediterranean, some Asian languages)
- Some cultures use indirect speech (suggestion-as-request); others direct
- Eye contact norms vary widely

For senior leaders working across geographies (Slalom is a global firm; Composable DXP engagements span continents):
- Don't assume the conversational style is the substrate
- Adapt your style to the room when possible
- Be patient with mismatches; many friction moments are style, not content
- Ask about preferences if uncertain ("how do you prefer pushback in meetings?")

## For neurodivergent leaders specifically

The conversational substrate is often more deliberate work for ADHD / autistic leaders. Specific moves:

- **Practice the 200ms timing.** If you're systematically too fast (interrupting) or too slow (gaps producing discomfort), become aware. Recording yourself helps.
- **Use explicit verbal turn allocation.** "[Name], I'd love your view next." Removes the gaze ambiguity.
- **Model repair openly.** "Wait, I missed the connection there — can you take me back?" Permission for others to do the same.
- **Pre-script high-stakes opening lines.** The first 10 seconds of a high-stakes meeting are when you're most likely to fumble. Pre-rehearse.
- **Use the back-channel deliberately.** "Mhm" / "right" / brief verbal acknowledgments signal engagement, especially on video.
- **Explicitly close meetings.** "We're at time — let me close. Decisions: [X]. Owners: [Y]. Next steps: [Z]." The close ritual is partly conversational substrate; making it explicit is leadership clarity.

## The "compete to talk" failure

Some meetings devolve into competing to take the floor. The leader's job is to allocate turns, not to compete for them.

Specific moves to avoid the competition:
- "Round the room — 90 seconds each on [topic]."
- "Pause — let me hear from [quiet person] on this."
- "Hold that — we need to give [other person] space to finish."

These are conversational-substrate moves. Without them, the loudest dominate.
