# Decision-Making and Communication Frameworks for Neurodivergent Leaders

## Decision-Making Under Uncertainty (Kahneman Framework)

### Understanding Bias in Decision-Making

**Neurodivergent leaders often**:
- Hyperfocus on details (missing the big picture)
- Impulsively decide and regret later (ADHD)
- Overthink edge cases (autism)
- Follow pattern recognition that may not apply

### Structured Decision Framework

**Step 1: Define the Decision Clearly**
- What exactly are we deciding?
- What's at stake? (Financial, reputational, team impact, strategic?)
- Who needs to be involved in the decision?
- What's the timeline? (Decision needed today? Next week? Can we gather more info?)

**Step 2: Gather Information (with Limits)**
- What do we already know?
- What do we need to know to decide well?
- Who has relevant expertise or perspective?
- When is additional information diminishing returns? (Stop researching; decide)

**Step 3: Identify Real Options**
- What are we actually choosing between? (Not infinite; narrow to 2-4 real options)
- What are the tradeoffs of each?
- Which option aligns best with our values and strategy?

**Step 4: Make the Call**
- Decide consciously; name the decision and why
- Acknowledge what you're uncertain about
- Communicate decision clearly

**Step 5: Commit and Execute**
- No second-guessing (until evidence suggests you were genuinely wrong)
- Monitor outcomes
- Adjust if needed, but don't waffle

### Common Biases That Derail Leaders

**Confirmation bias**: Seeking info that confirms your existing view; ignoring contradictions
- Antidote: Actively ask "What could prove me wrong?"

**Availability bias**: Overweighting recent or memorable examples
- Example: One failed hire makes you overly cautious on hiring
- Antidote: Look at base rates and historical data, not just memorable cases

**Analysis paralysis**: Needing perfect information before deciding
- ADHD leader: "I need to understand every scenario"
- Autism leader: "What if X happens? Or Y? Or Z?"
- Antidote: Good-enough information + timely decision > perfect information + late decision

**Sunk cost fallacy**: Continuing a project because you've already invested, not because it's the right call
- Antidote: Evaluate based on future value, not past investment

**Anchoring**: Getting stuck on the first number/idea presented
- Antidote: Generate multiple options independently before comparing

## Communication Frameworks

### Core Principles for Neurodivergent Leaders

**1. Be Direct and Clear**
- Autism + ADHD strength: You naturally speak directly, name issues, avoid passive-aggressive subtext
- Neurotypical colleagues may interpret this as harsh
- **Strategy**: Lead with context (why this matters) + directness (what you observe) + collaborative framing (we're solving this together, not blaming)

**Example**:
- Harsh: "That report was poorly done."
- Direct + collaborative: "I noticed the report has some gaps in analysis. Let's talk about what happened—I want to help you deliver stronger work."

**2. Ask Good Questions**
- Neurodivergent leaders often see systems and patterns others miss
- Use this by asking: "What am I missing here?" "What's the context I don't have?" "Help me understand your thinking."
- Powerful questions build trust and understanding

**3. Listen Without Solving**
- Neurodivergent tendency: Jump to solution-mode
- People often need to be heard first; solutions second
- Practice: Listen, paraphrase what you heard, ask clarifying questions. Only then offer solutions.

**4. Manage Nonverbal Communication**
- Neurodivergent challenge: Reading facial expressions, tone, body language
- Strategy: Ask explicitly. "I'm reading your body language as skeptical. Am I reading that right?" "How are you feeling about this?"
- Clarifying beats assuming incorrectly

### Communication Script Framework for Difficult Conversations

**Structure**: Context → Observation → Impact → Collaboration → Clarity

**Step 1: Context** (why are we talking about this?)
- "I want to talk about X because it affects our work/team/outcome in Y way"

**Step 2: Observation** (what did you observe, factually?)
- "I noticed that..."
- "In the last three meetings..."
- "The report showed..."
- (No interpretation yet; stick to facts)

**Step 3: Impact** (what impact did that have?)
- "That resulted in..."
- "It made it harder to..."
- "It created confusion because..."

**Step 4: Collaboration** (how do we solve this?)
- "I want to understand your perspective"
- "What was happening on your end?"
- "How do you see this?"

**Step 5: Clarity** (what's the path forward?)
- "Here's what I need from you going forward..."
- "Can you help me understand how we prevent this next time?"
- "Let's agree on..."

### Example: Addressing Missed Deadline with Direct Report

**Poor approach**: "You missed the deadline again. You're unreliable."

**Better approach (using framework)**:

1. **Context**: "I want to talk about the project deadline because it affects the client timeline."

2. **Observation**: "The deliverable was due Friday; it came in Monday morning."

3. **Impact**: "That put us in a tight spot with the client and shortened our review time."

4. **Collaboration**: "Help me understand what happened. What was on your plate?"

5. **Clarity**: "Going forward, I need updates 48 hours before a deadline if you see a risk. And I'd like to understand what support you need to hit deadlines consistently. What would help?"

## Influence and Persuasion (Chris Voss, Robert Cialdini Framework)

### Core Principles of Influence

**1. Reciprocity**: People feel obligated to return favors
- Strategy: Help others first; offer value without immediate ask
- Example: Share useful information, make introductions, lend your expertise

**2. Scarcity**: People value things that are rare or limited
- Strategy: Emphasize unique value or opportunity cost
- Example: "This project needs your expertise specifically. No one else has your combination of skills."

**3. Authority**: People respect expertise and credibility
- Strategy: Build your credibility; cite research or experts when appropriate
- Neurodivergent advantage: Systems thinking and pattern recognition build credibility
- Example: "Based on our data and what I've seen in similar situations, here's what I recommend..."

**4. Consistency**: People want to be consistent with prior commitments
- Strategy: Get small commitments first; build to larger ones
- Example: "Do you agree the goal is X?" (yes) "So we need Y to achieve that?" (yes) "Then let's do Z."

**5. Liking**: People are influenced by those they like
- Strategy: Find genuine common ground; show interest in others
- Neurodivergent challenge: Small talk doesn't come naturally
- Workaround: Ask about something you genuinely find interesting; listen; find authentic connection

**6. Social proof**: People follow what others are doing
- Strategy: Show examples of success; highlight what similar organizations have done
- Example: "Three other teams have adopted this approach. Here's what they've achieved."

### Persuasion Script: Getting Buy-In

**Goal**: Get your CEO/board to support your initiative

**Structure**: Why this matters → What you recommend → Why it works → What success looks like → What you need

1. **Why this matters**: Connect to organizational values/goals
   - "This addresses the market opportunity we identified in Q2"

2. **What you recommend**: Clear recommendation
   - "I recommend we invest in X initiative"

3. **Why it works**: Evidence and reasoning
   - "Here's what the data shows... Here's what similar companies have seen... Here's why I think we're positioned to succeed..."

4. **What success looks like**: Clear metrics
   - "Success means we achieve X metric by Y date"

5. **What you need**: Specific ask
   - "To make this happen, I need budget, these team members, and your executive cover"

## Managing Difficult People and Dynamics

### When Someone Challenges You

**Neurodivergent response**: Defensive, argumentative, or shutting down

**Better approach**:
1. Pause (don't respond immediately)
2. Assume positive intent: "I think you're raising a valid concern"
3. Ask clarifying question: "Help me understand your perspective"
4. Look for valid points: "You're right that we need to consider cost"
5. Find middle ground: "I agree with part of what you're saying. Here's where I see it differently..."

### Navigating Conflicting Priorities

**Your challenge**: Managing multiple stakeholders with different needs

**Framework**:
1. Make stakeholder priorities explicit: List what each stakeholder needs
2. Identify where they align (leverage this)
3. Name where they conflict (don't pretend they don't)
4. Propose integrated solution: "Here's how we can serve both X and Y"
5. If impossible: Make a conscious choice about which priority wins (and why)

**Example**:
- Engineering wants stability (no changes)
- Product wants innovation (constant new features)
- Your call: "We'll do two-week release cycles: one week for stability, one week for innovation"

## Asking for What You Need

### Your ADHD/Autism Strengths in Negotiations

- **Direct communication**: You ask for what you need without excessive softening
- **Pattern recognition**: You see what works and what doesn't
- **Clarity**: You communicate in straightforward terms
- **Authenticity**: People trust you're being honest

### Negotiation Script

1. **State your ask clearly**:
   - Not: "I was wondering if maybe we could think about budget for tools..."
   - Yes: "I need a $50K budget for tools. Here's why."

2. **Provide reasoning**:
   - "We'll save 20 hours per week, which translates to $X in productivity"
   - "This improves team efficiency"
   - "It's industry standard for teams of this size"

3. **Show you've thought about constraints**:
   - "I know budget is tight. I've looked at three options. Here's the most cost-effective."
   - "If budget is the blocker, what would make this possible?"

4. **Be willing to negotiate**:
   - "If we can't do $50K, could we phase it: $25K now, $25K next quarter?"
   - "What's the constraint we're working with?"

5. **Get clarity**:
   - "So we're agreed on the $30K budget for Q2?"
   - "I want to make sure we're on the same page."

## Managing Your Own Reactivity (Pause, Evaluate, Respond)

### The Neurodivergent Reaction Loop

**ADHD**: Impulse → Action → Regret
**Autism**: Overstimulation → Shutdown or Explosion → Recovery

**Better loop**: Stimulus → Pause → Evaluate → Respond

### Three-Breath Technique

When you feel triggered or want to react impulsively:

1. **Breath 1**: Notice you're activated ("I'm feeling defensive/angry/impulsive")
2. **Breath 2**: Pause. Don't speak or act yet.
3. **Breath 3**: Evaluate options. What's the best response? (Not the reactive one)

**Then respond** with intention, not impulse.

### Script for Buying Time in Real-Time

When you need to pause in the moment:

- "That's a good point. Let me think about that and get back to you."
- "I want to give you a thoughtful answer, not an off-the-cuff one. Let's circle back tomorrow."
- "I'm processing this. Can I take 24 hours and come back with my thoughts?"

This isn't weakness; it's wisdom. Rushed decisions are often wrong decisions.
