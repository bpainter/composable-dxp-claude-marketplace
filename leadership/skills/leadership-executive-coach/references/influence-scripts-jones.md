# Influence scripts — Phil Jones's "magic words"

Phil Jones's slim, direct book catalogs 23 phrasings that consistently move conversations. The premise: in any influence situation, the *exact* phrasing matters disproportionately. The right opening, the right framing, the right pivot — these change outcomes.

Useful for senior leaders in conversations where you need to land a specific message, shift someone's frame, or move past resistance. Particularly useful for neurodivergent leaders who benefit from explicit scripts where neurotypical "feel for the moment" is harder.

This reference catalogs the most useful phrases and their specific applications.

## The 23 phrases (the most useful for senior leadership)

### "I'm not sure if it's for you, but…"

**The phrase.** Used to introduce an idea or option without triggering defensiveness.

> "I'm not sure if it's for you, but a few of the senior architects have been asking about a more deliberate technical-leadership track. Want to talk about it?"

**Why it works.** Removes the pressure. The recipient doesn't have to defend against being told what to do; they're invited to consider.

**Use cases.**
- Introducing a coaching topic in a 1:1
- Floating a project / scope option to a direct
- Suggesting an external opportunity to a peer
- Bringing up a sensitive topic without a hard pivot

### "Open-minded?"

**The phrase.** Used to soften a direct ask or recommendation.

> "Open-minded? I've been thinking the engagement structure should be different on this one. Walk me through your view first."

**Why it works.** People say yes to "open-minded" easily. Once they've said yes, they've committed (small but real) to actually considering rather than reflexively defending.

**Use cases.**
- Before pitching a new approach to your boss or peer
- Opening a hard 1:1 conversation
- Pivoting from one topic to a harder one in a meeting

### "What do you know?"

**The phrase.** Used early in a conversation to surface what the other side already understands.

> "Before I dive in — what do you know about [topic]?"

**Why it works.** Calibrates your message to their actual baseline. Avoids over- or under-explaining. Makes them feel respected.

**Use cases.**
- Starting a briefing or readout
- Opening a complex conversation with someone you don't know well
- Beginning a teach-back or coaching moment

### "How would you feel if…"

**The phrase.** Used to walk the other side into imagining a future state.

> "How would you feel if we shipped the architecture review by end of next month with [team] leading?"

**Why it works.** Their imagination does the persuasion work. Once they've pictured it, the resistance is partly already overcome.

**Use cases.**
- Selling internal change
- Introducing an offer or proposal
- Opening a vision conversation

### "Just imagine…"

**The phrase.** Same family as "how would you feel if" but more visual.

> "Just imagine the practice in 18 months: 4 senior architects, 3 reusable accelerators, $8M revenue. The path to it starts with [specific]."

**Why it works.** Concrete future-imagining is more persuasive than abstract argument.

**Use cases.**
- Strategy conversations
- Selling a vision to your team or leadership
- Recruiting senior candidates ("just imagine running the practice's flagship engagement next year…")

### "When would be a good time?"

**The phrase.** Used to lock in a follow-up after introducing a topic.

> "...so I'd love to dig into that with you. When would be a good time?"

**Why it works.** Avoids "let me check my calendar" deferral. Concrete commitment.

**Use cases.**
- After introducing a topic that needs more time
- Closing the recruiting conversation
- Locking in coaching follow-up

### "I'm guessing you haven't got around to…"

**The phrase.** Used to follow up on something promised that hasn't happened, without making the other side defensive.

> "I'm guessing you haven't had a chance to draft the Q3 OKRs yet — what would help?"

**Why it works.** Frames the non-completion as expected (because life happens), removes the defensive accusation, and immediately pivots to "what would help?"

**Use cases.**
- Following up on commitments from peers or directs
- Moving past a stuck task without blame
- Re-engaging on a deferred conversation

### "Simple swaps"

**The phrase pattern.** Replace small wordings with versions that work better.

| Don't say | Say instead |
|---|---|
| "But…" | "And…" |
| "I'll try" | "I will" |
| "I think" | "I believe" / "I know" |
| "Hopefully" | (cut entirely) |
| "Sorry to bother you" | (cut; just ask) |
| "Does that make sense?" | "How does that land?" |

**Why it works.** Small word changes change perceived authority and clarity. Senior leaders compound these in their daily speech.

### "You have three options"

**The phrase.** Used to frame decisions for the other side.

> "You have three options. One: [X]. Two: [Y]. Three: [Z]. Which feels right?"

**Why it works.** Three is a magic number — it feels comprehensive without being overwhelming. The framing makes the decision feel manageable.

**Use cases.**
- Helping a direct decide between approaches
- Closing a candidate ("here's how this could work — three options")
- Resolving cross-team friction (three paths forward)

### "Two types of people"

**The phrase.** Used to gently bucket the listener and prompt self-identification.

> "There are two types of senior practitioners. The ones who treat asset development as someone else's problem, and the ones who see it as the path to durable practice value. Which kind are you?"

**Why it works.** People want to be the second type. The framing nudges without pushing.

**Use cases.**
- Coaching toward a behavior shift
- Recruiting toward a specific stance
- Selling a culture or value internally

### "I bet you're a bit like me…"

**The phrase.** Used to surface common ground before a request.

> "I bet you're a bit like me — you'd rather have one hard conversation now than 10 awkward ones over the next quarter."

**Why it works.** Establishes affinity. Lowers defensiveness. Pre-aligns on the underlying value.

**Use cases.**
- Approaching a hard conversation
- Coaching a peer through a frustrating situation
- Building rapport with a senior stakeholder

### "If… then…"

**The phrase.** Used to set conditional commitments.

> "If we land the [pursuit] this quarter, then I'll commit to a $50K asset investment next quarter. Sound fair?"

**Why it works.** Concrete reciprocity. Both sides know the deal.

**Use cases.**
- Negotiating with leadership for resources
- Setting expectations with a direct on a stretch role
- Closing a candidate

### "Don't worry"

**The phrase.** Used to defuse an unspoken fear.

> "Don't worry — we're not going to hold you accountable for the parts you didn't own."

**Why it works.** Names the fear (which the other side was carrying silently), which dissipates it.

**Use cases.**
- Reassuring a direct in a stressful conversation
- Calming a client in a difficult moment
- Opening a hard 1:1

### "Most people"

**The phrase.** Used to normalize an action or response.

> "Most people in your position would push back on this. I'm asking you to consider it anyway because [specific reason]."

**Why it works.** Social proof. Acknowledges the natural response while inviting the harder one.

**Use cases.**
- Asking for a stretch from a direct
- Coaching against a default reaction
- Recruiting someone to take an unconventional path

### "The good news…"

**The phrase.** Used to pivot from a hard message to a constructive one.

> "The good news is, this isn't going to require a re-org. Here's what we can do instead…"

**Why it works.** Sequencing the message. The bad news is heard; the good news re-frames toward action.

**Use cases.**
- Delivering hard news with a path forward
- Pivoting from a problem statement to a solution
- Handling a setback in a team meeting

### "What happens next…"

**The phrase.** Used to walk the listener through the immediate future.

> "What happens next is: I'll send the summary by tomorrow, you'll have it in your inbox by Friday morning, and we'll sync Monday at 10."

**Why it works.** Reduces ambiguity. Builds confidence. Makes commitments concrete.

**Use cases.**
- Closing any meeting
- Exiting a decision conversation
- Setting up a follow-up

### "What makes you say that?"

**The phrase.** Used to surface reasoning behind a strong claim.

> Them: "We can't possibly hit that timeline."
> You: "What makes you say that?"

**Why it works.** Invites the data. Doesn't argue with the conclusion; tests the basis.

**Use cases.**
- Engaging with pushback
- Coaching a direct to articulate their reasoning
- Surfacing hidden assumptions in a meeting

### "Before you make your mind up…"

**The phrase.** Used to slow down a decision that's heading the wrong way.

> "Before you make your mind up — let me walk through the [other consideration]."

**Why it works.** Earns 60 seconds without seeming to push. Often shifts the decision.

**Use cases.**
- A direct about to commit to the wrong call
- A client about to make a decision you'd advise against
- A peer leader making a hasty choice

### "If I can, will you?"

**The phrase.** Used in negotiation — conditional commitment for conditional commitment.

> "If I can get the budget approved by end of next month, will you commit to the start date?"

**Why it works.** Both sides commit conditionally. Fast forward motion.

**Use cases.**
- Closing a deal or hire
- Negotiating with leadership
- Aligning peers on a joint commitment

### "Enough"

**The phrase.** Used to signal the conversation has covered enough and it's time to act.

> "Enough on the diagnosis — let's pick a path."

**Why it works.** Permission-giving. Many conversations stall because no one closes them; "enough" closes them.

**Use cases.**
- Decision meetings that won't decide
- Coaching conversations that have circled back to the same point
- Strategy discussions that have surfaced the trade-offs

### "Just one more thing"

**The phrase.** Used at the end of a conversation to introduce a final, often most-important point.

> "Just one more thing — I want to make sure we close the [important thread] before we wrap."

**Why it works.** People expect the conversation to be over; they're relaxed; the final point lands more clearly than it would have in the middle.

**Use cases.**
- Closing 1:1s with a key feedback
- Ending a meeting with a critical commitment
- Recruiting closes ("just one more thing — the team you'd be working with…")

### "A favor"

**The phrase.** Used to make an ask without overpressuring.

> "A favor — could you take a look at this draft before Friday?"

**Why it works.** Frames it as a request, not an expectation. Creates the small social debt that motivates follow-through.

**Use cases.**
- Asking peers for input
- Requesting time from senior people
- Calling in informal favors

### "Just out of curiosity…"

**The phrase.** Used to surface information without seeming probing.

> "Just out of curiosity, what's the actual decision-maker timeline?"

**Why it works.** Sounds casual. Lowers defensiveness. Often surfaces information the other side wouldn't volunteer to a direct question.

**Use cases.**
- Recruiting (probing motivation, alternatives)
- Cross-team negotiations
- Client conversations about decision processes

## How to actually use these

Don't memorize all 23 and pepper a conversation. Pick 3–5 that fit your most common situations, internalize them, and use them when natural.

Common patterns for senior leaders:
- "Open-minded?" + "Just imagine…" for sales / vision conversations
- "What makes you say that?" + "How am I supposed to do that?" (Voss) for negotiations
- "I'm guessing you haven't got around to…" for follow-ups without blame
- "Just one more thing" for closing 1:1s with the real point
- "Don't worry" for opening hard conversations

## For neurodivergent leaders specifically

These scripts are particularly useful when:
- Improvisation is harder under stress
- "Reading the room" is more deliberate
- Pre-rehearsed phrases reduce cognitive load
- The right phrase at the right moment isn't available without the script

Build a small personal phrasebook. Practice in low-stakes settings. Deploy in high-stakes ones.

## When the scripts don't fit

These are tools, not commandments. The wrong script in the wrong context lands as manipulation. Sense the room; default to authenticity; reach for the script when it actually serves the conversation.

The deeper skill is the underlying observation: small words change outcomes. Whatever phrasing you settle into, deliberate phrasing beats accidental phrasing.
