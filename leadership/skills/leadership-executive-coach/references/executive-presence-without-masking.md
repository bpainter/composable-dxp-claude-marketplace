# Executive presence without masking

The neurodivergent leader's specific challenge: navigating a leadership culture that implicitly expects "executive presence" — composure, calibrated extroversion, smooth social fluency — while honoring how your brain actually works.

The standard advice on executive presence assumes a neurotypical baseline. For ADHD and autistic leaders, the standard advice often translates to "perform neurotypicality more convincingly" — which is masking, which is exhausting, which is unsustainable.

This reference is about the alternative: executive presence as authentic strength, not performed compliance.

## What "executive presence" actually means in practice

The phrase is vague. Different organizations mean different things. Common components:
- **Composure under pressure** — measured response, not reactive
- **Clarity** — articulate thinking, especially under uncertainty
- **Confidence** — appropriate certainty without arrogance
- **Gravitas** — being taken seriously by senior stakeholders
- **Adaptability** — reading the room and shifting style

These are real, useful capabilities. They become problematic when "executive presence" gets equated with a specific performance of:
- High-frequency small talk
- Effortless eye contact in groups
- Charismatic-extrovert energy
- Smooth social transitions
- "Reading the room" instinctively
- Standard professional warmth

For neurodivergent leaders, these expectations often map to masking. Mastering them costs energy that could be spent on the actual work.

## What masking is and what it costs

Masking is the deliberate suppression of natural neurodivergent expression to match neurotypical expectations:
- Forcing eye contact even when it's distressing
- Suppressing stimming or restless movement
- Performing energy levels you don't have
- Making small talk that doesn't engage you
- Hiding the way you actually think
- Pretending interest you don't feel
- Editing your communication style heavily for "polish"

The cost compounds:
- Cognitive load that competes with the actual work
- Burnout (masking is exhausting in ways the masker often doesn't fully feel until they crash)
- Inauthentic relationships (people relate to your mask, not you)
- Loss of self-knowledge (over years, you forget what's mask vs. what's you)
- Reduced creative capacity (the brain optimizing for performance can't also optimize for insight)
- Career trajectory built on a version of you that you can't sustain

Masking is sometimes necessary in moderation (specific high-stakes moments). Sustained masking as a leadership strategy is unsustainable.

## The alternative — authentic presence

The principles:

1. **Your neurodivergence is not a deficit to hide.** It comes with strengths (pattern recognition, systems thinking, deep focus, directness, clarity) that are leadership assets when you let them show.
2. **You can develop the components of executive presence that genuinely serve.** Composure, clarity, confidence, gravitas — these are real. Develop them in your way.
3. **You can decline to perform the components that don't.** Effortless small talk, charismatic-extrovert energy, smooth social transitions — these are styles, not requirements.
4. **You can disclose strategically.** Some neurodivergent leaders disclose openly; others selectively; others not at all. Each is valid; the choice depends on context.
5. **You can build a leadership style that compounds your strengths.** The senior leader you become should be a fuller version of who you are, not a smoothed-down compromise.

## Specific authentic-presence moves

### Composure under pressure (real, not performed)

The Stoic practice covered in `leadership-stoic-perspective` produces real composure — not the performed kind. Specifically:
- The reactivity gap (see `insults-anger-and-the-reactivity-gap.md`)
- Dichotomy of control (you can't control what just happened; you can choose your response)
- View from above (will this matter in a year?)

Real composure is visible to others. Performed composure feels brittle to others. Build the real version.

### Clarity (your specific kind)

Many neurodivergent leaders are exceptionally clear thinkers, especially on complex systems and patterns. The clarity often shows up best:
- In writing (where you can structure thinking deliberately)
- In small groups (where you don't have to compete for floor time)
- After processing time (versus extemporaneous)

Lean into these. The senior leader who writes a brilliant memo before the meeting, then in the meeting points to the memo and engages around its specifics, is using their brain effectively. Don't apologize for needing the memo.

### Confidence without performance

Real confidence comes from:
- Knowing your strengths (not just self-believing)
- Track record of judgment that holds up
- Doing the hard things and learning you can
- The advice-and-counsel network that calibrates you

Performed confidence (forced upright posture, alpha tone, never showing uncertainty) reads as defensive to perceptive people.

The alternative: appropriate certainty plus appropriate openness. "I'm certain about [X] because [reasoning]; I'm uncertain about [Y]; I'd want to test [Z] before committing." This is more credible than performed certainty.

### Gravitas via substance

The way to be taken seriously by senior stakeholders is to consistently produce substance: good calls, useful frameworks, specific contribution. Not performance.

The neurodivergent leader who shows up thoughtfully prepared, speaks specifically when speaking, and produces visibly excellent work over time accumulates gravitas regardless of charisma.

Gravitas-by-substance compounds; gravitas-by-charisma is brittle (one bad moment can destroy it).

### Adaptability without masking

Reading the room and adjusting style is real and valuable. The line is when "adjusting style" becomes "becoming a different person."

Useful adjustments:
- Different communication channels for different audiences (more visual for visual learners; more written for written processors; more verbal for verbal)
- Different pacing for different contexts (slower with senior clients; faster with technical peers)
- Different levels of detail for different stakeholders
- Different formats (formal slides for some forums; whiteboard sketches for others)

Not useful:
- Pretending interest in topics that bore you to "fit in"
- Suppressing your perspective because it doesn't match the group's
- Performing energy you don't have to "match the room"
- Hiding your processing style under social pressure

## Disclosure — the strategic choice

How much to disclose about your neurodivergence depends on context, role, organizational culture, and your own preference. Patterns:

**Full disclosure.** Some senior neurodivergent leaders disclose openly — in meetings, in 1:1s, in writing. Builds permission for others; can be exhausting; sometimes invites bias.

**Selective disclosure.** Disclose to your boss, a few close peers, your direct reports — but not broadly. Most common pattern at senior levels.

**No disclosure.** Some leaders never disclose externally. Operate via accommodations they design themselves; manage the mask carefully. Common in organizations or industries with documented bias.

**Functional disclosure without label.** "I process better in writing than verbally" or "I prefer agendas with pre-reads" without naming why. Often the most practical approach.

There's no universally right answer. Choose based on what serves the work and your sustainability.

For Bermon specifically: the project context indicates explicit ADHD / autistic-leader self-identification. The disclosure choice depends on Bermon's read of the firm, the practice culture, and the team. The skill should support whichever approach Bermon chooses, without prescribing.

## Common situations and the authentic-presence move

### Large-group meetings where you'd normally fade

The standard advice: "speak up more." The authentic move: identify the 1–2 substantive contributions you can make beforehand, deliver them deliberately, then let the meeting flow without forcing yourself into more.

Senior people who speak rarely but substantively often have higher gravitas than those who fill airtime.

### Networking events

The standard advice: work the room. The authentic move: pick 3–5 people you want to connect with substantively, have real conversations with them, leave when you've done the substance. Don't perform 2 hours of small talk.

### Public speaking / keynotes

The standard advice: be charismatic. The authentic move: deeply prepare the content, deliver it with your real voice (not a performance voice), let the substance carry. Many beloved speakers (Susan Cain comes to mind) are introverts who do this exactly.

### Difficult 1:1s

The standard advice: smooth over emotion. The authentic move: name the moment honestly. "This is a hard conversation. Let me be specific about what I'm trying to do." Calibrated directness lands better than performed comfort.

### Senior client conversations

The standard advice: match their energy. The authentic move: bring real preparation, real listening, and your actual perspective. Senior clients can usually tell when they're being performed at; they prefer actual substance.

## Building the long-arc identity

Over years, the authentic-presence path produces a leadership identity that is:
- Specific (others can describe what you're like and what you're known for)
- Sustainable (no daily masking debt)
- Recognized for what's actually exceptional about you
- A model for others (especially other neurodivergent leaders coming up behind you)

The masking path can produce career trajectory but at a sustainability cost that often catches up around 5–15 years in. The authentic path is slower in the short term and durable across decades.

## The recovery work if you've been masking

If you recognize you've been masking heavily for a long time, recovery has shape:

1. **Audit.** What specifically am I masking? In which contexts? At what cost?
2. **Identify low-stakes contexts to drop the mask.** Trusted peers, family, specific 1:1s — places where being more yourself has low downside.
3. **Build the network.** The advice-and-counsel relationships where you can be unguarded.
4. **Develop the strengths the mask was hiding.** Often the masking person also suppressed their actual gifts. Notice what's underneath; cultivate it.
5. **Disclose where it serves.** Doesn't have to be everywhere; pick the places where the unmasked version compounds.
6. **Allow grief.** Many people who recognize sustained masking grieve the years spent performing. The grief is real; the path forward is real too.
7. **Don't expect it to be fast.** Years of masking pattern don't unwind in a quarter. Build practices over years.
