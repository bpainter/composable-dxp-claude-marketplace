# Product Leadership — Banfield, Eriksson, Walkingshaw (O'Reilly, 2017)

**Source digest** for the product-management plugin (also referenced from the cx plugin). Cite as "(Banfield et al., *Product Leadership*, ch. X)".

## Thesis

Product *management* and product *leadership* are inextricably linked but not the same. Management is "doing things right"; leadership is "doing the right things." Most product orgs over-invest in tactical PM craft and under-invest in the leadership work that scales it — vision, principles, hiring, team design, organizational politics, partner ecology.

The book's argument: there is no single "great product leader" archetype. The right leader for a startup is the wrong leader for an enterprise. Three growth stages (startup, emerging, enterprise) need three different leadership styles, and most failures come from leaders who succeeded at one stage and didn't adapt for the next.

The book is structured around interviews with ~100 product leaders across stages, regions, and industries. The arguments are pattern-extractions from that pool, not prescriptive frameworks like Perri's. Read it for vocabulary and contextual judgment, not for a methodology.

## Part I — The product leader

### What product management is (Ch. 1)

Eriksson's canonical Venn definition: product management sits at the **intersection of business, user experience, and technology**. A good PM is "experienced in at least one, passionate about all three, and conversant with practitioners of all three."

Cagan's complement: "The job of a product manager is to **discover a product that is valuable, usable, and feasible.**" *Valuable* = business; *usable* = UX; *feasible* = technology.

The book is explicit that PM evolved through four eras:
1. **Brand management origins** (Procter & Gamble, 1930s) — outbound marketing function.
2. **Tech adaptation** (1990s) — moved into software but inconsistent across firms (sometimes outbound, sometimes technical, sometimes confused with project management).
3. **Agile era** — PM forced into close cadence with engineering, became more technical and continuous.
4. **C-suite era** — CPO becomes a recognized role; PM is recognized as a discipline, not a function.

### What product leadership *isn't* (Ch. 2)

The book is sharper on the negatives than the positives:

- **Not a senior PM.** Leadership is leading *people who lead products*, a different skill set.
- **Not "hero PM" at scale.** The mini-CEO archetype scales badly past 1–2 product lines.
- **Not just titular promotion.** A PM promoted to "Head of Product" without organizational design authority is still a PM with a bigger title.
- **Not project management with a different name.** The book repeatedly distinguishes the two.

What it *is*: getting results through other people. The phrase recurs as a near-thesis: "it is not about individual success, it's about getting the best out of others."

### Five things that keep product leaders awake at night (Ch. 2)

The book's working list of senior-PM-leader concerns. Useful as a diagnostic — if a PM leader doesn't recognize themselves in this list, they may still be operating at IC level.

1. **Prioritizing ideas, features, and projects.** Not the IC backlog problem; the cross-team / cross-product allocation problem.
2. **Collaborating effectively and productively.** Especially across functions where the PM has no direct authority.
3. **Training and developing the product team.** Hiring, onboarding, growing successors.
4. **Managing upward and outward.** Translating between the team and exec / board / customers / sales.
5. **Aligning and focusing the organization.** Saying no at scale.

### Being a great product leader (Ch. 3) — the leadership cascade

The most useful single framework in the book. A four-step cascade from the abstract to the actionable:

```
Product Principles  →  Product Vision  →  Strategy  →  Roadmap (Themes)
```

**Product principles** — the team's design and operating commitments, articulated up front so they constrain every later decision. Adams (Intercom) example: "Think big but start small. Ship to learn. Design from first principles." Principles are the screen for ideas; without them, every idea looks valid.

**Product vision** — Bezos: "Be stubborn on vision, flexible on details." A vision is *timeless and disconnected from technology or trends*. Disney's "Make People Happy" works as well today as in 1955. Microsoft's "PC in every home" was a *temporal goal* mistaken for a vision (Nadella).

The vision-vs-temporal-goal distinction is one of the book's sharpest tools. Test: would this still be true in 20 years? If not, it's a goal, not a vision.

**Strategy** — translating vision into 4–6 specific product goals for the year. Goals get OKRs; OKRs get experiments; experiments produce learning that feeds back into strategy.

**Roadmap (themes, not features)** — Spool: "Themes are a promise to solve problems, not build features." The book strongly endorses theme-driven roadmaps as the only roadmap shape that survives contact with reality. Detailed elsewhere in [[book-product-roadmaps-relaunched]].

### Discovery vs. delivery balance (Ch. 3)

The book frames continuous discovery as the practice that keeps the leadership cascade honest. Deliver-only teams build the wrong things confidently; discover-only teams produce decks. The right ratio depends on stage:

- **Startup:** ~70% discovery (you don't know what to build), 30% delivery.
- **Emerging:** ~50/50 — product-market fit found; ramping the build.
- **Enterprise:** ~20% discovery, 80% delivery — but the 20% is non-negotiable. Enterprises that drop discovery entirely become Kodak.

### Hiring product leaders (Ch. 4–5) — formula and timing

There is **no formula for product-leader success**. The book is explicit about this and treats the absence of a formula as the central problem.

**Common characteristics** of successful product leaders (extracted from interview pool):
- Empathy with team and customer.
- Lifelong learning posture.
- Comfort with ambiguity.
- Active listening over speaking.
- Willingness to be wrong publicly.
- Discipline around saying no.

**Timing for hiring** by stage:
- **Founder-led** — the founder *is* the product leader until ~10 employees. First product hire is often a senior PM, not a Head of Product.
- **Emerging** (10–100 employees) — Head of Product hire is the moment the founder needs to step out of day-to-day product. Founders who don't make this transition produce the "founder shadow" anti-pattern.
- **Enterprise** (100+) — VP / CPO. The hire is about portfolio, not single product.

## Part II — The right leader for the right time

The most differentiated section of the book. Three growth stages, three leadership styles. The argument: skill at one stage doesn't transfer to the next without explicit re-skilling.

### Startup product leadership (Ch. 6)

**Characteristics:**
- Founder-led or near-founder. Vision-heavy.
- Plays the customer constantly — the leader *is* the user proxy.
- Fast and noisy; few processes by design.
- Wears many hats; product, marketing, sales, ops blur.

**Common failure mode:** founder who can't let go of product decisions when scaling past 20 people. Anti-pattern: "product founder versus first product hire" — the first PM hire is set up to fail because the founder retains decision authority while delegating execution.

**Style cue:** Lead from in-front. The leader is visibly doing the work and is the loudest user voice in the room.

### Emerging organization product leadership (Ch. 7)

**Characteristics:**
- 10–100 employees. PMF found. Now scaling.
- The hardest stage. The book devotes the most attention here because most product orgs fail in this transition.
- Leader's job shifts from doing product work to *coaching others doing product work*.

**The journey from "technical master to people leader"** (the chapter's recurring frame):
- Technical PM excellence got the leader the role; people skills determine whether they keep it.
- The book is direct: many great senior PMs fail as Heads of Product because they continue to operate at IC level.
- Specific symptoms: micromanaging story-writing, taking back features from PMs, being the loudest voice in product reviews.

**Common failure modes:**
- **Ego unchecked.** "I built this; I know best." Erodes trust with the team.
- **Communication breakdown.** Stops communicating context once team grows past 20 — assumes everyone has it.
- **No connection between dots.** Multiple PMs working in parallel; no one connecting the work into coherent strategy.

**Style cue:** Lead from beside. Coach, set context, remove obstacles. Stop being the answer; start being the question-asker.

### Enterprise product leadership (Ch. 8)

**Characteristics:**
- 100+ employees, multiple product lines, P&L responsibility.
- The challenge is *not* execution — at this stage execution machinery exists. The challenge is staying customer-centered, avoiding sanitized data, and resisting complacency.

**Common failure modes:**
- **Complacency after success.** "We've made it" — the company stops investing in discovery.
- **Sanitized data.** Customer signal gets aggregated and politicized; the leader sees PowerPoint, not raw voice.
- **Loss of focus as scale increases.** Many parallel initiatives, none well-resourced — Perri's "peanut-buttering."
- **Internal-customer-only.** Leader spends 90% of time with internal stakeholders; loses touch with external customer.
- **Showing, not telling.** Enterprise leaders must constantly *demonstrate* customer obsession to a skeptical internal audience.

**Style cue:** Lead from behind. Set vision and principles, then trust the org. Step in only at portfolio-level decisions and at moments of cultural drift.

### "Thinking small to think big" (Ch. 8)

Amazon as the canonical example. Enterprise leaders who succeed treat each product line as if it were a startup — small autonomous teams, owned outcomes, real P&L visibility. The Bezos two-pizza-team rule sits inside this idea.

For Slalom Composable DXP work, this is the structural answer to "how do we keep our practice nimble inside a 13,000-person firm." The practice is the small startup inside the enterprise.

## Part III — Working with external stakeholders

### Mapping the partner ecology (Ch. 9)

Every product company has a "solar system" of partners, agencies, customers, vendors. Product leaders who can manage this ecology get more done. The chapter is mostly about *when* and *how* to use external help — directly relevant for consulting work.

**When and why to use consultants** (the section most relevant to Slalom):
- Filling experience and tactical gaps the internal team doesn't have.
- Managing change the internal team can't drive politically.
- Embedding outside help that *teaches* rather than *does* — capability transfer is the win.

**Common partner pitfalls:**
- Treating an agency as a vendor instead of a partner — adversarial procurement, no shared outcomes.
- Hiring a consultant for a problem that's actually political (consultants can't fix that).
- Letting consultants embed permanently — the relationship becomes operational instead of capability-building.
- No exit criteria for the engagement.

**Trust is the glue** (chapter's key claim): partner relationships fail on trust failures, not on capability gaps. Product leaders who invest in trust early get higher leverage from partners later. The corollary for consultants: invest in earned trust from week 1; do not lead with deliverables.

## Eight named frameworks worth pulling out

1. **Eriksson's Venn** — PM = business ∩ UX ∩ technology (Ch. 1).
2. **Cagan's three-axis test** — valuable, usable, feasible (Ch. 1).
3. **Leadership cascade** — Principles → Vision → Strategy → Roadmap (Ch. 3).
4. **Vision vs. temporal goal test** — would it still be true in 20 years? (Ch. 3).
5. **Themes-not-features roadmap** — Spool's "promise to solve problems, not build features" (Ch. 3; expanded in [[book-product-roadmaps-relaunched]]).
6. **Discovery/delivery ratio by stage** — startup 70/30, emerging 50/50, enterprise 20/80 (Ch. 3).
7. **Three stages of product leadership** — startup (lead from in-front), emerging (lead from beside), enterprise (lead from behind) (Chs. 6–8).
8. **Partner ecology** — when to use consultants, how to manage trust, exit criteria (Ch. 9).

## Cross-references

**vs. Perri, *Escaping the Build Trap*:** Perri is the methodology; Banfield et al. is the leadership context. Perri tells you *what* a product org should do; Banfield et al. tells you *how the human at the top of it should behave at each stage*. Pair them on any product-org-design engagement: Perri for the operating model, Banfield for the leadership transitions that make it work.

**vs. Lombardo et al., *Product Roadmaps Relaunched*:** Banfield et al. introduces themes (Ch. 3); Lombardo et al. is the deep treatise on theme-driven roadmaps. Read Banfield first for the leadership argument, Lombardo for the roadmap craft. See [[book-product-roadmaps-relaunched]].

**vs. Gothelf & Seiden, *Sense and Respond*:** Gothelf is the operating model behind continuous discovery; Banfield names the leadership behaviors that produce a culture where Gothelf's loops can run. The "lead from beside" emerging-stage style is the leadership behavior Gothelf assumes but doesn't name.

**vs. Cagan, *Inspired*:** Cagan is the more comprehensive PM-discipline book; Banfield is narrower (leadership specifically) and more biographical (interview-driven). For first-principles PM craft, default to Cagan; for stage-aware leadership transitions, default to Banfield.

**vs. CX plugin (this digest is shared):** Product leadership decides *what* the product is and *who it serves at which stage*. CX provides the customer evidence those decisions rest on. The two functions partner; they do not substitute. JTBD work, journey mapping, and feedback synthesis are inputs to the leadership cascade, not separate streams.

## Pitfalls

### Stage-mismatch failures
- Founder won't let go past 20 employees ("product founder vs. first product hire," Ch. 6).
- Senior PM promoted to Head of Product still operates at IC level (Ch. 7).
- Enterprise leader who never developed strategic muscle, only operational (Ch. 8).
- Hiring an enterprise CPO into a 30-person company; they will fail because the operating model isn't there yet.

### Vision failures
- Mistaking a temporal goal for a vision ("PC in every home"; Ch. 3).
- Vision tied to current technology — dies when technology shifts.
- Vision so abstract no one can act on it ("change the world").
- Vision that the leader can articulate but the team can't.

### Roadmap failures
- Feature-list roadmaps surviving in environments that need theme-based ones (Ch. 3; deeper in [[book-product-roadmaps-relaunched]]).
- "Now/Next/Later" populated only with features, no hypothesis or metric.
- Roadmap presented as commitments instead of intentions.
- No mechanism to update the roadmap when learning happens.

### Partner / consultant failures
- Treating consultants as labor instead of capability transfer (Ch. 9).
- No exit criteria on partner engagements.
- Trust failures masked as capability gaps.
- Consultant brought in to fix what's actually a political problem.

### Discovery failures
- Enterprise that stops investing in discovery after success (Ch. 8).
- Discovery without a decision attached (research theater).
- "Sanitized data" — research aggregated to the point that customer voice disappears (Ch. 8).
- Discovery and delivery on the same team with delivery always winning prioritization.
