# Innovation Capital — Dyer, Furr, Lefrandt (2019)

**Source digest** for the innovation plugin. Cite as "(Dyer, Furr, Lefrandt, *Innovation Capital*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

The highest-impact innovators aren't the ones with the best ideas — they're the ones who systematically build *innovation capital*: the political and social currency required to get bold ideas funded, protected, and sustained inside organizations. Most innovation talent fails not on idea quality but on capital deficit. Capital-building is **learnable**, not a personality trait.

The book studies disruptive innovators including Elon Musk, Marc Benioff, Reed Hastings, Indra Nooyi, plus a multi-decade dataset of innovation careers.

## The four sources of innovation capital

1. **Human capital** — your track record and demonstrable skills
2. **Social capital** — who you know, who knows you, the strength and reach of your network
3. **Reputation capital** — what you're known for; how decision-makers describe you to others
4. **Impression amplifiers** — how you tell the story (vivid demos, prototypes, narratives, presence)

Each source can be invested in deliberately. Most innovation training focuses on idea generation and validation; the capital frame argues that's necessary but insufficient — capital determines whether good ideas survive contact with the org.

## Building human capital

- Build a track record before the bet that requires it. Small wins compound.
- Acquire signal-bearing credentials when their absence blocks credibility (degree, certification, role title)
- Develop an unmistakable competence — the "they own X" reputation
- Be useful to senior leaders before you need them

## Building social capital

The book distinguishes *broad* networks (lots of weak ties) from *deep* networks (compounding strong ties). The argument: innovation work needs both, but most professionals over-invest in broad and under-invest in deep.

- Map the org's actual decision graph (different from the org chart)
- Invest in 5–10 sponsor / peer relationships that compound
- Maintain external relationships — board members, analysts, customers — because internal capital alone fails when leadership turns over
- Be useful to others *before* you need them

## Building reputation capital

- Choose what to be known for — innovation talent often defaults to "I do everything," which is reputation-flat
- Reinforce the chosen reputation with consistent signal — what you publish, who you speak to, what you decline
- Repair reputation damage explicitly when needed; don't ignore
- The "third-party amplifier" rule: what others say about you is more credible than what you say about yourself

## Building impression amplifiers (the most underrated source)

The book's most-quoted insight: **vivid demos beat decks**. Innovation gets funded by leaders who *see* the work, not who *read* about it.

- Prototype the experience, not the slide
- Narrative arcs > bullet points
- Live presence — innovation demos in person, with stakes
- Counter-intuition is memorable; design the surprise
- Use customer voice (real quotes, real reactions) as amplifier

Examples: Marc Benioff's elaborate demo theater at Dreamforce; Steve Jobs's keynote presentation craft; Reed Hastings's high-conviction storytelling on streaming when DVDs were the business.

## The capital-building career arc

The book's longitudinal observation: innovators who become consequential follow a recognizable capital-building arc:

1. **Early career** — accumulate human capital (skills, credentials, track record); begin social capital deliberately
2. **Mid-career** — develop reputation capital around a chosen domain; deepen social capital
3. **Senior career** — leverage capital to take bigger bets; invest in impression amplifiers
4. **Late career** — give capital to others; sponsor next generation

Most innovation talent stalls between 1 and 2 because they don't deliberately design reputation. They become "people who do many things" and lose distinctiveness.

## The capital-deficit failure mode

When innovation talent fails not on idea but on capital, the patterns are recognizable:

- Great work that nobody hears about (impression amplifier deficit)
- Strong track record in a niche, no broad reputation (reputation deficit)
- Brilliant ideas without sponsor (social capital deficit)
- Sponsor exits, the work dies (over-concentrated social capital)

The book's prescriptive answer: **diagnose which source is the deficit, and invest in it deliberately for 6–12 months before the next bet that needs it.**

## The reframe — capital-building isn't selling out

Most innovators feel "selling the work" is unethical or beneath them. The book's reframe:

> *If your work is good, capital-building is how you give the work a chance. Failing to build capital isn't humility — it's withholding good ideas from the people who need them.*

Capital-building is part of the work, not a separate political game.

## Three patterns worth replicating

- **Quarterly capital diagnostic** — innovation leader scores their four-source position, identifies the deficit, plans 6-month investment
- **Demo discipline** — every funding ask is paired with a vivid demo (prototype, customer voice, scenario walkthrough), not just a deck
- **Decision graph mapping** — early in any new role, map who *actually* decides on innovation funding, beyond the org chart; invest in those 5–10 relationships
