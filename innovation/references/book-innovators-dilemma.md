# The Innovator's Dilemma — Clayton M. Christensen (1997)

**Source digest** for the innovation plugin. Cite as "(Christensen, *Innovator's Dilemma*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

Well-managed firms fail not because they manage badly but because they manage *correctly* — listening to their best customers, investing in highest-margin products, and improving sustaining technology — which makes them systematically blind to disruptive technologies that initially serve over-shot, low-end, or non-consuming customers. The dilemma: the practices that produce success at one stage cause failure at the next.

## The five principles of disruptive innovation

1. **Resource dependence** — companies allocate resources to where their customers and shareholders pull them. Customers and shareholders pull toward sustaining innovation.
2. **Small markets don't solve big company growth needs** — disruptive technology starts small; large incumbents can't justify the investment given near-term return profiles.
3. **Markets that don't exist can't be analyzed** — disruptive markets must be discovered, not researched. Traditional market analysis (asking customers) won't surface them.
4. **Capabilities are also disabilities** — what you do well in your current business is exactly what you can't do in the disruptive business. Capabilities live in *resources, processes, and values* (RPV), not just resources.
5. **Technology supply may not equal market demand** — sustaining trajectories often over-shoot what mainstream customers need, opening space for "good enough" disruptors.

## Sustaining vs. disruptive innovation (the central distinction)

- **Sustaining innovation** — improves performance on dimensions mainstream customers value. Incumbents almost always win these.
- **Disruptive innovation** — performs *worse* on dimensions mainstream customers value but *better* on dimensions over-shot, low-end, or non-consuming customers value (price, simplicity, convenience, accessibility).

Most "disruption" claims fail this test. If a new entrant is *better* at what incumbents already do, it's sustaining innovation, and incumbents usually win.

## The textbook failure pattern

1. Disruptive technology emerges in a market that doesn't matter to incumbents
2. Incumbents observe but don't act — the market is too small, margins too thin
3. Disruptive technology improves on its own trajectory (often steeper than incumbents')
4. Disruptors capture low-end and non-consumption customers
5. Disruptive technology reaches "good enough" for mainstream customers
6. Incumbents lose, often suddenly, when the trajectory crosses

## The response pattern that works

Christensen's prescriptive answer: **a separate organizational unit** with its own resources, processes, values, and customers. Reasons:

- Cost structure matched to disruptive market (smaller, lower-margin)
- Decision rights to ship at lower quality / margin than the core would tolerate
- Metrics that don't kill the unit (no margin gates that the disruptive market can't support)
- Talent that wants to work on disruption, not sustain
- Cultural distance from the core

Examples: HP's printer division, IBM's PC unit (initially), Eastman Kodak's photo CD failures (got it wrong), Toyota's Lexus.

## Five concrete cases worth quoting

- **Disk drive industry** — the foundational case study; multiple disruption cycles in 25 years
- **Steel — minimills vs. integrated mills** — low-end disruption, tier-by-tier ascent
- **Excavators — hydraulic vs. cable** — disruption that took 30 years to play out
- **Department stores — discount retail disruption** — Kmart, Walmart entry, eventual conversion of mainstream
- **Notebook computers vs. desktops** — sustaining within personal computing; disruptive against minicomputers

## Subsequent clarifications (Christensen et al.)

The book has been mis-applied for 25 years; Christensen authored corrections:

- **Christensen, Raynor, McDonald, *What Is Disruptive Innovation?* (HBR Dec 2015)** — Uber is *not* disruptive (didn't start at low end, didn't serve non-consumption); Netflix DVDs *was* disruptive (started in non-consumption mailing market)
- **Christensen, Anthony, Roth, *Seeing What's Next*** — applying the framework forward, not just retrospectively

Use the test rigorously. Most things called disruptive are sustaining innovation under aggressive marketing.
