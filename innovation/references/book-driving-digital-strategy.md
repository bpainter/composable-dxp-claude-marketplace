# Driving Digital Strategy — David L. Rogers (2016)

**Source digest** for the innovation plugin. Cross-referenced by `consulting-digital-strategist`. Cite as "(Rogers, *Driving Digital Strategy*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

Digital strategy isn't a special case of strategy; it's a reframing of every strategic domain. Rogers identifies five domains where pre-digital assumptions break down — Customers, Competition, Data, Innovation, Value — and provides specific strategic moves for each. The book is the most-cited single-author DT framework in the post-2015 transformation literature.

## The five domains framework

| Domain | Pre-digital assumption | Digital reality | Strategic move |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Customers** | Mass market; one-way push | Network of dynamic engagement | Build network value; harness customer networks as the unit of strategy |
| **Competition** | Defined industry boundaries | Asymmetric and platform-based | Compete across boundaries; build platforms |
| **Data** | Costly, scarce, internal | Continuously generated, key asset | Make data a strategic asset; build data products |
| **Innovation** | Driven by intuition, market research | Rapid testing, validated learning | Innovate by rapid experimentation |
| **Value** | Defined by industry boundaries | Customer-defined; reframable | Adapt the value proposition |

Each domain has different strategic moves; none are technology in the narrow sense. **Most DT programs fail to reframe across all five — they only address Innovation and Data.**

## Key reframes per domain

### Customers — from mass to network

- Customer behaviors aren't just shaped by your offering; they're shaped by *each other*
- Network effects, virality, communities, social proof — the unit of customer strategy is the network, not the individual
- Five types of customer network behavior: access, engage, customize, connect, collaborate

### Competition — from defined to asymmetric

- Industry boundaries dissolve; competition comes from adjacent and unexpected categories
- Platform economics — be the platform or be on a platform; rarely both
- Disintermediation as a recurring threat; intermediation as a recurring opportunity

### Data — from scarce to abundant

- Data as a continuous output of digital operations, not an occasional study
- Four data templates: insights, targeting, personalization, context
- Data as competitive moat through scale and scope effects

### Innovation — from intuition to experimentation

- Rapid testing, A/B, experimentation as the default mode
- Convergent thinking traps (deciding too fast); divergent thinking discipline
- The minimum viable prototype as the medium of strategy conversation

### Value — from industry-defined to customer-defined

- Value propositions decay; reframe regularly based on customer outcomes
- Six value-proposition pivots: targeting, focusing, refocusing, repositioning, broadening, repurposing
- The "concept of value" itself is mutable; don't anchor to category orthodoxy

## The "Disruptive Business Model Map"

Rogers's diagnostic for whether and how a business is being disrupted. Two-axis:

- **Value proposition** — incremental vs. divergent
- **Value network** — held-constant vs. transformed

Four quadrants — incumbent improvement, new value proposition (sustaining), platform innovation, full disruption. Use to position incumbents and entrants.

## Three patterns worth replicating

- **Five-domains diagnostic** — apply to any DT program; surface which domains have been reframed and which haven't
- **Disruptive Business Model Map** — position incumbents and entrants; clarify the strategic threat
- **Value-proposition pivots** — six explicit reframings to choose from when value is decaying
