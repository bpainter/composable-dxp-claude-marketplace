# Innovation Lab Excellence: Digital Transformation from Within

**Source digest** for the innovation plugin. Cite as "(*Innovation Lab Excellence*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

Most corporate innovation labs fail in years 2–3 because they're chartered as theater, measured against the wrong KPIs, isolated from operating BUs that should absorb their output, and abandoned by sponsors who change roles. Lab excellence is *structural* — it requires explicit charter, archetype clarity, multi-year funding, talent rotation, sunset criteria, and survival mechanics built in from day one.

The book draws on global lab case studies (corporate R&D centers, customer experience centers, venture studios, accelerators) across financial services, retail, manufacturing, and healthcare.

## Lab archetypes (pick one — don't blend more than two)

The book's most-used contribution: clarifying that "innovation lab" is a category, not a thing. Six archetypes:

1. **Discovery Lab** — runs JTBD, reframing, validation work for the rest of the org. Doesn't ship products. Hands off to operating BUs.
2. **Venture Studio** — builds new businesses, often with separate P&L. Incubate, sometimes spin out, sometimes absorb back.
3. **Center of Excellence** — owns a capability (AI, design, composable, experimentation) that operates across the org.
4. **Customer Experience Center** — physical or virtual space to demonstrate, co-create, engage customers.
5. **Corporate Accelerator** — runs cohort programs with external startups.
6. **Hybrid** — most real labs blend 2 archetypes. Common pairs: Discovery + CXC; Venture Studio + CoE.

Mismatch between archetype and mandate is the most common diagnostic finding.

## The 8-section charter (the foundational artifact)

Every real lab has a written charter, ratified by the executive sponsor:

1. **Mandate** — one sentence; what the lab exists to do, for whom, why
2. **Scope** — in / out, especially what's *out*
3. **Funding model** — annual budget, source, multi-year commitment (≥3 years)
4. **Decision rights** — what the lab decides alone vs. escalates
5. **Talent model** — how people enter, exit, are compensated
6. **KPIs** — by year (capability in year 1, advancement in year 2, outcomes in year 3+)
7. **Sponsorship** — named executive sponsor + reporting line + escalation path + successor sponsor
8. **Sunset criteria** — when this lab should be retired or restructured

A charter without sunset criteria becomes a permanent department.

## The KPI mismatch problem (the #1 lab killer)

Labs fail when measured on the wrong KPIs. Pattern:

- **Year 1 KPIs** should be capability metrics — talent built, IP developed, partnerships formed, customer relationships engaged, experiments run
- **Year 2 KPIs** should be advancement metrics — bets through gates, pilots launched, validated learning produced
- **Year 3+ KPIs** can include outcome metrics — option value, revenue from H2/H3 bets, capability sustained

If finance demands revenue KPIs in Year 1 for an H3-heavy lab, the lab is being killed before it starts. Negotiate this in the charter.

## The "absorber" problem

Labs need *operating BUs willing to absorb their output*. Without absorbers, the lab becomes a cul-de-sac:

- Discovery labs without absorbers produce reports nobody acts on
- Venture studios without absorbers produce orphaned businesses
- CoEs without adopters produce capabilities the rest of the org ignores

Charter design: identify ≥2 BUs as named absorber relationships, with handoff protocols, before the lab launches.

## Year-2 collapse pattern

Most corporate labs collapse around month 18–24. Empirical pattern:

- **Month 0–12** — honeymoon; visible quick wins; sponsor enthusiastic
- **Month 12–18** — first big test (revenue, scale demand, sponsor change)
- **Month 18–24** — collapse: KPI mismatch surfaces; sponsor exits or attention drifts; org reabsorbs talent
- **Month 24+** — survive only if multi-year funding committed and successor sponsor named

Survival mechanics built into the charter beat heroics.

## Survival mechanics

The book's prescriptive list — build into Day 1 of the lab:

- **Multi-year funding** — 3-year minimum commitment in writing
- **Independent budget line** — not subject to BU cost cuts
- **Successor sponsor** — named in advance; cultivated before needed
- **Customer Love evidence pipeline** — measurable customer impact by month 12, even if not revenue
- **Output cadence** — visible quarterly drumbeat (whitepapers, prototypes, customer stories)
- **Operating BU partnerships** — ≥2 absorbers identified in charter
- **Talent rotation** — rotational seats (not pure-permanent priesthood)

## When to sunset

The book is unusually direct about when labs should be killed:

- 18+ months with no advanced bets (Discovery lab)
- 30+ months with no validated business (Venture studio)
- Capability not adopted by 50%+ of org (CoE)
- Customer engagement metric below baseline (CX center)
- Sponsor changed twice without succession

A lab that should sunset and doesn't poisons the next attempt.

## Three patterns worth replicating

- **The named successor sponsor** — at charter signing, name who picks up if the current sponsor leaves. Cultivate that relationship deliberately.
- **The quarterly visibility drumbeat** — quarterly output (whitepaper, prototype, customer story) keeps the lab visible during the year-2 collapse window.
- **The absorber commitment** — ≥2 operating BUs commit at charter signing to absorbing lab output. Without this, labs become cul-de-sacs.
