# Foundational Innovation Concepts

Shared reference for every skill in the `innovation` plugin. Covers the load-bearing models, the language they share, and the boundaries between them so skills don't drift into each other's territory.

## The five questions every innovation problem reduces to

Use this as the diagnostic spine before reaching for any framework.

1. **Where to play?** — Which horizon, which customer, which job? *(Strategist, Portfolio Architect, Discovery Coach.)*
2. **What's true vs. assumed?** — Which beliefs are we betting the company on without evidence? *(Method Validator, Discovery Coach.)*
3. **Why will customers pay, and how much?** — Willingness-to-pay before product. *(Monetization Strategist, Value Engineer.)*
4. **What's the value, and is it worth the bet?** — Quantified value across horizons; kill economics. *(Value Engineer, Portfolio Architect.)*
5. **What organizational conditions does this need to survive?** — Mandate, sponsorship, culture, lab vs. line, political capital. *(Lab Architect, Leadership Coach, DT Advisor.)*

If a request doesn't map cleanly to one of these, the request is probably mis-framed.

## The portfolio language

### Three Horizons (McKinsey — Baghai, Coley, White, *The Alchemy of Growth*, 1999)

Time-based portfolio model:

- **H1 (0–12 months)** — Defend and extend the core. Margin, retention, optimization. Operates on quarterly metrics.
- **H2 (1–3 years)** — Build emerging businesses. Adjacent markets, new offerings to existing customers, existing offerings to new customers. Operates on adoption metrics.
- **H3 (3+ years)** — Create viable options. New business models, new categories, market creation. Operates on learning metrics.

Default allocation: **70/20/10** (Nagji & Tuff, HBR 2012). Returns ratio inverts: ~10% / 20% / 70% — most long-run value comes from the smallest slice. Don't confuse activity allocation with capital allocation; resources must match strategic intent.

### Innovation Ambition Matrix (Nagji & Tuff)

Risk-based, not time-based:

```
                 │ Existing offering │  New offering
─────────────────┼───────────────────┼────────────────
Existing market  │  Core (incremental│  Adjacent
                 │  improvement)     │  (extension)
─────────────────┼───────────────────┼────────────────
New market       │  Adjacent         │  Transformational
                 │  (extension)      │  (disruptive)
```

Use Three Horizons for *time*; Ambition Matrix for *risk*. They're not the same axis — an H2 bet can be Core or Transformational.

### Stage-gate vs. discovery-driven planning

- **Stage-gate** (Cooper) — Discrete phases (Idea → Scope → Build Case → Develop → Test → Launch) with formal go/no-go reviews. Works well for H1 and well-understood H2. Fails on H3 because the gates demand certainty the data can't yet support.
- **Discovery-Driven Planning** (McGrath & MacMillan, HBR 1995) — Plan the *learning*, not the outcome. List assumptions, rank by riskiness, design tests, advance based on what's been validated. The right model for H3 and risky H2.

The portfolio architect chooses the governance model; mismatched governance is the #1 reason innovation fails inside enterprises.

## The discovery language

### Jobs-to-be-Done (Christensen)

Customers don't buy products; they "hire" products to make progress in a circumstance. The unit of analysis is the **job**, not the customer or the product. Three classes:

- **Functional** — what they're trying to accomplish
- **Emotional** — how they want to feel (or stop feeling)
- **Social** — how they want to be seen

Job story format: *"When [circumstance], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."*

The misuse: writing job stories about features you already built. The right use: discovery interviews about the last time the customer fired a previous solution.

### The five discovery skills (Dyer, Gregersen, Christensen, *Innovator's DNA*, 2011)

Innovators outperform on five behaviors. Four are *cognitive*; one is *catalyst*:

1. **Associating** (catalyst) — connecting unrelated domains
2. **Questioning** — challenging assumptions, asking "why" and "what if"
3. **Observing** — watching customers in context, not in surveys
4. **Networking** — talking to people unlike you for fresh inputs
5. **Experimenting** — building cheap prototypes to test ideas

The book's core finding: these are *practices*, not personality traits. Teams can be designed for them.

## The validation language

### The Innovator's Method (Furr & Dyer, 2014) — four sequential validations

H3 bets fail when teams skip a step. Validate in order:

1. **Insight** — is there a hot problem worth solving?
2. **Problem** — do customers describe the problem the way we do?
3. **Solution** — does our solution actually solve it (better than the alternatives, including doing nothing)?
4. **Business model** — does the value capture work — pricing, channel, cost structure?

Each stage uses different methods. **Don't build solution prototypes before problem validation. Don't price before solution validation.** Most innovation theater fails at validation #2.

### Lean Startup vocabulary (Ries) — used as shorthand

- **MVP** — minimum viable product (smallest thing that delivers a learning loop)
- **Pivot vs. persevere** — change strategy or hold direction based on validated learning
- **Build → Measure → Learn** — the experiment cycle
- **Pretotyping** (Savoia) — fake it before you build it; the simulated experience proves intent better than functional MVPs in many cases

## The typology language: Doblin's Ten Types (Keeley et al., 2013)

Categorizes innovation across three areas, ten types. Sustained advantage comes from combining ≥3 types — single-type innovations get copied.

**Configuration** (internal, business-model side):
1. **Profit Model** — how you make money (subscription, ads, freemium, marketplace fees, etc.)
2. **Network** — who you partner with and how
3. **Structure** — how you organize talent and assets
4. **Process** — how you do work better/faster/cheaper

**Offering** (the product itself):
5. **Product Performance** — features, function, quality
6. **Product System** — how products connect into a system

**Experience** (customer-facing):
7. **Service** — augment the offering with services
8. **Channel** — how you reach customers
9. **Brand** — identity and meaning
10. **Customer Engagement** — interactions that build affinity

Most companies over-invest in #5 (product performance) and under-invest in #1, #7, #10. The diagnostic question: *"Which types are we actually competing on, and which have we ignored?"*

## The economics language

### Value across horizons (the Value Engineer's spine)

Different horizons require different value math:

- **H1** — NPV, payback period, IRR. Cash flows are estimable. Use traditional DCF.
- **H2** — Expected value across scenarios; risk-adjusted NPV; option value of being in the market early. Cash flows have wide ranges; weight by probability.
- **H3** — **Real options** dominate. Don't compute NPV of the moonshot; compute the *value of the option* the small bet buys. *Black-Scholes-style intuition*: an out-of-the-money option still has time value. Kill economics: how much capital is spent before kill points?

### Willingness-to-pay (Monetizing Innovation, Ramanujam & Tacke, 2016)

The book's thesis: 72% of innovations fail to deliver expected returns because pricing is decided after product is built. Four innovation failure modes:

1. **Feature shocks** — over-loaded with features customers don't value
2. **Minivations** — under-priced relative to value created
3. **Hidden gems** — adjacent value that never ships because no champion
4. **Undeads** — products customers don't want but won't admit are dead

Rule: **Have the willingness-to-pay conversation before the product conversation.** Use conjoint, van Westendorp price-sensitivity meter, monadic concept tests. Price is a feature, not a postscript.

## The organizational language

### Lab vs. line

- **Line innovation** — happens inside operating business units; constrained by quarterly metrics; biases toward H1.
- **Lab innovation** — separate organizational entity; operates on learning metrics; can credibly hold H3 bets. Risks: theatre, isolation, talent reabsorption.

The diagnostic: *what are you protecting from what?* Labs protect *learning velocity* from *delivery cadence*. If your H1 doesn't crush H3, you don't need a lab.

### Innovation Capital (Dyer, Furr, Lefrandt, 2019)

The political capital to get bold ideas funded and sustained. Four sources:

1. **Human capital** — your track record and skills
2. **Social capital** — who you know
3. **Reputation** — what you're known for
4. **Impression amplifiers** — how you tell the story (vivid demos, prototypes, narratives)

Most innovation talent fails not on idea quality but on capital deficit. The Leadership Coach skill addresses this directly.

### Creative culture (Catmull, *Creativity, Inc.*, 2014)

Pixar's lessons: candor over comfort, the Braintrust as peer review without authority, separate the idea from the person, fail fast and visibly. The cultural mechanism that makes high-volume H3 work survivable.

## How skills should use these foundations

- **Don't re-explain frameworks already in this file.** Cite them. Skills should engage with the user's situation, not re-teach.
- **Always name the horizon.** Every recommendation should be tagged H1/H2/H3 — otherwise you're recommending H1 governance for an H3 bet (the most common failure mode).
- **Don't cross specialty lines.** If the question is about *pricing*, that's the Monetization Strategist. If it's about *value of the option*, that's the Value Engineer. The agent (`innovation-agent`) sequences across; individual skills stay sharp.
- **Bermon's frame.** He runs Composable DXP at Slalom. Default examples to digital experience platforms, content/commerce composability, MACH architectures unless asked otherwise. He's a builder, not a maintainer — bias toward *what to make*, not *what to study*.
