# The Ten Faces of Innovation — Tom Kelley with Jonathan Littman (2005)

**Source digest** for the innovation plugin. Cite as "(Kelley, *Ten Faces*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

Innovation teams need ten distinct *roles* — not ten people, but ten functions that someone in the team must own. Most teams over-index on a few faces (typically Anthropologist + Experimenter) and under-staff the others (especially Hurdler and Storyteller), which is why discovery work doesn't get funded and prototypes don't ship.

Built from IDEO's 30+ years of design and innovation consulting work.

## The ten faces, in three categories

### Learning roles (input — bring information into the team)

1. **The Anthropologist** — observes customer behavior in context. Watches, doesn't ask. Brings ethnographic rigor to product / service design.
2. **The Experimenter** — builds prototypes to learn. Cheap, fast, iterative. Treats the prototype as the medium of conversation.
3. **The Cross-Pollinator** — brings ideas from other industries. Reads broadly, travels, attends adjacent conferences. The associating skill (cf. Innovator's DNA) embodied as a role.

### Organizing roles (operational — make things happen inside the org)

4. **The Hurdler** — knows how to get things done across organizational friction. Navigates approvals, finds workarounds, builds coalitions. Often unrecognized in formal job descriptions; usually decisive in whether innovation work survives.
5. **The Collaborator** — connects teams across silos. Builds trust between groups that don't naturally work together. Strong relationship currency.
6. **The Director** — leads talent and shapes the lab's craft. Sets standards; protects the team's integrity. Not the same as a project manager.

### Building roles (output — produce the work)

7. **The Experience Architect** — designs end-to-end experiences across touchpoints. Thinks in journeys, sequences, emotional arcs.
8. **The Set Designer** — designs the physical / digital environment that supports the work. Studio space, tools, rituals, ambient signal.
9. **The Caregiver** — delivers customer-facing experience with empathy. The frontline; the human face of the innovation.
10. **The Storyteller** — communicates the work outward. Demos, narratives, customer stories, internal evangelism. The impression-amplifier role.

## The diagnostic — most labs over-index on Anthropologist + Experimenter

The book's most useful applied insight: when innovation labs fail to ship and to survive, the missing roles are typically:

- **Hurdler** — without a Hurdler, validated bets stall on internal approvals
- **Storyteller** — without a Storyteller, leadership doesn't see the work; sponsor enthusiasm fades
- **Director** — without a Director, the team's craft drifts into mediocrity
- **Set Designer** — without a Set Designer, the physical / digital environment doesn't reinforce the work

Run the diagnostic at lab staffing: which faces are covered, which are gaps, which need rotational seats?

## Roles, not people

The book's core distinction: a person can play multiple faces; a face needs to be owned, not necessarily by a dedicated headcount. A 12-person lab might have five faces "owned" by individuals and five played by rotating contributors.

The discipline: name the owner per face. Without ownership, faces drift into "everyone's responsibility, no one's accountability."

## Pattern — the Storyteller is structurally undervalued

The book repeatedly observes that "Storyteller" is treated as nice-to-have but is often the difference between funded and defunded innovation work. The Storyteller turns the team's discoveries, prototypes, and validated learning into vivid narratives that sponsors can hold and propagate.

This connects to *Innovation Capital*'s impression amplifier source — they're the same insight, named differently.

## Pattern — the Set Designer is invisible until it's broken

The physical / digital environment shapes how the team thinks. Studio rituals (daily standups, prototype walls, customer-quote posters) reinforce the work. When the environment doesn't reinforce the work, the team drifts toward generic-corporate norms.

## Three patterns worth replicating

- **Ten Faces audit at lab staffing** — every lab role gets named owner(s); gaps surfaced; rotational seats designed for under-covered faces
- **Storyteller protected time** — explicit slack for the Storyteller to produce quarterly narrative output (whitepaper, customer story, demo film)
- **Set Designer charter** — explicit ownership of studio space, tools, rituals; reviewed quarterly
