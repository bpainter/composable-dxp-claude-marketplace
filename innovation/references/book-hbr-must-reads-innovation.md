# HBR's 10 Must Reads on Innovation

**Source digest** for the innovation plugin. Anthology of 10 HBR articles, with the canonical featured piece *The Discipline of Innovation* by Peter Drucker. Cite as "(HBR Must-Reads on Innovation, [author], [article])" inside skill bodies.

## Anthology purpose

HBR's "Must Reads" series collects the most-cited articles in a domain. The Innovation volume serves as the consensus canon — the articles innovation-trained MBAs and consultants are presumed to know. Use as ground truth on framing, vocabulary, and stance.

## Featured article — The Discipline of Innovation (Peter Drucker, 1985)

Drucker's seven sources of innovation opportunity:

1. **The unexpected** — unexpected success, failure, or outside event
2. **The incongruity** — between reality as it is and reality as it ought to be / is assumed to be
3. **Innovation based on process need** — the "missing link" in a process
4. **Changes in industry / market structure** — that catch incumbents unaware
5. **Demographics** — slow but predictable shifts
6. **Changes in perception, mood, meaning** — cultural shifts
7. **New knowledge** — scientific or non-scientific breakthroughs

Drucker's central argument: innovation is *systematic*, not accidental. Most innovation comes from sources 1–4 (analytical, low-risk); sources 6–7 are higher risk and longer time horizon.

The discipline: **monitor the seven sources deliberately**, not opportunistically.

## Other key articles

### Stop the Innovation Wars — Govindarajan & Trimble

The structural conflict between **performance engines** (running the existing business) and **dedicated teams** (running new innovation work). Their argument: innovation work cannot survive inside the performance engine; it requires structural separation, custom metrics, custom decision rights — exactly the recommendations of Christensen's *Innovator's Dilemma* and Innovation Lab Excellence's archetypes.

### The Innovation Catalysts — Martin

Building organizational innovation capability through "catalysts" — a small cadre of internal coaches who teach design thinking and innovation methods to product/business teams. Embedded coaching beats centralized labs for cultural change.

### Building an Innovation Factory — Hargadon & Sutton

How to make innovation systematic, drawing on IDEO's mechanisms. Five practices:

1. Build a marketplace of ideas (capture and circulate)
2. Build a network of brokers (cross-domain connectors)
3. Treat ideas as building blocks (combine, recombine)
4. Make it real fast (prototype to learn)
5. Tell the world (storytelling and impression amplifiers)

### Why the Lean Start-Up Changes Everything — Steve Blank

Lean Startup applied to enterprise innovation. Customer development, MVP, pivot, validated learning. Core implication: the *startup* methodology is now the *enterprise* methodology for innovation work. Stage-gate with NPV is for sustaining; Lean Startup with validated learning is for new bets.

### How GE Is Disrupting Itself — Immelt, Govindarajan, Trimble

"Reverse innovation" — innovate in emerging markets, then bring the offering to developed markets. The classical innovation pattern (developed → emerging) is reversed when emerging-market constraints (cost, simplicity, accessibility) produce solutions that ultimately serve over-shot developed-market segments.

### The Customer-Centered Innovation Map — Bettencourt & Ulwick

JTBD-adjacent framing. Map the customer's job as a sequence of steps; identify under-served outcomes within steps; design innovation around closing those gaps. Operationalizes JTBD into a workflow.

### Is It Real? Can We Win? Is It Worth Doing? — Day

Three diagnostic questions for innovation funding decisions, applied at early-stage gates:

- Is there a real market need? (Insight + Problem validation)
- Can we win? (Solution + competitive position)
- Is it worth doing? (Business model + value capture + strategic fit)

Nine sub-questions per top-line question; produces a structured go/no-go.

### Six Myths of Product Development — Thomke & Reinertsen

Six counter-intuitive findings:

1. High utilization improves performance — wrong, slows everything down
2. Processing work in large batches is efficient — wrong, increases lead time
3. Sticking to the plan is a virtue — wrong, learning should drive change
4. Shorter projects increase quality — wrong, depends on the kind of risk
5. The more features, the better — wrong, see *Monetizing Innovation*'s feature shocks
6. We will be more successful if we get it right the first time — wrong, iteration is the discipline

### Discovery-Driven Planning — McGrath & MacMillan

The foundational article (1995) on discovery-driven planning — list assumptions, plan to validate, advance based on learning. The right governance for H3 work; described in detail in `innovation-foundations.md`.

- **Day's three questions** as a Gate 0–1 intake screen
- **Thomke/Reinertsen myths review** — annual de-bias workshop with product leaders
