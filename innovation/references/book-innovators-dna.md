# The Innovator's DNA — Dyer, Gregersen, Christensen (2011, updated 2019)

**Source digest** for the innovation plugin. Cite as "(Dyer et al., *Innovator's DNA*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

Innovators outperform peers by ~40% on five specific behaviors — they're not personality types, they're *practices*. Anyone can learn them; teams can be designed around them. The book is built on an 8-year study of 5,000+ executives and ~100 founders/leaders of disruptive companies (Bezos, Jobs, Lafley, Marc Benioff, Pierre Omidyar, etc.).

The discovery skills can be cultivated systematically. The 1/3 nature, 2/3 nurture finding overturned the "born innovator" myth.

## The five discovery skills

Four cognitive skills, organized around one **catalyst skill**:

### 1. Associating (the catalyst)

The cognitive ability to connect questions, problems, or ideas from unrelated fields. Without associating, the other four skills produce nothing — they collect inputs that never combine. Examples: Steve Jobs's calligraphy class informing typography on the Mac; Pierre Omidyar pairing collectibles trading with internet auctions to seed eBay.

Practice: deliberate input from unrelated fields. Force the question "what other domain has solved a structurally similar problem?"

### 2. Questioning

Innovators ask "Why?" "Why not?" "What if?" at rates 50–100% higher than non-innovators. They challenge assumed constraints, reverse premises, and live in the discomfort of unanswered questions.

Practice:
- **Q&A storming** — generate 50 questions about a problem before any solutions
- **Constraint flipping** — what if the opposite were true?
- **Five whys** to root cause
- **The Osborn list** — substitute, combine, adapt, modify, put to other uses, eliminate, reverse

### 3. Observing

Watch customers, products, environments — in context. Not surveys, not focus groups. Innovators spend 25% more time on direct observation than non-innovators.

Practice:
- **Shadow customers** for 90 minutes in their context
- **Watch the workarounds** — they reveal jobs no product is doing
- **Notice the un-said** — emotional moments, hesitations, embarrassment
- **Anthropology, not surveys** — stay quiet, watch behavior

### 4. Networking

Talk to people *unlike you*. Innovators have heterogeneous networks; their best ideas come from unrelated-domain connections. Most professionals network in homogeneous tribes (same industry, same function); innovators deliberately diverge.

Practice:
- **Diverse-domain interviews** — 1 per month with someone unrelated to your field
- **Conferences outside your industry**
- **Cross-functional meals** in your own org — same lunch, different teams

### 5. Experimenting

Build cheap prototypes. Test ideas in the world, not just in the head. Innovators experiment 50% more than non-innovators — and *learn* from invalidations rather than retreating.

Practice:
- **Pretotyping** (Savoia) — fake the experience before building it
- **Pilots in a corner of the business** before scaling
- **Personal experiments** — physical, behavioral, professional ("I'm going to try X for 30 days")

## The DNA framework (organizational)

Beyond individual skills, innovative companies design *organizational* systems for the five skills:

- **People** — hire and rotate for discovery skill, not just competence at execution
- **Processes** — Q&A storming, observation field trips, experimentation slack time, cross-functional rotation
- **Philosophies** — innovation as a belief, not a department; safety to question and experiment

Apple, Amazon, P&G under Lafley, Salesforce under Benioff, Intuit are profiled as DNA-deep organizations.

## The 1/3 nature, 2/3 nurture finding

Twin studies estimated ~33% of innovation behavior is genetic, ~66% learned. This was the book's big empirical contribution — innovation isn't a fixed trait. The implication: hire for discovery skill, design environments that develop it.

## Updated preface (2019 edition)

The 2019 reissue adds: pace of change since 2011, AI's impact on discovery work, the rise of "compete-by-experiment" cultures. The five skills are unchanged in substance; the practices have evolved to faster cycles, more digital tooling, and more emphasis on networked discovery.

## Three concrete experiments worth replicating

- **Q&A storming exercise** — 30 minutes; generate 50 questions about a known problem; rank by which would change the most if answered. Reliably surfaces non-obvious framings.
- **30-day observation log** — pick a category; record one observation per day in context. After 30 days, synthesize. Often produces 2–3 patentable insights for category newcomers.
- **Constraint-flip workshop** — list current operating constraints; for each, ask "what if the opposite were true?" Forces re-examination of dogma.
