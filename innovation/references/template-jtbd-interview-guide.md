# JTBD Switch Interview Guide — Template

Working interview guide for Jobs-to-be-Done switch interviews. Anchored on the Klement / Moesta switch-interview methodology (Christensen lineage), with the four-forces framework for synthesis.

## Interview goal

Understand the **last time** the customer hired a new solution and fired an old one — *for the job in question*. Not opinions, not hypotheticals. Real switch behavior.

The output is a **circumstance map**: what was happening that made the customer stop using their previous solution and pick a new one.

## Recruiting

- Recruit customers who switched in the last **6–12 months** (recall fades fast after 18 months)
- Mix segments: 4–5 per archetype, 2–3 archetypes
- Include "considered but didn't switch" (status-quo'd) — reveals switching anxiety patterns
- 12–16 interviews recruited to net 8–10 useful

## Interview structure (90 minutes)

### Opening (5 minutes)

> *"I'm researching how people make decisions like the one you made when you started using [new solution]. There are no right or wrong answers — I want to understand your actual experience. We'll focus on a specific switch you made. Can I record this?"*

Do not pitch the product. Do not signal what you hope to hear.

### Phase 1 — First Thought (10 minutes)

Goal: pin the moment when the customer first realized something needed to change.

Prompts:
- "Take me back to when you first thought you needed something different. When was that?"
- "What was happening in your work / life at the time?"
- "What were you using before? How long had you been using it?"
- "What event or moment made you start thinking about a change?"

Listen for:
- The **trigger event** (specific, datable)
- The **circumstance** (work pressure, life change, regulatory change, team change, technology shift)
- The **emotional state** (frustrated, anxious, embarrassed, ambitious)

### Phase 2 — Passive Looking (15 minutes)

Goal: what did the customer notice but not act on, and for how long?

Prompts:
- "After you had that first thought, what did you do — or not do?"
- "Did you notice [category] solutions in your environment? What did you think when you saw them?"
- "How long was it between first thinking about it and starting to actively look?"
- "What were you waiting for?"

Listen for:
- **Passive looking duration** — often weeks or months
- **Anchoring solutions** — what they noticed
- **Anxiety of switching** — why they weren't acting yet
- **Habit of the present** — what made the old solution still livable

### Phase 3 — Active Looking (20 minutes)

Goal: when did the customer start actively searching, and what triggered that shift?

Prompts:
- "What changed that made you actively start looking?"
- "Where did you look? Who did you talk to?"
- "What were you trying to figure out?"
- "What other solutions did you consider?"
- "What did you rule out, and why?"

Listen for:
- The **second trigger** (often a specific incident — escalation, embarrassment, deadline)
- **Consideration set** (competitors, adjacent solutions, doing nothing, building it themselves)
- **Decision criteria** as they emerged
- **Sources of trust** (who they listened to)

### Phase 4 — Decision (25 minutes)

Goal: what tipped the customer to commit?

Prompts:
- "Walk me through the moment you decided. What were you thinking?"
- "What pushed you toward [new solution]?"
- "What pulled you toward it — what did you imagine your life being like?"
- "What anxieties did you have about switching?"
- "What kept you tied to [old solution]?"
- "Who else was involved in the decision? What did they think?"

Listen for the **four forces**:
- **Push of the situation** — what was making the present untenable
- **Pull of the new solution** — what was making the new solution attractive
- **Anxiety of the new** — fears about switching
- **Habit of the present** — what made the present hard to leave

### Phase 5 — First Use (15 minutes)

Goal: the actual moment of progress (or disappointment).

Prompts:
- "What was the first thing you did with [new solution]?"
- "What were you hoping would happen?"
- "What actually happened?"
- "How was that different from what [old solution] did?"
- "How did you feel about the switch in the first 30 days?"

Listen for:
- The **moment of progress** (the actual job-to-be-done outcome)
- **Surprises** (positive and negative)
- **Adoption friction**

### Phase 6 — Ongoing Use (10 minutes)

Goal: how the customer's life / work has changed.

Prompts:
- "What's different about how you work / live since the switch?"
- "What still isn't quite right?"
- "What would make it perfect?"
- "Would you recommend it? Why / why not?"

Listen for:
- **Sustained progress** vs. honeymoon
- **Adjacent unmet needs** (the next switch)
- **Recommendation drivers** (loyalty signal)

### Closing (2 minutes)

> *"Last question — is there anything I should have asked but didn't? Anything that didn't come up that mattered to your decision?"*

Often the most useful answer of the whole interview.

## The four forces — synthesis framework

After each interview, plot:

```
            ANXIETY OF NEW (-)
                  │
                  │
PUSH OF SITUATION │  PULL OF NEW SOLUTION
       (+)        │       (+)
        ──────────┼──────────►
                  │
                  │
                  │
            HABIT OF PRESENT (-)
                  │
                  ▼
```

For each interview, capture:

- **Push** — specific events, frustrations, embarrassments
- **Pull** — what the new solution promised
- **Anxiety** — fears, switching costs, social risk
- **Habit** — comfort, sunk cost, learned workflow

A switch happens when **Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit**. If the Anxiety + Habit side is higher, customers status-quo. Innovation often fails by amplifying Pull when the right move was reducing Anxiety.

## Discipline rules

**Do:**
- Ask about specific recent experiences, not hypotheticals
- Stay in the past tense; resist drift into present-tense product opinions
- Dig into "why" 3–4 layers deep
- Note the customer's *language* (not your paraphrase) for use in messaging
- Capture moments of friction, embarrassment, and emotional weight

**Don't:**
- Pitch the product or signal what you hope to hear
- Ask "would you pay for…" — answers are notoriously biased
- Suggest solutions during discovery
- Aggregate jobs across customers too early; preserve circumstance distinctions

## Synthesis output

After 8–10 interviews, produce:

1. **Job statements** — *"When [circumstance], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]"* — 3–5 distinct
2. **Force map** — push/pull/anxiety/habit per archetype
3. **Trigger event taxonomy** — what makes customers switch
4. **Consideration set inventory** — what they considered (competitors, adjacent solutions, status quo, build-themselves)
5. **Anxiety inventory** — what almost stopped them; what messaging needs to address
6. **Customer language deck** — verbatim phrases for use in copy and product
