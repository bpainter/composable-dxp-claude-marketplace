# Why Digital Transformations Fail — Tony Saldanha (2019)

**Source digest** for the innovation plugin. Cross-referenced by `consulting-digital-strategist`. Cite as "(Saldanha, *Why Digital Transformations Fail*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

(Note: depending on the volume in the vault, this may be Tushman/Anand's edited collection of the same name. The frameworks summarized below cover both — Saldanha's five-stage maturity model and Tushman/Anand's failure-pattern diagnostics. Adjust citations to match the actual source.)

## Thesis

70–80% of digital transformations fail. The failure pattern is consistent and structural, not technological: programs are too big, too long, not portfolio-shaped, lack learning loops, treat technology as the deliverable, skip culture change, and lose sponsorship before they finish. The book offers a stage-based maturity model and a prescriptive set of practices that change the odds.

## The five stages of digital transformation maturity (Saldanha)

1. **Foundation** — basic IT modernization; the cost-out projects; "digital" means digitized
2. **Siloed** — pockets of digital innovation; no enterprise coordination; champions lonely
3. **Partially Synchronized** — enterprise digital strategy emerges; some integration; CDO often appointed
4. **Fully Synchronized** — digital is the way the business operates; integrated ops, talent, data
5. **Living DNA** — continuous re-invention; experimentation embedded; capability sustained beyond any single program

Most enterprises stall between Stage 2 and Stage 3 — pockets of digital innovation without enterprise integration. The book's central applied insight: **most "DT failures" are stage-3-stuck programs, not stage-1 failures.**

## Why most programs fail (the recurring patterns)

1. **Big-bang programs** — too long (3–5 years), too big ($100M+), no learning loops; by year 2 the world has changed
2. **Tech-led, not business-led** — "implement the new platform" framed as the deliverable; outcome metrics weak
3. **No portfolio shape** — every initiative treated as H1 (efficiency / cost-out); no H2/H3 bets
4. **Centralized decision-making** — slow; out of touch; can't run experiments
5. **Talent gap unacknowledged** — hire a "Chief Digital Officer," ignore the 5,000 people who actually do the work
6. **Culture change skipped** — assume new tools will produce new behaviors; they don't
7. **No success criteria** — "transform" defined fuzzily; everyone claims success when nothing changed

## The five disciplines (prescriptive)

Saldanha's prescription for changing the odds:

1. **Establish a sustainable digital transformation methodology** — staged, iterative, learning-loop-driven; not big-bang
2. **Drive ownership and alignment** — distributed; clear decision rights; CDO as enabler not gatekeeper
3. **Develop digital talent and culture** — hire/build/borrow mix; explicit upskilling; rotation
4. **Drive iterative change with discipline** — quarterly portfolio reviews; kill discipline; stage-gate where appropriate
5. **Deliver enterprise change** — full-org reach, not pockets; cross-functional integration

## How to apply — the central reframe

Replace "the digital transformation" (singular, monolithic) with **"the digital portfolio"** — a structured set of bets across H1/H2/H3 with horizon-appropriate governance. This is the central move that connects this book to the rest of the innovation plugin.

| Horizon | DT framing | Governance | Metrics |
|---|---|---|---|
| H1 | Defend / extend the core | Stage-gate | NPV, payback, NRR |
| H2 | Build emerging digital businesses | Discovery-driven | Pilot adoption, unit economics |
| H3 | Create new digital business models | Real options | Option value, capability |

Most DT programs are 100% H1 dressed as transformation. They modernize foundations and call it transformation, then can't explain why nothing transformed.

## The CDO trap

A recurring failure pattern: hiring a Chief Digital Officer with responsibility but without authority. The CDO becomes a fall guy when the transformation stalls. The book's prescription:

- CDO as *enabler* of distributed digital capability, not central gatekeeper
- Decision rights pushed to product / domain teams with clear interfaces
- CDO charter explicit about authority, not just responsibility
- Reporting line that gives the CDO actual leverage (CEO, not CIO)

## Three patterns worth replicating

- **Maturity-stage diagnostic** — score the org against the 5-stage model; identify where it's stuck
- **DT failure-pattern audit** — diagnose which of 7 patterns the current program is replicating
- **Reframe to portfolio** — replace "the transformation" with H1/H2/H3 bets at the strategy doc level
