# Design a Better Business — Patrick van der Pijl, Justin Lokitz, Lisa Kay Solomon (2016)

**Source digest** for the innovation plugin. Cite as "(van der Pijl et al., *Design a Better Business*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

Strategy and innovation work isn't well-served by traditional planning processes — it's served by structured *visual tools* that compress complex thinking, surface assumptions, and create shared understanding across teams. The book is a tool catalog: ~50 canvases, frameworks, and visual methods for the front end of innovation work. Companion to Strategyzer's Business Model Canvas / Value Proposition Design.

## Core tool families

### Discovery / context

- **Context Map** — external forces (customer trends, technology, competition, regulatory, economic) shaping the strategy
- **Trend Canvas** — name a trend, capture evidence, implications, opportunity
- **Customer Journey Canvas** — current-state journey with emotional weight per step
- **Empathy Map** — what customers think, feel, see, hear, say, do

### Vision / direction

- **Cover Story Vision** — imagine the magazine cover 5 years out; what does it say?
- **Strategy Sketch** — single-image visual strategy
- **Storyline Canvas** — narrative arc for the strategy

### Ideation / generation

- **How-Might-We Canvas** — convert problem statements into generative questions
- **Crazy 8s** — 8 ideas in 8 minutes per person; force quantity past the obvious
- **SCAMPER** — substitute, combine, adapt, modify/magnify, put to other use, eliminate, reverse
- **Reversal** — flip the problem statement; brainstorm against the inverse

### Prototyping / validation

- **Prototyping Canvas** — what to learn, how, what to build, what success looks like
- **Test Card** (Strategyzer) — hypothesis, test, success criteria, action threshold
- **Learning Card** (Strategyzer) — what we believed, what we observed, what we learned, what we'll do
- **Validation Sequence** — riskiest assumption first

### Business model / capture

- **Business Model Canvas** (Osterwalder, referenced) — 9-block model
- **Value Proposition Canvas** (Osterwalder, referenced) — customer pains/gains/jobs ↔ pain relievers/gain creators
- **Lean Canvas** (Maurya, referenced) — startup-adapted BMC

## The five-step design process

Van der Pijl's overarching framing for innovation work:

1. **Prepare** — clarify intent, set up the team, gather inputs
2. **Point of view** — develop a strong perspective on the opportunity
3. **Understand** — research the customer, market, context
4. **Ideate** — generate options, diverge before converge
5. **Prototype + validate** — build cheap probes, test, iterate

Loops back; not linear. The book's contribution is treating each stage as having explicit visual tools rather than narrative deliverables.

## The "tool catalog" stance

The book's overarching argument: innovation teams underuse structured visual tools. Most strategy / innovation work happens in PowerPoint and prose, which:

- Hides assumptions inside dense slides
- Prevents collaborative editing
- Makes synthesis hard

Visual tools (canvases on walls, in workshops, in digital whiteboards):

- Externalize assumptions
- Enable collaborative editing
- Compress complex thinking

This stance pairs with the Discovery Coach's facilitation work and the Lab Architect's studio environment design.

- **Test Card / Learning Card pair** (Strategyzer) — the cleanest hypothesis-test cadence available
- **How-Might-We Canvas** — converts problem statements into generative ideation prompts
- **Context Map** — surfaces external forces; useful as a five-domains-style scan (cf. Rogers)
