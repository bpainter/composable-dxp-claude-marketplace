# Ecosystem mapping

Drawn from Risdon & Quattlebaum (*Orchestrating Experiences*, ch. 3) and the stakeholder-mapping methods in Stickdorn et al.

An ecosystem map shows the actors, channels, value flows, and dependencies surrounding an experience. It's the artifact that converts "we make a product for users" into "we participate in a system, and these are the other participants."

## When to use this

- Scoping a new initiative — before journey mapping, to figure out which actors actually need to be in the journey.
- Diagnosing a stuck experience — the failure is often in a relationship or handoff the team hasn't named.
- Strategic positioning — where in the ecosystem does the offer create disproportionate value, and where is it a participant among many?
- Cross-functional alignment — the map names every team that touches the experience, even teams the lead doesn't normally talk to.

## What goes on the map

- **Actors.** Customers, prospects, employees (by role), partners, regulators, suppliers, third-party platforms, communities, competitors.
- **Channels.** Where actors interact — web, app, store, call, email, social, third-party marketplace, in-person event.
- **Information flows.** What each actor sends and receives. (Often the most surfacing layer — "marketing emails go out, but where does the response data go?")
- **Value flows.** Money, service, attention, data, reputation. Each flow has a direction.
- **Dependencies.** Where one actor cannot move until another acts. The points where the system stalls.
- **Boundaries.** What's inside our control, what's adjacent, what's external. Useful for scoping.

## How to build one

```
1. Put the customer at the center. Resist starting with the company logo.

2. Working outward, name every actor whose action shapes the customer's
   experience. Frontline staff, support, ops, sales, partners, regulators,
   adjacent product teams.

3. For each actor, list the channels they touch and the information / value
   flows in and out.

4. Mark dependencies — arrows where actor A's action is required before
   actor B can move. These are the system's chokepoints.

5. Annotate the map with what's broken: handoff failures, missing flows,
   actors that should be talking and aren't, redundant channels.

6. Identify leverage. Where would changing one flow or relationship
   disproportionately improve the customer's experience?

7. Use it. Pin it in the room. Refer to it during decisions. If it
   only lives in a deck, it's not an ecosystem map; it's an org chart.
```

## Three useful patterns

**Customer-centered ecosystem.** Customer at the middle, actors arranged by directness of impact. Best for diagnosing experience gaps. Use most of the time.

**Service ecosystem.** The service offering at the center, actors arranged by their role in delivering it. Best for service blueprinting handoff conversations.

**Value-flow ecosystem.** Value (money, attention, data) tracked across the system. Best for strategic positioning conversations and pricing/packaging design.

## What it isn't

- **Not an org chart.** An ecosystem map crosses organizational boundaries. Half the actors are typically outside the company.
- **Not a journey map.** Journey is sequential and customer-perspective; ecosystem is structural and system-perspective. They complement each other; one is not the other.
- **Not a service blueprint.** Blueprint is the operational view of one service path. Ecosystem is the system view of all paths and actors.

## Common failure modes

- **Org-chart ecosystem.** Only the company appears. The customer ends up as a recipient, not a participant. Useless.
- **Too many actors.** Forty boxes makes the map a wall poster nobody uses. Aim for eight to fifteen named actors. If more are needed, group by function.
- **No flows.** Boxes without arrows show who exists but not what's happening. The flows are where the insight lives.
- **Static maps.** Ecosystems shift. A two-year-old map is probably wrong about partners and channels.
- **Maps that don't change behavior.** If nobody refers to the map after the workshop, it didn't earn its keep. Make a decision against it within a week of building it.
