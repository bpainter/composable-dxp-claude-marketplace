# Experience principles

Drawn from Risdon & Quattlebaum (*Orchestrating Experiences*, ch. 6) and Stickdorn et al. (*This Is Service Design Thinking*, the five principles).

Experience principles are the shared "DNA" a team uses to evaluate every touchpoint, prototype, and decision. They are not platitudes ("delight the customer"). They are constraints with teeth.

## When to write experience principles

- After research and current-state mapping, before generating ideas. Principles screen ideas — without them, every idea looks valid.
- When a team is shipping touchpoints that work in isolation but feel disjointed across the journey.
- When stakeholders disagree about what "good" looks like and the conversation keeps cycling.

## What makes a principle useful

A principle is useful if it can rule out work that's already on the table.

- **Specific to this experience.** "Be helpful" applies to anything; doesn't help. "Tell the user what's about to happen before they commit" rules out surprise charges, blind handoffs, ambiguous CTAs.
- **Actionable across functions.** Same principle constrains design, content, ops, and engineering. If only marketing can act on it, it's a brand value, not an experience principle.
- **Tied to evidence.** "Founders said they're nervous about hidden costs" → principle: "Show price implications before they appear on a screen the user can't go back from."
- **Testable.** Can you point at a touchpoint and say yes, this honors the principle, or no, it doesn't? If not, rewrite.

## Format

```
**Principle:** [One sentence. Active voice. Constrains design.]

**Why this matters:** [The user evidence behind it. One or two lines.]

**What honoring this looks like:** [2–4 examples across channels — web, app, email, support, in-person.]

**What violating this looks like:** [2–3 anti-examples. Often more useful than the positive.]
```

## Worked example

```
**Principle:** Show the price implication of a choice before the user commits to it.

**Why this matters:** Founders in our research repeatedly described hidden cost
moments (legal fees, state filing surcharges, ongoing compliance costs) as the
single biggest source of distrust. Three respondents used the word "trapped."

**What honoring this looks like:**
- Formation flow displays state filing fee + service fee on the entity-selection
  step, not at checkout.
- Renewal email shows next-year cost increase 60 days out, not on the invoice.
- Support agents quote a price range before a call to legal counsel begins.

**What violating this looks like:**
- "Get started for free" CTAs that lead to required paid steps.
- Surprise add-on costs at submission.
- Vague pricing pages that require a sales call to get a number.
```

## Five service-design principles (Stickdorn)

A useful upstream check before drafting your own. Every experience principle should be consistent with these.

1. **User-centred.** Designed through the user's eyes, not the org chart's.
2. **Co-creative.** All stakeholders involved in the design — including frontline staff and the customer.
3. **Sequencing.** Visualized as a sequence of related actions, not as a static moment.
4. **Evidencing.** Intangible service made visible through artifacts (receipts, summaries, status updates).
5. **Holistic.** The entire environment of the service — backstage, frontstage, partners, physical space — considered.

If your experience principles violate any of these, rewrite the principles, not the framework.

## How many principles

Four to seven. Three is usually too few to cover the experience. More than seven and the team won't remember them in the moment a decision is being made.

## Common failure modes

- **Brand values dressed as principles.** "We're bold." That's voice, not a constraint.
- **Untestable principles.** "Make customers happy." Cannot rule anything out.
- **Principles without anti-examples.** Without the negative case, principles are inert.
- **Principles that contradict the operating reality.** "Always offer a human." Then explain how, with current staffing, that scales.
- **Principles imposed top-down.** Principles co-created with frontline staff stick. Principles handed down as a brand exercise die in a deck.
