# Relationship Selling Playbook

**Reference guide for sales-bd-strategist: relationship-based selling for professional services, executive engagement, land-and-expand strategy, cross-sell tactics, QBR preparation, and proposal-to-close execution.**

---

## 1. Relationship-Based Selling for Professional Services

Professional services differ fundamentally from transactional sales. Clients buy the people and the approach, not a product. Trust is earned over time.

### Core Principles

**Trust Before Sale:**
- Relationships precede revenue; invest in relationships with no immediate expectation of close
- Value-add first: Share insights, make introductions, ask thoughtful questions—before you pitch
- Consistency: Show up at quarterly business reviews, annual reviews, relevant industry events
- Credibility: Understand their business deeply; don't bluff

**Sell Outcomes, Not Services:**
- Never lead with "We do digital transformation."
- Lead with "We helped [Vertical] reduce platform sprawl from 12 to 3, cutting maintenance costs by 40%."
- Tie every recommendation to their measurable business outcome

**Stakeholder-Centric Selling:**
- Different stakeholders care about different outcomes
- CFO cares about ROI and cost; CTO cares about capability and risk; CEO cares about strategic impact
- Customize your message by audience; don't use one pitch for everyone

**Authenticity Over Aggression:**
- Consultative approach beats aggressive closing
- Admit what you don't know; refer to experts; don't over-promise
- If you're not the right fit, say so; you'll earn more respect

### Selling Model: Problem-Led, Not Solution-Led

**Step 1: Understand Their Problem**
- Ask before you pitch
- "What's keeping you up at night about your commerce platform?"
- Listen for: pain points, business impact, timeline urgency, stakeholder alignment

**Step 2: Validate with Evidence**
- "We're seeing this pattern across retail companies right now: older platforms are becoming maintenance-heavy and slow innovation. Is that resonating?"
- Case study: "We worked with [Retailer]; similar situation. Here's what we did and the outcome."

**Step 3: Propose an Approach**
- "Based on what you described, here's how we'd approach it: phased; minimize disruption; focus on your highest-impact initiative first."
- Avoid: "Here's our standard engagement model."

**Step 4: Quantify the Outcome**
- "If we achieve our success metrics, you'd reduce time-to-market by 6 weeks and cut platform maintenance costs by 25%."
- Tie to their metrics, not ours

**Step 5: Get Commitment to Next Steps**
- "What would it take for you to move forward? What questions do you still have?"
- Not: "When can we start?"

## 2. Executive Engagement & Stakeholder Mapping

### Stakeholder Map Template

**For complex deals, map by role and influence:**

| Stakeholder | Title | Department | Power | Interest | Sentiment | Engagement Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Name] | VP Tech | IT | High | High | Aligned | Monthly strategy calls; demos; early feedback |
| [Name] | CFO | Finance | High | Medium | Neutral | ROI case study; cost modeling; quarterly updates |
| [Name] | Director Commerce | Ops | Medium | High | Aligned | Working sessions; implementation planning; job aids |
| [Name] | CISO | Security | High | Low | Unknown | Risk/compliance briefing; security FAQ; reference |

**Power-Interest Grid Strategy:**

- **High Power, High Interest** (Manage Closely)
  - Monthly touchpoints, not quarterly
  - Incorporate feedback into recommendations
  - Escalate issues immediately
  - Provide strategic input on trade-offs
  - Example: Sponsor, CFO on large deal

- **High Power, Low Interest** (Keep Satisfied)
  - Keep informed; don't bore them
  - Exception-based escalation only
  - Annual relationship check-in
  - Understand their incentives (what makes them say yes?)
  - Example: CISO, Legal on backend decision

- **Low Power, High Interest** (Keep Informed)
  - Involve in working sessions
  - Gather their feedback; they're ground truth
  - Empower them as advocates
  - Regular updates (bi-weekly)
  - Example: IT operations, end-users

- **Low Power, Low Interest** (Monitor)
  - Minimal communication
  - Annual check-in
  - Escalate only on major changes
  - Example: Facilities, unaffected departments

### Executive Presence & Engagement Tactics

**Earning Executive Respect:**

1. **Do Your Homework**
   - Know their business, financials, strategy
   - Know competitive landscape
   - Understand their role and incentives
   - Don't ask questions you could Google

2. **Ask Insightful Questions**
   - "How does this initiative align with your 3-year strategy?"
   - "What's the board's view on timeline?"
   - "What would constitute failure for you?"
   - Not: "So what's your tech stack?"

3. **Share Relevant Insights**
   - "I was reading your Q3 earnings call; you mentioned platform consolidation. That's a pattern we're seeing across [Vertical]..."
   - Bring intelligence, not pitches
   - One insight per meeting, well-researched

4. **Recommend with Confidence**
   - Executives expect you to have a point of view
   - "Here's what I'd do: [Approach], because [Rationale]. Here's the risk I'd watch: [Risk]."
   - Avoid: "Well, it depends on..."

5. **Focus on Outcomes, Not Activities**
   - Not: "We'll do 8 weeks of discovery, then..."
   - Yes: "We'll have a detailed roadmap in 6 weeks. By month 4, you'll see first tangible results."

6. **Respect Their Time**
   - Every meeting should add value
   - 30-min calls should be 25 minutes, not 30
   - Send agenda 24 hours ahead
   - No slides unless they're essential

7. **Follow Up Quickly**
   - Decision needed? Bring recommendation and next steps, not questions
   - "Here's what we discussed. Here's my recommendation. Here's the path forward."

### Managing the Skeptic

**Every complex deal has a skeptic.** It's often a key stakeholder (CISO, Finance, competing practice head). Don't ignore them.

**Approach:**
1. **Identify them early** → Who's the likely objector? Who's been burned before?
2. **Understand their concern** → "I sense some concern about [X]. What's driving that?"
3. **Respect their caution** → "That's a fair risk to worry about. Here's how we'd mitigate it..."
4. **Bring them along** → Use them to validate your approach, not fight it
5. **Give them credit** → "We incorporated your feedback on [X]; it made this stronger"

**Example dialogue:**
- Skeptic: "Our last vendor locked us in. I don't trust outsiders."
- You: "That's a legitimate concern, and frankly, it should be. Here's how we're different: [X]. Want to talk to a reference who had the same worry?"

## 3. Land-and-Expand Playbook

**Land-and-Expand converts small wins into large, multi-practice relationships.**

### Land: Win the First Engagement

**Engagement Profile:**
- Smaller scope ($500K–$1.5M)
- Lower risk; clear success metrics
- Solves a real problem; builds trust
- Often single-practice (Build, Data, or Strategy)
- 3–6 month timeline; quick wins

**Positioning for Land:**
- Not a loss leader; solid margin
- Designed to uncover next opportunities
- Team composition: Include one relationship owner who will shepherd the account long-term
- Success metrics: Clear deliverables + relationship metrics (e.g., "Establish quarterly executive reviews")

**Execution Excellence (Land Phase):**
- Exceed SOW commitments (do 1 extra thing for free if it adds value)
- Over-communicate; no surprises (good or bad)
- Invest in the relationship: quarterly reviews with exec sponsor, not just project status
- Document wins: "We reduced time-to-market by 6 weeks" (ammunition for next conversation)
- Identify emerging needs: "While we're doing this, I'm noticing your CMS is becoming a bottleneck. Might be worth a conversation after this wraps."

### Identify Expansion Opportunities

**During Land Engagement, Map Expansion Vectors:**

1. **Same Practice, Larger Scope**
   - Current: "Build the storefront"
   - Expansion: "Build storefront + integration layer + data pipeline"
   - Trigger: Client sees value; wants broader solution

2. **Different Practice, Same Problem Domain**
   - Current: "Implement composable commerce"
   - Expansion: "Add Data practice for personalization/analytics"
   - Expansion: "Add AI/ML for recommendations"
   - Trigger: Client realized connected problems exist

3. **Different Practice, Different Business Unit**
   - Current: "Modernize e-commerce for Retail division"
   - Expansion: "Strategic planning for Finance division's digital transformation"
   - Trigger: Success in one unit creates credibility for others

4. **Account-Wide Consolidation**
   - Current: "Multiple Slalom teams working separately"
   - Expansion: "Consolidated relationship; steering committee; coordinated strategy"
   - Trigger: Client realizes fragmented approach; wants integrated vendor

**Expansion Identification Framework:**

| Dimension | Question | Expansion Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| **Stakeholders** | What other departments have similar problems? | Target other BUs/divisions |
| **Process** | What connected processes need addressing? | Additional practices (Data, Strategy) |
| **Technology** | What other systems are causing friction? | Broader platform strategy |
| **Outcomes** | What's the next business outcome they care about? | Phase 2 or new initiative |
| **Organizational Context** | Any board mandates, new executives, M&A activity? | Refresh account plan; new opportunities |

### Expand: Move from Single-Practice to Multi-Practice

**Expansion Sequence:**

1. **Quantify the Success of Land**
   - "We hit our targets: 40% faster time-to-market, 99.9% uptime, 25% cost savings."
   - Share the story with the broader organization (internal newsletter, case study)

2. **Identify the Expansion Opportunity**
   - "While delivering this, we noticed your analytics and personalization capabilities are disconnected from the platform."
   - Tie to their business outcome: "Better data → better personalization → higher conversion"

3. **Develop the Expansion Business Case**
   - Size: $1.5M–$3M engagement
   - Timeline: 6–12 months (slightly longer; more complex)
   - Team: Land relationship owner + new practice partner (Data, Strategy, etc.)
   - Outcome: Quantified impact (e.g., "3% lift in conversion; $2M incremental revenue")

4. **Social the Idea (Before the Proposal)**
   - Sponsor conversation: "We've delivered on the storefront. I have an idea for the next phase that would unlock personalization at scale."
   - Economic buyer conversation: "This would require [X] investment and deliver [Y] return. Here's the case."
   - Champion conversation: "Help me shape the approach; who else needs to be involved?"

5. **Formalize the Proposal**
   - Problem restatement: "You've mastered composable commerce. The next challenge is data integration and personalization."
   - Approach: Phased; separate work stream or integrated?
   - Team: Cross-practice (Land practice + Expansion practice)
   - Success metrics: Tied to their next business outcome

6. **Close the Expansion**
   - Separate SOW or addendum? (Usually separate)
   - Governance: How do you manage two (or more) concurrent work streams?
   - Executive sponsor: Ideally same person for both (continuity)

## 4. Cross-Sell & Up-Sell Identification

### Cross-Sell: Selling a Different Practice to Existing Client

**Trigger Points:**
- Client mentions pain point outside current engagement scope
- Discovery reveals adjacent problem
- New executive joins client; brings new agenda
- Board mandate or strategic initiative announced
- Competitive threat or M&A activity

**Example Cross-Sell Sequences:**

| Current | Adjacent Problem | Cross-Sell Practice | Why They'll Buy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Build (Composable) | Data integration issues | Data & Analytics | Realized they need data layer for personalization |
| Strategy | Technology risk | Cloud & Infra | Strategic roadmap exposed legacy tech risks |
| Cloud Migration | Legacy apps still tethered | Data & Analytics | Modernization incomplete without data strategy |
| Digital Product | CX frustration | Customer Experience | Product is efficient; experience is fragmented |

**Cross-Sell Process:**

1. **Identify the Problem** (from Land engagement or account intelligence)
2. **Validate with Client** (don't assume; ask)
3. **Develop Business Case** (quantify impact; size engagement)
4. **Introduce Practice Partner** (relationship owner + new practice lead)
5. **Co-Sell the Opportunity** (not transactional handoff)
6. **Execute Integrated Solution** (coordinated roadmap; single steering committee)

### Up-Sell: Selling More of the Same Practice

**Trigger Points:**
- Current scope is working; client wants more
- Scope creep in original engagement (opportunity to formalize expansion)
- New initiative announced in same problem domain
- Client sees ROI; ready to invest further

**Example Up-Sell Sequences:**

| Current | Client Outcome | Up-Sell Angle |
|---|---|---|
| Build Platform | "This is working great" | Expand to adjacent channels/regions; same platform |
| Data Lake | "Our analytics are better" | Move from BI to AI/ML; same data foundation |
| Strategy | "Recommendations are solid" | Execution support; implement the roadmap |

**Up-Sell Positioning:**
- Not: "We have more services."
- Yes: "You've succeeded here. The next phase is [outcome]. We'd extend our engagement to deliver it."

## 5. QBR (Quarterly Business Review) Framework

**QBRs are relationship-investment time, not status-report time. Done well, they surface next opportunities.**

### QBR Attendees

- **Slalom:** Account lead + practice partner + exec sponsor (MD or Partner)
- **Client:** Sponsor/champion + economic buyer + key user stakeholders (optional but valuable)
- **Not:** Project manager + operator folks (use separate project health calls)

### QBR Agenda (60 Minutes)

| Time | Segment | Owner | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5 min | Welcome & Agenda | Account Lead | Set tone; confirm next steps focus |
| 10 min | Slalom-Client Relationship Health | Exec Sponsor | "How are we doing as partners? What should we do more/less of?" |
| 15 min | Engagement Outcomes Review | Practice Partner | Previous quarter deliverables; did we hit success metrics? |
| 15 min | Business Impact | Account Lead | "What's changed because of our engagement? Revenue? Efficiency? Risk reduced?" |
| 10 min | Next Phase / Emerging Opportunities | Account Lead | "Here's what we're seeing; here's what could be next" |
| 5 min | Close & Action Items | Sponsor | Decisions needed; dates for next actions |

### QBR Materials (Prep Before Meeting)

**1. Executive Summary** (1 page)
- Engagement progress: On track? Ahead? Behind?
- Key wins: 2-3 quantified outcomes achieved
- Upcoming: Next phase or emerging opportunity
- Recommendation: What should we do next? (Decision needed)

**2. Outcomes Dashboard**
- Success metrics: Target vs. actual
- Business impact: Quantified results
- Team feedback: Client satisfaction; areas to improve
- Risks: Any issues from previous quarter? Mitigated?

**3. Opportunity Briefing**
- Problem identified: (From land engagement or client intelligence)
- Potential scope: (High-level; not detailed proposal)
- Value/ROI: (Why they should care)
- Timeline: (If we started today, when would we deliver results?)
- Next steps: (What do we need to move forward?)

### QBR Dos & Don'ts

**Do:**
- Bring good news; celebrate wins
- Acknowledge what's not working; don't hide problems
- Ask thoughtful questions about their business
- Listen for emerging problems (future opportunities)
- Come with recommendations, not just questions
- Drive decisions (not just information sharing)

**Don't:**
- Spend 40 minutes on status updates
- Read slides word-for-word
- Invite too many people (dilutes conversation)
- Focus only on your work (connect to their business outcomes)
- Leave without next steps or decisions
- Avoid difficult conversations (feedback, cost overruns)

## 6. Proposal-to-Close Tactics

### Proposal Strategy (Recap & Tactical Elements)

**Proposal Sections (In This Order):**

1. **Executive Summary** (1-2 pages)
   - Problem restatement (shows you listened)
   - Proposed approach (high-level)
   - Expected outcome/ROI
   - Why Slalom (differentiation)

2. **Situation & Problem** (1-2 pages)
   - Their context: "You're a $5B retailer with 12 commerce platforms..."
   - The problem: "Fragmentation is slowing innovation and increasing cost..."
   - Why it matters: "CEO stated goal of 6-week feature time-to-market..."

3. **Proposed Approach** (2-3 pages)
   - Phased (not one big bang)
   - Team composition (names, roles, relevant experience)
   - Timeline (realistic; include dependencies)
   - Success metrics (tied to their KPIs, not ours)

4. **Success Metrics & ROI** (1-2 pages)
   - Quantified outcomes: "Reduce time-to-market from 12 weeks to 6 weeks"
   - Cost impact: "Reduce platform maintenance by 25%"
   - Risk reduction: "Eliminate vendor lock-in"
   - Case study reference: "We achieved this for [Company] in [Vertical]"

5. **Team & References** (1 page)
   - Account lead: [Name], [Relevant experience], [Reference available]
   - Practice partner: [Name], [Relevant experience], [Reference available]
   - Key credentials: [Slalom certifications, awards, relevant expertise]

6. **Commercial Terms** (1 page)
   - Fee structure: Fixed, T&M, or value-based?
   - Timeline: Kick-off through completion
   - Resource model: Embedded? Dedicated? Hybrid?
   - Deliverables: Explicit list (in/out)
   - Payment terms: Net 30? Milestone-based?

7. **Competitive Positioning** (Subtle, Not Confrontational)
   - "Unlike vendors who typically lock clients into proprietary platforms, we use best-of-breed: Salesforce Commerce + Amplience + Contentful..."
   - Avoid: Direct attacks ("Deloitte is slow and expensive")

### Negotiation & Closing Tactics

**Price Negotiation (If They Ask for Discount):**

- **Anchor High:** Start at your ideal price, not your minimum
- **Respond with Trade-Offs:** "We can reduce cost by 20%, but that means [X scope reduction or Y timeline extension]. Which would you prefer?"
- **Value-Based Pricing:** "If you achieve 40% faster launch, let's share the upside. Here's how..."
- **Don't Discount on Price Alone:** Always get something in return (timeline acceleration, expanded scope, reference, equity stake in success)

**Example:**
- Client: "Your price is 15% higher than Deloitte's."
- You: "I understand. Our approach uses best-of-breed, so you're not locked in. Deloitte's is cheaper because it uses their proprietary platform, which you can't easily leave. What's more important to you: lower cost or long-term flexibility?"

**Closing Moves:**

1. **Trial Close** (Early, low-risk)
   - "If we moved forward, would next Monday work for a kickoff planning session?"
   - Response tells you if they're serious or still exploring

2. **Summarize Agreement** (Before signature)
   - "Here's what I'm hearing we agreed on: [Scope], [Timeline], [Cost]. Does that sound right?"
   - Document verbally before sending contract

3. **Identify Decision Blockers** (If stalled)
   - "What would it take for you to move forward? Is it something we can address?"
   - Could be: Legal review, budget approval, competing priority, competing vendor

4. **Escalate Appropriately** (If stalled)
   - If economic buyer is hesitant: Bring in your exec sponsor ("Let me get our Principal involved to align on approach")
   - If legal is blocked: Offer standard terms or common contract language
   - If competing vendor is tight: Bring proof point (reference, case study)

5. **Create Urgency (Ethically)**
   - Legitimate: "If we start in Q2, we'll deliver by Q3 earnings; if Q3, results won't show until Q4."
   - Legitimate: "This team is allocated; if we don't kick off next month, availability shifts."
   - Illegitimate: "This price is only good for 2 weeks." (Don't fabricate urgency)

6. **The Final Ask**
   - "I think this is the right approach for you. Should I send the contract?"
   - Direct close; no hemming
   - Their response tells you if they're ready

### Post-Close: Ensure Smooth Handoff

**Win Logistics:**
- PO number confirmed; funding verified
- Kickoff meeting scheduled
- Project charter drafted
- Reporting structure agreed (who talks to whom)
- Kick-off materials prepared (team introductions, project plan, communication cadence)

**Account Transition:**
- Relationship owner transitions from sales to account management
- Project manager takes operational lead
- Continue exec sponsor engagement (no disappearing after signature)
- Set up first QBR (even if engagement is only 3 months; build relationship habit)

## 7. Pricing & Packaging Strategy

### Pricing Models

| Model | When to Use | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Time & Materials (T&M)** | Uncertain scope; exploratory work | Flexibility; realistic for discovery | Client sees open-ended cost; incentive misalignment |
| **Fixed Fee** | Clear scope; defined deliverables | Price certainty; manages margin | Risk if scope shifts; requires airtight SOW |
| **Value-Based** | Clear ROI; client realizes significant benefit | Aligns incentives; share upside | Requires strong business case; complex to structure |
| **Blended** | Multi-phase (T&M discovery → Fixed build) | Combines flexibility + certainty | More complex to manage |

### Packaging Strategy

**Not All Scopes Should Be One Price:**

**Example Packaging Options:**

Option 1 (Conservative):
- Phase 1: Composable architecture design ($500K; 12 weeks)
- Phase 2: Implementation (T&M; estimated $1.5M)
- Total: $2M (if Phase 2 completes as estimated)

Option 2 (Aggressive/High-Risk):
- Full engagement: Design + Build ($2M; 24 weeks, fixed fee)
- Better for client (price certainty); riskier for you

Option 3 (Value-Based):
- Fee: $1.5M baseline
- If you achieve >6-week improvement in time-to-market: +$250K
- If you achieve >20% cost savings: +$250K
- Upside cap: $2M
- Aligns incentives; both win if success metrics hit

### Discount Guardrails

**Avoid the Spiral:**
- First ask: 10-15% discount → You counter with scope reduction
- Second ask: "Meet me in the middle" → They're testing limits
- Third ask: Disqualify; they're not the right client (margin-conscious, not value-conscious)

**Better Approach:**
- Hold price; adjust scope or timeline
- "We can deliver this in Q3 instead of Q2, and that would reduce cost by 15%."
- "We can reduce the scope to Phase 1 only, and that brings cost to $1.2M."
- Forces trade-off thinking; prevents death by a thousand cuts

## 8. Alliance & Partner-Led Deals

**Sometimes the best deals come through partners (SI partners, technology partners, resellers).**

### Identifying Partner-Led Opportunities

**Partner Categories:**
- **System Integrators** (SI) → Accenture, IBM, Atos; bring end-client relationships
- **Technology Partners** → Salesforce SI partners; AWS; cloud resellers
- **Referral Partners** → Boutique firms who don't compete; refer overflow
- **Solution Providers** → Experts in specific domains (HubSpot partners, Shopify experts)

### Co-Selling Framework

**Roles:**
- **Partner Leads** → Client relationship; overall engagement accountability
- **Slalom Leads** → Technical delivery; specific work streams
- **Shared Sponsor** → Single exec point for steering (ideally rotating or co-led)

**Deal Structure:**
- Partner SOW (if needed): Covers partner's fees and Slalom's role
- Slalom SOW: Detailed work streams, deliverables, success metrics
- Single budget: Easier if partner handles billing; we invoice partner

**Margin Considerations:**
- Partner takes a cut (15-30%, depending on their role)
- You lose margin but gain volume/access
- Evaluate: Is partner-led deal better than no deal?

### Managing Partner Relationships

**Do:**
- Weekly sync during execution (no surprises)
- Exceed their expectations (they'll bring more deals)
- Build relationship at multiple levels (business + operational)
- Clearly define who owns which piece (avoid duplication or gaps)
- Co-invest in pursuit (don't let partner do all the work)

**Don't:**
- Undercut the partner (they'll fire you)
- Sell directly to their client (violates trust; kills future deals)
- Blame them when things go wrong (own your part)
- Disappear after close (relationships span multiple deals)
