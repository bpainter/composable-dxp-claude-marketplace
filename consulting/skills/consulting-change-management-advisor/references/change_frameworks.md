# Change Frameworks: ADKAR, Kotter, Bridges, McKinsey 7-S, and Readiness Assessment

## 1. Prosci ADKAR Model: The Diagnostic Powerhouse

ADKAR is a change curve diagnostic. Use it to *locate* where transformation efforts break, not as a rigid linear path.

### The Five Elements

**Awareness: "Do people understand WHY?"**

Diagnostic questions:
- Can your target audience articulate why change is happening?
- Do they understand the business drivers (market pressure, cost, competitive threat, customer expectation)?
- Is there a burning platform, or does it feel arbitrary?
- Do middle managers understand it well enough to defend it to their teams?

Intervention if stuck:
- Executive town halls + Q&A (not lectures; answer hard questions).
- Manager briefing sessions with talking points (arm them to lead locally).
- Video messaging from sponsor + business case summary (1-pager for all-hands).
- Pulse surveys to gauge understanding; identify misconceptions to correct.

---

**Desire: "Do people WANT to change? Is it safe to want it?"**

Diagnostic questions:
- Do people see personal benefit (WIIFM)? Or does it feel like change for change's sake?
- Are there organizational incentives (bonus, recognition, career path) tied to adoption?
- Do people fear job loss, role change, or loss of status?
- Is there trust in leadership to handle the transition fairly?

Intervention if stuck:
- Reframe the personal narrative. What's in it for *them*? (e.g., "You'll spend less time on manual data entry; more on insight.")
- Address fears directly: job security statements, role transitions, reskilling paths.
- Tie incentives (bonus, rating, promotion criteria) to adoption behaviors.
- Build trust through transparency: "Here's what we know, what we're working on, and what we'll tell you next."

---

**Knowledge: "Can they DO it?"**

Diagnostic questions:
- Do people have the skills to operate the new system/process?
- Is training timely (too early = forgotten; too late = panic)?
- Are there decision trees, job aids, or reference materials available?
- Can people practice in a safe environment before go-live?

Intervention if stuck:
- Role-based training (not generic; tailor to how *this person* will use the system).
- Hands-on labs + sandbox environments for practice.
- Peer coaching + buddy systems (user A helps user B).
- Just-in-time learning (microlearning, videos, chatbots, in-app guidance).
- Decision trees and workflows for common scenarios.

---

**Ability: "Can they perform under real conditions?"**

Diagnostic questions:
- In week 2 post-launch, can users execute their role competently without panic calls?
- Are error rates or workarounds increasing or decreasing over time?
- How much hypercare support is still needed 30 days in?
- Are users building muscle memory, or are they white-knuckling it?

Intervention if stuck:
- Extended hypercare (don't cut off support too early).
- Reinforcement training for high-error populations.
- Process simplification (maybe the process itself is broken, not the training).
- Peer coaching and champions network (redistribute knowledge from power users to struggling users).

---

**Reinforcement: "Does the organization reward the new behavior, or slip back?"**

Diagnostic questions:
- Are leaders visibly using and supporting the new system?
- Do performance metrics reward the new behavior, or the old way?
- Is there celebration of adoption milestones (not just go-live day)?
- Are workarounds killed, or do people get away with them?
- Is the new way becoming "how we do things," or is it still "the new system"?

Intervention if stuck:
- Change incentive structures. If old behavior is still rewarded, people will choose it.
- Celebrate and recognize early adopters and champions.
- Kill workarounds: Make the old way impossible (deactivate access, block manual processes).
- Reinforce through leadership modeling (executives must visibly use new system, not skip it).
- Regular pulse checks (surveys, focus groups) to gauge cultural shift.

## 2. Kotter's 8-Step Change Process

For enterprise-scale, multi-phase transformations. Use this to sequence initiatives and avoid common failure modes.

| Step | Action | Failure Mode | Recovery |
|------|--------|-------------|----------|
| 1. Create Urgency | Articulate the burning platform or market imperative. Avoid fake urgency (loses credibility). | No one believes change is necessary. Status quo bias wins. | Bring customer data, market data, financial data. Show the cost of inaction. |
| 2. Build Coalition | Assemble executives, change champions, respected informal leaders, and even early-stage resisters (to understand their concerns). Not a committee; a movement. | Change is led top-down only. Cynicism spreads. Middle management doesn't defend it. | Invest time in coalition-building before day 1. Include unlikely allies. |
| 3. Form Vision & Initiative | Clear answer to "What does success look like?" and "What's the strategy to get there?" Vision should be inspiring, not just data. | Unclear direction. Competing priorities. People pursuing change in different directions. | Articulate in 1-minute and 30-minute versions. Test it with resisters; refine. |
| 4. Communicate Vision (Relentlessly) | Tell the story 10x more than you think necessary. Multiple channels, multiple voices, multiple formats. | People heard it once. Noise and silence alternate. Different groups hear different messages. | Plan communication calendar (weekly for 6 months, then monthly). Cascade from top. Use managers, peers, and champions to reinforce. |
| 5. Empower Broad-Based Action | Remove structural blockers. Give teams autonomy to act. Celebrate experiments and iteration. | Bureaucracy stalls change. Fearful decision-making. Only HQ can approve anything. | Decentralize authority. Create fast-track approval processes. Shield teams from noise. |
| 6. Generate Short-Term Wins | Fast, visible, credible evidence that change is working. Not go-live day (too far out); earlier. | Cynicism builds. No one believes it's working. Urgency wanes. | Plan quick wins in first 90 days. Celebrate publicly. Tie wins to larger vision. |
| 7. Consolidate Gains & Deepen | Don't declare victory at go-live. Use momentum to go deeper. Process optimization, culture shift, scaling. | "Mission accomplished" followed by relapse to old ways. Training goes away. Leaders move on. | Extend reinforcement for 12+ months post-go-live. Champions network. Sustaining governance. |
| 8. Anchor in Culture | Make the new way "how we do things here." Update stories, rituals, symbols, hiring, promotion criteria. | Change evaporates when leaders turn attention elsewhere. New hires revert to old culture. | Embed in values, hiring profiles, promotion rubrics. Tell success stories. Make old way inefficient. |

## 3. Bridges' Transition Model: The Emotional Journey

Change is cognitive (new skills) + emotional (loss, uncertainty, identity shift). Bridges focuses on the latter.

**Ending** (What's being lost)
- Acknowledge what people are grieving: old tools, old role identity, old relationships, old ways of working.
- Don't skip this or minimize it ("You'll love the new system!"). It reads as dismissive.
- Action: Create space to say goodbye. Reflect on what was good about the old way.

**Neutral Zone** (The messy middle)
- Old way is dying, new way isn't fully alive. Worst time for morale.
- People are running both systems in parallel. Confusion, redundancy, exhaustion.
- Errors spike. Patience wears thin.
- Action: Shorten the neutral zone (fast cutover vs. parallel run). Provide extra support and communication. Celebrate small progress. Normalize the messiness.

**New Beginning** (Energy returns)
- Identity shifts. "I'm good at this now." "This is how we work."
- Energy and engagement return.
- Action: Recognize people's adaptation. Celebrate adoption milestones. Embed new behaviors in systems/structure.

## 4. McKinsey 7-S: Spot Systemic Misalignment Early

Change fails when one element pulls against the others.

| Element | Questions for Change Leaders |
|---------|------|
| **Structure** | Does org structure support the new way? Are approval chains too long? Do silos prevent collaboration the new system enables? |
| **Systems** | Do IT systems integrate? Are manual workarounds baked in? Do compensation, performance mgmt, and incentive systems reward new behaviors? |
| **Skills** | Do people have the skills? Do we need to hire, retrain, reskill? Is there a plan to address gaps? |
| **Staff** | Do we have the right people in key roles? Are there flight risks? Do new roles make sense to people? |
| **Style** | Does leadership behavior model the new way? Are leaders collaborative, data-driven, distributed decision-making? Or command-and-control? |
| **Shared Values** | Does the culture support change? Is risk-taking rewarded or punished? Is collaboration or silos the norm? |
| **Strategy** | Is the change strategy clear? Do we know what success looks like and how to measure it? |

**Red flag**: One element strongly misaligned. Example: New system demands collaboration, but org structure is functional silos + compensation is individual-based + manager is still command-and-control.

## 5. Readiness Assessment Template

Run this 6-12 months before go-live or major change initiation.

### Organizational Readiness Scorecard

**Sponsorship & Leadership (0-10 scale)**
- Sponsor is committed and visible: ___
- Steering committee is aligned: ___
- Senior leadership models new behaviors: ___
- *Average: ___* | Target: 8+

**Organizational Capacity (0-10 scale)**
- Available resources (people, budget, time): ___
- Current change saturation (other initiatives running): ___ (note: 8+ is dangerous)
- Technical readiness (systems integration, data quality): ___
- *Average: ___* | Target: 7+

**Stakeholder Readiness (0-10 scale)**
- % of target population that understands why change is needed: ___
- % that sees personal benefit: ___
- % with required skills or access to training: ___
- *Average: ___* | Target: 6+ (3 months before go-live)

**Culture & Trust (0-10 scale)**
- Leadership credibility/trust score: ___
- Historical success with change: ___
- Risk appetite (willingness to experiment): ___
- *Average: ___* | Target: 6+

## 6. Change Saturation Analysis

How many concurrent initiatives can your org absorb without breaking?

**Formula**: Count all major change initiatives (system implementations, org redesigns, process reengineering, M&A integration, cost-cutting, policy changes, etc.).

- **0-2 concurrent**: Healthy. Organization can focus.
- **3-4 concurrent**: Manageable. Requires strong governance and sequencing.
- **5-6 concurrent**: Red flag. Resource conflicts. Quality suffers. Fatigue sets in.
- **7+**: Dangerous. Plan for slippage, quality issues, talent flight.

**Action**: If saturation > 5, recommend postponing lower-priority initiatives or reducing scope of lower-impact ones.

## 7. Resistance Archetypes & Interventions

Most resistance is rational (I don't see the benefit, I'm afraid, I don't have the skills, the incentive structure punishes me). Address the root, not the symptom.

| Archetype | What They Do | Why (Usually) | Intervention |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| **Silent Resister** | Doesn't speak up; complies outwardly; doesn't use new system; spreads doubt quietly | Fear of being "difficult"; loss of status; doesn't believe it'll work | 1:1 listening sessions; address specific concerns; create safe space to voice doubts |
| **Vocal Skeptic** | Questions everything; points out risks; slow adoption | Protective (wants to do this right); high standards; burned by past changes | Invite to coalition; make their expertise part of design; give them influence, not dismissal |
| **Workaround Artist** | Uses new system minimally; builds manual workarounds to get work done | New system doesn't fit their workflow; training didn't prepare them; incentive structure rewards old way | Simplify process/system; targeted training; kill the workaround (make it impossible) |
| **Flight Risk** | Disengages; job hunting; "I'll leave before I learn this" | Role obsolescence fear; identity loss; too much change too fast; better opportunity elsewhere | Honest conversation about role future; reskilling investment; stay conversation with leadership |
| **Burnout Case** | Exhausted; overwhelmed; adopts slowly because no energy; cynical | Change fatigue (this is change #4 in 18 months); no buffer time; not enough support | Pause non-critical initiatives; extend timelines if possible; provide extended hypercare; coach on resilience |

## 8. Go-Live Support (Hypercare) Model

**Weeks -2 to +4** (intensive support phase)

Structure:
- **War room** (onsite or virtual): senior tech leads, change champions, process experts, escalation lead.
- **Extended hours** (6am-10pm or 24/5 depending on geography/criticality).
- **Tiered escalation**: Level 1 = support desk; Level 2 = power users/super users; Level 3 = war room; Level 4 = vendor/architect.
- **Rapid incident management** (SLA: critical = 1 hour, high = 4 hours).
- **Daily standups** (30 min, 8 am): What's broken? What's the fix? What do we communicate?
- **Pulse surveys** (daily or every other day): Are users in crisis? Are error rates spiking?

**Transition plan**:
- Week 1-2: War room staffed 24/5, all hands on deck.
- Week 3-4: Shift to day-hours support; hand off to BAU support team.
- Week 5-6: Spot-checks; champions network owns escalation.
- Month 2-3: BAU support; champions fill gaps; training reinforcement if needed.

**Success metrics**:
- % critical issues resolved in SLA: 95%+.
- User satisfaction (support satisfaction survey): 7/10+.
- System availability: 99%+ (or SLA target).
- Adoption curve: 70% active users by week 2, 85% by week 4.

## 9. Sustaining Change: Champions Network

Post-go-live (month 3+), shift from central change team to distributed champions network.

**Structure**:
- 5-10% of target population as formal champions (20-30 hours/month during ramp, 5-10 hours/month steady state).
- Representatives from each function/location (geographic/organizational proximity matters).
- Monthly refresher training + peer learning sessions.
- Recognition program (bonus, promotion consideration, public celebration).

**Their role**:
- Go-to person for local questions (before escalating to central support).
- Spot trends ("User feedback is: the reports are too complicated").
- Reinforce adoption and new behaviors.
- Beta test new features or process changes.
- Feedback loop to central team (what's working, what's not).

**Success**: By month 6, 70% of adoption questions are answered by champions, not central team.

## 10. Key Diagnostic Questions for Change Leaders

Before designing change strategy, ask:

1. **Why now?** What's the burning platform, and does the org believe it?
2. **Who leads?** Is the sponsor visible, credible, and committed for the long haul?
3. **What's the impact on people?** Which roles disappear, change, or emerge? What reskilling is needed?
4. **What's the cultural shift required?** (E.g., moving from waterfall to agile, from siloed to collaborative, from report-driven to data-driven.)
5. **What's our capacity?** Other initiatives + BAU workload + change absorption = realistic timeline?
6. **Who might resist, and why?** What's the intervention strategy per group?
7. **What's the proof point?** What quick wins can we generate in the first 90 days?
8. **How do we sustain?** What changes to structure, incentives, culture make the new way stick?
