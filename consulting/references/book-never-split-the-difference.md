# Never Split the Difference — Chris Voss with Tahl Raz (2016)

**Source digest** for the consulting plugin. Page numbers refer to the HarperBusiness ebook edition (no fixed pagination — citations use chapter + topic anchor; supplemental page ranges are the traditional hardcover, ~270 pp.). Cite as "(Voss, *NSTD*, ch. X)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

Voss spent 24 years as the FBI's lead international kidnapping negotiator and watched the Harvard *Getting to Yes* canon — separate the people from the problem, focus on interests not positions, generate win-win options, use objective standards (Fisher & Ury, 1981) — fail repeatedly in the field. After Ruby Ridge (1992) and Waco (1993), the FBI concluded most real negotiations are not rational problem-solving but emotion-driven crisis intervention; their answer became **tactical empathy** — listening as a martial art, balancing emotional intelligence with assertive influence (ch. 1). Where Fisher & Ury treat emotion as an obstacle to rationality, Voss treats it as the medium of every decision (Kahneman's System 1 drives System 2; ch. 1, ch. 6). The book is the operationalization of that shift: a toolkit of voice, listening, and questioning techniques designed to disarm a counterpart, suspend their unbelief, and lead them to discover *your* solution as their own — without ever splitting the difference.

## The 9 Voss tactics

### 1. Tactical Empathy (ch. 1, ch. 3)

**Definition.** "The ability to recognize the perspective of a counterpart, and the vocalization of that recognition" — understanding feelings *and* what is behind them in order to increase influence in subsequent moments (ch. 3).

**Mechanism.** Listening triggers neural resonance and reduces amygdala activity in the counterpart, producing the feeling of being understood — a precondition for behavioral change (Behavioral Change Stairway: active listening → empathy → rapport → influence → behavioral change; ch. 5). Empathy ≠ sympathy or agreement; it is paying attention to the other person and committing to understanding their world.

**Example.** Harlem high-rise, 1998: three armed fugitives barricaded on the 27th floor, no phone. Voss spent six hours through the door using only DJ voice and labels — "It seems like you don't want to come out … you worry that if you open the door we'll come in with guns blazing … you don't want to go back to jail." The fugitives surrendered: "We didn't want to get caught or get shot, but you calmed us down" (ch. 3).

### 2. Mirroring — last 1–3 words (ch. 2)

**Definition.** Repeat the last 1 to 3 words (or the critical 1–3) of what the other person said, with a slight upward inflection — the "Please help me understand" tone. Then go silent for at least 4 seconds.

**Mechanism.** Isopraxism — humans (and other animals) imitate to bond. "We fear what's different and are drawn to what's similar." Mirroring triggers the counterpart to elaborate, restate differently, and reveal more. Wiseman's waiter study: mirroring patrons earned **70% higher tips** than positive reinforcement (ch. 2).

**Example.** A student's boss did "drive-bys" demanding rush copies. She mirrored — "I'm sorry, two copies?" … "Anywhere?" — paused after each. He talked himself out of the project. "A week of work avoided" (ch. 2).

**Use in confrontation — the 4-step disagreement protocol (ch. 2):**
1. Late-night FM DJ voice
2. Start with "I'm sorry…"
3. Mirror
4. Silence (≥4 seconds)
5. Repeat

### 3. Labeling — "It seems like…" (ch. 3)

**Definition.** Verbally name the counterpart's emotion: *It seems like…*, *It sounds like…*, *It looks like…* — never start with "I." After labeling, go silent.

**Mechanism.** Lieberman's UCLA fMRI study: when subjects label an emotion, neural activity moves from the amygdala (fear) to the regions governing rational thinking (ch. 3). Naming a negative emotion diffuses it; naming a positive one reinforces it. Address *underlying* feelings, not surface behavior (the cranky grandfather is lonely, not grumpy; ch. 3).

**Example.** Voss, having entered Canada without country clearance, called the angry FBI legat: "Bless me, Father, for I have sinned" — labeled the negative directly. Cleared the violation in one call (ch. 3).

**Universal label templates (Appendix):**
- *It seems like ___ is valuable to you.*
- *It seems like you don't like ___.*
- *It seems like you're reluctant to ___.*

### 4. Accusation Audit (ch. 3)

**Definition.** Before the conversation, list every negative thing your counterpart could say about you. Then say them aloud, first, with exaggeration. "Taking the sting out" — defense-lawyer technique (ch. 3).

**Mechanism.** Denying the negative gives it credence; voicing it dissolves it. Once spoken aloud the accusations sound exaggerated, and the counterpart's instinct is to soften them ("No, no, you're not the big bad prime contractor"; ch. 3).

**Example.** Anna, prime contractor, had to cut subcontractor ABC's headcount from 5.5 to 3 people on a soured contract. She opened: "You're going to think we are a big, bad prime contractor when we are done … You may feel like we have treated you unfairly … You believe you were promised this work." ABC's response: "No, no, we don't think that." Anna got the cut and saved the relationship — $1M to her firm (ch. 3).

### 5. Calibrated Questions — "How am I supposed to do that?" (ch. 7)

**Definition.** Open-ended questions starting with **"How"** or **"What"** (rarely "Why" — accusatory in every language). Avoid verbs that yield yes/no — *can, is, are, do, does*. They give the counterpart the illusion of control while letting you frame the conversation (ch. 7).

**Mechanism.** Forces the counterpart to think and speak expansively, suspends "unbelief" (Dutton's concept; ch. 7), triggers forced empathy by making them solve *your* problem. Combined with deferential tone, removes aggression. *"How am I supposed to do that?"* is a polite "no" that pushes the counterpart to bid against themselves and contemplate your constraints.

**Example.** Voss vs. Mnookin & Blum at Harvard role-play: to every threat about his "kidnapped son," Voss responded only with "How am I supposed to do that?" and "How do I know he's even alive?" Two Harvard professors got nowhere (ch. 1).

**Standard calibrated questions (ch. 7, Appendix):**
- What about this is important to you?
- How can I help to make this better for us?
- How would you like me to proceed?
- What is it that brought us into this situation?
- How can we solve this problem?
- What's the biggest challenge you face?
- How am I supposed to do that?

### 6. "That's Right" vs. "You're Right" (ch. 5)

**Definition.** "That's right" is the negotiator's gold — a sign the counterpart has assessed your statement and embraced it as their own conclusion. "You're right" is a counterfeit — what people say to make you go away without committing (ch. 5).

**Mechanism.** Drives toward Carl Rogers's *unconditional positive regard*. Behavioral change happens only when the counterpart feels truly understood; "you're right" indicates compliance without internalization — they will revert. Trigger "that's right" with a **summary = paraphrase + label** that articulates the world according to the counterpart (ch. 5).

**Example.** Abu Sabaya, the Abu Sayyaf rebel leader holding Jeffrey Schilling for $10M "war damages," refused for four months to budge. Voss scripted negotiator Benjie to summarize Sabaya's grievances — five centuries of Muslim oppression, fishing rights, war damages — using effective pauses, minimal encouragers, mirrors, labels, paraphrases, and a final summary. Sabaya: "That's right." The ransom demand vanished (ch. 5).

### 7. The Late-Night FM DJ Voice (ch. 2)

**Definition.** Three voice tones: (1) **Late-night FM DJ** — slow, low, downward inflection, conveys "I've got it covered" / "I'm in control"; (2) **positive/playful** — the *default*, smile in your voice, light and encouraging; (3) **direct/assertive** — almost never use, triggers pushback (ch. 2).

**Mechanism.** Mehrabian's 7-38-55 ratio (see below). Tone bypasses the verbal channel and acts directly on System 1 emotional state. Downward inflection on a statement produces certainty; upward inflection invites response; both signal safety when paired with warmth.

**Example.** Chase Manhattan bank robbery, Brooklyn 1993: bank robber Chris Watts had been hanging up on the lead negotiator. Voss came onto the line cold with the DJ voice — "Joe's gone. This is Chris. You're talking to me now." A statement, downward inflection, no question mark. Watts stayed on the phone (ch. 2).

### 8. Bending Reality (ch. 6)

A bundle of prospect-theory tactics for shifting the counterpart's reference points — explicitly built on Kahneman & Tversky (ch. 6). Five sub-techniques:

**8a. Anchor extreme + accusation audit + loss aversion.** Before naming a number, anchor the counterpart's emotions low ("you're going to think I'm a lousy businessman… first big project out of the FBI, he screws it up"). Then offer the deal as the chance to *avoid* loss to a competitor. UAE training contractors took $500/day instead of $2,000 with no counteroffer (ch. 6).

**8b. Let the other side go first on price (most of the time).** When you don't know market value, you give up information by anchoring first. Exception: with a rookie counterpart or in a known market, you may set an extreme anchor yourself — humans adjust insufficiently from any number on the table (Kahneman's anchoring effect, ch. 6).

**8c. Establish a range.** Cite a comparable: "At top places like X Corp., people in this job get $130K–$170K." Columbia Business School research: range-givers got significantly higher offers than single-number-givers, especially with a *bolstering range* (low end = your real target) (ch. 6).

**8d. Pivot to nonmonetary terms.** "Let's put price aside and talk about what would make this a good deal." Memphis Bar Association traded a magazine cover story for a steep discount on Voss's $25K/day fee — zero cost to them, priceless to him (ch. 6).

**8e. Use odd numbers.** Round numbers feel like placeholders; nonround numbers ($37,263) feel calculated and immovable. Voss closed a Haitian kidnapping at **$4,751** in part because the precision conveyed exhausted bottom (ch. 6, ch. 9).

**Deadlines.** Almost always self-inflicted bogeymen. Voss has found one executive in hundreds who suffered real consequences from a missed deadline. Reveal your deadline — Don A. Moore (Berkeley Haas) found hidden deadlines *increase* impasse risk (ch. 6).

**Fairness.** Three uses of the F-bomb: (1) defensive judo — "We just want what's fair" — triggers concession; (2) accusatory — "We've given you a fair offer"; counter by mirroring "*Fair?*" and labeling. (3) Constructive — early in negotiation: "I want you to feel treated fairly at all times. Stop me if you feel I'm being unfair." (ch. 6).

### 9. Black Swans / Known Unknowns (ch. 10)

**Definition.** Borrowed from Taleb. In every negotiation Voss assumes there are **3 to 5 pieces of information** that, if uncovered, would change everything. Three categories: known knowns, known unknowns, **unknown unknowns** (the Black Swans) (ch. 10).

**Mechanism.** Black Swans are leverage multipliers. Three kinds of leverage: **positive** (you can give what they want), **negative** (you can hurt — threats with poise, not anger), **normative** (using the counterpart's own rules and "religion" — worldview, values, code — against them) (ch. 10).

**Example.** Dwight Watson, North Carolina tobacco farmer who towed a tractor onto the National Mall in 2003 and threatened to blow up "organophosphate" bombs. Watson was a devout Christian and 82nd Airborne veteran. Agent Winnie Miller noticed the religious subtext; Voss told Watson the next morning was the **Dawn of the Third Day** — when Christ rose from the tomb. Watson surrendered (ch. 10).

**When the counterpart seems "crazy," ask (Malhotra & Bazerman, *Negotiation Genius*, ch. 10):**
1. Are they ill-informed (GIGO)?
2. Are they constrained (legal, organizational, face-saving)?
3. Do they have hidden interests?

**Black Swan hunting tactics:** get face time (email is too sanitized — and removes the 38% + 55% of the message); observe unguarded moments (first/last 60 seconds, breaks, side conversations); use backup listeners on every call.

## The 7-38-55 Rule (ch. 8)

Mehrabian (UCLA, two studies on liking/disliking): of the message a counterpart receives,
- **7% words**
- **38% tone of voice**
- **55% body language and face**

Voss applies it operationally:
1. Tone and body language are the **assessment tools**, not the words. He flies long distances for face-to-face meetings even when phone would seem to suffice (ch. 8).
2. When tone or body contradict the words ("Yes" said with hesitation), use a label to surface the incongruence: *"I heard you say 'Yes,' but it seemed like there was hesitation in your voice."* Pair with the **Rule of Three** — get the counterpart to confirm three times in three different ways (commitment / "that's right" / calibrated How question on implementation). It is hard to lie three times convincingly (ch. 8).
3. Pinocchio Effect (Malhotra et al.): liars use **more words** and **more third-person pronouns** to distance themselves from the lie. Important decision-makers use *we/they/them* (deferring to absent stakeholders); the heavy use of *I/me/my* signals low authority (ch. 8).

## Bargaining model — the Ackerman system (ch. 9)

Voss's haggling protocol, learned from CIA-veteran Mike Ackerman and validated by Howard Raiffa (Harvard). Six steps:

1. **Set your target price** (the goal).
2. **Open at 65% of target** — extreme anchor; triggers fight-or-flight, drops the counterpart's cognitive bandwidth.
3. **Three calculated raises in decreasing increments — 85%, 95%, 100% of target.** Each raise is preceded by empathy and a calibrated "How am I supposed to…" so the counterpart bids against themselves.
4. **Use lots of empathy + non-word "no"** between raises. (See "the four ways to say No without saying No": *How am I supposed to do that?* → *Your offer is very generous, I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me* → *I'm sorry but I'm afraid I just can't do that* → *I'm sorry, no.*)
5. **Final number is precise and odd** — $37,893 not $38,000. Conveys careful calculation, signals bottom.
6. **Throw in a nonmonetary item** they don't really want — signals you're at your limit, triggers reciprocity.

**Worked example: the Salsa Red Pearl 4Runner (ch. 9).** Sticker $36,000. Voss opens at $30,000 cash. Salesman: $34,000 → "wow, your offer is very generous, but…" → $32,500 → "you've been very generous, I just can't" → $30,000. Drove off in the 4Runner.

**Pre-bargaining: identify counterpart's negotiating style (ch. 9).**
- **Analyst** — methodical, data-driven, silence = thinking. Use clear data, no surprises, give them time.
- **Accommodator** — relationship-first, talks a lot, may overpromise. Use calibrated How questions on implementation.
- **Assertive** — time = money, must feel heard before listening. Use mirrors, labels, summaries, get to "that's right."
- **The "I am normal" paradox**: 66% of counterparts are not your type. *Treat them the way they need to be treated, not how you want to be treated.*

## 5 named techniques worth quoting verbatim

1. **The "Have you given up on this project?" email** (ch. 4). For non-responsive counterparts: a one-line, "no"-oriented email that exploits loss aversion and the autonomy-affirming feel of saying No. The implicit threat is that you're walking away. *"It makes the implicit threat that you will walk away on your own terms."*

2. **The Chris discount** (ch. 8). Use *your own* name, not theirs. "My name is Chris. What's the Chris discount?" Humanizes you and triggers forced empathy in the clerk. Got Voss a 10% discount in an outlet mall.

3. **"How am I supposed to do that?"** (ch. 1, ch. 7, ch. 8). Voss's signature calibrated question — a polite no that simultaneously asks for help, acknowledges their power, and forces them to consider your constraints. Used against Harvard professors, Haitian kidnappers, Ecuadoran kidnappers, used-car salesmen, billing clients.

4. **"Look, I'm an asshole"** (ch. 3). Voss's go-to when he's made a mistake. *"I've found the phrase 'Look, I'm an asshole' to be an amazingly effective way to make problems go away. That approach has never failed me."* The accusation audit applied to oneself.

5. **Behavioral Change Stairway Model** (ch. 5). FBI CNU model. Five stages: **active listening → empathy → rapport → influence → behavioral change**. Most negotiators try to start at influence; you cannot — each stage is a precondition for the next. Built on Carl Rogers's unconditional positive regard.

## Cross-references

**vs. Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow*.** Voss explicitly grounds his approach in Kahneman & Tversky (ch. 1, ch. 6). System 1/System 2 → why "feeling is a form of thinking" and why words alone (7%) are weak. Prospect theory and loss aversion → *Bend Their Reality* (ch. 6) is essentially applied prospect theory: anchoring extreme, framing as a loss-to-be-avoided, the certainty effect, the endowment-flavored mug example. Kahneman supplies the *why*; Voss supplies the *what to say*. Cite Kahneman for mechanism, Voss for script. (See `book-thinking-fast-slow.md` for the Kahneman digest.)

**vs. Fisher & Ury, *Getting to Yes* (1981) — BATNA / ZOPA.** Voss admires *Getting to Yes* but argues its rationalist frame fails wherever emotion dominates (ch. 1). Three direct rebuttals:
- **"Separate the people from the problem"** — wrong when their emotions *are* the problem (ch. 3).
- **BATNA traps you into aiming low.** Researchers have shown humans gravitate to whichever focus point has the most psychological weight; obsessing over BATNA makes it your target. Voss prescribes a high-end ambitious goal instead (Appendix).
- **ZOPA assumes rational sellers and buyers.** Real bargainers open with extreme anchors well outside any "zone" (ch. 9). "Everybody has a plan until they get punched in the mouth" — Tyson, quoted by Voss.
- **Win-win / split the difference** — a "wimp-win" mentality. *"Never split the difference"* — wear two black shoes or two brown, never one of each (ch. 6).

**vs. Camp, *Start with NO*.** Voss credits Jim Camp explicitly (ch. 4). "No" is the start of the negotiation: it preserves autonomy, gives the speaker a sense of safety and control. The Republican fund-raising script swap (Yes-pattern → No-pattern) lifted donations 23% (ch. 4).

## How a `consulting-negotiation-coach` skill should use it

Pull list — concrete frameworks for the skill body:

1. **The Behavioral Change Stairway** (ch. 5) as the master sequencing model — you cannot influence a client until you have rapport, and you cannot have rapport until you have demonstrated empathy through active listening. Most consulting "objection handling" tries to skip to influence.

2. **The four-step disarm** — DJ voice → "I'm sorry…" → mirror → 4-second silence (ch. 2) — for handling pushback in steering committees and skeptical sponsors.

3. **Accusation audit** for project kickoffs and recovery conversations (ch. 3). Before delivering bad news (scope cut, timeline slip, budget overrun), list every accusation the client could level and voice them first. Anna's $1M contract case is the canonical example.

4. **Calibrated "How"/"What" questions** as the consulting interview backbone (ch. 7) — the same technique Voss used at Harvard. Replace *"Can we…"* / *"Should we…"* with *"How would we…"* / *"What's the biggest challenge you face?"* / *"How does this affect everybody else?"*

5. **"That's right" via summary** as the consulting recommendation gate (ch. 5). Before presenting your recommendation, summarize the world according to the client (paraphrase + label) until they say "that's right." Without it you'll get "you're right" and they'll revert post-meeting.

6. **The Ackerman model** for fee/scope negotiation (ch. 9) — 65/85/95/100 with odd final numbers and a nonmonetary throw-in. Especially the **pivot to nonmonetary terms** (ch. 6) when stuck on price: a magazine cover story, case-study rights, joint conference appearance, exclusivity in a vertical.

7. **7-38-55 + Rule of Three** for executive sponsorship checks (ch. 8). When the sponsor says "Yes" with hesitation in their voice, label the incongruence and get the commitment confirmed in three forms before relying on it for project planning.

8. **Black Swan hunting — the three "crazy" diagnostics** (ch. 10): when a stakeholder is acting irrationally, ask whether they are *ill-informed*, *constrained* (legal, political, face-saving), or pursuing *hidden interests*. The Coca-Cola contact case (ch. 10) — sponsor went silent because his division had been gutted internally — is a textbook consulting failure mode.

## Pitfalls Voss flags

- **Splitting the difference** (ch. 6). The defining anti-thesis of the book. Compromise is socially safe and substantively bad. The mismatched shoes parable: black-and-brown is worse than either black-black or brown-brown. Compromise satisfies neither side and rewards counterparts who refuse to compromise.

- **Win-win mythology / "wimp-win"** (ch. 6, Appendix). The terms "win-win" and BATNA together produce mediocre deals because they pull negotiators down toward the focus point of safety rather than up toward an ambitious goal. *"People who expect more (and articulate it) get more."*

- **Fairness as a weapon** (ch. 6). When your counterpart drops "We just want what's fair" or "We've given you a fair offer," they are weaponizing your guilt. Don't concede; mirror, label, and ask them to explain how they're being mistreated.

- **The "Yes" trap — three kinds of yes** (ch. 4, ch. 8). Counterfeit (escape), Confirmation (reflexive affirmation), Commitment (real). Hard-sell scripts manufacture counterfeit yes. Drive toward "that's right" instead.

- **"You're right" as failure mode** (ch. 5). Hearing "you're right" means you have been dismissed politely. The counterpart has not internalized the conclusion and will not act on it.

- **Hidden deadlines** (ch. 6). Conventional wisdom says hide your deadline; the research says revealed deadlines reduce impasse and accelerate concessions.

- **Niceness as ruse** (ch. 4). Plastic affability ("Be nice") suppresses the real disagreement and produces counterfeit yes. Honest, calibrated *No* is more respectful and more productive than performative agreement.

- **Email-only negotiation** (ch. 10). Email strips tone (38%) and body language (55%) — 93% of the signal — and gives the counterpart unlimited time to compose. Ten minutes face-to-face often reveals more than days of email.

- **Treating people as rational actors** (ch. 1, ch. 6). The Ultimatum Game shows that even MBA students reject "rational" $1 offers because of perceived unfairness. Iran has forgone $100B+ in oil revenue to defend uranium enrichment that meets 2% of its energy needs — for fairness, not energy. Plan negotiations around the *predictable patterns* of irrationality, not assumed rationality.

- **The "I am normal" paradox** (ch. 9). Projecting your negotiating style onto your counterpart. With three types (Analyst / Accommodator / Assertive), there is a 66% chance you are wrong about their normal. Silence means *thinking* to an Analyst, *anger* to an Accommodator, *opportunity to talk more* to an Assertive.
