# Essentials of Organizational Behavior — Stephen Robbins & Tim Judge (2018, 14th ed.)

**Source digest** for the consulting plugin's change-management and organizational-behavior work. Page numbers refer to the Pearson 14th Global Edition. Cite as "(Robbins & Judge, *Essentials of OB*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

This book is also relevant to the **behavioral-economics-organizational-behavior-specialist** skill — both plugins can reach for it.

## Thesis

Organizational behavior (OB) is "a field of study that investigates the impact individuals, groups, and structure have on behavior within organizations" (Ch. 1, p. 39). Robbins & Judge's *Essentials* is the most-adopted OB textbook in the world precisely because it is conservative: it surveys the consensus, names the originating researchers, and tells you which theories the evidence supports and which are kept around mainly because managers still use the vocabulary (Maslow, MBTI, Herzberg). The book's organizing scheme is **inputs → processes → outcomes** at three levels — individual, group, organizational — and that scheme is the closest thing OB has to a unifying frame (Ch. 1, pp. 50–53). Use this book when you need a defensible, citable framework rather than a clever one.

## Personality models (Ch. 4)

### The Big Five (pp. 80–82)

Five dimensions — **Conscientiousness, Emotional stability** (vs. neuroticism), **Extraversion, Openness to experience, Agreeableness** — that have replicated across most cultures and predict job behavior far better than alternative inventories. **Conscientiousness is the strongest single predictor of job performance** across complexity levels (p. 81). Emotional stability predicts life and job satisfaction; extraversion predicts leadership *emergence* (not always effectiveness); openness predicts adaptability to change and creativity; agreeableness predicts OCB but, perversely, lower career earnings (p. 82). Use Big Five for selection, team composition, leadership development.

### Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (pp. 79–80)

The most widely used assessment in the world; four dichotomies (E/I, S/N, T/F, J/P) yielding 16 types. Robbins & Judge's caveat is unambiguous: **"results from the MBTI tend to be unrelated to job performance"** (p. 80), retest reliability is poor, and forcing dichotomies on continuous traits is psychometrically wrong. Mention MBTI because clients use it; do not build a deliverable on it.

### The Dark Triad (pp. 82–84)

**Machiavellianism, narcissism, psychopathy** — socially undesirable but normal-range traits. Not clinical pathologies; expressed most strongly under stress. Narcissists are often more charismatic and emerge as leaders, but derail. Mid-range scores are optimal for leadership effectiveness; both very low and very high scores predict ineffectiveness (p. 218). Useful when diagnosing toxic leaders, succession planning, and "charismatic but corrupt" patterns at Enron, Tyco, etc. (pp. 225–226).

## Motivation theories (Chs. 7–8)

| Theory | Definition | When to use | When it fails |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Maslow's hierarchy** (pp. 131–132) | 5 needs ordered physiological → safety → social → esteem → self-actualization | Familiar shorthand with executives; intuitive | "Most research has not [validated it], especially when applied to diverse cultures" (p. 131). Treat as vocabulary, not science. |
| **Herzberg two-factor** (pp. 132–133) | Hygiene factors (pay, conditions, policy) prevent dissatisfaction; motivators (achievement, recognition, growth) cause satisfaction | Diagnosing why pay raises don't motivate; redesigning roles | Methodology criticized; "two-factor theory has not been well supported in research" (p. 133) but heavily used in Asia |
| **McClelland's needs** (pp. 132–134) | nAch, nAff, nPow — learned, not hierarchical | Predicting which roles fit which people; high-nPow + high-nAff = best leaders | nAch presupposes individualism + moderate-risk acceptance; weaker in collectivist/high-power-distance cultures |
| **Self-determination theory** (pp. 134–135) | People prefer autonomy, competence, relatedness; extrinsic rewards can crowd out intrinsic motivation (cognitive evaluation) | Knowledge work, creative tasks, volunteer settings | Where work is dull and pay-for-performance is appropriate (intrinsic motivation predicts quality, incentives predict quantity, p. 135) |
| **Goal-setting (Locke & Latham)** (pp. 135–138) | Specific, difficult goals + feedback drive performance; MBO operationalizes it | Performance management, sales targets, project plans | Complex/interdependent tasks; collectivist cultures prefer moderate goals; goals can drive unethical shortcuts (p. 138) |
| **Equity theory / organizational justice** (pp. 141–144) | Workers compare outcome/input ratios to referents; perceived inequity drives 6 responses (p. 141). Justice = distributive + procedural + informational + interpersonal (Exhibit 7-6) | Compensation design, layoff communication, post-merger integration | "Equity theory's propositions have not all held up" (p. 141) — but the *justice* successor framework holds strongly |
| **Expectancy theory (Vroom)** (pp. 145–146) | Motivation = Effort→Performance × Performance→Reward × Reward→Personal-goal | Incentive design; diagnosing why people exert minimum effort | When any of the three links is weak or unclear, predictions degrade — which is "why a significant segment of the workforce exerts low effort" (p. 146) |
| **Job Characteristics Model (Hackman & Oldham)** (pp. 151–153) | 5 core dimensions — **skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, feedback** — drive 3 psychological states (meaningfulness, responsibility, knowledge of results) | Job redesign, role enrichment, motivating potential score (MPS) calculation | Individualistic bias; effects weaker in collectivist cultures (p. 153) |

## Group dynamics (Ch. 10)

**Stages of group development.** Robbins & Judge teach the **punctuated-equilibrium model** as their dominant theory (pp. 184–185), not Tuckman's forming/storming/norming/performing (which the book does not foreground). The PE pattern: first meeting sets direction → Phase 1 inertia → midpoint transition (alarm-clock awareness) → Phase 2 inertia → final-burst completion. Apply only to **temporary groups under deadline.** For permanent teams, the book uses the inputs-processes-outcomes lens.

**Social loafing** (pp. 192–193) — individuals exert less effort in groups than alone (Ringelmann). Strongest in Western/individualist contexts; collectivist cultures show reverse (p. 192). Counter with: smaller groups, identifiable individual contributions, peer evaluations.

**Status** (pp. 190–191) — socially defined rank. Status characteristics theory: status comes from power over others, ability to contribute, or personal characteristics. Status incongruence drives turnover and conflict.

**Cohesiveness** (pp. 193–194) — degree members are attracted and motivated to stay. Cohesiveness × performance norms is a 2×2 (Exhibit 10-4): high-cohesion + high-norms = high productivity; high-cohesion + low-norms = low productivity (the worst quadrant — your team works hard at the wrong things).

**Groupthink** (pp. 196–197) — "norm for consensus overrides realistic appraisal." Symptoms: pressure on dissenters, illusion of unanimity, self-censorship. Defenses: smaller groups, impartial leader, devil's advocate, discuss risks before gains. Samsung Galaxy Note 7 cited (p. 196).

**Group polarization / groupshift** (pp. 197) — groups exaggerate the dominant initial position, drifting toward either greater risk or greater caution. Special case of groupthink.

## Communication (Ch. 9)

**Formal vs. informal channels.** Formal channels follow the org chart (downward, upward, lateral); informal = the **grapevine** (pp. 170–171), which managers should monitor — accuracy is surprisingly high (~75%) and it carries the rumors that signal anxiety.

**Channel richness** (pp. 172–174). Channels rank by capacity to (1) handle multiple cues, (2) give rapid feedback, (3) feel personal. Order from richest to leanest: face-to-face → video → phone → voicemail → email → memo/letter → bulletin/report. **Routine messages → lean channel; nonroutine/ambiguous → rich channel.** Mismatch is a top cause of communication failure.

**Barriers** (pp. 176–179): filtering, selective perception, information overload, emotions, language, silence, communication apprehension, lying.

**Cross-cultural** (pp. 181–183): high-context (Asia, Middle East, Latin America — meaning carried by relationships, status, setting) vs. low-context (US, Northern Europe — meaning carried by words). Adjust channel and explicitness accordingly.

## Leadership theories (Ch. 12)

**Trait theories** (pp. 217–218) — Big Five (extraversion strongest predictor of *emergence*; conscientiousness + openness predict *effectiveness*); EI also predicts emergence (p. 218); Dark-Side traits in moderation can help emergence but hurt effectiveness.

**Behavioral theories — Ohio State** (pp. 218–219). Two dimensions: **initiating structure** (task-oriented; defines roles, assigns work, sets standards) and **consideration** (people-oriented; trust, respect, support). Both relate positively to outcomes; consideration matters more in cultures averse to unilateral decision-making (e.g., Brazil, p. 219). Michigan studies (briefly mentioned) parallel Ohio State with "production-oriented" vs. "employee-oriented."

**Contingency theories.**
- **Fiedler** (pp. 219–221). LPC (Least Preferred Coworker) score classifies leader as task- or relationship-oriented; situational favorability = leader-member relations × task structure × position power. Task-oriented leaders win in very favorable or very unfavorable situations; relationship-oriented win in moderate.
- **Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership (SLT)** (p. 221). Leader style depends on follower readiness (ability + willingness). Four styles: telling, selling, participating, delegating. "Intuitive appeal… research efforts to test and support the theory have generally been disappointing" (p. 221).
- **House's path-goal** (pp. 221–222). Leader clears path to goals via directive, supportive, participative, or achievement-oriented behavior, contingent on task and follower characteristics.

**LMX (Leader-Member Exchange)** (pp. 222–223). Leaders form ingroup/outgroup distinctions early and stably. Ingroup members get higher performance ratings, lower turnover, more satisfaction. Self-fulfilling — leaders invest more in those they expect to perform.

**Transactional vs. Transformational** (pp. 226–229). Transactional = contingent reward + management-by-exception; transformational = idealized influence + inspirational motivation + intellectual stimulation + individualized consideration. Transformational outperforms transactional in nearly every meta-analysis; especially effective when followers see direct impact on customers/beneficiaries (p. 226).

**Charismatic** (pp. 224–226). Vision, willingness to risk, sensitivity to followers, unconventional behavior. Born + made (twin studies show heritability; can also be trained). Dark side: Enron, Tyco, WorldCom, Hitler — charisma is power, usable for good or ill.

**Authentic** (pp. 229–230). Self-aware, transparent, values-driven; produces *trust*. Often paired with *humility* and *ethical leadership*.

**Servant** (pp. 230–231). Listening, empathy, stewardship, growth of followers. Negatively correlated with narcissism; predicts higher OCB, team potency, creativity, and follower well-being.

## Power and politics (Ch. 13)

**French & Raven's bases of power** (pp. 238–240). Two groups:
- **Formal:** Coercive (fear), Reward (positive benefits), Legitimate (position).
- **Personal:** Expert (knowledge/skill), Referent (admiration/identification).

**Personal sources are most effective.** Expert and referent power positively predict satisfaction, commitment, and performance; coercive damages all three; reward and legitimate are roughly neutral (p. 240).

**Dependence is the key to power** (p. 240). B's dependence on A increases when what A controls is **important, scarce, and nonsubstitutable** (Emerson).

**Political behavior** (Ch. 13, pp. 246–253). Activities not required by formal role that influence distribution of advantages. Driven by individual factors (high self-monitoring, internal locus, high Mach, high investment) and organizational factors (resource scarcity, ambiguous goals, zero-sum reward systems, low trust). Impression management tactics: conformity, favors, excuses, apologies, self-promotion, flattery, exemplification.

## Conflict and negotiation (Ch. 14)

**Conflict process — five stages** (pp. 259–264): potential opposition (communication, structure, personal variables) → cognition/personalization → intentions → behavior → outcomes.

**Thomas-Kilmann conflict modes** (Stage III, p. 261). Two axes — assertiveness and cooperativeness — yield five intentions:
- **Competing** (high assert / low coop) — win-lose, scarce resources
- **Collaborating** (high / high) — integrative, both fully satisfied
- **Avoiding** (low / low) — withdraw, suppress
- **Accommodating** (low / high) — yield to preserve relationship
- **Compromising** (mid / mid) — split the difference

**Distributive bargaining** (pp. 268–269) — fixed-pie, zero-sum; each party tries to claim the bigger share; classic price negotiation. Tactic: aggressive first offer (anchoring).

**Integrative bargaining** (pp. 269–271) — assumes win-win is possible; share information, focus on interests not positions, build long-term relationships. Most workplace negotiations *should* be integrative but default to distributive because parties don't trust each other.

## Organizational culture (Ch. 16)

**Definition** (pp. 295–297). Culture = shared meaning system that distinguishes one organization from another; *descriptive* (how things are perceived), not evaluative.

**Schein's three levels** (cited via Schein's *Organizational Culture and Leadership*, ref. 39, p. 21610): **Artifacts** (visible: dress, layout, rituals) → **Espoused values** (stated norms, mission) → **Underlying assumptions** (taken-for-granted beliefs that actually drive behavior). Real culture lives at the assumption layer; mismatch between espoused values and assumptions is the most common consulting finding.

**Dominant culture vs. subcultures** (pp. 296–297). Dominant culture = core values shared by majority. Subcultures = department- or geography-defined microcultures. Strong cultures have intensely held, widely shared core values; predict behavior more reliably than weak ones.

**How culture is created** (pp. 303–304). Founders are the ultimate source. Three mechanisms: hire-for-fit, indoctrinate/socialize, model behavior personally.

**How culture is sustained** (pp. 304–306). Three forces: **selection** (filter for fit), **top management** (signal what is rewarded/punished), **socialization** (prearrival → encounter → metamorphosis). Stories, rituals, material symbols, and language transmit culture day-to-day.

## Change management (Ch. 17)

**Resistance to change** (pp. 315–319). Sources: individual (habit, security, economic factors, fear of the unknown, selective info processing) and organizational (structural inertia, group inertia, threat to expertise/power, threat to resource allocation). **Resistance can be positive** when it surfaces real concerns (p. 317).

**Eight tactics to overcome resistance** (pp. 317–319): communication, participation, building support, developing positive relationships, implementing changes fairly, manipulation/cooptation (use carefully), selecting people who accept change, coercion (last resort).

**Lewin's three-step model** (p. 320). **Unfreeze** (increase driving forces and/or decrease restraining forces — see force-field analysis, Exhibit 17-3) → **Move** (implement quickly to keep momentum) → **Refreeze** (anchor in new equilibrium so it sticks).

**Kotter's 8-step plan** (pp. 320–321), explicitly built on Lewin (steps 1–4 = unfreeze; 5–7 = move; 8 = refreeze):
1. Establish a sense of urgency
2. Form a powerful guiding coalition
3. Create a vision
4. Communicate the vision
5. Empower others to act on the vision (remove barriers)
6. Plan for and create short-term wins
7. Consolidate improvements and produce more change
8. Institutionalize new approaches in the culture

**Action research** (p. 321) — diagnosis → analysis → feedback → action → evaluation. Reduces resistance because employees participate; makes change data-driven.

**Change agents** (p. 317) — people with formal or informal responsibility for managing change. Can be internal or external (consultants). The role demands vision, motivation, and influence rather than positional authority.

**Organizational development (OD)** (pp. 321–323) — humanistic, participative interventions: sensitivity training (T-groups), survey feedback, process consultation, team building, intergroup development, appreciative inquiry.

## Five named frameworks worth pulling out

1. **Big Five → Job fit / leadership selection.** Most defensible personality model in the book (Ch. 4, pp. 80–82).
2. **Job Characteristics Model + MPS.** The one-pager for any "this work is meaningless" engagement. Five dimensions, three psychological states, predictable outcomes (Ch. 8, pp. 151–153).
3. **Organizational justice (4 types).** Distributive, procedural, informational, interpersonal — the four checkboxes for any compensation, layoff, or post-merger comms plan (Ch. 7, pp. 142–144, Exhibit 7-6).
4. **French & Raven bases of power + dependence.** The diagnostic for any influence map. Personal > formal; coercive damages outcomes (Ch. 13, pp. 238–240).
5. **Thomas-Kilmann conflict modes.** Five-mode 2×2 (assertiveness × cooperativeness). The vocabulary every facilitator needs (Ch. 14, p. 261).
6. **Lewin + Kotter sequenced together.** Lewin gives the three-phase metaphor; Kotter gives the 8 operational steps mapped onto it (Ch. 17, pp. 320–321).
7. **Schein's three levels of culture.** Artifacts/values/assumptions. Most consulting culture work mistakes the artifact layer for the assumption layer.
8. **Transformational leadership (4 I's).** Idealized influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, individualized consideration (Ch. 12, pp. 226–229).

## Cross-references

**vs. Block, *Flawless Consulting*.** Block is about the consultant's stance and the contracting conversation; Robbins & Judge are about what's actually happening inside the client. Use Block for *how I show up*; use Robbins & Judge for *what I diagnose*. They are complementary — Block tells you to surface resistance; Robbins & Judge (Ch. 17) tells you the eight tactics to address it once surfaced.

**vs. Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow*.** Kahneman gives you the individual decision-making biases (anchoring, availability, framing, loss aversion); Robbins & Judge tell you what those biases do once embedded in groups, hierarchies, and reward systems. Pair Kahneman's *planning fallacy* and *optimism* with Robbins & Judge's *groupthink* (p. 196) and *escalation of commitment*.

**vs. Slalom Behavioral Design Model.** The Slalom model treats behavior change as a design problem with motivators, ability, and prompts. Robbins & Judge are the academic ancestor — JCM (motivation by design), goal-setting (ability + commitment), expectancy theory (perceived link to reward) all predate and underpin a behavioral-design intervention. Cite Robbins & Judge to defend the academic provenance of any behavioral-design recommendation.

## How `consulting-change-management-advisor` should use it

1. **Lewin's unfreeze-change-refreeze** (p. 320) as the metaphor for any change narrative
2. **Kotter's 8 steps** (pp. 320–321) as the operational checklist mapped onto Lewin
3. **Force-field analysis** (Exhibit 17-3, p. 320) to diagnose driving vs. restraining forces
4. **Eight resistance tactics** (pp. 317–319) — especially *participation*, *fair process*, *positive relationships*
5. **Kotter step 6 = short-term wins** plus **goal-setting theory** (Ch. 7, pp. 135–137) to design milestone reinforcement
6. **Schein's three levels of culture** (Ch. 16) to scope the depth of any culture-change ask — usually the client wants assumptions changed but is funding only artifacts
7. **Organizational justice (4 types)** (pp. 142–144) for layoff, restructuring, and pay-change communication plans
8. **Action research** (p. 321) when the client wants a participative, data-driven change

## Pitfalls — common OB-textbook traps when applied to real organizations

1. **Over-individualizing.** Textbook OB diagnoses individuals (personality, motivation, satisfaction) when the actual driver is structural (incentive design, span of control, reporting lines). Always ask whether the structure rewards the behavior you're trying to change before recommending a personality-style intervention.
2. **Treating discredited theories as live.** Maslow, Herzberg, MBTI all appear in the book *because managers still use them*, not because they survive empirical scrutiny (pp. 80, 131, 133). Cite them by name, but back deliverables with Big Five, JCM, expectancy, goal-setting, and organizational justice.
3. **Ignoring cultural fit.** Most theories were developed in US samples. Goal-setting in collectivist cultures, social loafing in collectivist cultures, the JCM in collectivist cultures, equity perceptions across cultures — all show different patterns (pp. 137, 153, 192). Always ask "where did this evidence come from?"
4. **Charisma without checks.** Charismatic and transformational leadership work, but the book's own examples include Hitler (p. 226) and the Enron-era CEOs. Pair charismatic-leader recommendations with authentic-leadership and ethical-leadership scaffolding.
5. **Refreeze too early.** Kotter's biggest single warning is "do not declare victory too soon" (p. 320). Most failed change programs are technically completed but socially un-refrozen — people revert because the new behavior was never anchored in selection, evaluation, and promotion criteria (Ch. 16, pp. 304–306).
6. **Mistaking artifacts for culture.** Open-plan offices, ping-pong tables, "values posters" are all artifacts. Schein's point is they signal nothing reliable about the underlying assumptions. Diagnose at the assumption layer or you will optimize the wrong thing.
7. **Engineering away conflict.** The Thomas-Kilmann model treats avoiding and accommodating as legitimate; not all conflict should be collaborated through. Sometimes the right move is to compete, sometimes to walk away. Default-to-collaboration is itself a bias.
8. **Treating resistance as pathological.** Robbins & Judge are explicit: "resistance to change can be positive" (p. 317) when it surfaces real concerns. Change-agent tactics that suppress resistance produce compliance theater, not change.
