# Template — Negotiation Prep (Voss + BATNA/ZOPA)

**Sources:** Voss, *Never Split the Difference* (HarperBusiness, 2016) — see [`book-never-split-the-difference.md`](book-never-split-the-difference.md). Classical BATNA/ZOPA terminology from Fisher & Ury, *Getting to Yes*.

A pre-negotiation worksheet that combines Voss's tactical empathy with classical analytical prep. Use it before high-stakes deal conversations: contract terms, scope/SOW disputes, salary, vendor renewals, partnership terms, internal stakeholder showdowns.

## When to use it

- Before any negotiation where the outcome materially matters
- Especially when the counterparty is hostile, intimidating, or has more leverage than you
- When you've been told to "just split the difference" and your gut says no
- Before SOW change-control conversations (see `consulting-sow-risk-advisor`)

## The seven-section prep worksheet

Fill these out before the call. Don't skip — Voss's research shows hostage negotiators with thorough prep close 90%+ of cases; without prep, much lower.

### 1. Goal — what's the actual outcome you want?

State the concrete result. Avoid "win" or "fair." Specifically:

- **Best realistic outcome:** [the result you'd take with a smile]
- **Acceptable floor:** [the worst result you'll accept and walk]
- **What you'd reframe as a win even if you don't get the price you wanted:** [non-monetary throw-ins, terms, payment schedule, future relationship]

### 2. BATNA — Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement

If this deal collapses, what do you do instead? Concrete, actionable, not aspirational.

- **My BATNA:** [the alternative I'll execute if we don't reach agreement]
- **My BATNA's value:** [in dollars, time, opportunity cost — make it real]
- **Counterparty's likely BATNA:** [what they do if you walk]

> **Voss caveat:** Don't anchor on your BATNA. Voss argues classical BATNA-thinking can be a trap because it makes you settle for "anywhere above BATNA" instead of pushing for what's actually possible. Use BATNA to know your floor; pursue your goal. (Ch. 6)

### 3. ZOPA — Zone of Possible Agreement

Where do their needs and yours overlap?

- **What they probably want most:** [from your research / past conversations]
- **What you want most:** [link back to Goal]
- **Where the overlap is:** [the trades that satisfy both]

### 4. Accusation Audit — preempt their worst objections

List every negative thing they could say about you, your offer, your firm, your past performance. Then plan to **say it first** to defuse it. (Voss, Ch. 5.)

> "You probably think we're too expensive. You probably think we're going to over-promise like the last vendor. You probably think this proposal doesn't reflect that we listened to you in the last meeting."

The audit takes the bullets out of the gun. Counter-intuitively, naming their objections strengthens your position.

### 5. Calibrated questions to ask

Voss-style "How am I supposed to do that?" or "What's the biggest challenge you face?" Open-ended, starting with "How" or "What" — never "Why" (which sounds accusatory).

Avoid yes/no questions in tough moments — they shrink the space. Calibrated questions force the counterparty to think about your problem and propose solutions.

| Where in the conversation | Calibrated question |
|---|---|
| When they push for a discount | "How am I supposed to do that?" |
| When they're stuck on price | "What's the most important factor here for you?" |
| When you're running into a wall | "What about this is most challenging for you?" |
| When they make a threat | "How do you see that going?" |

### 6. Labels and mirrors prepared

Voss's "It seems like…" labels and mirroring (repeating last 3 words) make the counterparty feel heard and elicit more information.

| Likely emotional state | Label |
|---|---|
| Frustrated with timeline | "It seems like timing is the real issue here." |
| Worried about budget | "It sounds like the spend is hard to defend internally." |
| Distrustful of vendors generally | "It feels like you've been burned before." |

### 7. The Ackerman model — for hard-anchored numerical negotiation

If you have to negotiate a specific number (price, fee, salary), Voss's Ackerman model:

1. Set your target price (your real Goal — the most ambitious *defensible* number)
2. Open with **65%** of target (an extreme anchor that signals)
3. Calculate raises to **85%, 95%, 100%** of target
4. Use empathy and varied calibrated questions before each new offer ("How am I supposed to make this work?")
5. Offer non-monetary throw-ins
6. Use **odd, specific numbers** (not round) — feel calculated, hard to negotiate against
7. Final number includes a non-monetary item — the throw-in

Example for a $100k engagement target:
- Open: $65,000
- Raise to: $85,000
- Raise to: $95,000
- Final: $98,750 + an extra workshop day at no charge

## Communication discipline during the negotiation

Voss's 7-38-55 rule applied (Mehrabian via Voss): 7% of meaning is words, 38% tone, 55% body language. So:

- Use the **late-night-FM-DJ voice** — calm, slow, downward inflection — when you need to lower temperature.
- Use the **direct/positive voice** for most exchanges.
- Use the **assertive voice** sparingly — it puts the counterparty on the defensive.
- **Listen 80%, speak 20%.** The temptation is to fill silence; resist.

Watch for signals:
- "**That's right**" from them = breakthrough. They've internalized your view.
- "**You're right**" = polite dismissal. They want you to stop talking. Re-engage.
- Three "yeses" in a row = either committed or just tired of you. Probe with a calibrated question.

## Pitfalls Voss flags (Ch. 1, 7, 9)

- **Splitting the difference** — Voss's titular warning. The classical "fair compromise" gives both sides something neither truly wanted. Don't do it. (Ch. 1)
- **Win-win mythology** — assumes the other side is a rational, good-faith collaborator. Real negotiations include people who are actively adversarial, irrational, or operating from their own un-articulated needs.
- **Fairness weaponization** — when they say "I just want to be fair," watch for it being deployed as manipulation. The fair-fairness rule: if they say "fair" repeatedly, they're priming you to give in.
- **Hidden deadlines** — your deadline is your weakness; don't reveal it. Their deadline is your leverage; surface it through calibrated questions ("What's driving the timing on your end?").
- **The niceness ruse** — being nice doesn't equal being agreed with. You can be warm AND hold the line.
- **Email-only negotiation** — strips away tone and body language; reduces information exchange. Get on a call for the high-stakes moments.
- **Rational-actor assumption** — you negotiate with humans, not Econs. The counterparty's emotions are data, not noise.

## Worked example — SOW pricing pushback

**Situation:** Client says "Your $250k SOW is way over budget. We can do $150k."

**Goal:** $250k or close to it; alternatively, scope reduction that keeps margin
**BATNA:** Walk; competing pursuit is queued
**Counterparty's BATNA:** Take it in-house (probably worse than they think)
**ZOPA:** Likely $200–$240k; possible scope-trim to $200k
**Accusation audit:** "I bet our number feels high. I bet you've heard ambitious quotes from us before. I bet your CFO is breathing down your neck."
**Calibrated question:** "How am I supposed to deliver the outcomes we discussed at $150k?"
**Label:** "It seems like the budget is more of a hard constraint than the scope."
**Ackerman style fallback if you must negotiate the number:** anchor at $245k (you're going down, not up — but use specific, not round); next move $235k; next $225k; final $217.5k + an extra week of post-launch support. (Reverse Ackerman because you're moving down from your asking price.)
**Throw-in option:** Free 2-week diagnostic to validate the $250k scope, with full refund if scope changes — reduces their perceived risk.
