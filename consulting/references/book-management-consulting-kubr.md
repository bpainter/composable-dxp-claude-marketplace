# Management Consulting: A Guide to the Profession — Milan Kubr (ed., 4th ed., 2002)

**Source digest** for the consulting plugin. Page numbers refer to the 4th-edition ILO/Geneva imprint (927 pp.). Kubr uses chapter+section numbering (e.g. §8.2, §30.4), so each citation records both. Cite as "(Kubr, *MCG*, §X.Y, p. N)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

Kubr's *Management Consulting* is the canonical, encyclopedic reference for the profession — commissioned by the ILO across four editions (1976–2002), drafted by Kubr with 30+ practitioner contributors, designed to codify a global, profession-grounded view of consulting independent of any one firm's house style. The 4th edition spans 38 chapters across five parts: (I) consulting in perspective, (II) the consulting process, (III) functional/sectoral specialties, (IV) managing a consulting firm, (V) developing the profession.

It differs from the rest of the canon in scope and posture: **vs. Block** (philosophical, relational — *who am I being in the room?*) Kubr is structural — *what is the next phase, deliverable, pricing arrangement?* **vs. Burtonshaw-Gunn** (tool-rack of frameworks divorced from a process spine), Kubr embeds tools in an end-to-end process and inside the economics of the firm. **vs. Mabee** (individual consultant's practice discipline), Kubr operates at the level of the profession, the firm, and the international development context — the ILO origin shows in the treatment of public-sector, SME, informal-sector, and developing-country consulting. Kubr is the text to reach for when the question is "what is the standard of practice?"

## The five-phase consulting process (§1.4, pp. 21–24; Part II, Chapters 7–11)

Kubr's signature framework. He acknowledges 3- to 10-phase rivals but adopts a "simple five-phase model" (§1.4, Fig. 1.2, p. 21) used throughout. Phases overlap, can be revisited, or omitted (p. 22).

### 1. Entry (Ch. 7)

*What happens.* First contacts; preliminary problem diagnosis; assignment planning; proposal; contract (§7.1–7.6). *Deliverables.* Terms of reference, assignment plan, written proposal, signed contract. *Pitfalls.* Accepting client's problem statement at face value; over-detailed proposals "giving away expertise before the assignment has been approved" (§7.5, p. 172); contracting on vague problem definitions that diagnosis must renegotiate.

### 2. Diagnosis (Ch. 8)

*What happens.* Restate problem and purpose; diagnose purposes then problems; define and collect facts; analyse and synthesize; feed back (§8.1–8.6). *Deliverables.* Restated problem definition; focus purpose (Box 8.1, p. 183); fact base; diagnostic synthesis; feedback memo. *Pitfalls* (Box 8.2, p. 186). Symptoms-as-problems; preconceived causes; single-discipline lens; top-only perception; wrong urgency; unfinished diagnosis; unclear focus purpose.

### 3. Action planning (Ch. 9)

*What happens.* Develop alternative solutions; evaluate; present proposals; plan implementation including human-side strategy (§1.4, p. 23). *Deliverables.* Alternatives with evaluation; recommended solution; implementation plan addressing resistance. *Pitfalls.* Solutions that merely extrapolate the diagnosis; trivial proposals; single-solution presentation; no human-side rollout plan.

### 4. Implementation (Ch. 10)

*What happens.* Assist with implementation; adjust proposals as reality deviates; train staff. *Deliverables.* Changes installed; staff trained; revisions documented. *Pitfalls.* "Probably not more than 30 to 50 per cent of consulting assignments include implementation" (§1.4, p. 24). The "report-as-deliverable" misconception: "an excellent report cannot provide a guarantee that a new scheme will actually work" (p. 24).

### 5. Termination (Ch. 11)

*What happens.* Evaluation; final report; settling commitments; follow-up plans; withdrawal, possibly gradual. *Deliverables.* Six kinds of benefit to evaluate (§11.2, pp. 249–250): new capabilities, systems, relationships, opportunities, behaviour, performance. *Pitfalls.* No evaluation; missed withdrawal signals (§11.1, p. 247); the consultant who stays too long — "like a fungus that gets inside the client … sucking the blood out" (p. 247); short-term wins masking long-term damage.

## Consulting modes and styles (§3.4–3.5, pp. 69–78)

**Two basic roles** (§3.4):

- **Resource / expert / content role** — consultant supplies technical expertise, diagnoses, designs, recommends; client provides information and accepts or declines proposals (p. 70).
- **Process role** — consultant as agent of change, helping the organization solve its own problems by raising awareness of organizational processes. Schein-derived; "the resource consultant tries to suggest *what* to change, the process consultant suggests mainly *how* to change" (p. 72).

In modern practice the two are combined and switched within a single assignment (pp. 72–73). Schein's "process consultation must be a part of every consultation" (Box 3.4, p. 71): trust, joint problem ownership, recognition that data-gathering is itself intervention.

**Lippitt & Lippitt directive/non-directive continuum** (§3.5, Fig. 3.1, p. 74) — eight roles: Reflector → Process specialist → Fact-finder → Identifier of alternatives → Collaborator → Trainer/educator → Technical expert → Advocate.

**Engagement-shape modes** (§1.4, pp. 24–25): one-time assignment (default); **retainer** (generalist or specialist); **framework contract** (umbrella terms, case-by-case call-off); standing-committee, board, faculty, or counsellor roles.

## The diagnostic phase in depth (Ch. 8, pp. 179–211)

Kubr's deepest treatment of any phase.

**Five-dimension problem definition** (§8.2, pp. 183–184): substance/identity (basis of comparison); organizational and physical location; problem ownership; absolute and relative magnitude; time perspective. Then add causes, other significant relationships, client's potential to solve, and possible directions of further action.

**Purpose-first diagnosis** (§8.2, p. 182, after Nadler & Hibino's *Breakthrough Thinking*). Build an array of purposes from narrow to over-broad, then identify the *focus purpose*. Box 8.1 (p. 183) gives the canonical example — a quality-deterioration problem reframed as "increasing customer satisfaction."

**Defining necessary facts** (§8.3, pp. 195–198). Plan before collecting: what facts, what detail, what sources. Selectivity is the governing principle; cost of fact-gathering must be weighed (§8.3, p. 196).

**Fact sources** (§8.4, pp. 198–200): records, special recordings, observation, memoranda, interviews, surveys, attitude surveys, group discussions.

**Data analysis to synthesis** (§8.5, pp. 201–207). "Analysis evolves gradually to synthesis. Analysis and synthesis are two sides of one coin." The synthesist identifies basic relationships, trends, causes — "probably one of the main things that a new management consultant has to learn" (p. 206).

**Feedback to the client** (§8.6, pp. 207–211) is frequent, honest, distinguishes preliminary from final — and is itself a change intervention.

## The proposal and contract (§7.5–7.6, pp. 171–178; Appendix 4)

**Proposal — four standard sections** (§7.5, p. 171):
1. **Technical aspects** — problem, purpose, approach, work programme. Calibrate detail: too global = no clarity, too detailed = giving away expertise (p. 172).
2. **Staffing** — named seniors and team; availability normally held 6–8 weeks (p. 172).
3. **Consultant background** — overweighted by consultants, underweighted by evaluators (pp. 172–173).
4. **Financial and other terms** — often sealed separately under formal selection (p. 173).

World Bank-style evaluation weights: 5–10% experience, 20–50% methodology, 30–60% key personnel, 0–10% knowledge transfer, 0–10% national participation (p. 174). "Even a highly competent consulting firm stands little chance if it does not propose consultants of the right calibre" (p. 174).

**Contract types** (§7.6, pp. 175–177): verbal (only with familiarity + trust + small scope); letter of agreement (prevailing form, treated as written); written contract (formal, public sector).

**15-item contract checklist** (Box 7.6, p. 176): parties; scope; outputs; inputs; fees/expenses; billing/payment; professional responsibilities; representations; confidentiality; IP/copyright; liability; subcontractors; termination/revision; dispute resolution; signatures.

**Pricing arrangements** (§30.4, pp. 685–693):
- **Fee per unit of time** — traditional; senior:junior ratios up to 3:1.
- **Flat (lump-sum) fee** — cost certainty for client; under-quote risk for consultant; standard in public sector and donor work.
- **Fee plus royalty** — for productized methodology transfer.
- **Contingency fees** — paid on results; once banned, now permitted in most jurisdictions; common in US; attribution disputes (p. 689).
- **Equity participation** — dot-com-era; aligns long-term but ties payment to factors outside consultant control.

**Common contract pitfalls.** Vague problem definitions deferring disputes to diagnosis; detail-level mismatch in proposal; fee-rate negotiation eroding standing; contingency-fee attribution disputes; under-scoped termination and IP ownership.

## Specialist consulting domains (Part III, Chapters 12–26)

- **General and strategic management (Ch. 12).** Corporate strategy, processes/systems/structures, culture, corporate governance. CEO-level work.
- **Information technology (Ch. 13).** Distinct for intangibility of deliverable, technology obsolescence, and vendor-symbiosis ethical dilemmas (§13.2, p. 285); demands hybrid technical-business consultants.
- **Financial management (Ch. 14).** Working capital, capital structure, M&A, accounting/budgetary control, cross-border. Often paired with audit, raising independence questions.
- **Marketing and distribution (Ch. 15).** Product / production / market orientations (§15.1, p. 328); strategy, operations, international, distribution, PR.
- **E-business (Ch. 16).** Freshly crystallized as a 2002 practice; converges strategy and IT.
- **Operations management (Ch. 17).** Operations strategy, product/process perspectives, human aspects.
- **Human resource management (Ch. 18).** HR audit, planning, recruitment, motivation/remuneration, HRD, labour relations. Longest engagement-tail.
- **Knowledge management (Ch. 19).** High tacit content, unclear ROI; close to OD.
- **Productivity & performance (Ch. 20); TQM (Ch. 21); transformation (Ch. 22); CSR (Ch. 23).**
- **Small-business (Ch. 24).** Owner *is* the business; consultant must be a generalist; reluctance-to-use barriers (§24.1, p. 550); 50% mortality in first two years (p. 549).
- **Informal sector (Ch. 25).** Developing-country focus; consultant-as-trainer.
- **Public sector (Ch. 26).** Most problems embedded in larger social/political issues (§26.2, p. 590); rule-bound procurement; four service categories — strategy/policy advice, programme design, machinery adjustment, change facilitation (§26.1, pp. 588–589).

## Managing a consulting firm (Part IV, Ch. 27–35; Part V, 36–38)

- **Fundamentals (Ch. 27).** Managing a *professional service* differs from a product business: asset is people, product is intangible, quality judged by client. Balance professional excellence and business viability (§27.4).
- **Strategy (Ch. 28).** Scope, client base, growth, internationalization, image — which clients, problems, methods, geographies.
- **Marketing (Ch. 29).** Constrained by ethics; client perspective is starting point; existing-client marketing is highest leverage (§29.5).
- **Costs and fees (Ch. 30).** Five fee-setting methods (see Pricing); value billing (§30.6); collections (§30.8).
- **Assignment management (Ch. 31); quality (Ch. 32); financial control (Ch. 33); KM (Ch. 34); structure (Ch. 35); careers (Ch. 36); training (Ch. 37); future (Ch. 38).**

**Professionalism and ethics (Ch. 6).** Six elements (§6.2): technical competence; client interest first; impartiality; confidentiality; commitment to results; high ethical standards. Conflict-of-interest non-obvious traps in Box 6.2, pp. 133–134.

## Cross-references

- **vs. Block (*Flawless Consulting*).** Block: authentic-self contracting, 50/50 responsibility, naming resistance. Kubr's contract checklist asks *what is in the document?* — Block asks *what is in the room?* Block's contracting model fills the gap Kubr glosses in §7.4–7.6.
- **vs. Burtonshaw-Gunn (*Essential Tools*).** Tool-rack (SWOT, PESTLE, BCG, Porter, Ansoff) without a process spine. Use them together — Kubr says *do diagnosis*, Burtonshaw says *here are 30 tools for it*.
- **vs. Mabee (*The Management Consultant*).** Individual practice discipline — daily habits, personal craft. Kubr spans individual, firm, and profession.
- **vs. Maister (*Managing the Professional Service Firm*) — implicit.** Maister goes deeper on firm economics than Kubr's Part IV; push to Maister for firm-leadership questions.

## Pitfalls Kubr documents

- **Mistaking symptoms for problems** (Box 8.2, p. 186) — most common diagnostic error.
- **Pre-conceived causes** (Box 8.2) — pattern-matching suppresses fresh evidence.
- **Single-discipline lens, top-only perception, unfinished diagnosis, unclear focus purpose** (Box 8.2).
- **Vague problem statement in contract** (§8.1, p. 180) — disputes surface at diagnosis.
- **The "report-as-deliverable" misconception and stopping before implementation** (§1.4, p. 24) — only 30–50% of assignments include implementation.
- **Aggressive diagnostic posture** (§8.1, p. 181) — questions read as fault-finding.
- **Spontaneous mid-diagnosis change of work methods** (§8.1, pp. 181–182).
- **Withdrawal-signal blindness; staying too long** (§11.1, p. 247) — the "fungus" pattern.
- **No evaluation** (§11.2, pp. 248–249) — both parties lose the learning.
- **Contingency-fee attribution disputes** (§30.4, p. 689).
- **Conflict-of-interest blind spots** (Box 6.2, pp. 133–134).
- **SME engagement reluctance** (§24.1, p. 550) — designed-around constraint, not avoidable error.
- **Public-sector "private-sector experience suffices" error** (§26.2, p. 589).
