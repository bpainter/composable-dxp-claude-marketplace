# Consulting Foundations

**Shared practices across consulting-strategy skills: management-consultant, digital-strategist, innovation-strategist, project-manager, personalization-strategist, proposal-strategist, sow-risk-advisor**

---

## 1. Engagement Structure

Classic consulting engagement phases, each with distinct objectives:

- **Discovery** (2–4 weeks)
  - Understand current state, pain points, organizational structure, strategic goals
  - Conduct interviews, review existing documentation, observe workflows
  - Build stakeholder map and identify blockers
  - Deliverable: Discovery summary or baseline assessment

- **Diagnosis** (2–6 weeks)
  - Root cause analysis of problems identified in Discovery
  - Assess capability gaps, process inefficiencies, technology fit
  - Quantify impact (cost, time, quality, risk)
  - Deliverable: Problem statement and recommendations

- **Solution Design** (4–8 weeks)
  - Design target-state architecture or approach
  - Build business case, define success metrics, estimate resource needs
  - Create implementation roadmap with phases and milestones
  - Deliverable: Design document, detailed implementation plan, business case

- **Implementation** (4 weeks–12+ months)
  - Execute planned work, manage day-to-day delivery
  - Track progress against plan, manage scope and risks
  - Conduct change management and stakeholder enablement
  - Deliverable: Working solutions, training materials, process documentation

- **Close-Out** (1–2 weeks)
  - Confirm all deliverables accepted and signed off
  - Complete knowledge transfer and handover documentation
  - Conduct retrospective, document lessons learned
  - Establish support model (if ongoing)

**When to use each phase:**
- Always include Discovery and Diagnosis for complex, strategic work
- Skip or condense for well-scoped, tactical engagements
- Implementation phases scale with engagement duration (can run parallel in agile models)
- Close-Out is non-negotiable for formal engagements

## 2. Stakeholder Management

Identify stakeholders early and maintain engagement throughout.

**Power-Interest Grid:**
- **High Power, High Interest** → Manage closely; regular updates, incorporate feedback
- **High Power, Low Interest** → Keep satisfied; inform regularly; escalate issues
- **Low Power, High Interest** → Keep informed; involve in solutions; gather ground-truth feedback
- **Low Power, Low Interest** → Monitor; minimal communication; escalate only on changes

**RACI Matrix Roles:**
- **R (Responsible)** → Does the work
- **A (Accountable)** → Final decision-maker (one person per activity)
- **C (Consulted)** → Provides input and expertise
- **I (Informed)** → Receives updates on status/decisions

Always assign RACI for major work streams to avoid role confusion.

**Stakeholder Mapping:**
- Map by function (Finance, IT, Ops, etc.) and level (executive, manager, individual contributor)
- Identify sponsor (executive champion who unblocks), users (affected by change), influencers (opinion leaders)
- Assess sentiment: aligned, neutral, or resistant
- Plan engagement differently for each group (e.g., sponsors need milestone reviews; users need hands-on enablement)

**Managing Sponsors vs. Users vs. Influencers:**
- **Sponsors** → Monthly steering reviews, exception-based escalations, strategic input on trade-offs
- **Users** → Working sessions, feedback loops, training and job aids
- **Influencers** → Involve early to shape opinions, leverage to cascade messaging

## 3. Scope & Change Control

Prevent scope creep and align stakeholders on what's in and out.

**Scope Definition (in Statement of Work or charter):**
- Explicitly state what is included (deliverables, timeline, resources)
- Explicitly state what is excluded or out of scope
- Define acceptance criteria for each deliverable (testable, measurable)
- Document assumptions (e.g., "Client will provide test data by Week 4")

**Change Request Process:**
1. Log request with description, rationale, and estimated impact
2. Assess impact on timeline, budget, and other work streams
3. Present trade-offs to sponsor: approve, defer, or reject
4. If approved, update SOW, schedule, and resource plan
5. Document decision and communicate to team and stakeholders

**Impact Assessment:**
- Quantify effort (hours/cost) and timeline (delay in weeks)
- Identify downstream dependencies (what else is affected)
- Surface risk (e.g., "Adding 2 weeks to timeline puts us past budget")

**Scope Creep Indicators:**
- Requests that don't come through formal change process
- Expanding acceptance criteria mid-delivery
- New stakeholders introducing new requirements
- "Quick asks" that grow into larger efforts

**Mitigation:** Say "we can do that" but ask "what will we not do instead?" Force trade-off conversations.

## 4. Client Communication

Build trust through transparency and predictable cadence.

**Status Reporting Cadence:**
- **Weekly** (teams delivering) → Traffic light (red/yellow/green), tasks completed, blockers, next week plan
- **Bi-weekly** (medium engagements) → Milestone progress, timeline status, budget burn, risks and decisions needed
- **Monthly** (steering level) → High-level progress, strategic decisions, financials, upcoming milestones

**Steering Committee Structure:**
- **Chair** → Sponsor or engagement lead (runs decision-making)
- **Members** → Key stakeholders (IT, finance, operations, end-user reps)
- **Cadence** → Monthly (or per major milestone for short engagements)
- **Agenda** → Executive summary, 2–3 decisions needed, risks, next milestone preview
- **Outcome** → Documented decisions, signed-off minutes

**Escalation Paths:**
- Define upfront: what issues escalate, to whom, when
- Example: Budget overruns >5% escalate to Finance VP; timeline slips >2 weeks escalate to Sponsor
- Aim for prevention, not surprises: escalate early when issues are emerging, not when crisis is imminent

**"No Surprises" Principle:**
- Bad news travels up the chain first (to sponsor privately), not to the full steering committee
- Sponsor is briefed on issues before steering meeting so they can shape narrative
- Early warning signals are surfaced as soon as risk is identified, not when impact is certain

## 5. Risk Management

Identify, assess, and actively manage risks throughout engagement.

**Risk Identification:**
- Conduct risk workshop in Discovery/early Planning phases
- Invite cross-functional stakeholders to surface threats
- Categories: technical, resource, schedule, organizational change, scope, external
- Document in a risk register (log updated monthly or per milestone)

**Probability × Impact Matrix:**
- **Probability** (1–5) × **Impact** (1–5) = Risk Score (1–25)
- Plot risks visually: high-probability/high-impact risks require immediate attention
- Use to prioritize mitigation efforts and escalation decisions
- Example: "Data migration failure (Prob 3, Impact 5 = 15) needs contingency plan"

**Response Strategies:**
- **Mitigate** → Reduce probability or impact (e.g., hire expert, run pilot, add time buffer)
- **Accept** → Acknowledge risk and build contingency reserve (budget, time, resources)
- **Transfer** → Shift risk to a third party (e.g., vendor SLA, insurance, subcontractor agreement)
- **Avoid** → Eliminate the threat (e.g., change approach, out-source, descope)

**Risk Register Basics:**
- Row per risk: ID, description, category, owner, probability, impact, mitigation plan, status
- Update monthly; re-assess as conditions change
- Track mitigations to completion
- Present summary to steering committee (top 5–10 risks only)

## 6. Deliverable Quality

Ensure deliverables meet expectations and gain formal acceptance.

**Review Cycles:**
- **Internal review** → Consultant team reviews work for completeness, accuracy, consistency
- **Client review** (draft) → Stakeholder reviews; 1–2 rounds of feedback, 5–7 business days per cycle
- **Formal review** (final) → Sponsor/acceptance authority reviews for sign-off; address remaining comments

**Acceptance Criteria:**
- Defined upfront for each major deliverable (e.g., "Code passes automated tests + manual QA; documentation is 100% complete")
- Test against criteria before sending to client review
- Use as basis for sign-off conversations ("Does this meet all acceptance criteria?")

**Sign-Off Process:**
1. Confirm deliverable meets all acceptance criteria
2. Obtain formal written approval (email or sign-off form)
3. Archive signed-off version in deliverables folder with date/approver name
4. Update status report to reflect acceptance
5. Move to next deliverable or phase

**Version Control for Documents:**
- Naming: `[Deliverable]_v[#]_[Date]_[Approver Initials].docx`
- Example: `Implementation_Plan_v3_2026-02-15_JD.docx`
- Store in shared folder with read-only access to old versions
- Include "Version History" table in document footer (what changed, when, by whom)
- Final approved version clearly marked "APPROVED" or "FINAL"

## Cross-Cutting: The Engagement Discipline

Strong consulting execution balances **clarity** (scope, roles, decisions), **transparency** (communication, risks, trade-offs), and **rigor** (acceptance criteria, sign-offs, documentation). These practices apply to every role in consulting-strategy.
