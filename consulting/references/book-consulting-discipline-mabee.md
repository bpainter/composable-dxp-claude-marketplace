# The Consulting Discipline: Five Core Habits — Bruce Mabee (2009)

**Source digest** for the consulting plugin. The book is a 32-page "Short Book" workshop companion; page numbers refer to that pagination (the cover/intro is unnumbered, then "1" begins at the Introduction). Cite as "(Mabee, *TCD*, p. X)" inside skill bodies. Note: the book is genuinely short and repetitive by design — it is a workshop reinforcement card more than a treatise. Pull quotes and structure are tight; do not pad citations.

## Thesis

Mabee's distinct angle is that consulting is a **discipline of habits**, not a body of knowledge or a relationship philosophy. Five recurring micro-practices — done badly by default, done well only with deliberate attention — separate professionals who help clients reach outcomes from professionals who deliver impressive but useless work. Where Block frames consulting as a relational philosophy ("authentic behavior" and the contracting conversation) and Kubr frames it as a profession with a multi-phase process, Mabee frames it as a **personal practice**: posture, outcome focus, scanning, teamwork, agility — checked moment by moment, half hour by half hour. The book opens with a definition: "Facilitating people to achieve their own goals" (p. 2), and a hard claim — "There is no consultant except when there is a client. Only the client makes consulting happen" (p. 2). The five habits are presented as a Cairn (a stacked-rock trail marker), not a pyramid: the foundation is Attitude and Outcome; the advanced layer is Scanning, Teamwork, Agility (cover and p. 4).

Mabee's voice is workshop-trainer pragmatic — short cases, "Sacred Cow" sidebars that name received wisdom worth challenging (Problem Solving, p. 12; Strategic Plan, p. 18; Team Building, p. 22), and explicit drill cycles. He treats every habit as something a consultant can practice "like using machines at the gym" (p. 10).

## Mabee's core frameworks

### 1. The Five Core Habits — Cairn structure (cover, pp. 6–7, 17)

Two **Foundation** habits and three **Advanced** habits, layered:
- **ATTITUDE** — Who do you seem to think you are?
- **OUTCOME** — Where are they getting?
- **SCANNING** — What else will make or break the outcome?
- **TEAMWORK** — Who can make or break the outcome?
- **AGILITY** — How will we stay ahead of the changes?

Each habit is presented with a Main Idea, a Simple Model, and a Useful Tool (pp. 7, 17). The five habits map to a five-pointed star reminder (p. 30).

### 2. The Four Postures of Influence (Habit 1, pp. 8–11)

Mabee's signature engagement model. Two axes — push vs. pull, and us vs. them — produce four postures that the consultant must shift between in real time:

| Posture | Action | When to use | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TO Them** | Push, sell, order; classic doctor/police model | If authority is respected; in crisis; if client lacks capability | Resistance, dependency (Sue: "right but gone in a year") |
| **FOR Them** | Serve, deliver what asked; classic service model | If client doesn't trust you yet; if no skill transfer needed | Low ownership, unchallenging (Doug: "delivered, dejected, underappreciated") |
| **WITH Them** | Join as teammate, partner | If flexibility required; if trust is present; to transfer complex skill | Expensive, slow (Mr. Teamwork: "perfect but late and over budget") |
| **BY Them** | Let them, delegate, get out of the way | If client is independent or resisting; if client feels insecure | Misfires, may appear lazy (Mrs. Delegado: "magic was undocumented") |

Source attribution: "From the article by Bruce Mabee in *Training Today*, Chicagoland Chapter of ASTD, 2000" (p. 11). Tagged as Element I (Engagement) in Mabee's Improvement-Leader's Framework.

### 3. The Three Core Questions (Habit 2, pp. 13–14)

Before designing any solution, the consultant clarifies:
1. **WHO ELSE?** Who is involved, like it or not? Who is affected? Who must pitch in? Who wants your help (the client)?
2. **WHERE TO?** What outcomes do these people all care about?
3. **HOW?** What actions will get these people to these outcomes?

Mabee credits the underlying **S-T-P** (Situation–Target–Plan) model to Frederick Foxmire and John Wallen, and the A-B-C variant to Gerard Egan (p. 14). Tagged Element II (Investment).

### 4. STP — Situation–Target–Plan (p. 15)

A change-the-plan-as-the-situation-changes loop. Three nodes (Situation, Target, Plan) joined by six change-questions ("How is this better? What would 'better' look like? What can you do to change this? What actions create this? What creates this? What will these actions create?"). The diagram is drawn as a triangle with bidirectional arrows — every change in one node forces re-examination of the others.

### 5. Three Rings — Using This Moment Strategically (Habit 3, p. 20)

Concentric strategic frame:
- **Outer ring — CONTEXT.** What larger forces are affecting this situation?
- **Middle ring — PROJECT.** What are we working on?
- **Inner ring — THIS MOMENT.** What is the best use of our time right now?

The discipline: scan all three rings before acting in this half hour. "It matters what we do in this half hour" (p. 20).

### 6. Half-Hour Strategic Plan / Force-Field Priorities (p. 21)

A speed-strategy template. Four boxes: **Mission** (core), **Vision** (best future) and **Worst Case** (where we must not end up), **Strategic Forces** shaping the future, and **Strategic Actions** to address those forces. Force Field Analysis is credited to Kurt Lewin (p. 21). The provocation: a thirty-minute force field every few weeks beats a year-long planning process every five years (p. 27).

### 7. The Change Team — Four Roles (Habit 4, pp. 23–24)

The team that can span existing coalitions:
- **Sponsors** — authority, influence, budget for major goals; pull together leaders of conflicting groups. Common mistake: insulting people deemed "not essential."
- **Client Leaders** — business unit executive and leadership group, formal and informal; lead business and human issues together. Common mistake: managing numbers and human reactions separately.
- **Action Team** — managers/professionals with trusted links to each stakeholder group. Common mistake: hero behavior, then giving up.
- **Change Consultant** — internal manager, OD/HR, or external; convenes and supports. Common mistake: taking over and taking sides — "forgetting to consult."

### 8. Quick Loop Action Research / One Good Hour (Habit 5, pp. 27–28)

The agility tool. A repeating Listen–Act loop, demonstrated as a 60-minute meeting structure with seven timed transitions (0:00 greetings, 0:05 where to, 0:15 big picture, 0:25 alternatives, 0:35 try something, 0:50 what we learned, 0:55 next action). The principle: "Listen — Act — Listen — Act — Listen…" repeated forever (p. 27). The Action Research Sequence: Entering, Contracting, Diagnosis, Planning, Acting, Feedback, Institutionalizing, Ending (p. 27).

### 9. Waves of Change (p. 29)

A change-leadership model derived from Beckhard, Blanchard, Bridges, Kubler-Ross, Lawler, Lewin, Senge, Tichy, Weisbord. Three sequenced tasks for each wave:
1. **Support stressed people** (tool: Stages of Grieving — Denial, Guilt, Anger, Depression, Acceptance)
2. **Align the visions** (tool: Force Field Analysis — drivers and restraints)
3. **Act on the opportunities** (tool: Continue / Stop / Start feedback)

### 10. Consulting Project Map — five elements on one page (back cover, p. 32)

A worksheet capturing Goals, Situation, Forces (helping/interfering), Stakeholders (Owners/Sponsors, Actively Involved, Indirectly Affected, Consulting Parties), and Plans (Continue / Stop / Start). The single-sheet integration of the five habits.

## The consulting habits — what disciplined practice looks like

Mabee names recurring practices that distinguish disciplined from undisciplined consultants. Each is keyed to a habit and is what the consultant does *moment to moment*:

- **Watch your attitude in real time** — notice the posture you're projecting and the posture the client is reacting to; "shift gears" before the relationship calcifies (pp. 8, 11).
- **Substitute outcome for solution** — when the client asks for "the latest blah-blah-blah they saw at a conference," ask "where do you want to get with the solution?" The Three Core Questions are a forcing function (p. 13).
- **Find Shared Goals before setting clear goals** — premature goal-setting is dangerous when the situation isn't understood; "Don't set a goal until you know the possibilities" (p. 13). Test goals against all key stakeholders.
- **Scan the outer ring** — before acting, look at markets, economy, culture, politics; "the tectonic plates may have shifted this morning" (p. 18).
- **Pursue Win-Win-Win** — always go for the largest scale of winning; serving competitors and even adversaries (Fisher and Ury cited, p. 23).
- **Build coalitions, not teams** — "team building" that builds just The Team creates barriers with other teams. Build the team of people who will make or break the outcome across boundaries (p. 22).
- **Act in short loops** — small action, observe, adjust; "If an assessment is off because it is even one hour out-of-date, it is simply harmful" (p. 26).
- **Re-contract during the work** — the saga case shows Chris calling time out: "Am I helping you get where you want to get?" (p. 31).
- **Practice tools on small issues before a crisis hits** (p. 27).
- **Celebrate explicit endings** — note unfinished business, name who needs to know (p. 27, step 8).

## The relationship with the client

Mabee's framing overlaps Block on three points and diverges on three:

**Overlap with Block.**
- Both reject the expert-pill cartoon. Mabee: "the iconic scene is a cartoon. It is rare in the life of real consultants" (p. 2). The expert-with-blue-suit-and-PowerPoint is the antithesis.
- Both insist the client owns the work. Mabee: "the client's work is the real work. Whatever the consultant does may greatly help the client's work, but it remains the background work" (p. 2).
- Both treat re-contracting as ongoing, not one-shot.

**Where Mabee differs.**
- **Posture flexibility over authentic behavior.** Block prizes one stance — authenticity, putting words to what is happening. Mabee prizes *gear-shifting*: TO/FOR/WITH/BY, picked by situation (pp. 8–11). For Mabee a good consultant is a posture-switcher, not a single voice.
- **Habit and drill, not philosophy.** "Anyone gets stronger with regular practice of each posture, like using machines at the gym" (p. 10). Block reflects; Mabee drills.
- **Three sister practices alongside consulting**, not consulting in isolation. Mabee distinguishes Consulting from Selling (persuading) and Serving (Burger King "have it your way"), giving each its own purpose-outcome-cost table (pp. 5–6). The discipline is partly knowing which mode you're in.

## Engagement and lifecycle

Mabee provides a compact eight-step Action Research Sequence (p. 27) that is his lifecycle view:

1. **Entering** — Are we being asked to help something that can make a difference?
2. **Contracting** — What are key people ready to take on? Are they ready to persist together?
3. **Diagnosis / Discovery** — What forces will make or break our efforts?
4. **Planning** — Strategy, who/what/when, contingencies.
5. **Acting / Monitoring** — What is actually happening? What is unexpected?
6. **Feedback / Adaptation** — Continue / Stop / Start.
7. **Institutionalizing / Refreezing** — Are effective actions and processes becoming habits?
8. **Ending** — Have we celebrated successes and noted unfinished business? Who needs to know?

The "Consulting Saga" two-page comic (pp. 30–31) walks the same lifecycle through 12 panels — Pat asking Chris, exploring interests, becoming a client, scanning, the business story, facing the politics, encouraging action, expanding stakeholders, adjusting as they go, re-negotiating the relationship, learning in real time, follow-through. The saga makes one structural point: "There is no straight path to the important goals" (p. 31). Lifecycle is iterative, with re-contracting in the middle.

The "How to Consult" formula (p. 30) compresses the lifecycle into a single page: start small FOR Them, ask outcomes, scan outside the project, hook in the right people early, do steps 1–4 in 30 minutes then test for a few days, then cycle the star again at larger scale.

## Common consulting failure modes

Mabee names four explicitly on p. 30 ("What can go wrong?"):

- **Weak discipline** — "You don't do anything that resembles the process above. It's too hard." Default drift back to ad-hoc reaction.
- **Giving up** — discouragement when clients constantly demand "cutting edge solutions" or things you know won't work.
- **Seduced by fame** — "You find out how much fun it can be to do things TO Them as they applaud. You lose sight of helping them achieve their goals, because that feels tedious." The expert-celebrity trap.
- **Arrogance of being right** — "After a few too many successes, you feel you don't have to prove yourself. You increase your advice TO Them, which becomes AT Them, and ends up WITHOUT Them."

Other failure modes scattered through the book:
- **Solving the wrong problem** — the Sales Pay case (p. 19): a $135K compensation study fixed the wrong issue; the right diagnosis was an industry shift to internal selling.
- **Naïvely accepting the official Strategic Plan as the real strategy** (Sacred Cow, p. 18).
- **"Team building" that builds barriers** — building just The Team beats the department down the hall (Sacred Cow, p. 22).
- **Premature goal-setting** — setting clear goals before understanding the situation wastes effort and creates dangerous certainty (p. 13).
- **The Change Consultant taking over and taking sides** — "forgetting to consult" (p. 24).
- **Rigid problem-solving steps in volatile situations** — the canonical Define-Assess-Create-Choose-Implement-Evaluate sequence "is often a good approach, but it is often not a good approach. When the situation is in volatile change, or when groups of people exhibit strong emotional and political dynamics, the step-by-step approach may not work" (Sacred Cow, p. 12).

## Cross-references

**vs. Block, *Flawless Consulting*.** Block is the relational philosopher: contracting, authentic behavior, dealing with resistance, the inner stance of the consultant. Mabee shares the rejection of the expert-cartoon and the client-owns-the-work claim, but his answer to "how do I show up?" is multi-modal (TO/FOR/WITH/BY), not single-stance authenticity. Where Block writes essays, Mabee writes drill cards. Pair them: Block for *why*, Mabee for *what to practice this morning*.

**vs. Kubr, *Management Consulting: A Guide to the Profession*.** Kubr is the encyclopedic profession-handbook — phases, deliverables, methods, careers, ethics, sectors. Mabee's Action Research Sequence (p. 27) is a compressed Kubr-style lifecycle — Entering through Ending — but the book is otherwise indifferent to the profession qua profession. Kubr is for "what does management consulting look like?" Mabee is for "what habits do I drill?"

**vs. Burtonshaw, *Essential Tools for Management Consulting*.** Burtonshaw is a tools catalog — frameworks, matrices, models. Mabee names tools but treats each as a *habit prompt*: the Three Core Questions exist to interrupt premature solutioning, not to be filled out. Burtonshaw says "here is a tool"; Mabee says "here is a moment to reach for this tool, and here is the failure mode if you don't."

**vs. Block on the Four Postures.** Block's "expert / pair-of-hands / collaborative" trio prefigures Mabee's TO/FOR/WITH; Mabee adds **BY Them** (delegation/empowerment) as a fourth posture and frames all four as gears, not types of consultant.

## How consulting skills should use it

### `consulting-management-consultant`
1. **Four Postures** (pp. 8–11) — gear-shift discipline; pair with Block's stance language.
2. **Three Core Questions** (p. 14) — anti-solutioning forcing function for any new request.
3. **Three Rings** (p. 20) — pre-meeting strategic scan template.
4. **Action Research Sequence** (p. 27) — 8-step lifecycle compressed to one page; cross-reference with Kubr's longer phase model.
5. **Consulting Project Map** (p. 32) — single-sheet engagement integration.
6. **Failure modes** (p. 30) — "Weak discipline / Giving up / Seduced by fame / Arrogance of being right" as a self-check.

### `consulting-proposal-strategist`
1. **Three Core Questions** (p. 14) — write proposals around outcomes (WHERE TO), not deliverables.
2. **Half-Hour Strategic Plan** (p. 21) — Mission/Vision/Worst-Case/Forces/Actions as a proposal scaffold.
3. **Shared Goals** (p. 13) — test the proposal against all key stakeholders before submission; reject premature goal-setting in proposals.
4. **STP** (p. 15) — the proposal's plan must show how it adapts as situation changes (anti-static-SOW).
5. **Outcome vs. Solution** distinction (p. 12) — "the iconic blue-suit cartoon is rare in real life" (p. 2); proposals frame the client's work, not the consultant's deliverables.
6. **The Sales Pay case** (p. 19) — $135K compensation study fixed the wrong issue; cite when arguing for diagnostic scoping over fixed-bid solutioning.

### `consulting-change-management-advisor`
1. **The Change Team — four roles** (pp. 23–24) — Sponsors, Client Leaders, Action Team, Change Consultant; each role's "common mistake" is the most useful column.
2. **Waves of Change** (p. 29) — Support / Align / Act as a recurring cycle, not a one-time sequence; integrates Kubler-Ross grieving stages with Lewin force-field.
3. **Coalition Behavior** (p. 23) — build the team of people across boundaries who will make or break the outcome, not just the work team.
4. **Win-Win-Win** (p. 23) — always go for the largest-scale of winning; serve adversaries (cite with Fisher & Ury).
5. **Action Research Sequence** (p. 27) — Institutionalizing/Refreezing as the under-loved step: "are the most effective actions and processes becoming habits?"
6. **One Good Hour** (p. 28) — Listen-Act meeting template for stakeholder check-ins during change rollout.

## Pitfalls — undisciplined consulting failure modes

A consolidated list, citing the book:

- **Giving the client the magic bullet they asked for** when it hurts long-term capability (p. 12).
- **Setting clear goals before understanding the situation** — the submarine analogy (p. 13).
- **Following the Strategic Plan as if it were the real strategy** (p. 18).
- **Building team-building that creates barriers with other teams** (p. 22).
- **Managing numbers and human reactions separately** (Client Leader mistake, p. 24).
- **Hero behavior on the Action Team, then giving up** (p. 24).
- **Change Consultant taking over and taking sides** — "forgetting to consult" (p. 24).
- **Plan-and-then-work-the-plan in fast-moving situations** — assessments become harmful when they go an hour out of date (p. 26).
- **Procrastination disguised as thoroughness** — "Sometimes, thoroughness is a form of procrastination" (p. 27).
- **The four named meta-failures** — Weak discipline, Giving up, Seduced by fame, Arrogance of being right (p. 30).
