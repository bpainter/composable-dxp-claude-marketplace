# Essential Tools for Management Consulting — Simon A. Burtonshaw-Gunn (Wiley, 2010)

**Source digest** for the consulting plugin. Page numbers refer to the 147-page Wiley 2010 edition (ISBN 978-0-470-74593-9). Cite as "(Burtonshaw-Gunn, *ETMC*, p. X)" inside skill bodies.

## Thesis

Burtonshaw-Gunn distils a working consultant's day-to-day kit into a single short volume. Where Kubr's *Management Consulting: A Guide to the Profession* is encyclopedic about the trade and Block's *Flawless Consulting* is philosophical about the engagement relationship, *Essential Tools* is a curated catalogue: roughly forty named tools, models, and checklists drawn mostly from the author's earlier *Essential Management Toolbox* (2008), each presented with a one-paragraph definition, a "when to use it" note, and a one- or two-figure visual. The book is structured around an engagement spine — profession (Ch. 1), winning the work (Ch. 2), managing the project (Ch. 3), delivering it (Ch. 4) — and ends with a "Top-Ten Consultancy Tools" chapter (Ch. 5, pp. 107–133) that the author argues a consultant should be able to deploy in any combination across most assignments. The unifying claim: tools are interlinked and should be sequenced through the assignment, not reached for ad-hoc (Introduction, p. xviii; Ch. 5, p. 108).

## Tool catalogue summary

### Strategic analysis (mostly Ch. 5)

| Tool | One-line definition | Typical use | Where it fails |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Porter's Five Forces** (pp. 108–110, Figs 5.1–5.2) | New entrants, substitutes, rivalry, supplier power, buyer power as the structural determinants of an industry's profitability | Industry-attractiveness diagnostic at start of strategy work | Assumes static industry boundaries; weak on platforms, ecosystems, complementors |
| **SWOT** (pp. 109–116, Fig 5.3) | Internal strengths/weaknesses × external opportunities/threats grid | Snap-shot summary in strategy, change, marketing | Treated as the analysis instead of a starter; volume of bullets confused with weighted impact (p. 113) |
| **PEST / PESTLE** (pp. 117–118; also Ch. 3, p. 93) | Political, Economic, Social, Technological (+ Legal, Environmental) external scan | Environmental analysis inside a strategy formulation cycle | Becomes a bullet-point dump with no "so-what"; legal/environmental layered on without rigour |
| **Strategy Formulation Process** (pp. 116–118, Fig 5.4) | Three-question frame — where are we now / where should we go / how do we get there — wrapping environmental, industry, company, and SWOT analyses | Master spine for a strategy engagement | Linearity disguises the iteration that real strategy needs |
| **Value Chain (Porter)** (pp. 16–17) | Primary and support activities as the unit of competitive analysis | Internal capability and benchmarking work | Author notes it is hard to apply to consultancy services themselves |
| **Understanding the Product/Service** (pp. 118–119, Fig 5.5) | Workshop-friendly question set linking current performance to vision and barriers | Strategic planning at corporate, BU, or work-group level | Overlaps SWOT; can produce wishlists rather than a plan |
| **Marketing Mix / 4Ps (+ 4Cs)** (pp. 120–121, Fig 5.6) | Product, Price, Place, Promotion — matched to Customer, Cost, Convenience, Communication | Aligning offer to identified customer needs | 4Ps under-cover services (Booms & Bitner's 7Ps); used as taxonomy not as a decision tool |
| **BCG Growth/Share Matrix** (pp. 121–123, Fig 5.7) | Star, Cash Cow, Question Mark, Dog quadrants on relative market share × market growth | Multi-product portfolio resource-allocation decisions | Reduces strategy to four labels; relative-share metric ignores margin and synergies |
| **Pressure-Points Matrix** (Ch. 2, pp. 44–46) | Sectors / activities where consulting demand concentrates — shapers, enablers, hot spots, passives | Consultancy-firm market positioning | Specific to consulting-firm marketing; not a client tool |
| **Trilateral "C" Model of Consultancy Governance** (Ch. 1, pp. 26–28, Fig 1.5) | Capability, Control, Command across Company processes, Conduct, Competency | Governance diagnostic for the consultant–client relationship | The author's own model; less established than the others |

### Diagnostic / problem-solving (mostly Ch. 4)

| Tool | One-line definition | Typical use | Where it fails |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ishikawa / Fishbone** (Ch. 4, pp. 90–91, Fig 4.3) | Cause-and-effect spine grouping causes by Materials, Resources, Method, Plant, Measurement | Group diagnosis of a defined problem in a workshop | Forces causes into pre-set bones; large fish, no insight |
| **Mind Mapping** (p. 91) | Free-form visual association from a central concept | Creative thinking when fishbone categories are too constraining | Hard to converge on actions |
| **Force Field Analysis (Lewin)** (Ch. 4, pp. 91–93, Figs 4.4–4.5) | Driving forces vs. resisting forces around a desired change | Diagnosing readiness for change in a facilitated setting | "Forces" are often listed without weighting or evidence |
| **Six Honest Serving Men (Kipling)** (pp. 132–133, Fig 5.11) | What / Why / When / How / Where / Who as a question scaffold | Continuous-improvement interviews and workshop probes | A question set, not an analysis — easy to skip the answers |
| **Research Strategies grid** (Ch. 4, pp. 85–89, Fig 4.2) | Survey / structured / semi-structured / unstructured interviews / participant observation, plotted on numbers studied × researcher involvement | Picking a data-collection strategy fit to scope and time | Does not address sampling rigour or analysis plan |
| **Quantitative vs. Qualitative checklist (Robson)** (pp. 87–89) | Eight-item readiness check for picking a research mode | Scoping research-led engagements | Treats the two as either/or rather than mixed-methods |

| Tool | One-line definition | Typical use | Where it fails |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Business Plan Feasibility Check** (Ch. 5, pp. 130–131, Fig 5.10) | Three-block question bank — Management, Marketing, Money | Stress-test of a client business plan before investor presentation | Long question list, no scoring; produces concerns, not decisions |

| **Change Pyramid** (Ch. 5, pp. 123–125, Fig 5.8) | Tasks → Roles → Structure → Behaviour → Culture, easy-to-hard | Sequencing change interventions; harvesting "low-hanging fruit" first | Implies culture is always last; skips the case for top-down values work |

### Project management

| Tool | One-line definition | Typical use | Where it fails |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Project Variables triangle (Time / Cost / Quality + Scope + Customer Satisfaction)** (Ch. 3, pp. 67–73, Fig 3.3) | The classic triangle re-drawn with scope and satisfaction as anchors | Setting and re-setting expectations on every consulting project | Treated as a slogan; trade-offs not actually negotiated with the client |

| **Risk-Management Benefits map** (Ch. 3, p. 74, Fig 3.4) | Eleven benefits arranged around a central node | Selling the value of risk management to a sceptical sponsor | Uncritical; every risk programme claims all eleven |

The book does **not** cover (despite the publisher's framing): RACI, Kotter's eight-step model by name, ADKAR, Six Sigma DMAIC, Lean / VSM, BPR in any depth, Gantt or critical-path mechanics, work breakdown structures, decision matrices, or scenario planning. These are referenced only obliquely or pushed to the sister volumes (*Essential Tools for Organizational Performance*, *…for Operations Management*, *…for Marketing*).

## The five most consequential tools — deeper treatment

### 1. Schein's three modes — Expert, Doctor–Patient, Process (Ch. 1, pp. 8–11)

The single most load-bearing model in the book. Mode is determined by **who owns the problem**: Expert = client transfers ownership and has correctly diagnosed the problem; Doctor–Patient = consultant diagnoses then prescribes; Process = client retains ownership throughout and the consultant teaches diagnosis. Burtonshaw-Gunn's gloss: Expert mode fails when the client has framed the wrong problem (p. 10); Process mode is the only mode that builds long-term client capability (p. 10). Use to set engagement stance in writing — most disputes about consultant value are really disputes about which mode the engagement is in.

### 2. Project Variables — Time, Cost, Quality, Scope, Customer Satisfaction (Ch. 3, pp. 67–73, Fig 3.3)

Re-draws the classic triangle to put **scope** and **customer satisfaction** as anchor points alongside the three constraints. The author's argument: most consulting failures are scope failures, not time/cost/quality failures (p. 72). Burtonshaw-Gunn's drumbeat through the chapter is to make scope a written, agreed, re-baselined artefact — not a one-line bid commitment. Cite when arguing for a written scope letter, change-control process, or fixed-fee scope-creep clause.

### 3. SWOT — done properly (Ch. 5, pp. 109–116)

The book's longest single tool treatment. Two warnings worth quoting verbatim: "It should not be assumed that completing each quadrant is the full analysis — whilst an organization may have 10 strengths and only two weaknesses it is not the volume of each category but their severity. Indeed, two major weaknesses may easily outweigh 10 strengths based on their weighted impact on the business" (p. 113), and the "snap-shot at a particular moment in time" caution on the same page. Comes with a four-list checklist (pp. 114–116) followed by "the brain still needs to be engaged" (p. 114). Cite when SWOT is being used as decoration.

### 4. Change Strategies — five options driven by four factors (Ch. 5, pp. 126–130, Fig 5.9)

Directive (speed, but resistance), Expert (technical problems, narrow audience), Negotiating (concessions, slower), Educative (hearts and minds, longest), Participative (highest commitment, most resource). Choice driven by **degree of opposition, power base of the change initiator, need for information and commitment, current culture** (p. 127). Pair with the Change Pyramid (pp. 123–125) for sequencing. The model that should be on the table during every change-management proposal conversation.

### 5. 4Ts Risk Response inside the full Risk Management Process (Ch. 3, pp. 73–81)

Treat / Terminate / Tolerate / Transfer (Fig 3.7), embedded in the full Identify → Assess (qualitative) → Evaluate (quantitative) → Action → Control → Report → Recover loop (Figs 3.5–3.6). The chapter's strongest practical claim: consultants who do not run a visible risk register lose credibility on grounds of project-management professionalism, regardless of advisory quality (p. 80). Cite for any sow-risk-advisor work.

## Cross-references

**vs. Kubr (*Management Consulting: A Guide to the Profession*, ILO).** Kubr is the encyclopedic profession reference — sectors, fee models, regulation, ethics, the full consulting lifecycle. Burtonshaw-Gunn cites Kubr (p. 6) for the canonical definition of management consulting but spends his pages on the *tools*. Use Kubr for "how the profession works," Burtonshaw-Gunn for "what to put on the slide on Tuesday." Tools shared in depth: Schein's modes (both go deep), the engagement lifecycle. Tools Burtonshaw-Gunn covers in more practical depth than Kubr: SWOT, BCG, Force Field, Fishbone, the Top-Ten chapter overall.

**vs. Block (*Flawless Consulting*).** Different genre. Block is on contracting, authentic relationship, and the politics of advice; he barely touches frameworks like SWOT or BCG. Use Block for **how to enter, contract, and close** an engagement; use Burtonshaw-Gunn for **what to do once you are inside**. The two are highly complementary — Block sits at Schein's "Process" pole (Ch. 1, p. 10).

**vs. Mabee.** Mabee tends to be referenced in the consulting plugin for sector-specific or firm-craft material; Burtonshaw-Gunn is sector-agnostic and tool-first. No direct overlap.

## Pitfalls — common tool misuse

- **SWOT as analysis.** Filling four quadrants is data collection. Without weighting, severity assessment, and a "so-what," it is a list, not an analysis (p. 113). Two major weaknesses can outweigh ten minor strengths.
- **PESTLE as bullet dump.** Six headings, twenty bullets, no implication. PESTLE is a scan; the work is converting items into opportunities/threats that feed the SWOT and the strategy options (p. 118).
- **BCG as a verdict.** Calling a product a "Dog" and divesting ignores margin, strategic complementarity, and downstream pull-through. The matrix is a starting hypothesis (pp. 122–123).
- **Risk register as decoration.** A register no one updates and that does not drive decisions provides liability protection and nothing else (Ch. 3, p. 80). The 4Ts only work if probability and impact are honestly estimated.
- **Force Field Analysis without weighting.** Listing forces with no estimate of strength or addressability produces equilibrium, not a plan (Ch. 4, p. 93).
- **Fishbone with pre-set bones.** Materials/Method/Machinery/Measurement is a manufacturing convention. In a services or digital context the pre-set bones force false categorization; let the categories emerge.
- **Five Forces on platforms.** Porter assumes static industry boundaries. For platforms, marketplaces, and ecosystem businesses, complementors and network effects are first-class forces and do not appear in Fig 5.2.
- **Schein-mode drift.** Engagements quietly slide from Process to Doctor–Patient to Expert as deadlines tighten. Re-state the mode in writing at every milestone.
- **Scope as one bid line.** Project variables fail in practice because scope is treated as a sentence, not an artefact. The author's repeated point (Ch. 3, pp. 72–73) is that scope must be written, agreed, and re-baselined.
- **Change Strategies picked by default.** Directive is fastest, so it gets chosen; Participative is sponsor-friendly, so it gets chosen. The four factors on p. 127 — opposition, power base, communication need, culture — are what should drive the choice.
- **Workshop labels instead of work.** Calling someone a "Protester" or "Prisoner" (Ch. 4, pp. 95–96) is a diagnostic, not a strategy. The work is addressing what is generating the stance.
