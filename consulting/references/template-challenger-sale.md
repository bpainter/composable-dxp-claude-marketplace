## When to use it

- New pursuit qualification — early-stage to mid-stage opportunities
- Building or refreshing the **Commercial Insight** for a service/solution offering
- Preparing for a high-stakes pitch where the customer thinks they already know what they want
- When the deal feels stuck and you suspect the customer hasn't been challenged enough on their assumptions
- Coaching a sales rep through a complex pursuit

## The Challenger model in brief

In complex B2B sales, **Challengers** (44% of star reps) win — not Relationship Builders (7% of stars, despite being 21% of average reps). Challengers do three things consistently:

1. **Teach** the customer something new about their business
2. **Tailor** the message to the specific stakeholder's drivers
3. **Take control** of the sale — comfortable with constructive tension, especially around price

This template walks through each pillar with a worksheet.

## Section 1: Pursuit qualification (do we even pursue?)

Before investing pursuit cycles, qualify:

- **Does the customer have a real problem worth solving?** Not "are they polite to us" — but is the cost of the status quo high enough that they'd actually buy?
- **Do we have a Commercial Insight (see Section 2) that would change how they think?** If we'd just describe what they already know, we're a Relationship Builder, not a Challenger.
- **Is there a Mobilizer in the buying group?** (See Section 4.) If we only have Talkers and friendly contacts, the deal won't close.
- **Is there budget OR willingness to find budget?** Real money or potential money — not "interest."
- **Can we win on more than price?** Challengers win on insight + tailoring; if the customer is fully price-driven and won't be moved by reframing, walk.

If 3+ of these are weak, no-bid or downscale.

## Section 2: Build the Commercial Insight

A Commercial Insight is a teaching moment that **reframes how the customer thinks about their own business**. Not a product pitch. Not generic industry benchmarking. A specific reframe that points at a problem they're under-investing in (and that we happen to solve).

### The 4 rules for a good Commercial Insight

1. **Leads to your unique strengths** — if it doesn't, you're educating the customer to buy from a competitor
2. **Challenges the customer's assumptions** — if they already know it, it's not insight
3. **Catalyzes action** — implies that doing nothing is more dangerous than doing something
4. **Scales across customers** — if the insight is bespoke to one customer, it's not scalable for the business

### The 6-step Challenger pitch (the Reframe sequence)

| # | Stage | What you do | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Warmer** | Show you understand their world; demonstrate domain credibility (NOT product) | 2–3 min |
| 2 | **Reframe** | Introduce a perspective that contradicts their current view; point at a hidden cost or missed opportunity | 3–5 min |
| 3 | **Rational drowning** | Quantify the reframe with data; show the cost of the status quo is higher than they think | 3–5 min |
| 4 | **Emotional impact** | Connect the data to *their* situation — characters they'll recognize, near-miss stories, "this is you" | 5–7 min |
| 5 | **A new way** | Generic capabilities the customer needs to address the reframed problem (not your product yet) | 3–5 min |
| 6 | **Your solution** | NOW your product — and only now — as the differentiated way to deliver "a new way" | as long as needed |

## Section 3: Tailor — adapt the insight to the specific buyer

The same Commercial Insight needs to land differently with different buyers. Tailor on:

| Layer | Tailoring move |
|---|---|
| **Industry** | Use the specific industry's vocabulary, regulations, competitive set |
| **Company** | Reference their public strategy, recent news, public commitments |
| **Function** | Frame value in the function's metrics — finance: cost/risk; ops: efficiency/throughput; marketing: pipeline/brand |
| **Individual** | Their specific drivers — career arc, pet peeves, past wins/losses |

If you can't tailor at the individual level for the meeting, push it to the next call. A generic Commercial Insight will land 20% as hard as a tailored one.

## Section 4: Take control — managing the buying group

Per *The Challenger Customer* (the 2015 sequel), the average B2B buying group has **5.4 people** and consensus is the default failure mode. Don't try to win the whole group; identify the **Mobilizer**.

### The 7 stakeholder types

Three types are **Mobilizers** — they can drive change inside the customer:

- **Go-Getters** — driven by improving their company, want to fix things
- **Skeptics** — push back on everything but become powerful champions when convinced
- **Teachers** — love to spread ideas they believe in

Three types are **Talkers** — friendly but ineffective at driving change:

- **Friends** — accessible, agreeable, not influential
- **Climbers** — focused on personal advancement, transactional
- **Guides** — share information but don't drive decisions

One type to **avoid**:

- **Blockers** — actively obstructive; isolate or work around them

## Section 5: Constructive tension on price and scope

Challengers don't fold on price. The Take-Control pillar specifically means being comfortable with tension when:

- The customer asks for a discount you don't think is justified by their situation
- The customer wants more scope at the same price
- The customer challenges your timeline aggressively
- The customer threatens to go to a competitor

Before the meeting, decide:
- **What you won't compromise on:** [the line]
- **What you'd trade for what:** [scope reductions, payment terms, etc.]
- **The walk-away:** [your BATNA — see `template-negotiation-prep.md`]

Practice the tense moment out loud before the meeting. Most reps fold on price because they haven't rehearsed not folding.

## Pitfalls

- **Insight isn't actually new.** If the customer says "yeah, we know that," your Commercial Insight is generic industry-speak. Go deeper.
- **Tension without empathy.** Challengers aren't aggressive; they're confident. The 9-step pitch is grounded in the Warmer (genuine understanding) before the Reframe (tension).
- **Premature pricing pressure.** Don't bring up price before the customer is sold on the value. The Reframe → Emotional Impact → A New Way sequence creates the buying urgency *before* the price conversation.
- **Lone-wolf cargo cult.** Lone Wolves win on instinct but don't scale. Challenger is reproducible; Lone Wolf is not. Don't romanticize.
- **Free consulting trap.** If you're scoping the customer's problem free in pursuit, you're working for them already. Time-box discovery; turn it into a paid engagement at the right moment.
- **Mistaking Talkers for Mobilizers.** Friendly conversations feel like progress but predict nothing. Disqualify and move on.
