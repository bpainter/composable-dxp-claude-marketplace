# Book digest: *Meeting Design*

**Author**: Kevin M. Hoffman, with foreword by Jeff Gothelf.
**What this book is**: A treatment of the meeting itself as a designed object — same intellectual move as treating a website or a product as a designed object. Hoffman's thesis is that bad meetings are bad design, not bad personalities, and the design discipline transfers cleanly.

The book is the canonical anchor for the `facilitation-meeting-architect` skill in this plugin. Where the Collaboration Code is about workshops (multi-hour to multi-day), Hoffman is about meetings (15–90 minutes), where the cognitive constraint is fundamentally different.

## Structure

Two parts, nine chapters.

### Part 1 — The Theory and Practice of Meeting Design (Chapters 1–6)

The principles. How to think about meetings as designed objects.

| Ch | Title | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | How to Design a Meeting | Meeting-as-design-object; thinking like a designer about gatherings |
| 2 | The Design Constraint of All Meetings | The cognitive limit of the human brain — the hard constraint that most meetings violate |
| 3 | Build Agendas Out of Ideas, People, and Time | Three-axis agenda design; how much content, how many people, how much time |
| 4 | Manage Conflict with Facilitation | A working definition of facilitation and how productive conflict moves work forward |
| 5 | Facilitation Strategy and Style | When to ask the right question; when to adapt style |
| 6 | Better Meetings Lead to Better Organizations | Meeting culture as cultural diagnostic; using meetings to change orgs |

### Part 2 — Designed Meetings (Chapters 7–9)

The patterns. Specific meeting designs for specific moments.

| Ch | Title | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| 7 | Get Started with Beginning Meetings | Meetings that reduce ambiguity and set direction at project start |
| 8 | Chart the Course Using Middle Meetings | Course-correction meetings mid-project |
| 9 | Find Closure with End Meetings | Wrap-up meetings that lead to learning and continued growth |

The Beginning / Middle / End taxonomy is Hoffman's signature contribution. It maps the meeting type to the moment in the project lifecycle, which is more useful than the standard "type" taxonomies (status, retro, planning, etc.).

## The headline ideas

### Meetings have outcomes, not just outputs

Borrowed from the Lean UX vocabulary (Gothelf wrote the foreword): a meeting that "took place" is an output; a meeting that produced a measurable change in shared understanding or commitment is an outcome. The shift in framing rules out a lot of meetings entirely — if you can't name the outcome, don't have the meeting.

### The cognitive constraint of all meetings

The human brain's working memory and attention budget is the hard limit. A meeting that exceeds it produces nothing. Hoffman uses this to argue for shorter meetings, fewer participants, fewer ideas-per-meeting, and visible artifacts that offload memory.

### Agendas built from three things

- **Ideas** — what concepts the meeting needs to handle
- **People** — who must be there to handle them
- **Time** — how long each idea takes given the people

The three are interdependent. More ideas require more time or fewer people. More people require fewer ideas or more time. This is the planning calculus most agendas fail to do.

### Productive conflict via facilitation

Hoffman's working definition of facilitation is anchored to conflict — a meeting where conflict is suppressed produces nothing of value, and a meeting where conflict erupts unmanaged also produces nothing. Facilitation is the practice that makes conflict productive. (This pairs naturally with the `facilitation-group-dynamics-coach` skill.)

### Beginning / Middle / End meetings

The three classes:

- **Beginning meetings** — set direction. The most leverage. Examples: kickoffs, stakeholder alignment, scope definition. The best place to invest meeting design effort because the assumptions made here propagate forward.
- **Middle meetings** — chart the course. Most everyday meetings. Examples: status, decision points, design reviews, course corrections. Where the cognitive constraint bites hardest because attention is divided across many concurrent threads.
- **End meetings** — find closure. Wrap-up, retro, handoff. Underrated and routinely skipped. Where the most learning lives if you actually do them.

## What this book adds beyond the Collaboration Code

Two things:

1. **Meetings, not workshops.** The Collaboration Code assumes you have hours or days. Hoffman teaches the discipline at 30-minute scale, where the design moves are subtler.
2. **Agenda-building as math.** The Ideas × People × Time formulation gives you a way to defend (or kill) an agenda before it's run.

## How to reach for this in real work

- **Before scheduling any meeting** — Chapter 2 (the cognitive constraint) and Chapter 3 (Ideas/People/Time). If the math doesn't work, fix the meeting before you send the invite.
- **Project kickoff** — Chapter 7 (Beginning Meetings).
- **Status meetings that have stopped producing value** — Chapter 8 (Middle Meetings).
- **End-of-engagement closure** — Chapter 9 (End Meetings) plus the retro literature in `project-management-retro-facilitator`.
