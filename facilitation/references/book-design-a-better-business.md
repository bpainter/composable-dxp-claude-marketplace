# Book digest: *Design a Better Business*

**Authors**: Patrick van der Pijl (Business Models Inc.), Justin Lokitz, Lisa Kay Solomon. Designed by Maarten van Lieshout and Erik van der Pluijm.
**What this book is**: The visual canvases playbook for strategy and innovation. The book treats canvases not as templates but as facilitation surfaces — physical artifacts the team builds, marks up, and argues with.

This is the canonical anchor for the `facilitation-strategic-visualizer` skill. Where the Collaboration Code Tools lists canvases as one type of module among many, this book is *only* about canvases — when to use which, how to facilitate them, what each one produces.

## Structure: the seven-stage journey

The book organizes the material as a strategy/innovation process with named stages. Each stage has its own toolset.

| Stage | Question | Sample tools |
|---|---|---|
| **Prepare** | How will we work together? | Team Charter, Screenplays, How You Work (Together) |
| **Point of View** | What's our vision? | 5 Bold Steps Vision®, Cover Story Vision®, Design Criteria, Storytelling Canvas |
| **Understand** | What do we know about the world? | Context Canvas®, Customer Personas, Customer Journey, Empathy Map, Stakeholder Map |
| **Ideate** | What might we do? | Brainstorm rules, Inspiration Card Decks, Idea Sketching, Pitch It |
| **Prototype** | What does it look like? | Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder/Strategyzer), Value Proposition Canvas, Business Model Mix |
| **Validate** | Does this work? | Riskiest Assumption, Validation Canvas, Experiment Canvas |
| **Scale** | How do we make it real? | Scaling Roadmap, Innovation Matrix, Portfolio Map |

Plus an introductory **Prepare** + closing **Appendix** with extended canvas references.

## The marquee canvases

These show up so often they deserve named recognition:

### Team Charter Canvas
The team's shared agreement on how it will work together — purpose, members, responsibilities, values, working conditions, decision-making rules. **Use early in any new team formation.**

### 5 Bold Steps Vision® Canvas
A vision in five concrete steps with named supports and challenges for each step. Forces the team to commit to a direction with operative steps, not a wall-poster vision statement. **Use when the team has a vague vision and needs to convert it to commitment.**

### Cover Story Vision® Canvas
Imagine your future success on a magazine cover. What's the headline? The pull-quotes? The author byline? **Use to surface latent ambition and shared images of the future.**

### Design Criteria Canvas
The constraints and requirements the design must meet — the rubric the team will judge ideas against later. **Use after Point of View, before Ideate, to keep ideation grounded.**

### Storytelling Canvas
Hero, audience, journey, ending. Forces narrative discipline on what would otherwise be a deck. **Use whenever you need to communicate a strategy externally.**

### Context Canvas®
The PESTEL-style scan of the world the business operates in — political, economic, social, technological, environmental, legal forces, plus customer trends and uncertainties. **Use in Understand stage.**

### Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder / Strategyzer)
The famous nine blocks — Customer Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams, Key Activities, Key Resources, Key Partnerships, Cost Structure. **The default canvas when modeling any business.**

### Value Proposition Canvas (Strategyzer)
The two-circle drill-down — customer profile (jobs, pains, gains) vs. value map (products/services, pain relievers, gain creators). **Use to test fit between offer and customer.**

## The signature design move: canvases as facilitation surfaces

The book's central craft point: a canvas is not a slide template. It's a *facilitation surface* — a public artifact that the team builds together with sticky notes, marks up, photographs, throws away, and rebuilds. The canvas exists to *externalize the argument* — to get the disagreement out of people's heads and onto a wall where the team can manipulate it.

This is the same intellectual move as visible process in the Collaboration Code Patterns (104 Everything speaks; 247 Use the power of compelling visuals). The book operationalizes it for strategy work specifically.

## The five facilitator skills the book teaches

In the Prepare and Point of View sections the book names five skills the strategy facilitator builds:

1. **Observe** — see what's actually in the room and the canvas, not what you expected
2. **Storytelling** — narrative as a working medium, not just a presentation skill
3. **Ideate** — generate at scale, suspend judgment
4. **Prototype** — build to think; rapid, low-fidelity
5. **Design** — synthesize with intention; make choices visible

## How to reach for this in real work

- **Strategy off-site (Slalom internal or client)** — work the seven stages in order; pick one canvas per stage based on time available.
- **Pursuit-stage vision workshop** — 5 Bold Steps + Cover Story + Storytelling Canvas. Three hours, leaves the room with a teachable vision.
- **Discovery phase of a Slalom Summit engagement** — Context Canvas + Customer Journey + Business Model Canvas + Value Proposition Canvas.
- **Innovation portfolio work** — Innovation Matrix + Portfolio Map + Riskiest Assumption.
