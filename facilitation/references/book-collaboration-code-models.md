# Book digest: *The Collaboration Code — Models*

**Authors**: Matt Taylor, Rob Evans, Kelvy Bird (illustrated)
**What this book is**: The MG Taylor Modeling Language. A library of conceptual frameworks for understanding the systems your facilitation is trying to move. Not exercises; not patterns. Models — the lenses you and the room use to see what's happening.

## The premise: Ten Design Assumptions

The book opens with ten foundational claims about the world the work is operating in — humanity creating change faster than it can comprehend, the Anthropocene, civilizational pressure, the limits of localized fixes, the gap between individual capacity and collective action, the commodification of people and the planet. The point: if these are even half-right, the design problem facing organizations is at planetary scale, and the models in this book are calibrated for that scale.

For Slalom Composable DXP work, the Ten Design Assumptions are useful as a reminder that any client transformation lives inside a much larger system. The recognition keeps the room from over-claiming what a 10-week engagement can change.

## How to read this book

The authors recommend three passes:

1. **Scan** — become broadly familiar; read the Terms of Art glossary.
2. **Spend time with what catches you** — pull the models that match your current work.
3. **Read in depth and connect** — establish your own internal map of the models.

The models are sequenced as a "Glass Bead Game" (after Hesse's *Magister Ludi*) — the order is intentional but you are encouraged to recreate the sequence yourself.

## The Modeling Language has four registers

- **Graphical depictions** — each model has a canonical visual.
- **Origin stories** — each model has a context in which it was created.
- **Glyphs** — shorthand visual notation for stages or parts of each model. Combinable.
- **Terms of Art** (notation: ^ToA) — precisely-defined language. Everyday language is too imprecise for technical communication in a community of work.

## The four highest-leverage models for Bermon

These are the models to internalize first; they show up across nearly every Slalom workshop:

1. **Scan-Focus-Act** — the macro-shape of any well-designed session. Memorize this.
2. **Creating the Problem** — the discipline of reframing the problem before designing the solution. The most common workshop failure is solving the wrong problem precisely.
3. **Creative Process** — the canonical arc from inspiration to integration. Tells you where energy peaks and dips during a creative session.
4. **You-Me-Us** — the three-position frame for facilitator presence. Knowing which position to inhabit at which moment.

Two more for engagements involving major change:

5. **Rate of Change** — for any digital transformation conversation
6. **Net Vector** — for any conversation involving competing stakeholder interests

## How to reach for this in real work

When designing a workshop, models help you *see* what's happening; they don't tell you what to *do*. Reach for *Tools* for the doing, *Patterns* for the why, *Models* for the diagnosis.

In a moment of confusion mid-session — "what's wrong with this room?" — running through Scan-Focus-Act, Creative Process, and Net Vector usually surfaces the diagnosis.
