# Book digest: *Facilitating Collaboration*

**Authors**: Brandon Klein & Dan Newman, published by The Value Web (Swiss non-profit) in 2016. A re-write and extension of Newman's earlier *From the Front of the Room: Notes on Facilitation for Experienced Practitioners*. The book sits in the same MG Taylor / DesignShop tradition as the Collaboration Code series.

**What this book is**: A practitioner's manual for the facilitator's own work — what *you* do, sequenced from scoping through delivery to value capture. Less a catalog (those are in the Collaboration Code) and more a working notebook from two decades of practice.

## The headline framework: the Six Jobs

> *"What is my role as a facilitator? As the name suggests, I am there to make things easy for others. As a facilitator, I have six jobs."*

The Six Jobs (sometimes counted as seven if you split off Satisfaction) form the backbone of the facilitator's working life:

| # | Job | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Scoping** | Clarifying outcomes, what decisions are already made, what's out of scope |
| 2 | **Working with Sponsors** | Building trusting content-relationship with sponsors; understanding business + personal + political stakes |
| 3 | **Preparation** | Logistics, knowledge inputs, deep learning of the issues, pre-event mobilization |
| 4 | **Designing** | Building the agenda — choosing modules and ordering them to achieve intermediate outcomes and create a narrative |
| 5 | **Delivery** | Running the event — energy management, plot, common language, music, difficult participants |
| 6 | **Value Capture** | What gets carried forward — recap, artifacts, follow-through |
| (7) | **Satisfaction** | The facilitator's own integration — what the work meant, what to learn |

This is the natural backbone for the `facilitation-facilitator` persona skill in this plugin. Every engagement walks these six jobs in order, with looping back as the situation requires.

## Chapter-by-chapter

### Chapter 1 — Facilitation, Design, and the Rest
Frames facilitation as design discipline. Distinguishes:

- **Essential Elements for a Successful Event** — the non-negotiables
- **Graphic Facilitation** — visual capture as a working medium
- **Data & Artificial Intelligence** — early notes on AI-augmented facilitation (2016 vintage; the territory has moved fast since)

### Chapter 2 — The Facilitator's Role
Defines the **Six Jobs** above and the **Facilitation Skills** the practitioner builds across years of practice.

### Chapter 3 — Scoping
The first job in detail. The book treats scoping as the most leveraged time you spend — the conversation that decides whether the event will succeed before any agenda is built.

### Chapter 4 — Working with Sponsors
Includes **The Sponsor Team's Jobs** — what the sponsor team owes the facilitator (and what they owe their own organization). Sponsor Design Teams (SDTs) are central in this tradition; they meet days in advance, often live in the room with the facilitator the night before, and act as the trusted co-designer.

### Chapter 5 — Preparation
Logistics, knowledge inputs, intangible preparation (deep learning of the client's world). The intangible work usually outweighs the tangible.

### Chapter 6 — Designing
Agenda construction — module choice and ordering. The discipline is *intentional* sequencing for narrative and intermediate outcomes, not a list of activities.

### Chapter 7 — Delivery
The biggest chapter. Sub-sections include:

- **Why we do this** — the orienting purpose
- **The Centrality of Common Language** — naming things precisely so the room can think together
- **Plot and Energy** — the session as a story with rising and falling action
- **Keeping Our Distance** — facilitator non-attachment to specific outcomes
- **Setting Patterns** — establishing rhythms early that compound across the event
- **Music** — soundscape as facilitation tool (a wavespace signature move)
- **Difficult Participants** — the common typologies and what to do

### Chapter 8 — Value Capture
What the room produces and how it travels forward.

### Chapter 9 — Satisfaction
- **Roundness** — the facilitator's own integration; the work as practice, not project

## What this book adds beyond the Collaboration Code

Three things:

1. **Sequencing of the facilitator's own work** — the Six Jobs make the facilitator's labor explicit and ordered. The Collaboration Code assumes you know your job; this book teaches it.
2. **Delivery craft, especially Plot and Energy** — narrative pacing as a facilitation skill. Most failed sessions fail in delivery, and most delivery failures are pacing failures.
3. **Practitioner voice** — Klein and Newman write personally. Anecdotes from real events (often with names changed) make the principles stick.

## How to reach for this in real work

- **Starting a new engagement** — read Chapters 3–4 (Scoping, Sponsors). Most workshop pain originates here.
- **Mid-design crunch** — Chapter 6 (Designing) is the agenda-construction chapter; pair with the *Tools* catalog from the Collaboration Code.
- **Pre-event nerves** — Chapter 7 (Delivery) and the Plot and Energy sub-section.
- **Post-event reflection** — Chapter 9 (Satisfaction). Learning from each session is what compounds the practice.
