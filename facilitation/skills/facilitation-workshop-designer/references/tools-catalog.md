# Collaboration Code — full tool catalog

Per-tool digests for the ~110 tools in Rob Evans' *Collaboration Code: Tools* (EY wavespace edition, 2021). Each entry: what it is, what to use it for, how it works (steps), timing, and what to keep in mind.

For the high-level book digest, see [`book-collaboration-code-tools.md`](../../../references/book-collaboration-code-tools.md). For sequencing logic, see [`scan-focus-act-design-guide.md`](scan-focus-act-design-guide.md).

## How tools are organized

Tools are grouped by phase of a collaborative session:

| Phase | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Pre-Session** | The setup work that decides whether the session lands |
| **Scan** | Open the field — divergence, surfacing, perspective-taking |
| **Focus** | Narrow to what matters — convergence, trade-offs, decision |
| **Act** | Commit and mobilize — owners, plans, communication |
| **Reporting** | How small-group work gets shared back |
| **Deliverables** | What leaves the room |
| **Additional Reference** | Axioms, starter kits, cautionary lists |

Each entry follows the source's standard documentation: What It Is / What to Use It For / How It Works / Example / Timing / Keep in Mind.

## Pre-Session Tools

### Graphical Strawdog

**What it is.** A visual model that depicts the different modules and modes of work across a session — plenary, break-out teams, individual work like Take-A-Panel — in a form the viewer can take in at a glance. ("Strawdog" is the playful alternative to "straw man.")

**Use it for.** Showing the full session at once so people steeped in traditional facilitation can see how participants move between modes; helping the facilitator stress-test the design for robustness.

**How it works.**
1. On a WorkWall or in a notebook (or in an Excel spreadsheet for a digital version), lay out a template that shows the entire flow of the session over time.
2. Develop a key to distinguish phases of the process and types of activities — e.g. Scan, Focus, and Act in different colors or symbols.
3. Populate the template with the modules, including enough identifying info to indicate the nature of each activity.
4. Replicate the example on a WorkWall when presenting it to the Sponsor Design Team.

**Timing.** Not specified — a planning artifact built before the session and presented to the SDT.

**Keep in mind.**
- Also useful for training novice facilitators and orienting new Sponsor Design Teams — it helps them see the flow from one activity to the next.
- Matt Taylor's hand-drawn five-day PatchWorks notebook is the canonical example; the digital condensed version on a single page works too.

### Knowledge Wall

**What it is.** A surface (traditionally the convex side of the Radiant Wall, but any wall or foam-core construction works) dedicated to displaying useful information throughout the session — articles, facts, original scribing, infographics relating to the wavespace challenge.

**Use it for.** Setting context on arrival; engaging the group in conversation with the KreW and the outside world; modeling participant work as a visual Group Memory; reinforcing that the wavespace is a knowledge-rich environment.

**How it works.**
1. Begin identifying material early in Sponsor Design Team planning sessions and record those ideas in the Workshop Design Document.
2. On Prep Day, give one or more KreW members responsibility for laying out the initial Knowledge Wall and keeping it refreshed and current.
3. Throughout the event, the KreW listens to group work and reports, identifies additional case studies, articles, news reports etc. that respond to questions arising or augment examples cited, and posts them.
4. Document the documentation — take digital photos of individual elements and of the entire wall, since the layout itself becomes part of how participants remember the information.

**Timing.** Creating an effective Knowledge Wall takes considerable time on Prep Day; budget ongoing KreW time to keep it fresh.

**Keep in mind.**
- Invite the KreW to push their own creativity in constructing and populating it; the more creative, the more engaging.
- One reliable way to draw participants to the wall: print and post candid pics of "happy clients hard at work" — collected from breakfast on Day One. Iron rule: always show participants at their best; never a "gotcha."
- Document. Document. Document.

### Mind Mapping

**What it is.** A right-brained note-taking technique developed by Tony Buzan that captures information radially rather than as a linear outline — a better match for how the brain stores information, and a lot more fun.

**Use it for.** Capturing emotionally accurate notes on a presentation or experience; preparing accessible notes for delivering a presentation; building flexible workshop prep that lets you add points without disrupting the structure.

**How it works.**
1. Write the title, main theme, or central idea in the middle of your page or whiteboard.
2. Write the main points radiating out from the center, beginning at one o'clock and going clockwise.
3. Write sub-points and other details as branches off the main limbs, working from center outward.
4. Use at least two different colors for the main limbs, alternating them to keep areas separate.
5. Use images and pictures to make concepts come alive — don't worry about artistic precision; over time you'll build a repertoire of commonly used images and symbols.

**Timing.** Not specified — a personal prep technique.

**Keep in mind.**
- Other people's Mind Maps may be interesting (sometimes, on a good day), but the most valuable ones are those you create for yourself.
- You don't need artistic ability — just willingness to experiment and have fun. A few graphical tricks go a long way.
- Working from a Mind Map during facilitation lets you pay more attention to the group while still staying on track.

### On-Line File Structure

**What it is.** A cloud-based repository (Box.com, DropBox) that holds all inputs, working documents, and outputs. The KnowledgeWorker team uses it throughout the event; afterward it's cleaned up and elements become the client's On-Line Journal deliverable. Part of "the Secret Sauce" — the discipline required to use it well dramatically reduces error and rework.

**Use it for.** The KreW's primary working store for all client inputs, drafts, and outputs; the back-up and detailed record supporting the Executive Summary deliverable.

**How it works.**
1. The Facilitator or Process Facilitator (PF) sets up the online journal in advance of the opening Circle-Up on Prep Day and distributes links/invitations to the KreW.
2. During the event, the PF is the steward of the file structure.
3. After the event, the PF or designee identifies which elements to include in the client-facing On-Line Journal.

**Structure.** Three levels of organization. Top level includes: `00 Inputs` (pre-reads), the dated client-facing wavespace folder (numbered modules `01 Welcome`, `02 Strategy Framework`, etc., each containing scribing JPGs, presentation files, video files), `Drop Zone` (KreW working folder, module-by-module), `Post Day Sponsor Meeting Walls`, and `zz KreW Working Folder` (Assignments, Design Documents, Graphics, Inputs, Maps, People Pics, Production, Scribing, etc.).

**Keep in mind.**
- Yes — we actually do keep a record of everything. Really. Everything. And this is where we keep it.
- The level of detail crosses into TMI territory but is absolutely essential for an accurate, usable record.

### Participant Invitation

**What it is.** The first communication to participants — which marks the beginning of their Scan phase. A "hold the date" goes out 4–6 weeks ahead (whatever it takes to get must-have calendars), followed by a detailed joining letter ~3–4 weeks out once logistics and objectives are confirmed.

**Use it for.** Communicating objectives and logistics; establishing a productive "mental set" for participants regarding what they'll experience and what's required of them.

**How it works.**
1. As soon as the session date is set — even before the participant list or venue is finalized — ask the SDT to send a "hold the date" message to people you're sure you'll invite.
2. Offer to draft the invitation/joining letter for the SDT. Key elements: aim and intent of the session; formal objectives; date and location; basic ground rules on full participation; a personal statement by the ultimate sponsor.
3. The primary sponsor sends the letter — not the facilitator.
4. If pre-work is required, send it in a separate letter within a week of the session.

**Timing.** Hold-the-date 4–6 weeks out; full invitation 3–4 weeks out; pre-work ~1 week out.

**Keep in mind.**
- The more personal and in the voice of your main sponsor, the better.
- Do NOT send a detailed agenda in advance, even if participants ask. Objectives, overall time commitment, and pre-work are enough.
- The standard letter sets explicit expectations: clear calendars, away messages on voicemail and email, phones/tablets off, arrive the night before, stay until the morning after.

### Participant Pre-work

**What it is.** A request that participants do work before the session — most commonly read items or watch online media (TED Talks, RSA Videos), occasionally collect data, conduct interviews, or go on field trips.

**Use it for.** Assembling the knowledge base needed for a successful session; initiating the Scan Phase for participants.

**How it works.**
1. Work closely with the SDT to identify specifically what pre-work participants will be required to do, and send the notice as a personal request from the SDT.
2. Allow sufficient time — perhaps 1–2 weeks — for any data collection, interviews, or field trips.
3. Identify and "chunk" the pre-work so no individual has more than an hour or two to accomplish.
4. Send pre-reads no more than a week ahead. Wherever possible, include digital versions of readings and links to online media in the message itself.

**Timing.** 1–2 weeks lead time for active pre-work (interviews, fieldwork); ≤1 week for pre-reads. Cap individual workload at 1–2 hours.

**Keep in mind.**
- You don't have to send the same pre-reads to everyone — one effective strategy is to compile many "chunks" and tell each participant they alone are responsible for mastering theirs.
- Another strategy: pre-assign teams to a different collection of TED Talks or articles, then convene those teams early on Scan Day to discuss, apply, and report on what they viewed in common.
- Occasionally SDTs send entire books — same book to everyone, or many different books with only one or two participants per title. In that case include the piece "How to Read a Book in an Hour" (in the Scan section) with the assignment.

### Scoping and Scaling Workshops

**What it is.** A reading on how long a session needs to be to be truly effective — built to push back against the common pre-determined "we need a two-day session" request from clients and engagement directors who haven't experienced a real breakthrough collaborative session.

**Use it for.** Scoping conversations with clients and engagement teams who are guessing at session length without understanding what each duration can actually accomplish.

**How it works.** Length is driven by these factors — the more present, the longer the session needs to be: low initial alignment; low understanding of the issues; problem complexity; number of participants required; participants with little experience working collaboratively; range of options to be explored (developing options from scratch takes longer than testing a straw case); degree of change in the potential outcomes (Sleep-On-It-At-Least-Twice rule); detail needed in the final action plan.

**Session lengths.**
- **One-Day:** generate ideas, build baseline understanding, achieve alignment on a single narrowly defined issue. Risk: no recovery time for ambush problems; produces "alignment" rather than "ownership."
- **Two-Day:** test multiple hypotheses, drive options where alignment already exists (warning — "considerable alignment" is usually wishful thinking). Produces ~one-fourth the output of a three-day.
- **Three-Day:** the most versatile offering. Develop new strategy, redesign inter-related processes, complete an ERP phase, build understanding/alignment/enthusiasm for an action plan. Lets participants "sleep on it twice."
- **Four-Day:** for complex problems requiring detailed design then and there (Boeing aircraft redesign, SAP global rollout at Lilly).
- **Multiple Sessions:** for large-scale programs spanning months.

**Keep in mind.**
- Scoping length is one of the most important factors for success — a wavespace can dramatically accelerate a group, but only if given enough time.
- Clients often overestimate how prepared participants are to do detailed design work and how aligned everyone is with "the Vision."
- Chip Saltsman uses the Vantage Points model to explain to teams that groups can generally cover only one level of the Vantage Point model per day. E.g. "Understand corporate vision (Policy) and articulate implications" = 1 day; "Define a goal (Policy), map programs (Strategy), and define initiatives (Tactics)" = 3 days.

### Sponsor Design Team Planning Session

**What it is.** A 4–8 hour design session (not a "meeting") with potential sponsors that establishes purpose, alignment, and tone for the wavespace. Patterns and behaviors set here will carry through to the wavespace itself.

**Use it for.** A Discovery Day to introduce the possibility of a collaborative workshop to interested decision-makers and implementers; a planning session to gather input and gain alignment among sponsors for a scheduled wavespace.

**How it works.**
1. Identify participants — the executive in charge, your immediate project sponsor, other key decision-makers/influencers, and key implementation leaders. 4–5 minimum for a truly collaborative design session.
2. Coordinate room logistics: classroom-style open seating (no conference table), a vertical surface for Group Memory (whiteboard ideal; foam core or butcher paper works), Take-A-Panel surfaces, music. Avoid a PowerPoint projector unless you have specific need.
3. Set clear objectives in advance — gain alignment on purpose, define final outputs and necessary inputs, finalize participant list, define SDT role, resolve logistics, establish meeting cadence.
4. Prepare a Workshop Design Document in advance to introduce as the working document.
5. Run an agenda: Welcome and timing review → main sponsor's purpose remarks → wavespace Overview → Future State Take-A-Panel → Dialogue/Synthesis/Design Questions → Rules of Engagement → Logistics → Next steps and cadence → Wrap-up.
6. Use a comprehensive question set to drive design: "one breath" purpose, sense of urgency, 3–5 measurable objectives, deliverables, assumptions/givens/non-negotiables, in/out of scope, key decisions, barriers, required data, right participants (decision-makers + influencers + SMEs + implementers — whole system), SDT membership (3–5 people), admired companies/case studies, theme/title, pre-reads.
7. Discuss the Agreements (Rules of Engagement): sponsors commit participants who play to win and own the solution; participants make decisions, not recommendations; broad cross-section; no visitors/tourists; days begin promptly at 8am and end on time; full attendance required; phones/computers/tablets off with away messages set.
8. Immediately after the session, complete the first Workshop Design Document, review with the KnowledgeWorker team, and distribute to the SDT.

**Timing.** 4–8 hours.

**Keep in mind.**
- Scrupulously avoid the term "meeting" — it misleads people about what's going to happen. Try "wavespace Design Session" instead.
- Pay attention to team dynamics during this session — they preview the behavior you can expect at the wavespace itself (see Pattern 205: Sponsor Design Team as Microcosm).

### Sponsor Design Team Prep Day Circle Up

**What it is.** A 3–4 hour gathering held the afternoon before the wavespace starts. Sometimes the first time the SDT has been together since the initial Planning Session — occasionally the first time face-to-face at all.

**Use it for.** Confirming alignment and getting SDT input to the agenda, team lists, and assignments; setting behavioral expectations for the SDT.

**How it works.**
1. During the morning of Prep Day, work with the KreW to prepare and post: SDT name badges; detailed Strawdog (agenda) for Day One with overview of subsequent days on a WorkWall; Objectives/Scope/Non-Negotiables on a WorkWall; Day One assignments; team strips for Day One break-out teams.
2. Deploy a scribe to support the Circle-Up and brief them on what to expect.
3. Run the agenda: Welcome and intros → "Current Events" check ("what's happening right now in the organization that may distract participants?") → Review Objectives and give the SDT one final chance to edit (they probably will) → Confirm/develop the Theme and its graphical expression → Review Day One Strawdog in detail and flag decisions the SDT must make this afternoon → Review Day One assignments → Create team lists for Day One break-out teams → Review SDT behavior expectations and the schedule for Morning/Evening Circle-Ups and Post-Day SDT Circle-Up.
4. Budget time to review the Sponsor Opening with your key sponsor.
5. Budget time after the Circle-Up for a 15–20 minute check-in with Trade Show hosts to review their preparation and expectations.

**Timing.** 3–4 hours.

**Keep in mind.**
- Have the environment, Strawdog, objectives, assignments, and team lists prepared before the SDT arrives. Walking into an integrated, well-ordered space — even with rush prep happening in the background — builds sponsor confidence, especially for those new to the environment.
- Some argue for developing the Strawdog from scratch in the afternoon with the SDT. This pulls in KreW experience early but makes the SDT and engagement team extremely nervous and forces a long, long night for the KreW writing knowledge objects.
- SDT Circle-Ups look and feel more like traditional facilitation than a normal wavespace — you have specific proposals to make and resolve with them.

### Standard Assignment Page Format

**What it is.** A consistent template for written break-out assignments that you reuse across modules and sessions. Saves time (often late at night) and lets you focus on adding value to assignment content rather than fiddling with formatting.

**Use it for.** Creating a standard look and feel within a session and across sessions; building efficiency into the assignment-writing process.

**How it works.** The template has nine numbered elements:
1. Subheading describing the module name (Arial, bold, 14pt).
2. Topic of this team's assignment (Arial, bold, italic, 24pt).
3. An appropriate wavespace axiom (Times New Roman, bold, italic, 14pt; identifier on second line, Times New Roman, 12pt).
4. Section header "Context" (Arial, bold, 14pt).
5. Background statement setting up context for the assignment (Times New Roman, 12pt).
6. Section header "Process" (Arial, bold, 14pt).
7. Detailed steps for the team to follow (Times New Roman, 12pt) — typically opens with "Identify a facilitator to keep the process moving and a scribe to take notes on your Work Walls," then a list of questions/tasks ending with "What else is important to understand…"
8. Timing statement — always described as "approximately" to give the facilitation team flexibility (Times New Roman, 12pt).
9. Footer (or header) with branding, theme, date, location.

**Timing.** N/A — a template, not a delivered module.

**Keep in mind.**
- A simpler format is fine — the point is having your own format you can rely on, not copying this one point for point.
- Some facilitators prefer PowerPoint templates for assignments. The author wouldn't, but the standardization principle still applies.
- Check branding images in the header/footer with the SDT before sending a lot of assignments to the printer.
- The author prefers branding in the footer because the eye is drawn to the top of the page — that real estate should belong to what the assignment is about. The sub-header module name is intentionally smaller than the unique team topic so KnowledgeWorkers can route the right assignment to the right break-out team.

### Storyboard

**What it is.** A way to organize ideas for a workshop, presentation, document, or film in a logical, visual manner — analogous to PowerPoint's Slide Sorter view, but without its strict rules, allowing much greater creative range.

**Use it for.** Designing a workshop (alone or with a team); designing a presentation; designing the deliverable from a wavespace experience.

**How it works.**
1. Write a clear, succinct statement of your objectives or purpose for the workshop or presentation. Print it. Then turn off your computer.
2. Create a one-page Mind Map of the high-level flow of the workshop and the subsidiary points in each section.
3. Using large Post-It Notes or index cards, brainstorm a list of all the points you might cover, activities you might do, etc.
4. Using a whiteboard or flipchart, lay out large areas representing the different days of the workshop.
5. Organize your Post-Its or cards into the time slots — use an Affinity Diagram or other "chunking" process to sort topics. Balance logical development of ideas with appreciation of group energy and need for variety. Identify the overarching theme of each chunk and confirm it relates to central purpose; put themes on Post-Its.
6. Go back to the computer and translate the high-level design into a detailed, minute-by-minute outline.

**Variation.** For a single presentation rather than a workshop, after Step 4 sketch out every PowerPoint slide, graphic, or interactive exercise with pen and paper rather than the PowerPoint Wizard. Match visuals to talking points; push for emotionally compelling images rather than typical PowerPoint bullet points and slide junk.

**Timing.** Not specified — a design technique done before/during workshop prep.

**Keep in mind.**
- When using a Storyboard to write the deliverable itself, keep the previous edited version visible so you can compare revisions and confirm you've captured them all. Lay pages out in horizontal rows with the new version on top and the previous version below.

### Workshop Design Document

**What it is.** A working document used with Sponsor Design Teams to guide planning for a wavespace and record input, decisions, and issues as they arise. (The author credits Tom Kehner of The Difference, LLC for the prototype.)

**Use it for.** Guiding conversations with SDTs to ensure all relevant planning questions are addressed and resolved; recording the ongoing dialogue with the SDT.

**How it works.**
1. At your first SDT meeting, introduce the Workshop Design Document and explain its function.
2. Update the document after every meeting and call, and send it to the SDT as soon as possible — preferably within 12–24 hours.
3. Italic text in each section explains what's covered or required there. The author typically gives the document to clients with the italic descriptions intact so they understand what's being asked.

**Sections.**
1. **Purpose** — Background; Objectives ("Create…", "Decide…"); In Scope; Out of Scope; Assumptions/Givens/Non-Negotiables; Theme (tag line, reinforced graphically throughout the workshop space and on name badges).
2. **Product** — Outcomes/Decisions/Issues (asks the SDT to each open a file of detailed questions that need raising for a complete solution; these become the basis for assignments — covering Setting Context, What People Need to Learn, the Tricky Bits, Solution Elements to Test, Considerations re outcomes); Workshop Deliverables (Executive Summary, Web-based Journal, optional short video).
3. **Promises** — SDT roster and responsibilities (identify clear goals, ensure right participants attend, co-design with facilitation team, model behavior and uphold rules).
4. **Preparation** — Inputs table (Input / Who / By When / Done); participant pre-work; Knowledge Wall content.
5. **Participants** — full stakeholder map; invite at least representatives of every stake.
6. **Place** — dates and times, location/venue, logistics contact, facilitation team.
7. **Process / Draft Agenda** — time-blocked grid for Prep Day and each session day with Start, Duration, Activity, Responsibility.

**Timing.** Living document maintained from first SDT engagement through the wavespace.

**Keep in mind.**
- Maintain strict version control over this document.
- Send a revised Design Doc after every significant SDT interaction.
- Alert clients early that the draft agenda is the LAST piece to be completed — it's foolish to schedule the wavespace until you fully understand the issues and questions to be addressed (the real work).
- Keep a blank Workshop Design Document Template on the desktop and spin up a new version as soon as a new engagement is confirmed.

## Scan Modules and Tools

### Affinity Diagram

**What it is.** A process for generating a large number of ideas/issues and then organizing and identifying natural groupings among them to understand the essence of a problem and a solution.

**Use it for.** Finding connections among large sets of ideas; overcoming "team analysis paralysis" in the face of a large data set.

**How it works.**
1. Phrase the issue under discussion in a full sentence.
2. Brainstorm a large number of ideas (at least 20).
3. Write each idea on a Post-It in bold, large block print visible 4-6 feet away (noun + verb, four to seven words).
4. Invite the group to sort the Post-Its into 5-10 natural groupings without talking - no permission needed to move any note.
5. For each grouping, build header cards by consensus: first a draft word/phrase, then a concise sentence combining the central idea and what the specific notes add. Sub-divide large groupings as needed.

**Timing.** 30 to 45 minutes, depending on the number of items to be clustered.

**Keep in mind.** Sort in silence to focus on meaning rather than emotional history. If a note keeps moving back and forth, create a duplicate. It is okay for some notes to stand alone - "loners" can matter as much as the clustered ones. Spend extra time on solid header cards; shortcuts here greatly reduce effectiveness.

### Artform Process

**What it is.** A structured conversation (originating in the Thresholds Volunteer Program in Corrections, drawing on Suzanne Langer's Problems of Art) that walks individuals or groups through a sequence of questions of increasing psychological complexity to help them process an emotionally laden experience and consciously assign their own meaning to it.

**Use it for.** Helping individuals and groups gain objective clarity about an experience, integrate it through reflection that expands self-understanding, and demonstrate that meaning is a function of the human mind and can be brought under intentional control.

**How it works.**
1. Choose an object or event that is relevant to the group but content-free enough to avoid laying hidden messages on participants (Thresholds avoided overtly religious or political art).
2. Set the three rules: everyone answers every question; answer the question that was asked; no repeated answers.
3. Move sequentially through the six question levels - Objective (recall discrete elements), Subjective (modify the experience), Expressive (insert yourself in it), Interpretive (title it, tell its story), Concretizing (where have you seen this in your own life?), Responsive (what does it say to you, what do you say back?).
4. Acknowledge each answer with a quick "thank you" and move on - be firm in pursuing answers that actually answer the question, but offer participants an "escape hatch" in how they frame personal involvement.
5. Wrap up by reminding participants that the meaning belongs to them, then give a short period of silent reflection.

**Timing.** 45-60 minutes depending on the number of questions.

**Keep in mind.** The transformative idea that we make our own meaning is not intuitively obvious - pair with Frankl's Man's Search for Meaning. The Process can be used impromptu to help a group get psychological distance from a difficult shared experience.

### Authors

**What it is.** A homework assignment given at the end of Scan Day in which participants pick a book, "read it in an hour" using the How to Read a Book in an Hour instructions, and the next day deliver a short report in the voice of the book's author - typically positioned at the Focus-to-Act transition after a Why It Won't Work round.

**Use it for.** Getting a wide range of useful ideas into play and creating an opportunity for participants - masked as their authors - to give the group feedback and advice at a critical juncture.

**How it works.**
1. Pull a wide-ranging library (three books for every two participants), insert a copy of How to Read a Book in an Hour, and put a Post-It with the book/author on each cover.
2. Announce the homework at the end of Scan Day; have each participant stick the Post-It on their name badge and leave the badge behind.
3. Overnight, the KreW makes a second name badge with the author's name and slides it behind the participant's name badge.
4. After a Why It Won't Work round on Focus Day, call a break for participants to retrieve their books, then have them switch badges with their author - a Big Reveal.
5. Each report is two minutes or less, in first person as the author, covering: main points and why it mattered to write; "news we can use" for this wavespace; and sage counsel for moving into the Act phase.

**Timing.** 45 to 60 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Your matter-of-fact tone when assigning it must communicate that reading a book in an hour is genuinely doable. Oscar Wilde: "Give a man a mask and he will tell you the truth" - the author persona masks people enough to tell the truth. A variation substitutes a TED Talk for the book.

### Best Case / Worst Case Planning

**What it is.** A planning and modeling exercise that explores the full range of probable outcomes for a project or venture - one team writes a Best Case scenario, another a Worst Case, and another a Probable Case, creating an "envelope of possibility" for the future.

**Use it for.** Establishing the full range of possible futures for a project; building confidence by having contingency plans for a range of internal/external conditions. As a Scan exercise it focuses on conditions; as a Focus or Act exercise it focuses on designing the optimal response in each case.

**How it works.**
1. Consider presenting the Best Case/Worst Case model to the large group either before assignments or just before report-outs to reveal the structural logic.
2. Write team assignments that clearly specify which "case" each team has been assigned - Best Case is "plans went reasonably well" (not utopia), Worst Case is "plans went wickedly awry" (not the end of the world), Probable Case sits between.
3. Each team picks a facilitator/scribe, dialogues on outcomes, prioritizes the top five-to-seven conditions, and identifies critical success or failure factors.
4. In the debrief, push for the assumptions that drove the specific outcomes each team described, and ask how those assumptions can be tested.
5. Identify what contingencies to prepare for now.

**Timing.** 45 minutes of team work plus 30-45 minutes for the debrief.

**Keep in mind.** One option is to assign only Best and Worst Case to teams and derive the Probable Case through group discussion. The exercise is not about predicting the future - the point is to identify today what we can do to prepare for a range of outcomes.

### Brainstorming - Open

**What it is.** A tool for generating and documenting team ideas using sequential turn-taking.

**Use it for.** Developing a large number of ideas in a short period of time; stimulating team creativity; promoting group participation.

**How it works.**
1. Define the purpose of the brainstorming clearly and write it on a flipchart.
2. Review the rules: each person takes a turn in sequence; one thought at a time; no discussion or criticism; build on others' ideas; continue until all ideas have been stated (passing is OK).
3. Record ideas in the words of the speaker on the flipchart.
4. Continue until all ideas are written down.
5. Review the list and combine similar ideas.
6. Move on to Multi-voting, Prioritization Matrix, or another vehicle for selecting ideas to carry forward.

**Timing.** Variable, but usually no more than 30 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Brainstormed ideas must be substantiated with real data. The common preface "any idea is a good idea" is patently false - there are plenty of bad ideas; what is true is that without an open attitude we self-censor. This tool is just the beginning of ideation - it must be followed by careful analysis. An alternative is to have participants document ideas on Post-Its during the individual portion.

### Brainstorming - Balanced

**What it is.** A way of balancing input across an entire group when extroverts tend to dominate open Brainstorming - it gives introverts time to consider their responses and ensures everyone is heard.

**Use it for.** Getting input on a topic; ensuring every individual has an equal chance to participate, especially where there are status disparities or multi-lingual groups.

**How it works.**
1. Distribute index cards and pens to all participants.
2. Pose the question and invite participants to silently write their top three answers individually for several minutes.
3. Go around the group asking each person for one - and only one - of their answers; the scribe records it on the Work Wall; if someone gives an answer already on your list, cross it off.
4. Keep going around until every answer from every person is up on the board.
5. Group similar answers despite the no-repeats rule.
6. Use Multi-voting or another method to prioritize for further use.

**Timing.** Variable, but usually no more than 30 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Especially useful where there are status disparities or multi-lingual groups. An alternative to individual work is to have people self-organize into "groups of three or four - not two! Not five!" - colloquially known as Kotter Groups after John Kotter.

### Bus Exercise (Organizational Culture)

**What it is.** An exercise that uses the metaphor of a bus on a journey to give participants a way to describe their organizational culture and then envision a future state.

**Use it for.** Mapping the dynamics of an organization's culture and giving participants a metaphorical language for the unique dynamics, positive and negative, of their culture.

**How it works.**
1. Allocate participants into teams of 3-4 max with flipcharts or whiteboards.
2. Round One verbal instructions: draw this organization as if it were a bus on a journey - any kind of bus, any landscape - representing where you are today, what kind of journey, where you are going, what it's like on the bus, what relationships are like. 5 minutes to gather data from anyone in the room, then 20 minutes to draw. "This is not a Rembrandt - kindergarten skills will do."
3. Report outs - keep a light tone but push for explanations of salient details, then ask the group to summarize themes across the pictures.
4. Round Two: draw a bus that represents the ideal Future State in [X years]; capture the legacy you want to leave. 20 minutes.
5. Report outs and a summary of themes for changes or improvements.

**Timing.** Round One: 25 minutes team work plus 5 minutes per team report. Round Two: 20 minutes team work plus 5 minutes per team plus 10 minutes of dialogue.

**Keep in mind.** The purpose is the debrief - schedule plenty of time to explore real cultural dynamics. You can ask groups to create a Shift Paper. Jim Collins' Good to Great quote ("design the bus, get the right people in the right seats") is a useful frame. Pair with Employee of the Future for cultural sanity-checks.

### Trade Shows

**What it is.** A way of getting new information into play through a series of relatively short (30-45 min), concurrent small-group dialogues - each set up in its own breakout area and hosted by someone knowledgeable on the topic.

**Use it for.** Establishing a common knowledge base in Scan; introducing leading practices or case studies; bringing a group up to speed on the "state of play" of a large program.

**How it works.**
1. Identify foundational information participants need; plan 5-10 participants per trade show, offering more shows than any one participant can attend (more than three).
2. Identify and prepare hosts: what to remember about the topic, engaging memorable ways to convey it, evocative questions for participants, visuals/graphics without PowerPoint (sent in advance so scribes can build them).
3. Schedule prep-day time to set up trade shows and brief presenters; map which show goes where.
4. Facilitator describes the process to the large group: three rounds, key rule that if a show is full, go to your second choice. Each host gives a brief pitch.
5. Conduct three rounds, circulating to give time signals, using music to mark round endings.
6. After all three rounds, reconvene and Round-Up: who attended each show, what were the headlines, what's the "news we can use." Scribe captures on a Work Wall.

**Timing.** 10 min introduction plus host pitches; 10 min for teams to organize; 30 min per round; 60-75 min for team debrief (often working lunch); 30-40 min for Domino Report Out.

**Keep in mind.** Preparation is key - work well in advance with hosts and tell them to spend no more than half their time presenting; the format invites conversation. Three rounds is ideal: "The Fourth Repetition (of anything) is Boring!" One option is to put participants in survey teams that ensure at least one member attends every show, then reconvene to share takeaways.

### Deepening Conversation

**What it is.** A structured conversation, adapted from Jack Canfield, that takes pairs (then quartets) through a sequence of open-ended questions designed to accelerate authentic engagement among participants in a remarkably short period.

**Use it for.** Helping participants quickly engage with each other and developing an atmosphere of authenticity and trust.

**How it works.**
1. Have participants find a partner (preferably a stranger) and sit knee to knee.
2. Frame the exercise: open-ended questions, complete control over the answer, roughly a minute or two each, the facilitator times it on an iPhone.
3. For each question: read it aloud, model an answer yourself, pick who goes first by some criterion (longest hair, darkest eyes, most red), say "you have one minute, starting now," keep time, then alternate clockwise/counter-clockwise direction.
4. After several dyad questions, combine two dyads into a group of four and continue with deeper questions ("What is your life story so far?"; "What is something that is up for you these days?").
5. Close with Strength Affirmations - 90 seconds each, popcorn-style, with the recipient just listening and taking it in. Optionally invite the group to hug or otherwise show appreciation.

**Timing.** 45-60 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** You set the tone with how you answer - be intentional, brave, and model personal disclosure. Try out different questions depending on session topic. You can also start in groups of three and keep them together throughout. Mandy Len Catron's "To Fall in Love with Anyone, Do This" identifies 36 questions that accelerate intimacy among strangers - "use with extreme caution."

### Design a Vacation / Design a Home

**What it is.** A simulation that uses a family planning a vacation (or designing a home) as the metaphor for collaborative decision-making with diverse stakeholders and real constraints.

**Use it for.** Giving an experience of the dynamics of collaborative design that meets different stakeholder requirements; exploring the role of limitations - budget, time, etc. - in design.

**How it works.**
1. Divide the group into teams of five or six and hand each person a unique role description (Grandpa, Mom, 17-year-old Billy, etc.) - they read only their own. Roles are written so there is a range of ages, specific likes/dislikes, and no obvious choice that satisfies everyone.
2. Distribute the assignment to design a vacation everyone can enjoy.
3. Hold the family meeting (about 30 minutes) - participants stay in role and pay attention to the trade-offs they make.
4. Team debrief (about 20 minutes) covering questions like: what process did you use? Who took the lead? How did you balance incompatible objectives? Where were the biggest compromises?
5. Large-group debrief connecting the experience to the actual design decisions ahead in the wavespace.

**Timing.** 30 minutes for breakouts, 20 minutes for team debriefs, 20-30 minutes for large-group debrief.

**Keep in mind.** Participants often take their roles seriously - this can be a useful warm-up for the real design decisions to come. Be prepared for a real dialogue in the large-group debrief; have fun, but "land" the real lessons about making design decisions without compromising away legitimate stakeholder interests.

### Design Principle Posters

**What it is.** A practice (developed by Dan Newman) of writing a curated set of Design Principles each on its own large Post-It Note and placing them around the workshop room walls - then engaging the group with the principles as the session unfolds.

**Use it for.** Teaching, illustrating, and reinforcing core principles of collaborative work; creating an opening for participants to use these principles to think through problems in their own work together.

**How it works.**
1. Prepare a "starter kit" of principles on large Post-Its (e.g., "Done is better than perfect," "Knowledge is generated in the room," "Make the intangible tangible," "There is no out there").
2. Place the Post-Its on the walls on Prep Day, building them into the wavespace Opening.
3. Ask participants to stand under the Design Principle that speaks most directly to them, then create teams by randomly matching two principles together - team members explain their preference and prepare a common report.
4. Report out in the large group; supplement with your own stories about the principles that were not chosen.
5. Take every opportunity to engage the group with the principles - ask the group which one applies in a given case rather than declaring it yourself; you can direct them with just a glance.
6. Add to the list as the session evolves.

**Timing.** 5 min explanation; 10 min small-team dialog; ~3 min per principle for reports and dialogue.

**Keep in mind.** A good Design Principle has a short title plus 1-3 sentences of supporting insight, but you only write the title on the Post-It. Start with "the magic number seven, plus or minus two" and grow the list as opportunities arise. With repeat groups, post the same set on the same walls to create continuity. TED Talks can also generate one principle per talk.

### wavespace Opening

**What it is.** The 45-60 minute kickoff of a classic Three-Day wavespace following the executive sponsor's welcoming remarks - the facilitator's opening that gives participants insights and behavioral cues for how to get the most out of the experience.

**Use it for.** Creating an open, ordered environment for the session; embedding implicit, sub-consciously effective instructions on what to expect and how to behave.

**How it works.**
1. Sponsor welcome - the senior sponsor covers why this matters and why now, formal objectives, In-Scope/Out-of-Scope/Givens/Non-negotiables, and personal requests (no calls/texts/emails in session).
2. Involve the group by asking first-time participants to identify themselves and answer "What surprised you when you walked in this morning?" - their answers let you cover the purpose-built environment, focus and no multi-tasking, Scan/Focus/Act, time compression, knowledge resources, the importance of multiple iterations, Work-Wall and hypertile/flipchart standards (legible printing, marker not pen), and Axioms as they arise.
3. Turn to wavespace veterans for advice to colleagues on getting the most out of the session.
4. Cover logistics - schedule, breaks, meals.
5. Deliver a "Model Talk" using a wooden model to explain the process and importance of modeling (see Pattern 307).
6. Lead directly into the next exercise - not a break - to set the cadence for a fast-paced Scan day.

**Timing.** 20-60 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Participant introductions are conspicuously missing from the standard opening - use Facebook Pages instead. Each facilitator must make the opening their own; aim to make eye contact and get a personal response - a nod, a smile - from every participant before moving on.

### The Elephant in the Room

**What it is.** An exercise that brings the metaphor to life with a large stuffed toy elephant - participants anonymously pin "undiscussable" topics on index cards onto the elephant, and at intervals the group brings them up and talks them through.

**Use it for.** Making a clear statement that no topic is "off limits" and allowing the group to surface difficult topics for discussion.

**How it works.**
1. Introduce the elephant as early as possible in the session and invite participants to write any difficult topic on an index card and pin it to the elephant.
2. At convenient intervals - end of day is a good time - review the cards with the Sponsor Design Team and agree how to address them with the group.
3. Hold a group dialogue on these topics.

**Timing.** Group dialogue varies from 10 minutes periodically to 30-40 minute conversations.

**Keep in mind.** It is essential to deal with every issue pinned on the elephant and avoid censoring difficult topics - to do so defeats the purpose. The bigger the physical elephant, the better. In virtual sessions, leave space for "Ellie" at the bottom of an electronic board.

### Empathic Interview

**What it is.** A user-research conversation method for understanding a person's thoughts, emotions, and motivations - one of the two main avenues (with observation) for developing empathy in a user-centered design process.

**Use it for.** Better understanding the users for whom you will be designing and developing insights to inform your designs - particularly for surfacing needs people may not be aware of, the values and emotions that guide behaviors, and stories that reveal deep beliefs about how the world works.

**How it works.**
1. In a team, brainstorm all potential questions, then use Affinity Diagram to group them into themes.
2. Structure the flow: introduce yourself; introduce the project and purpose; build rapport; evoke stories; explore emotions; confirm understanding; thank and wrap up.
3. Refine questions for clarity (no question longer than ten words) - plenty of open-ended "Why?" questions, "Tell me about the last time you ____" prompts, and feeling questions.
4. Test-drive the protocol with a team member who resembles the target audience and revise.
5. Conduct the interview: capture results (interview in pairs or use a voice recorder), one question at a time per person, no binary yes/no questions, avoid "usually" - ask about specific instances, never suggest answers, ask neutrally, get comfortable with silence, watch non-verbal cues, look for inconsistencies between what people say, do, and feel.

**Timing.** 30 to 60 minutes per interview.

**Keep in mind.** Your greatest asset is your own curiosity - direct it to the person, not the protocol. Pay attention to your own emotional reactions and record them after the interview.

### Empathic Observation: What? How? Why?

**What it is.** A structured observation method that drives the observer from concrete description through understanding to motive interpretation - the companion observation method to the Empathic Interview.

**Use it for.** Better understanding the users for whom you will be designing by driving observations to deeper levels and developing insights to inform designs.

**How it works.**
1. Create a three-column template: WHAT (what are they doing?), HOW (how are they doing it?), WHY (why are they doing it this way - take a guess).
2. Select the most representative and evocative photos and arrange them where you can see all of them; number photos and template entries if they aren't all referenced.
3. Start with concrete observations in the WHAT column - use descriptive phrases packed with adjectives.
4. Move to understanding in HOW - is the person rushed, pained, happy? Does the activity appear to impact their state positively or negatively? Use as many descriptive phrases as possible.
5. Risk an interpretation in WHY - a humble but informed guess at motivation and emotions; project meaning into the situation. This step reveals assumptions to test with users and often uncovers unexpected realizations.

**Timing.** 30 to 60 minutes per observation period, plus roughly the same time again to record and analyze findings.

**Keep in mind.** With subjects' permission, take as many photos as practical - they help you reflect more fully later. Our minds filter information; learn to see with "fresh eyes" and a "beginner's mind." In empathic observation your own emotions are key information - trust what you are feeling as a key input, not as the absolute truth.

### Empathy Map

**What it is.** A four-quadrant synthesis tool (Say, Do, Think, Feel) for capturing observations from empathy-building activities and drawing out unexpected insights about user needs.

**Use it for.** Synthesizing observations from a range of empathy exercises and giving you and your team a Group Memory template on which to derive insights about user needs.

**How it works.**
1. Create a four-quadrant template (Say, Do, Think, Feel) on a whiteboard or large butcher paper, leaving room to the left and right.
2. Populate the map with Post-Its, starting with the more objective categories Say and Do, then move to the inferred Think and Feel quadrants.
3. Remember thoughts/beliefs and feelings/emotions cannot be observed directly - infer them from body language, tone, choice of words.
4. Ensure the map is fully populated with observations and inferences.
5. Identify Needs (verbs - activities and desires the user could use help with) directly from observed traits or from contradictions between traits, and list them to the left of the map.
6. Identify Insights (realizations you could leverage) - they often arise from contradictions between user attributes or from asking "Why?" about strange behavior. Write them to the right of the map.

**Timing.** 30 to 60 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Remember the axiom that "the only valid test of an idea, concept, or theory is what it allows you to do" - the same is true of insights, which are just the starting point. Don't fall in love with your own ideas without rigorously testing them with those for whom you are designing.

### Employee of the Future

**What it is.** A module that uncovers implicit cultural assumptions by surfacing the characteristics of the "right kind" of employees an organization selects, hires, and will need going forward.

**Use it for.** Identifying hidden assumptions regarding the skills and personal characteristics of "our kind of people," and identifying specific characteristics necessary for a Future State.

**How it works.**
1. Distribute the individual exercise with about 25 Post-Its per person. Each participant identifies four individuals who would be the kind of team members the company will need in the future they imagine, then writes five descriptive words for each - one per Post-It.
2. Once individual work is done, explain the Affinity Diagram tool.
3. Invite participants to swarm the wall in plenary and use the important-characteristic Post-Its to create an Affinity Diagram of the characteristics needed.
4. Get the group to agree on labels for the major clusters.
5. Debrief with questions: What does this say about what you believe you need to succeed? Surprises? Anything left out? Is this the diversity needed for this market and talent pool? What challenges in attracting and retaining this kind of talent?

**Timing.** 10-15 minutes for individual work, 20-30 minutes for dialogue.

**Keep in mind.** Pair with the Bus Exercise to tease out cultural assumptions and unconscious bias in the group.

### Facebook Pages

**What it is.** A module that lets large groups introduce themselves and learn about each other without lengthy, forgettable verbal introductions - using printed "Facebook Page" templates and photos posted on the Knowledge Wall.

**Use it for.** Sharing personal information in an efficient and fun way; signaling from first entry that this session is a different way of doing things.

**How it works.**
1. Prepare your Facebook Page format in advance and review with the Sponsor Design Team.
2. Prepare sample "5-line self-portraits" to inspire creativity and print page templates at A4 or larger - the largest size your Knowledge Wall can hold.
3. As participants arrive and pick up name badges, take their photos and ask them to complete a Facebook Page information sheet (alternately, have them take a selfie at the Opening and email it to a dedicated Gmail account with their name in the subject line).
4. Over the Scan Day, the KreW prints participant photos and glues them to the Facebook Pages.
5. Arrange the pages on the Knowledge Wall by the start of Focus Day at the latest, preferably earlier.

**Timing.** 5-10 minutes for participants to fill out pages and another 5-10 minutes if doing the selfie variation; main commitment is backstage KreW work.

**Keep in mind.** On Act Day, supplement with KnowledgeWorker team pages. Keep templates filed for reuse. A collected set of Facebook Pages makes a lovely unexpected Deliverable gift; Executive Summary teams have used the five-line portraits as a playful design element.

### Field Trips

**What it is.** A module that sends teams out from the wavespace venue into surrounding organizations to explore how others actually deliver services or perform functions - especially valuable for sessions on customer experience and service delivery.

**Use it for.** Gaining insights into how other organizations actually perform certain functions or deliver unique customer experiences; reinforcing the spirit of active discovery; getting "back stage" with external organizations to interview them on their frameworks.

**How it works.**
1. Set up locations carefully in advance - select sites, brief hosts at those organizations, work out logistics, make cameras available.
2. Write field trip guides covering: background on the organization (printouts of their web pages), questions to ask or topics to consider, and logistics for getting to and from.
3. Teams set out with ample travel time and observe widely - questions like "What is the operating model?", "Who are its customers?", "What value does it deliver?", "How does work actually flow?", "How are decisions made?"
4. Teams gather artifacts and photos and "guide their guide" with prompting questions.
5. Teams return, debrief in breakouts, and prepare an 8-minute report covering site visited, what was learned, and main takeaways.

**Timing.** Varies with travel time. Typically 10 min set-up, 30-60 min field visit, 20-30 min team debrief, 8-10 min per team for reports and dialogue.

**Keep in mind.** Field trips work best in urban environments with easy access to a number of different organizations. One reporting modality uses an MG Taylor erasable tabletop as canvas - the team rolls it up front, the image is captured on video and photographed. A tamer alternative is a web-based experience comparing the host organization's online service delivery to competitors'.

### Fishbowl Conversation

**What it is.** A conversation in which two or more people discuss a topic in front of a larger group who actively listen but do not participate - the format insulates the "fish" from having to react to the audience so they can have a real conversation among themselves.

**Use it for.** Allowing participants to observe and understand the perspectives, insights, and thinking processes of those in the fishbowl; allowing the fish to express themselves conversationally rather than in formal presentation. Typically used in the Scan phase.

**How it works.**
1. Arrange the room either theater-in-the-round or panel-at-the-front-of-the-room.
2. Introduce the topic, participants, and rationale for the format.
3. Hold the conversation.
4. At the formal conclusion, elicit feedback from the observers: "What did you observe? What are your take-aways?"

**Timing.** 30-60 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Introduce the exercise carefully and explain the "no questions at this time" rationale to keep things focused. You can rotate fish in and out of the bowl, or run a "tag-team" where a new participant taps in and replaces a current participant - be very clear about the rules for tapping in.

### Haiku

**What it is.** A short poem in the Japanese form (17 syllables across three lines: 5/7/5) that uses sensory language to capture a feeling or image - used in wavespace as a creative way to deliver reports on evocative subjects.

**Use it for.** Inspiring the creativity of a group to reflect deeply on a subject; inviting a more poetic treatment of the subject than a typical report does.

**How it works.**
1. Frame the assignment with brief context on the haiku form (5-7-5 syllables, sensory language, traditional embrace of the shadow side).
2. Give the team two parts: write a haiku that captures the essence of your insights about [topic], and prepare an artistic way to present it - illustrate, dramatize, or present it musically.
3. Allow ~30-45 minutes for team work; presentations the following morning.
4. Each team has 6-8 minutes to present.

**Timing.** Approximately 30-45 minutes for team work and 6-8 minutes per team for reports.

**Keep in mind.** Haiku from this exercise often provide images and insights that are important throughout the rest of the session - find a way to post them on the Knowledge Wall after they are presented.

### Hassle Map

**What it is.** A tool from Adrian Slywotzky and Karl Weber that drives a detailed study of the problems people experience using a product or service - mapping the gap between what people buy and what they truly need from the customer's perspective. Slywotzky and Weber: "The Hassle Map is really a treasure map - a chart of hidden opportunities to create new demand by fixing customer hassles once and for all."

**Use it for.** Identifying current hassles or opportunities for dissatisfaction with a product or service; brainstorming new products or services.

**How it works.**
1. Identify the focus for the Hassle Map.
2. Take the customer's perspective and ask: What is the psychology of our customers? Which hassles drive them crazy? Which hassles barely register because they are so familiar - but might be fixable? Where does our product waste time, squander money, create confusion, require extra steps, or generate avoidable risk - even slightly? Consider different customer segments with different use profiles.
3. Chart the answers onto a Flowchart.
4. Validate the Hassle Map through customer interviews or, better yet, direct observation of customer behavior.
5. Redesign the product or service to eliminate the hassles.
6. Quantify the difference.

**Timing.** 30-45 minutes for team work and 6-8 minutes for each team's report.

**Keep in mind.** Download "The Art of Hassle Thinking" by Slywotzky and Weber for additional examples. The Henk Kwakman / Nespresso quote applies here: "When you discover a problem, you discover a business."

### History of the Future (Timeline)

**What it is.** A module that creates a common, group picture of how we got where we are today and then invites participants to project trends and events into the future, building a shared visual timeline.

**Use it for.** Helping groups with different perspectives hear each other and develop a common view of past trends and events; putting participants in a mode of curiosity about how we got here and where we are going; assessing a group's hopes and fears about the future.

**How it works.**
1. Select a large wall or long sheet of butcher paper and add tiles for the current year, X years back, and Y years forward.
2. Add small thought-starter category tiles - Politics, Science and Technology, Demographics, Society and Culture, etc.
3. Have participants self-organize into groups of 3 or 4 and sum up the Current State of [topic] in 3 words; collect and scribe under the current date.
4. Invite participants to come up to the wall, write a trend or event that contributed to the current state, and explain its significance. Demonstrate first.
5. When energy ebbs (or the wall fills), step X years into the future and from that perspective report on the trends and events important in understanding that time.
6. Close with: "The point is not to predict the future. Nobody can do that with 100% accuracy. The point is to identify the things we need to do today to prepare for an uncertain future."

**Timing.** Approximately 45-60 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Invite several people to write at the board at once to keep things moving. Have scribes elaborate and add visuals to the timeline. Optionally hand out 4x4 Post-Its for written contributions. One variation is to offer a "really far out there" future - 50, 100, or 1000 years hence.

### How a Team Can Read a Book in an Hour

**What it is.** A module that helps teams take in the information from entire books efficiently by chunking the book into bite-sized portions, processing each separately, and recomposing the book through a sequenced dialogue.

**Use it for.** Resource for groups doing the Metaphors or Syntopical Reading modules; helping the large group digest material that will become a basic framework for their work.

**How it works.**
1. Identify the book and prepare enough copies of the book or of the chunks.
2. Prepare assignments that chunk the reading material into equal portions digestible in the time allotted.
3. Distribute among small Kotter Groups in the plenary space - each team gets a chapter or chunk plus a short reading on its strategies (e.g., for Switch by the Heath brothers, separate teams get Direct the Rider, Motivate the Elephant, Shape the Path).
4. Each team scans the material for ~25 minutes (don't read every word, just get the main points), discusses for ~15 minutes, and prepares a 3-4 minute presentation.
5. Report out in the order of the chapters of the book to recreate its flow; scribes capture key points in plenary, and photos go to the Knowledge Wall as ongoing reference.

**Timing.** 5 min set-up, 25-30 min reading, 15-20 min team dialogue, 5-10 min per team report.

**Keep in mind.** In the large-group debrief, take the time it takes to recompose the book's logic and main points - people may feel rushed in their small teams, so let the take-aways "land." Keep the chunks of books for reuse.

### How to Read a Book in an Hour

**What it is.** A one-page set of instructions, loosely adapted from Mortimer Adler and Charles Van Doren's How to Read A Book, that teaches strategies for taking in a book efficiently by scanning, skimming, and reading only the most essential parts.

**Use it for.** A resource for groups doing the Metaphors or Syntopical Reading modules; an adjunct to the Authors homework assignment.

**How it works.** The page is built around four basic questions efficient readers ask:
1. What is the book about as a whole? Read the Preface or Introduction; scan the Table of Contents to understand the author's organizing logic.
2. What is being said, and how? Read the first and/or last chapters. Make a list of main points and conclusions; for each, list key words. Use those to navigate the Table of Contents or Index to detail sections; read and take notes.
3. Do you accept that the book is true, in whole or part? Once you know what is being said, decide whether you find the arguments convincing.
4. So what? What is the significance? Did the book teach you anything new? Inspire you to learn more? Most importantly, does it inspire you to actually do anything?

**Timing.** 5 minutes to introduce, 10 minutes for participants to pick up books at end of day; the exercise itself takes about an hour of individual time.

**Keep in mind.** Print the page and insert it into the books available for prework, homework, or Authors. The approach actually works although some may regard it as "cheating" to take in information so efficiently - reassure the group that it most definitely is not.

### Idea Rave (Roving Take-A-Panel, Walkabout)

**What it is.** A module in which different topics are written on different Work Walls (or flip charts) throughout the environment, and individuals walk about adding their personal contributions; teams then review, synthesize, and present the "sense of the group" on those topics.

**Use it for.** Quickly getting group input on a range of topics. Can also be used in Focus to generate feedback on emerging ideas or to surface concerns without attribution.

**How it works.**
1. Develop a list of topics for which you want input - balance the number against wall real estate and the small teams that will synthesize one or two topics each.
2. Write each topic on its own WorkWall.
3. Explain the module and invite participants to walk about and contribute to different topics. The only rule: you can only add; you cannot erase anything anyone else has written.
4. Appoint teams to work with one (or two) topics and prepare to report.
5. Reports and dialogue.

**Timing.** 20 minutes for walk-about input, 15-20 minutes for team review, 5-10 minutes per team for reports and dialogue.

**Keep in mind.** This module is also useful in Focus for getting concerns out without attribution.

### In the Shoes of... (Stakeholder Scenarios)

**What it is.** A module in which teams take on a perspective different from their typical role - their customers, those above or below them in the hierarchy, suppliers, even competitors or adversaries - and work as if they were that stakeholder. This is "one of the most important and versatile modules in the toolbox."

**Use it for.** Getting the full range of perspectives on a problem and giving each a voice; moving people beyond stereotypes of the "other"; developing empathy and appreciation for the role and challenges of other players in a complex system.

**How it works.**
1. Identify the full range of players in the system.
2. Collect background information on each player - rational, emotional, and political dimensions of being in that position (see the Rational/Emotional/Political Model).
3. Carefully form teams so that some - but not all - members have personal experience with the role.
4. Part 1: Teams read background material in silence, then share key points; conduct a SWOT analysis as their assigned stakeholder. (~60 min)
5. Part 2: Identify priorities and the top 3-5 actions they need to take in the next 6-12 months, plus the assets and capabilities required. (~30 min)
6. Part 3: Reports and dialogue - other teams listen for how other players are viewing the issue, what they are planning, and how that affects their own group. (6-10 min per group)
7. Part 4: Teams revise their plans in light of what other stakeholders are planning, and reflect on what it felt like to be in this role and what implications that has for products and services. (~75 min)

**Timing.** Total ~4 hours including reports.

**Keep in mind.** Spread your "experts" on a particular role across groups - don't place them all in the group dealing with that role, so others get to walk in those shoes. The basic approach is also useful for setting up more intricate Simulations. See Patterns 241 (Lead and learn with experience), 244 (Don't argue, test), and 245 (Work as play).

### Inventions

**What it is.** A hands-on team exercise in which each team is given an "impossible" assignment - to invent, build, and demonstrate a working device that performs an unusual task using odd items, hardware, and simple hand tools - then a Round Two combines four teams' inventions into a Rube Goldberg-like mega-invention.

**Use it for.** Having participants act rather than just think; having fun and developing a sense of accomplishment by solving an "impossible" assignment as a team; revealing the dynamics of team creativity and observing different problem-solving styles.

**How it works.**
1. Collect a wide range of "Stuff" well in advance (plywood platforms, dowels, tennis balls, buckets, balloons, mousetraps, PVC pipe, wind-up clocks, toy trucks, etc.) plus a Tools Table and a Sawing Station.
2. Before participants enter, each picks three items from the Stuff Table and goes to their team area for full instructions.
3. Round One: small groups get one of four different inventions (e.g., move a tennis ball around a 3-foot circle and raise a flag; load a tennis ball into a vehicle and move it 7 feet; launch a tennis ball 5 feet at a target). Triggering must be constructed, not human; the structure must complete the task without further human intervention. Approximately 90 minutes.
4. Round Two: three or four teams combine their devices into one integrated invention - the tennis ball must move through all four without being touched. Reduce material count without losing functionality. 60 minutes.
5. Demonstrations, judging (lowest qualified score wins - 1 point per second plus 1 point per material), and a 30-45 min team debrief tying the experience back to organizational dynamics ("How is what happened in the exercise like what happens on the job here?").

**Timing.** Set-up 15 min, Round One 90 min, Round Two 60 min, Demonstrations 15 min, Debrief 45-60 min - total ~4 hours.

**Keep in mind.** Preparation is crucial - the odder the Stuff, the better. High-quality tools sell the seriousness of the module. Increase debrief time if results went either extremely well or extremely poorly. Typically conducted at end of day so the KreW has time to clean up.

### It's Just Lunch

**What it is.** A variant of the Field Trip module where small teams of participants are assigned a lunch date with an interesting local person - someone with knowledge, experiences, or skills relevant to the Scan.

**Use it for.** Gaining insights directly from an individual who has important knowledge, experiences, or skills relevant to the session's objectives.

**How it works.**
1. Set up carefully in advance - select and brief lunch guests individually, arrange restaurant reservations and travel logistics.
2. Write discussion guides covering background on the lunch date (printouts of their web pages), questions to ask, and logistics.
3. Teams set out with ample travel time. The guidance: "no pressure! This is not a date. It's just a chance to break bread with a fascinating person." Sample questions: What are you passionate about? How do you connect those passions to your work? How did you decide to start your venture? What kinds of transitions has it been through? What did you need to get right? If you had to do it over again, what would you do differently?
4. Pay for the meal, save receipts, return at the appointed time.
5. Team debrief (~60 minutes) on the experience and key learnings, then 8-10 minute reports.

**Timing.** 10 min set-up, variable travel, 60-75 min lunch, 60 min team debrief, 8-10 min per team report.

**Keep in mind.** Don't be shy about reaching out to people with no prior connection - many accomplished people are happy to share what they've learned over a free lunch. Facilitate a thank you note from the wavespace sponsor on letterhead, signed by the sponsor.

### Jeopardy!

**What it is.** An adaptation of the TV game show Jeopardy used in wavespace as a fun way to share, review, and reinforce information needed for the session - players "give the answer in the form of a question" with point values rising by difficulty.

**Use it for.** Reviewing strategy, mission, or other information that is widely but not universally known; reviewing pre-work and level-setting; reviewing the outcome of Trade Shows.

**How it works.**
1. Identify five broad categories - these become column headers.
2. Work with the SDT to identify five answers and questions per category, sorted easiest to hardest. Test for clarity and accuracy. Create a master sheet for the host.
3. Prepare the game board: for each item, tape three 8.5x11 pages bottom-up - the "answer in the form of a question," the item to be identified, and the value.
4. Hide the board until you introduce the game; prepare flipcharts to record team scores; buy small identical flashlights, one per team; download the Jeopardy theme.
5. Divide into teams (no more than 5) - large groups can populate three or four teams by drawing names from a hat. Each team elects a spokesperson with the flashlight as the buzzer. Pick the first team by lot; they choose category and value; questions must be answered in order from low to high within a category. Correct answers add the value, wrong answers subtract. If all are wrong, the choosing team picks again. The team with the most points wins.

**Timing.** 45 to 60 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Double-check questions - what seems obvious to insiders may be opaque to players. It's OK to have your executive sponsor or project leader add "color commentary" to questions as you play - the point is to get information across. Digital versions exist at superteachertools.us/jeopardyx and jeopardylabs.com.

### Kotter Groups

**What it is.** A way of quickly forming small teams in the plenary space, named after John Kotter who used it in Partner Leadership programs at Ernst & Young's Cambridge ASE.

**Use it for.** Rapidly forming small teams of 3 or 4 to consider an issue together; creating the groups for Balanced Brainstorming.

**How it works.**
1. Tell the group: "Organize yourselves into small groups of 3 or 4 people - Not two! Not five! - and move your chairs together so you can talk to each other."
2. Distribute the assignment verbally or on hypertiles or clipboards.
3. Give clear instructions on how much time the groups have to work.

**Timing.** ~5 min to set up and form, 6-10 min team work, 5-10 min per team for reports and dialogue.

**Keep in mind.** Extremely useful and versatile technique to generate participant input "on the fly."

### Legends (A Day in the Life)

**What it is.** An exercise in which participants project themselves ten years into the future and recount, in legendary or mythic terms, how they overcame obstacles and setbacks to achieve a successful outcome - skits to be delivered as creative report-outs. A variant is "A Day in the Life" describing the organization at a future point.

**Use it for.** Using humor and storytelling to draw out deep truths and possible solutions; having participants embody the possibility that success is attainable and experience the conditions that might result.

**How it works.**
1. This module typically follows other Future State work.
2. When the assignment drops, the KreW puts out a selection of costumes, props, and other materials to inspire fun and creativity.
3. Groups work for ~30 minutes at the end of the day; props and costumes will be just as they left them in the morning.
4. Reports start first thing the following morning - groups sometimes need a little additional time to prepare. Six minutes of stage time per team.
5. After reports, hold a dialogue to reflect on the truths delivered through stories and skits.

**Timing.** 30-45 min team working, 10 min per report (manage tightly to avoid creep).

**Keep in mind.** The more attention to props and costumes - even a "dress-up box" or large white T-shirts with markers - the easier it is to get participants in the spirit. The KreW's willingness to source unusual props matters (a memorable late-night Walmart run produced a homemade gorilla costume from brown bathmats). Use theme music; most songs have a karaoke version on YouTube. Vary the setting - awards dinner, retirement party, ten-year reunion. Be ready to use the opening that humor provides to land serious points.

### The Marshmallow Challenge

**What it is.** An interactive competitive team exercise (popularized by Tom Wujec's TED Talk) in which teams have 18 minutes to build the tallest freestanding tower they can using twenty pieces of dry spaghetti, one yard of string, one yard of masking tape - with a marshmallow on top.

**Use it for.** Demonstrating the power of rapid prototyping; exploring the dynamics of effective teams.

**How it works.**
1. Assemble a kit per 4-person team in a paper lunch bag (twenty spaghetti, yard of tape, yard of string, one campfire-sized marshmallow), plus tables, yardsticks/measuring tapes, a countdown app, video projector and sound, an upbeat music playlist, and a prize.
2. Read the rules to the large group: tallest freestanding structure measured tabletop to top of marshmallow (not suspended); the entire marshmallow on top (no cutting); use as much/little of the kit as you want; spaghetti, tape, and string can be broken/cut; 18 minutes; touching the structure when time ends disqualifies you.
3. During the exercise, walk the room, call out time signals (12, 9, 7, 5, 3, 2, 1 min, 30 sec, 10-second countdown), call out which teams have standing structures, build friendly rivalry, raise the energy.
4. After the clock, sit everyone down. Measure shortest to tallest, recording on a flipchart. Identify the winning team and award the prize.
5. Play Tom Wujec's TED Talk "Build a tower, build a team," covering the key lessons: kids do better than business school students; prototyping matters (start with the marshmallow); the marshmallow is a metaphor for hidden assumptions of a project that need to be tested early.
6. Facilitate a discussion on lessons the group wants to take away.

**Timing.** 5-10 min set-up, 20-25 min team work and judging, 20-30 min debrief.

**Keep in mind.** Use music, energy, and the promise of valuable prizes during the exercise; then land the messages with substantive dialogue after the TED Talk. Try to ensure most members haven't done it before; if some have, ask them to apply lessons they learned the first time.

### Masterpiece

**What it is.** A module in which teams are given a piece of a "masterpiece" to recreate using paints - the reveal is that each team's piece is just one element of a larger picture that fits together.

**Use it for.** A good warm-up for teams whose objectives include building a solution out of previously independent elements; having creative fun.

**How it works.**
1. Assemble supplies: a piece of art appropriate to the group and topic (typically divided into eight pieces), foam core or other painting surface, water-based paints and brushes, drop cloths and smocks.
2. Give the briefest of introductions to get teams to work - this is about experience and discovery. The rules: "Use the materials provided to recreate your masterpiece. See rule #1. See rule #2. There are no further rules."
3. Allow ~30 minutes for team painting.
4. Assemble the pieces into the full masterpiece (~10 min).
5. Debrief with a question or two: "What did you learn that we need to remember?" Themes typically surface around the importance of having the Big Picture from the beginning, paying attention to connections between pieces, and communication between groups working on parts of a problem.

**Timing.** 5-10 min set up, 30 min team work, 10 min to assemble, 10-20 min debrief.

**Keep in mind.** Tom Kehner uses Georges Seurat's The Circus (Zazzle.com) - the seated couple in the third row, just left of the horse-riding lady's elbow, gets divided into 8 sections; the reveal then shows that what they painted was only a small piece of a much larger artwork. Two "Aha" moments: pieces fit together (integration) and the painted piece is only one small part of a larger ecosystem.

### Metaphors

**What it is.** A variation of Syntopical Reading where the topics of the readings are metaphorically related to the wavespace problem rather than directly related - drawing on Howard Gardner's "searchlight intelligence" to discover useful connections across spheres of activity (e.g., ants for organizational design, Velcro from burrs).

**Use it for.** Broadening thinking by "making the strange familiar"; igniting creativity; subtly introducing new, promising avenues of thought or discovery.

**How it works.**
1. With the SDT and KnowledgeWorker team, identify metaphors that might be useful: "What is this problem like?"
2. Well in advance of the wavespace, develop a reading list for each topic - challenging but reachable.
3. Write the assignment in three parts: Read (frame the metaphor's connection to objectives, instruct on materials and how to distribute among the team), Dialogue (share what you learned, generate questions specific to the metaphor), and Apply (build a set of principles for the actual organization based on the metaphor).
4. Carefully coordinate the distribution of materials - many moving parts.
5. In conducting, let teams know how much time remains in each part. Monitor time and noise carefully in Read; you can distribute Dialogue early if groups are ready.

**Timing.** Read 45-60 min; Dialogue 30 min; Apply 30-45 min; Reports and dialogue 7-10 min per team.

**Keep in mind.** "Read widely and weirdly" in areas far afield from your natural expertise. Natural biological metaphors have been especially useful - Bees, Gardening, Rainforests, Evolution, Anthropology, Salt Marshes, Tide Pools, Marriage, Living Systems, Cultures. Build a reusable library for common metaphors. In environments with extensive libraries, you can have participants search for materials themselves (extend the Read time by ten minutes).

### Multi-Faith Reflections

**What it is.** Periods of prayer, worship, and reflection within multi-faith wavespace experiences - structured by simple guidelines so that authentic expressions of particular religious/spiritual communities don't devolve into a bland lowest-common-denominator experience.

**Use it for.** Creating periods of calm reflection within the on-going work; creating space for communal multi-faith learning and celebration; engaging multiple faith traditions in affirmation; inviting prayerful worship into the community.

**How it works.**
1. Think clearly with the SDT about the role of Multi-Faith Reflections in the session.
2. Prepare guidelines for facilitators including a fair, open, unbiased process for choosing leaders.
3. Open the opportunities to the full range of faith groups in attendance.
4. Schedule sessions not only at the beginning and close of days but anytime contemplative reflection can move the group forward (or inward).
5. At the outset, describe the approach - intent and mechanics - and invite the group to enter in the spirit of Respect, Integrity, and Generosity (illustrated with the Zulu words Sawubona/"I see you and call you by your right name," Sikhona/"I am here," and Ubuntu/"I am because we are").
6. Acknowledge that multi-faith work is messy and there will be misses; offer each other respect, integrity, and generosity at those times.

Practical guidance for those leading reflections: credential the multi-faith network (acknowledge diversity in the room), credential your own tradition, invite others into your experience (allow them to participate or not in ways appropriate for them), and practice self-focus when praying in the name of your God or Higher Power (use "I" rather than "we").

**Timing.** Usually 10-15 minutes, but varies with topic.

**Keep in mind.** No one has the Final Word on how to design these without error or possible offense. Stay humble, pay attention, and invite the entire group into the process of discovery.

### Multi-Voting

**What it is.** A technique for narrowing down a list of ideas or options - used in conjunction with Brainstorming or Balanced Brainstorming.

**Use it for.** Selecting among or prioritizing a set of options or ideas.

**How it works.**
1. Use Brainstorming to generate a list of topics; review, clarify, and combine ideas where necessary.
2. Have each person assign ten points distributed across one or more ideas any way they choose (all ten on one, five and five, etc.).
3. Vote using one of three options: ten small Post-Its placed next to items on the flipchart; reading items and having team members hold up the number of fingers representing their votes; or going around the team having members call out votes.
4. Tally votes for each idea. Narrow the list down to the four to six items that received the most votes.

**Timing.** 10-15 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** To preserve anonymity, Multi-Voting can be done by written ballot. To avoid stacking the deck, use a variation called 10/4 Multi-Voting - individuals get ten votes but no more than four to any one item.

### One Word

**What it is.** An icebreaker exercise (developed by Tom Kehner) that brings rudimentary People Science (Brandon Klein's approach) into an event by asking each participant to write One Word in response to a question and then forming Kotter Groups based on similar, synergistic, or identical words.

**Use it for.** Beginning to connect participants with each other; introducing the concept of Patches and Nodes.

**How it works.**
1. Identify a topic and put it in question form (e.g., "What is an area of possible future success for this company?").
2. Distribute a writing surface and a Sharpie fine point pen to each participant.
3. Tell the group you will read a question and they are to write One Word - and only one word - that captures where they are. They can elaborate later.
4. Read the question aloud and show them your own One Word, written large and legibly (Tom K's is always LOVE).
5. Give the group 2 minutes to come up with their word.
6. "Stand up and, in silence, hold up your One Word, and form [Kotter] groups of three or four (Not two! Not five!) based on similar, synergistic, or exactly the same words."
7. As a team, introduce yourselves, expand on your One Word, and identify two or three reasons why your words are the best answer. 10-15 minutes.
8. Optional: Debrief in plenary; or create a Wordle by collecting the One Words, sorting and rationalizing them in a spreadsheet, then pasting into wordle.net for the Knowledge Wall.

**Timing.** Set up: 5 min. Individual work: 2-4 min. Team working: 10-15 min. Debrief: 10-15 min.

**Keep in mind.** Set an energetic and interested tone throughout, especially as an icebreaker. Experiment with different trigger questions.

### Panel Discussion

**What it is.** A moderated conversation among two to four subject matter experts who have information to contribute to the wavespace experience - typically held on the first morning of Scan Day. The model below is from Carolyn Buck Luce.

**Use it for.** Getting a range of information on a particular subject into the room and into play; modeling a way of exploring different sides of an issue by guiding the interaction of people with different points of view.

**How it works.**
1. Secure participation - this requires lead time. With the SDT confirm: panel topic and learning objectives, A-list panelists and who will contact them, an experienced moderator who will interview panelists in advance, stipend or expense coverage, panelists' attendance scope, tight confirmation timeline, and a phone "script" for outreach.
2. Moderator prepares an outline using Buck Luce's "Three Chapters Model." A typical 60-minute panel: Introductions (5-10 min) - moderator introduces the topic, panelists introduce themselves; Chapter One (10-15 min); Chapter Two (10-15 min); Chapter Three (10-15 min, the most thought-provoking question); Q&A with participants (5-15 min); Final Words from each panelist (4-8 min).
3. As soon as panelists are confirmed, the moderator interviews each by phone (~30 min) - reviews session objectives, shares and iterates the Three Chapters questions, becomes conversant enough to ask good follow-ups and surface divergent views.
4. On Prep Day the KreW arranges tall bar stools and determines whether mics are needed (for room acoustics or for video recording).
5. Moderator meets all panelists at breakfast on Scan Day morning and leads the panel according to the planned flow.

Choreography note: ensure panelists have equal talk time and three large (3-5 min) plus one small (1-2 min) "bites at the apple" on the learning objectives.

**Timing.** 60-75 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Actively discourage panelists from bringing PowerPoint - prepare them for a conversation, not a mini-presentation. The Three Chapters flow also works for a one-person interview. Set a count-down timer where the moderator can see it. Giving panelists the almost-final word is a useful wrap-up - the moderator uses the actual final word to put a bow on what participants "just learned." Follow with a short break so participants can engage informally with panelists.

### Planning for Uncertainty

**What it is.** A module loosely adapted from Royal Dutch/Shell's scenario planning approach in which the group identifies the two greatest areas of uncertainty, organizes them into a 2x2 matrix, and systematically explores the resulting four scenarios. Per de Geus: "The point of scenario planning is not to predict the future. It is to identify those things that we must do today to prepare for an uncertain future."

**Use it for.** Identifying possible future state conditions; identifying weak signal indicators that will help determine the direction of trends; identifying actions that must be taken in the near term to prepare for challenging futures.

**How it works.**
1. Explain the intent and invite the group to Brainstorm in plenary or Kotter Groups the areas of greatest uncertainty.
2. Use dialogue or Multi-Voting to identify the top two areas of uncertainty. For each, make the extreme forms come alive in specific concrete examples; build a 2x2 matrix on a WorkWall with the extremes clearly marked (e.g., ACA Intact-to-Repealed crossed with National Economy Expansion-to-Recession).
3. Assign teams to each of the four quadrants. Their assignment is to: explore what the world looks like for the organization, identify positive and negative implications, develop an organizational strategy, identify early warning signals, develop 3-5 actions the organization needs to take today, and give the scenario a name.
4. Reports and dialogue.
5. In your next SDT meeting, explore how to build the outcomes into the rest of the wavespace.

**Timing.** ~20-25 min to identify the two axes; 75 min for team work; 8 min per team report.

**Keep in mind.** The set-up matters - stress that all this work is extremely tentative and not in any way predictive. There is also a variant where the facilitator does the initial ideation with the SDT and crafts fully developed scenarios for teams; this version has the entire participant group help identify the most significant axes.

### Plus/Delta

**What it is.** A quick feedback tool that captures a group's sense of what is working - the Pluses - along with what could be done differently - the Deltas.

**Use it for.** Capturing feedback after an experience, especially at the end of each day during a wavespace.

**How it works.**
1. Draw a vertical line down the center of a WorkWall or flipchart.
2. Label the left side with a + symbol and the right side with a Δ symbol.
3. Explain the purpose and invite the group to brainstorm what is working - the pluses.
4. Brainstorm the deltas - things to do differently moving forward.
5. Conclude by summarizing the changes you will make moving forward.

**Timing.** 10 minutes or so. Keep it moving.

**Keep in mind.** If you use this at the end of one day, at the start of the next day be sure to let the group know what changes you have built in as a result of their feedback.

### Quaker Conversation

**What it is.** A communal conversation modeled on Quaker (Religious Society of Friends) worship - the group sits in silence until a member feels moved to speak, then silence again until another rises. Each person speaks his or her own message; cross-talk is discouraged. Each person should speak only once or at most twice.

**Use it for.** Providing space for a different kind of communication; giving participants time to reflect, clarify feelings, and speak from the heart and give voice to their personal truth.

**How it works.**
1. Often scheduled ad hoc as an "audible" in response to a major shift in group energy or "disturbance in the Force." Always entail a quick conference with the SDT before changing the agenda.
2. Held in the plenary space; if time permits, the KreW can reset the room into a circle or rows facing each other (the format of a traditional Friend's Meeting House).
3. The facilitator and KreW set the proper tone of respectful attention. Cameras are turned off.
4. After a brief introduction of the process and guidelines, the facilitator remains silent unless restating the rules - usually in response to cross-talk or someone speaking more than once or twice.
5. Follow the conversation with a break, perhaps in silence, with soft reflective music.

**Timing.** 45-75 minutes depending on group energy and topic challenge.

**Keep in mind.** People are uncomfortable with silence - the facilitator must be patient and willing to wait it out; your strong presence creates the container that invites people to give voice. This module often creates an energetic shift; be prepared to make changes to the rest of the agenda. The term "Quaker Conversation" is optional - alternatives include "Critical Conversation" or "Community Conversation."

### Question-storming

**What it is.** A technique that applies the basic technique of Brainstorming to coming up with better questions about a problem - inviting groups to dig deeper and challenge assumptions ("Why does this problem even exist? Why are we doing this in the first place?"). Also known as "frame-storming" (Matthew E. May) or the Question Formulation Technique (Right Question Institute).

**Use it for.** Deepening a group's understanding of a problem; identifying heretofore unspoken concerns or challenges; identifying avenues for analyzing a problem.

**How it works.**
1. Identify an issue and "problematize" it as a short statement (e.g., "Only 65% of our current customers say they are 'highly likely' to refer us to others.").
2. Form groups of 4-6, with one scribe per group, and follow normal Brainstorming rules - one question at a time, speaker offers exactly one question, scribe writes it exactly as stated, no discussion or criticism, build on others' questions, passing is OK.
3. Review and improve - combine, expand, or tighten; one strategy is to open all closed (yes/no) questions and close the open ones.
4. Prioritize the top two or three questions to share with the larger group.
5. Report out and use dialogue or Multi-Voting to determine the most useful questions.
6. Develop an Action Plan to move forward with those questions.
7. Open a dialogue regarding what people learned about the issue and about using questions in this way.

**Timing.** 60 min total - 5 min set-up, 10-15 min Question-storming, 5 min per group report-out, 10 min dialogue and Multi-Voting, 15-20 min Action Plan, 10-15 min closing dialogue.

**Keep in mind.** Per Kevin Kelly in The Inevitable, answers are now ubiquitous online; the value is in better questions. A good question challenges existing answers, cannot be answered immediately, creates new territory of thinking, reframes its own answers, and is the seed of innovation.

### Say Boom Whack!

**What it is.** A musical opening tool (developed by Suzan Lutke) that uses Boomwhackers - large plastic tubes tuned to a musical scale (recommended: C Major Diatonic Scale Set, one per participant) - to give a group an experience of finding a shared beat and "tone of the group" before composing a short piece together.

**Use it for.** Starting the wavespace experience or a day; giving a musical break to energize the day.

**How it works.**
1. Without Boomwhackers, ask participants to stand in a circle and clap in unison - then pick a faster or slower beat without conducting them.
2. "Play the Circle" - around the circle, men clap once (quarter note), women clap twice (eighth notes), people wearing glasses are a rest. Don't conduct - let it happen. Go around twice.
3. Find the "Tone of the Group" - everyone sings a note when you signal; have them listen carefully and settle on one note (closing eyes can help if leadership-heavy groups struggle to listen). When they have it, conduct louder/softer; with hands together they sing the Tone, hands apart adds harmonic richness. Return to it throughout the session as a touchpoint.
4. Distribute Boomwhackers, group people by matching color/size, and "Play the Circle" again with each group sounding their note.
5. Rearrange large to small to create a Boomwhacker piano you (or a musician from the group) can play.
6. Form groups of 5-6 each with a different Boomwhacker and assign them to create a short piece (start and end on D/Orange, follow the beat, everyone participates) - 5 minutes to create, then perform.
7. Combine all team songs into one composition; perform it.

**Timing.** Approximately 30-45 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Designed for a group to experience their shared heartbeat - literally, not just metaphorically. The Tone of the Group is a useful touchpoint to restore them to each other. Avoid becoming the conductor where noted - create space for leadership to arise from the group. Icelandic soccer supporters' clap on YouTube can give an inspirational start or finish.

### SDT Morning Circle Up

**What it is.** A 7am Sponsor Design Team meeting on Days 1 and 2 of a typical three-day wavespace - giving the SDT an hour to review changes made overnight to assignments and to pre-caffeinate before participants arrive.

**Use it for.** Building alignment among the SDT; reviewing the flow of the day, edits to assignments, and final team lists.

**How it works.**
1. Preparation: Have the writing team complete, print, and post one copy of latest team lists and assignments on a WorkWall the night before (don't rely on morning printing). Review assignment edits before or right after the 6:30 KreW Circle Up. Objectives, Scope, and Non-negotiables should be scribed and posted in plenary. Schedule breakfast so KreW eats at 6:30 and sponsors at 7:00. Have red pens for SDT edits.
2. Cover the agenda: Review the Day's Strawdog. Review assignments at the WorkWall - on the single posted copy to avoid contradictory edits you can't reconcile. Review team lists.
3. Complete the work by no later than 7:45 so the SDT has time to greet participants and the primary sponsor is ready (and miked, if needed) for the Sponsor Opening.

**Timing.** 30-45 minutes. It is important to set the tone of the wavespace experience on time.

**Keep in mind.** Keep your own mood bright and upbeat. Have a KnowledgeWorker supporting you to convey changes to your Process Facilitator and edits to the Writing Team.

### SDT Scan Day Evening Circle Up

**What it is.** An evening checkpoint with the Sponsor Design Team after the long Scan Day - reviewing the experience of the entire group, developing the Strawdog for Day Two, and confirming engagement of the SDT itself. The Circle Up cannot end until you have a Day Two plan.

**Use it for.** Taking stock of progress and learnings from the first full day; confirming the Strawdog, assignments, and team lists for the second day.

**How it works.**
1. Preparation - have as much as possible posted on WorkWalls before the Circle Up: Day Two Strawdog; team strips for Day Two team lists; Day Two assignments (or a structure to develop them after). If venue/culture permit, have surprise "adult beverages" - beer and wine - waiting in the wings to be brought out half way through. Assign a scribe.
2. Cover the agenda: Review the day - "What did you learn? Were there any surprises?" Review the Day Two Strawdog and call out decisions the SDT must make tonight. Get SDT input to written assignments or to an outline. Once you have a clear sense of all topics, ask the SDT to distribute participants into teams.
3. Close by reminding them of the morning Circle Up timing where they will review the edits suggested.

For Future State Scenarios, set up the basic structure on a WorkWall with two elements per scenario: 1-2 introductory paragraphs locating the scenario and 5-8 specific questions; invite SDT members to write input directly on the WorkWall.

**Timing.** 30 minutes to two hours, depending on complexity and challenge of open issues.

**Keep in mind.** The basic approach for Future State Scenarios works equally well for First Draft of the Future. The best time to bring out the surprise Adult Beverages is as the SDT begins the somewhat tedious task of putting team lists together - but not until you can see the finish line.

### Speed Dating

**What it is.** An energizing module that gives the group an opportunity to meet many other individuals - half the group - in a short period via two concentric circles where the inner ring rotates between brief introductions. Often used as an opening exercise for groups whose members do not know each other.

**Use it for.** Introducing group members to each other rapidly and with fun; starting an event with energy.

**How it works.**
1. Assemble in an empty plenary space with chairs stacked on the periphery and a line of masking tape down the center.
2. "We are going to start with a group challenge. Notice the line down the center. We are going to form two circles..." Inside the line is the inner circle, outside is the outer.
3. Push for "really good circles" (not ovals); move people so the numbers are roughly equal.
4. Inner circle members are the movers. On GO, they have 30 seconds to introduce themselves to the person they are facing. STOP (or bell), and the outer circle person has 30 seconds. Then the inner circle moves one person to the right.
5. 30 seconds is enough for name, role, and something memorable - and you don't have to give the same fun fact each time. Run for 20 minutes.
6. "OK, you are probably ready to sit for a while. Your chairs are waiting on the perimeter. Pick up a chair you really like and organize yourselves into four rows, theater style with an even number of chairs in each row." And the room is set up.

**Timing.** 5-10 min set-up, 20-25 min for the exercise.

**Keep in mind.** Play energizing music that rises and falls during the moving part. The KreW may help with row spacing. Pull up the masking tape to indicate the end of the exercise as you turn the room over to the sponsor.

### Speed Thinking Session

**What it is.** A rapid brainstorming exercise based on the principle of coming up with SEVEN ideas in two minutes.

**Use it for.** Generating a large number of potentially useful ideas in a limited amount of time; breaking habits of self-censorship, over-analyzing, and procrastination.

**How it works.**
1. Form pairs and provide each with a clipboard, hypertile, or Work Wall.
2. Give them a question (or distribute different questions to different pairs randomly).
3. Tell them they have two minutes to come up with seven good answers.
4. Post their output for the group to review.
5. Distribute ten small Post-Its per person; have them review all answers and vote on the most interesting and worthy of pursuing.
6. Capture all the output and work with the writing team to incorporate the items with the most votes into subsequent Design Challenges.

**Timing.** Set-up 5 min; Exercise 5 min; Voting 5-10 min.

**Keep in mind.** If possible, precede with an exercise that involves both mental and physical activity - physical activity enables mental speed and agility.

### Spoze

**What it is.** A module - short for "suppose" in colloquial American English - that asks groups to set aside their assumptions for an hour and play with a far-out idea as if it were the most interesting and challenging idea they had ever encountered. Related to the MG Taylor Spoze model.

**Use it for.** Helping groups see their situation differently and get outside the limits of their normal frames of reference; introducing new information to creatively destabilize the system. Generally used in Scan just before moving on to Focus, especially if the group has not engaged fully with their Scan.

**How it works.**
1. Identify one or more stretch scenarios relating to the wavespace topic but pushing the envelope.
2. Write assignments as though the outrageous thing has occurred (e.g., a global pandemic forces all-virtual operations; describe how we are able to stay in business and where we are playing biggest).
3. In introducing the assignment, stress the importance of "suspending disbelief" and accepting the challenge of acting AS IF this were true. "'Spoze it really WERE true!"
4. The debrief should concentrate on the good ideas generated; take time to validate and record them.

**Timing.** 60-75 min team work; 7-12 min per team for Reports and Dialogue.

**Keep in mind.** A Spoze Scenario often involves a "take-away" - the removal of an essential capability or change in the picture. It works because of the team's willingness to GO WITH the scenario and turn it into a success. The debrief is critical for drawing out ideas that actually could be made to work. One variation is "Everything You Know Is Wrong" - current assumptions are turned on their head and the team must make it work.

### Story Circles

**What it is.** A module (developed by Jonah Evans) that introduces personal storytelling, authenticity, and careful listening in small groups. The facilitator models a 2-minute story, then in small groups of 5 each participant has 2 minutes to share something honest and real about themselves while teammates simply listen.

**Use it for.** Challenging colleagues to be more authentic, honest, and connected; demonstrating the power of listening without responding or interjecting as a way of helping others go deeper.

**How it works.**
1. Introduce the purpose - stories let us express values as lived experience, and you'll go first because you wouldn't ask others to do something you wouldn't do.
2. Form groups of 5 with people they don't know well; chairs facing each other, knees almost touching. Avoid teams of 4 or 6.
3. Set the rules: each person gets 2 minutes; the only rule is that what you share is honest and real ("That's NOT what we're asking" - it doesn't have to be the worst thing that ever happened). Listeners just hold space and listen - no need to relate, fix, or fill silence.
4. Share your own story while someone times you; be authentic.
5. Give 2 minutes for individuals to think of their story; play soft music. Pick a random first speaker ("darkest shirt goes first," "person with the coolest shoes"). Time each round and announce when time is up; listeners can thank with nods, high-fives, or hugs.
6. Conduct the remaining four 2-minute rounds.
7. Debrief while in small groups - start with "What surprised you about the experience?" and surface four typical themes: "Amazing how much can be shared in two minutes," "I feel closer to my group," "I shared something I haven't shared with my own friends/family," "It was great to speak and know you wouldn't be interrupted."

**Timing.** ~45 min total - 10 min intro and your story, 2.5 min individual prep, 2.5 min per round, ~15 min debrief.

**Keep in mind.** Consider a physical icebreaker first (Rock Paper Scissors Championship) to wake people up. Alternatives for similar territory: Who Am I Really? Introduction (less time) or Deepening Conversation (more time, deeper).

### Strategic Modeling

**What it is.** A tool that gives a simple framework for representing complex ideas in a single graphical image - introducing the skill of visual modeling so groups can later create models of business, market, or organizational elements. Related to the Single Frame deliverable.

**Use it for.** Unpacking the idea of a visual model; "priming the pump" before asking participants to create models, especially before a Take-A-Panel module.

**How it works.**
1. Distribute hypertiles or clipboards and paper, plus several colored Sharpies per person.
2. Explain the purpose: a graphic representation that helps one understand and make decisions; can show structure and process over time.
3. Describe the four elements of a strategic model: Frame (the area in question and the context), Actors (entities within the frame), Relationships (how actors are connected or interact), Annotations (labels, words, symbols that bring further clarity).
4. Have participants pair up and test-drive: draw blobs for Actor A and Actor B; draw a Frame for A and a different Frame for B; draw a partnership between them; a frame with two inside and one outside; a commercial relationship; "A is part of B." Then add structure and process: A acquires B; A inside B with B growing four times its size over time; B growing outside the original relationship. Whenever you draw a boundary, ask what's inside, outside, and what the relationship to the frame is. Add a random doodle and incorporate it into your model.
5. Teach scribing tricks - Bean People, Peg People, Star People, simple geometric shapes, shading and shadowing, images common to the client's world; invite the group to "stump the stars."
6. Suggest resources - Brandy Agerback's The Graphic Facilitator's Guide, Mark Kistler's Draw Squad, Dan Roam's Back of the Napkin, Mike Rohde's The Sketchnote Handbook.

**Timing.** Minimum 30 minutes; can expand to 60 or 75 with additional scribe training.

**Keep in mind.** It is important to not just talk about Strategic Modeling but to give people a chance to actually practice it.

### Success Matrix

**What it is.** A two-by-two matrix tool (developed by Carolyn Buck Luce at EY's Center for Strategic Transactions) that helps individuals clarify what winning looks like for their organization and for themselves in a given deal - across Organizational/Personal and Financial/Non-Financial dimensions.

**Use it for.** Understanding what constitutes success for different parties in a transaction; providing a way for individuals to discuss what matters to them most.

**How it works.**
1. Prepare two copies of the matrix - one filled out (with examples like Revenue/Profitability/Cost Reduction in Org Financial; Market Share/Brand/New Channels in Org Non-Financial; Bonus/Compensation Trajectory in Personal Financial; Personal Brand/Visibility/Legacy in Personal Non-Financial) and one with blank quadrants.
2. Introduce the Success Matrix as a comprehensive way of discussing what matters in a deal.
3. Walk through the four categories in order: Organizational Financial; Organizational Non-Financial; Personal Financial; Personal Non-Financial.
4. Lead a dialogue on Organizational Financial and Non-Financial elements; record on the blank matrix.
5. Give participants a few moments to consider and write down what each personally has to gain.
6. Have each individual share what they each stand to gain from Personal Financial and Non-Financial perspectives.

**Timing.** 30-45 minutes, depending on group size.

**Keep in mind.** Best with small groups where participants have developed enough trust to share honestly. The aim is information sharing, not to challenge anyone's answer - per the wavespace Axiom: "Whatever someone tells you is true; they are reporting their experience of reality. To argue with someone else's experience is a waste of time. To add their experience to your experience, to create a new experience, is possibly valuable."

### Syntopical Reading (Directed Reading)

**What it is.** A Scan module in which participants are given an eclectic mix of essays, articles, and books on a topic relevant to the wavespace objectives. The team divides material to skim, dialogues about what they read, and applies it to their work - with three parts (Read, Dialogue, Apply) plus a Report Out.

**Use it for.** Bringing insights and trends from other industries or parts of society into the wavespace conversation; introducing participants to ideas and concepts they may need to arrive at effective solutions.

**How it works.**
1. With the SDT, identify the different areas of knowledge (technologies, organizational innovations, leading practices, case studies) the group will need.
2. Well in advance, develop the reading list for each topic.
3. Write the assignment: the Read assignment frames why this topic matters to outcomes and instructs participants on materials and how to distribute them. The Dialogue assignment provides starter questions to generate genuine dialogue beyond description. The Apply assignment connects the readings to the actual work of the wavespace.
4. Carefully coordinate distribution to the correct teams - many moving parts.
5. In conducting, signal time remaining for each part. Monitor time and noise in Read; you can distribute Dialogue early if groups are ready.

**Timing.** Read 45-60 min; Dialogue 30 min; Apply 30-45 min; Reports and dialogue 7-10 min per team.

**Keep in mind.** Strength depends on (1) the quality of articles and books and (2) the connection between readings and objectives. Have 50% more items than there are members of the group so people read different things. Use Post-Its in advance to mark the most relevant parts of books. An alternative is "How to Read a Book in an Hour"; another is to divide chapters of one book among members per "How a Team Can Read a Book in an Hour." If pressed for time, use TED Talks instead.

### Take-A-Panel / Share-A-Panel / Synthesis

**What it is.** A four-module sequence (named after the "panels" - 4'x8' WorkWalls - of a classic MG Taylor environment) in which participants individually depict their responses to a set of questions on their own panel (TAP), report their panels to a small team (SAP), then synthesize the team's responses into a report (SYN) and share out (R/O). A regular element of wavespace because it gets participants physically active and requires them to organize ideas in a reportable form.

**Use it for.** Getting the full range of ideas, concerns, and insights of each individual into play in Scan; ensuring each participant gets a forum and a fair hearing; can also be used as a "pattern interrupt" to start the session or in Focus to lay out ideas after learning.

**How it works.**
1. Begin collecting topics from your first SDT meeting; record in the Workshop Design Document. Ask: "What is the real work that you are bringing people here to do? What is hard or tricky? Where do people have very different views?"
2. Build the TAP assignment to fit on one printed page: framing instructions, a 3-5 sentence context-setting scenario set in the future (often a "best case"), and a series of 7-10 specific questions.
3. Review with sponsors to confirm the most important questions and adjust terminology. Print one per participant; set the environment with enough writable surfaces and colored pens.
4. Assign Share-A-Panel teams that mingle the group; prepare team lists or projected images.
5. Write the Synthesis assignment to invite broad reflection beyond the individual output.
6. Conducting: brief overview, then quickly get them up and to their WorkWalls. Encourage silence and individual work during TAP ("There will be time to share later"). Allow ~30 minutes for individual TAP, or move on earlier when energy dissipates and noise rises. The facilitation team takes digital photos of panels after each is reported (not before, since reporters often make changes during reporting). Mark each panel after capture. Move teams to a group area for Synthesis.

**Timing.** Read 45-60 min; Dialogue 30-45 min; Apply 30-45 min. Significant time commitment - avoid trying to "just do everything faster" if pressed.

**Keep in mind.** Remind participants this is part of Scan - it is more important to understand all points of view than to defend your own or resolve differences. Plan logistics carefully - enough supplies and space. Variations: instead of reporting to the large group, "ship" output as standalone flipcharts or hypertiles for a gallery; or use "Take-A-Box" with 1-foot white cardboard cubes (Dan Newman) for environment-constrained situations.

### TED Talks

**What it is.** A module that uses TED (Technology Entertainment and Design) talks - 18-minute presentations from world-class speakers - as a Scan input. Different TED Talks are loaded onto iPads (one per device) and a roomful of participants can watch different talks via a Belkin 5-way splitter and earphones without disturbing each other. Often used as an alternative to Syntopical Readings or Metaphors.

**Use it for.** Igniting creativity in the search for new solutions; introducing new, promising avenues of thought or discovery.

**How it works.** Four parts: Experience, Dialogue, Prepare, Report.
1. Follow the same thought process as Syntopical Readings to identify useful TED Talks. Load iPads early on Prep Day - allow time for inevitable technical glitches.
2. Experience: small teams (~5) connect their personal headphones to the iPad and watch the Talk (~20 min).
3. Dialogue: discuss the Talk - main points, what they learned, what stood out personally, the "news we can use" (~20 min).
4. Prepare: agree on one really good idea and design a stand-alone flipchart poster - images and words so others can understand without explanation - identifying the topic and presenter (~20 min).
5. Report: meet with two other teams; each takes ~5 minutes to present and discuss; leave posters on the WorkWalls.

**Timing.** Set-Up 5-10 min; Talk 20 min; Dialogue 20 min; Prep 20 min; Reports ~5 min per team.

**Keep in mind.** Bring ear buds even if you ask participants to use their own. Keep your list of useful Talks current - new Talks released weekly. TED Talks can also serve as participant pre-work before a session.

### Terms of Art

**What it is.** A module that invites groups to explore, clarify, and even invent Terms of Art - pieces of technical jargon with field-specific meanings - that will help them make useful distinctions in their work together.

**Use it for.** Helping groups clarify new distinctions relating to their work; introducing new bodies of knowledge (e.g., Complexity Theory, an ERP system); helping groups from different technical disciplines clarify terms for joint activities.

**How it works.**
1. Collect a large selection of important Terms of Art in the area to explore - more than the number of individuals or pairs involved.
2. Collect books, articles, and useful websites for defining the terms in context.
3. Double-check the list with the SDT or other SMEs for completeness.
4. Write each term on its own index card or hypertile and place on the Knowledge Wall just before the module.
5. Introduce in the spirit of discovery and exploration; identify where participants can access the Body of Knowledge to define their terms.
6. Each individual selects one term, returns to their team, develops an understanding using the resources in the breakout area, throughout the environment, on the Knowledge Wall, via the Internet, and from each other - searching for connections and creating examples that evolve the term into something useful for the organization.
7. Once team members have described their individual terms, share them as a Body of Knowledge: discuss relationships between terms, play with combinations, explore implications.
8. Report out via plenary report with discussion, posting Terms with definitions on the Knowledge Wall, or putting the Term on the badge of the person who defined it (they become the "expert").

**Timing.** ~60 min for the exercise; 45-60 min for Report Out.

**Keep in mind.** A standard Report Out can take a fair amount of time but is often worth it for the clarity. Alternatives: share Terms in small teams or use a PatchWorks system of interlacing team members; simply post defined terms back on the Knowledge Wall; create a glossary. Whichever format, create opportunities (or even requirements) to use these terms in subsequent work as soon as practical.

### Walk in the Park

**What it is.** An explicit time for personal reflection - sometimes called Walk on the Beach or Walk Around the Block - that can be used at any time during a multi-day session.

**Use it for.** Giving people a daytime break during an extensive multi-day process; giving the group (especially introverts) time for inner reflection; getting people outside for exercise, sunshine, and fresh air.

**How it works.**
1. Give clear instructions: this is not a break and definitely not a time to check voicemails, emails, or make calls - it is for inner reflection on what is happening in the process.
2. Suggest a question on which to reflect or meditate, picking up on the themes emerging at that moment.
3. Give some ideas about where to walk locally and still get back in time.
4. Ask the group not to talk with each other during the time set aside.
5. Tell the group when to return.
6. Play quiet meditative music during the reflection period.
7. Set up a public timer (iPad, etc.) so people can track when to return.

**Timing.** Approximately 20-45 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** It is important for the entire KreW to maintain a respectful silence during this period - and to get outside if possible.

### Who Am I Really? Introduction

**What it is.** A four-quadrant introduction (developed by Carolyn Buck Luce) that lets a person introduce themselves from the perspective of their purpose rather than current work or demographics - covering Personal/Who, Thought Leadership/What, Professional/Where, and Legacy/Why - timed at one or two minutes.

**Use it for.** A novel way for people to introduce themselves to a partner, small Kotter Group, or the whole group; changing one's personal narrative to "predict" who one is from the perspective of future intentions and purpose.

**How it works.**
1. In advance, prepare a graphic of the 4 quadrants on a flip chart or WorkWall and time your own introduction to use as an example.
2. Form pairs.
3. Frame the exercise: people usually introduce themselves "name, rank, and serial number" - this tells nothing about who they are, what they care about, or how to connect. This tool is a 4-quadrant way to share complex information about yourself in 60 seconds. A brand is "how people feel when they engage with you."
4. Use the 4-quadrant graphic to explain: Quadrant 1 (Personal/Who) - who are you as a human being? Quadrant 2 (Thought Leadership/What) - what are you passionate or curious about? Quadrant 3 (Professional/Where) - where does your passion show up in your work, company, industry? Quadrant 4 (Legacy/Why) - why are you doing what you're doing? Why does it matter to you, your team, your company, the world?
5. Tell them they have two minutes to jot notes and prepare, then each will have one minute strictly timed with their partner.
6. Demonstrate with your own introduction, timed by someone with a smartphone.
7. Conduct the exercise: 2-3 min individual prep, 1 min per partner.
8. Debrief by asking the large group general questions about their experience.

**Timing.** ~5-8 min set up, 5-6 min exercise, 10 min debrief.

**Keep in mind.** Drawing on prospection (Dan Gilbert's "The psychology of your future self," Ben Hardy's Personality Isn't Permanent), the model invites you to speak from your purpose and heart rather than the usual title-and-location greeting. Keep to the timing - one of the points is how much information can be transmitted and taken in when communication is intentionally designed.

### Win as Much as You Can

**What it is.** A competitive simulation based on the classic Prisoner's Dilemma in which clusters of four partnerships play X or Y over ten rounds with bonus rounds at 5 (x3), 8 (x5), and 10 (x10). The exercise demonstrates the impact of win-lose situations and the conditions under which cooperation becomes difficult by intentionally creating a competitive environment that mirrors many organizational settings.

**Use it for.** Giving a powerful and memorable demonstration of the conditions that lead to competitive, win/lose behaviors; gaining agreement to a different set of environmental conditions that drive cooperation.

**How it works.**
1. Prep: hide a Work Wall titled "Win as Much as You Can"; plan to form clusters of 4-8 people each containing exactly four partnerships; queue loud, aggressive music ("Rocky Theme," "Eye of the Tiger").
2. Introduction: get three ground rules agreed - no telling others if they have done this before; replace blame/justify with "be responsible"; agree to silence when it is not OK to talk.
3. Reveal "Win as Much as You Can!" loudly and ask the group to repeat it enthusiastically. Form clusters; in silence, each person decides whether to be solo or in a 2-person partnership (each cluster must have exactly four partnerships).
4. Hand out the Score Sheet. Walk through scoring with the group: payoffs depend on the pattern of all four partnerships' choices in your cluster (4Xs lose $1 each, 4Ys win $1 each, 3X/1Y splits +$1 to Xs and -$3 to Y, etc.). Cumulative scoring; bonus rounds at 5 (x3), 8 (x5), 10 (x10).
5. When asked "are we maximizing partnership or cluster scores?" answer only with "What's the name of the game?" Cue loud music and rush them into the game.
6. Conducting: each round, give 1 minute (or 30 sec from Round 2). After Round 3, allow 2 minutes of cluster talk; after Round 6, 90 seconds; after Round 8, 60 seconds; otherwise break up cross-talk. Make a big deal of bonus rounds. Before Round 10 emphasize "this is your last chance."
7. Debriefing (the actual payoff): write scores in a grid; have fun recounting fortunes; introduce "Add Value" - the only Win/Win cell is 4Ys. Compute the actual cluster score vs. the maximum possible (cluster of 4 scoring 25 each = 100); the ratio is your "collaboration score." Discuss the environment: noisy, competitive metaphors, no communication - "not so far from the normal metaphors we use to describe our business." Surface "dead righteous" players ("Here lies the body of Justin Gray... He was right and his case was strong / But he's just as dead as if he were wrong"). Discuss why people throw X (scarcity, fairness, drama hooks). Connect to Round Ten dramatics (people behave differently when they think they won't be in relationship anymore - business closures, divorces). Share Robert Axelrod's research from The Evolution of Cooperation: best strategy is start nice, recognize and respond when others stop cooperating, forgive and return to cooperation; only 5% of a system cooperating can shift the dynamics of the whole; retaliate fast and at ~90% force. Take 10 min for clusters to review what happened in light of what they now know.

**Timing.** Set-Up 10 min; Playing 35-45 min; Debrief 30-60 min.

**Keep in mind.** Without careful execution, the module can backfire and create distrust - including of you - rather than understanding. The debrief is the payoff; rushing it can leave people feeling tricked. Be self-deprecating and humble - any hint of "caught them being bad" can be disastrous. Read Axelrod's The Evolution of Cooperation to internalize the messages. Check with the SDT whether many in the group are likely to have encountered the exercise before.

### Yellow Pages / Landing Page

**What it is.** A warm-up module in which teams are given advertisements (or landing pages) of two wildly different businesses and asked to imagine their two founders are sisters who have decided to combine ventures - design the new combined business.

**Use it for.** Exercising creativity by imaginatively combining opposites; warming up a group with a useful metaphor for Combination of Opposites - especially useful when two traditionally separate or opposing groups are coming together (merger, reorganization).

**How it works.**
1. Either select half-page or full-page ads from local Yellow Pages, download landing pages of two businesses, or have the KnowledgeWorker team create such ads.
2. Conduct the exercise: teams design an ad for the new combined company - give it a name, describe products and services, make the ad come alive. ~30 minutes.
3. In the debrief after report-outs, emphasize the value of going beyond initial barriers to make a seemingly odd combination of opposites actually work.

**Timing.** Exercise 30 min; Report Out ~5 min per team.

**Keep in mind.** This is a light-hearted exercise - maintain that spirit while groups work. The serious points come later, in making real-world combinations actually happen despite obstacles. Fewer groups today have personal experience with actual Yellow Pages phone books, but Landing Pages work just as well for the digerati.

### Zwicky Box

**What it is.** A systematic way of exploring all the options and possibilities of a project by laying out Categories/Attributes across columns, brainstorming alternative sub-solutions under each, then methodically (or randomly) combining choices across categories. Developed by WWII scientist Fritz Zwicky to expand the design space beyond what experts were "starting in the middle" by considering. Also known as Morphological Analysis or Morphological Forced Connections.

**Use it for.** Expanding the range of design assumptions at the beginning of a project; avoiding hidden assumptions; breaking limiting mindsets; systematically considering a full range of possible solutions; identifying ideas that are "just crazy enough to work." Per Matt Taylor: useful to expand SDT challenge definitions, design Simulations and Future State Design Challenges, frame the problem in a Creating the Problem module, or help a team frame their Take-A-Panel Synthesis.

**How it works.**
1. Establish Categories/Attributes for the situation and list them horizontally across a WorkWall. Redundancy or overlap is OK.
2. Under each Category, Brainstorm all the alternative sub-solutions. It is OK to jump around and add Categories as you go.
3. Decide whether you will run systematically (every combination) or take a "random walk" (draw lots: Category A 4th option, Category B 7th, etc.). For each combination, write the full sentence so the possible solution is clearly stated.
4. Continue until you have an order of magnitude more choices than you think you need - if looking for 5 good options, run 50+ combinations. Even wacky ideas may contain an element worth saving.
5. Model out the most promising alternatives - play 'Spoze and ask what this combination would really be like (opportunities, implications, costs).
6. Make combinations of combinations and be willing to repeat the Zwicky Box.
7. Move the most promising options into Engineering: plan, test, prototype, confirm, implement, document.

Classic example from Koberg and Bangle's The Universal Traveler: improving the ball-point pen with attributes (Form, Material, Cap Detail, Ink Cartridge) and options under each yields a "square pen made of plastic with no cap and a plastic ink cartridge" - a Cube Pen, where one corner writes and the six faces hold ads, calendars, photos.

**Timing.** Highly variable - depends on the number of categories and options modeled.

**Keep in mind.** Extremely versatile - apply to a whole system or its parts; iterate by taking the best options into a new Zwicky Box; reduce a big idea or scale a small one up. Related to the Solution Box model (which combines Creative Process, Vantage Points, and Design Formation into a 3D matrix - see Models). Read pre-1977 copies of The Universal Traveler. Robert Heinlein's Time Enough for Love (1973) references the Zwicky as a "morphological box" - in its day the term was familiar enough to use in casual fiction.

## Focus Modules and Tools

### Business Model Canvas

**What it is.** A comprehensive, visual depiction of the nine building blocks of any business model — Customer Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams, Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partnerships, and Cost Structure — developed by Osterwalder and Pigneur in *Business Model Generation* (2010).

**Use it for.** Analyzing the dynamics of an existing business; developing new business models for new or existing enterprises.

**How it works.**
1. Prepare a large Canvas template for each break-out group with Post-Its and Sharpies; with the SDT, identify possible areas for business model development.
2. Introduce and unpack the Business Model concept and the Canvas.
3. Often pair with a Syntopical Reading sequence using the *Business Model Generation* book or summaries.
4. Send teams to apply the Canvas to assigned scenarios (e.g., disruption in another industry, or a Future State Scenario for their own business).
5. Have each team build a Canvas describing how they go to market in the future state.
6. Coach teams as they work; have a scribe capture each team's Strategic Model.
7. Report out, roughly 8 minutes per team.

**Timing.** Variable — example: 30-minute presentation, 30-minute Read, 45-minute Dialogue, 75-minute Apply, ~8-minute reports.

**Keep in mind.** Assign a scribe to each team to render the essence of each business model as a Strategic Model. The Canvas is a standard template in most electronic boards (Miro, Mural).

### Contingency Diagram

**What it is.** A reverse-logic exercise that generates promising solutions by brainstorming all the ways you could make a problem worse. Doing so often surfaces things you are already doing today that drive the unwanted outcome.

**Use it for.** Developing actions to improve a situation; energizing a group.

**How it works.**
1. Identify the outcome you want or the problem you want to solve, then identify its opposite.
2. Write the problem on the right of a flipchart, circle it, and draw arrows pointing at the circle.
3. Brainstorm the things you could do to achieve that negative outcome or make the problem worse.
4. Look at the list and identify the things someone is already doing today; discuss ways to stop doing them.
5. Develop an Action Plan with specific, actionable ways to prevent the listed items, which will support achievement of the original goal.

**Timing.** 20–30 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Have fun with it — the reverse framing helps groups accept that what they are doing today may actually be part of the problem they seek to solve.

### Create the Game of (X)

**What it is.** A module that uses the metaphor of a game (Monopoly for real estate, Chess/Go for war, Sim City for urban planning) to invite teams to design and play games that surface the dynamics of their current state, future state, or both.

**Use it for.** Laying out and experiencing the dynamics of a situation; exposing hidden assumptions; having fun with even the most difficult problems.

**How it works.**
1. Identify the situation you want to explore.
2. Assemble gaming pieces (dice, spinners, Monopoly money, pieces) plus arts-and-crafts materials and a large foam-core board for each team.
3. Each team creates a game (~60 minutes), brainstorming favorite games as a model and defining objective, players, rules, field of play, and how unique current dynamics show up.
4. Pair teams up; each plays the other team's game (~30 minutes).
5. Teams debrief each other in original teams (~30 minutes).
6. Conduct a full-group debrief: who were the players, how did you win, what rules were most useful, what parallels real life, what to keep in mind.

**Timing.** Create 60 min; play 20–60 min; debrief 30 min; report-out ~8 min/team.

**Keep in mind.** Production values matter — quality boards and pieces drive serious engagement. Consider having one team build a Current-State game and another a Future-State game, then run a Gap Analysis. TED Talks on play (Stuart Brown, Jane McGonigal) make a good warm-up.

### Create the Problem (Create the Gap)

**What it is.** Defines "problem" as neutrally as possible — a set of conditions that differs from the way one would like things to be. The module unpacks gaps between current conditions and the ideal state.

**Use it for.** Identifying the characteristics of current conditions and a more favorable future state, and clarifying the detailed work needed to close the gap.

**How it works.**
1. Identify the topics where gaps might exist between current and future state.
2. Assign two groups per topic — one to describe current-state conditions, the other to describe future-state conditions.
3. Groups work independently first. Each follows brainstorming rules: any answer works, no evaluation, use the speaker's words, stay neutral, don't try to solve.
4. Groups present their work to each other (~8 minutes each).
5. As one combined team, "create the problem" by identifying the gaps between Current and Future State.
6. Prioritize the gaps in terms of importance to achieving the Future State.
7. At a high level, develop initial solutions for closing the gaps.

**Timing.** Current/Future State 30 min; Creating the Problem 40 min; report-out 8–10 min/team.

**Keep in mind.** When cut-and-pasting topic-specific assignments, bold the topic everywhere it appears so you can manage variants. Stay neutral so participants define the problems themselves. Review the *Creating the Problem* model in Models.

### First Draft of the Solution

**What it is.** An early, business-like work round on Focus Day inviting participants to begin designing "the answer" — possible solutions — so they can be tested and adjusted with feedback.

**Use it for.** Developing prototypes of possible solutions; eliciting feedback before coming to closure.

**How it works.**
1. With the SDT, "chunk" the total solution into team-sized bites; firm them up at the SDT meeting on the evening of Scan Day.
2. Carefully assign participants to each chunk so each team can develop a plausible solution to its assigned topic.
3. Keep the assignment short and sweet — typically 5–7 bullet points of specific tasks or questions after a brief topic frame.
4. Make any relevant earlier work available (printed copies or hypertiles on walls).
5. Send teams to work, reminding them they have time for deep consideration.
6. Iterate later in the Second Draft round.

**Timing.** 90–120 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** First Draft rounds run longer than earlier modules; remind teams they have enough time. Stage prior work carefully so teams can pull what they need.

### Fishbone Diagram

**What it is.** A cause-and-effect diagram (also called Ishikawa, after Kaoru Ishikawa) that maps a large number of possible causes for a problem off a small number of main causes — visually resembling a fish skeleton. One of the seven basic processes of quality control.

**Use it for.** Tapping team members' personal knowledge to identify possible causes; seeding data collection and solutions; getting the big picture of a problem.

**How it works.**
1. Write the problem on the right of a flipchart (the fish's head); draw a large arrow toward it.
2. Draw arrows for the main types of causes pointing toward the central arrow; modify categories as needed.
3. Brainstorm specific possible causes and attach each to an appropriate main cause.
4. Break causes down further by brainstorming sub-causes.
5. Use dialogue or multi-voting to identify the most likely root causes you'll target.
6. Identify ways to collect data to confirm the true root causes.

**Timing.** ~30–45 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Like any brainstorm, this relies on participant judgment — start with the right people who actually know the conditions. As Deming said, "Judgment IS data if it comes from the right people." Most Fishbones need follow-up data collection to confirm or refute hypotheses.

### Force Field Analysis

**What it is.** A tool for assessing the Driving Forces pushing toward your goal and the Restraining Forces pushing the opposite way. By eliminating or mitigating Restraining Forces, you increase the odds of successful change.

**Use it for.** Developing actions to improve a situation; identifying the forces producing the current state and targeting those most likely to move it; energizing a group.

**How it works.**
1. Draw a large T on a flipchart; identify characteristics of the current state and the future state and record them in their boxes.
2. Brainstorm the most significant Restraining Forces currently pushing in the opposite direction. Focus on what is, not what could be.
3. Brainstorm the most significant Driving Forces currently driving toward the goal.
4. Discuss Restraining Forces first: drop the ones beyond your control, pick 3–5 you can affect, and develop an action plan to eliminate or mitigate each.
5. If time permits, do the same for Driving Forces, looking for ways to augment them.
6. Add additional Driving Forces only after analyzing the forces already at play.

**Timing.** 30–45 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Use a Contingency Diagram's reverse psychology to attack Restraining Forces. Rob Evans calls this one of the most versatile, all-round useful tools in his personal kit.

### Future State Scenarios / Design Challenges

**What it is.** A module that lets teams test and evaluate a range of possible future scenarios simultaneously to ask "What if?" or "Why not?" A Design Challenge adds special constraints (drastically less time/budget, unusual users, prohibited or required partners, daunting adversaries) to stretch thinking beyond the obvious.

**Use it for.** Testing possible solutions; stretching a group's creativity and appreciation of what's possible.

**How it works.**
1. Begin collecting scenario ideas from your earliest SDT conversations and record them in the Design Document.
2. Mine Take-A-Panel/Share-A-Panel output for additional scenarios.
3. With your KnowledgeWorker team, develop a draft set of Future State Scenarios to present to the SDT, with scribe time reserved to convert SDT ideas into actual assignments.
4. Format each assignment in two parts: 1–2 paragraphs locating the scenario in the future and setting context, plus 5–8 specific questions on the details the team will wrestle with.
5. Have the SDT pay special attention to participant assignments to teams.
6. Distribute assignments carefully so each team gets the one they were assigned.

**Timing.** Team working 75–90 minutes; report-out 7–10 min/team.

**Keep in mind.** Don't be timid about the level of "stretch." This module gives the SDT plausible deniability if the group rejects their preferred solution. Consider a Red Team scenario where teams play a challenging competitor — the best reactions range from "Oh crap" to "that's awesome and crazy enough to happen."

### Lay of the Land (Mapping Your Market Space)

**What it is.** A module that uses the metaphor of a map and the process of mapping to describe the market a company serves today and to propose markets it might serve — or create — in the future.

**Use it for.** Capturing ideas about current and future market state; identifying the gap and the "difference that makes a difference" between current and possible futures.

**How it works.**
1. Give teams one of three assignments: map the market today, map the market 5 years out as a projection of today, or map the market the company could create 5 years out.
2. Frame teams as cartographers: surveying the landscape and depicting it to inform and delight the user, with permission to position different market components and include unserved areas.
3. Encourage exploration of different map types — topographic, political, climate, road, resource — as metaphorical options.
4. Allow ~60 minutes for team work.
5. Report out in order (today, projected, could-be); in dialogue, focus on the assumptions teams made about future possibilities and their different viewpoints of what's possible.

**Timing.** Team working 60 min; reports and dialogue ~10 min/team.

**Keep in mind.** Decorate break-out areas with different kinds of maps — including literary or mythical spaces (Middle Earth from *The Hobbit*) — to demonstrate that maps can be drawn of imaginary as well as real spaces.

### Magic Buttons

**What it is.** A creative thinking exercise during the Focus phase in which teams program three buttons on a Magic Remote Control that — individually or together — would solve an identified problem.

**Use it for.** Helping teams open up their options; helping teams get unstuck.

**How it works.**
1. Set up beforehand with exercises that identify specific problems or challenges — the more specific, the better.
2. Introduce with a tone of "serious playfulness," optionally giving teams a remote-control-graphic hypertile.
3. In ~30 minutes, teams identify what actions their three buttons would initiate to solve the problem (only three!).
4. In a follow-on 30-minute round, remove the "magic" and discuss real-world solutions to achieve the same ends, examining: what assumptions you made, how you prioritized the three over others, what the "magic" was overcoming, and how challenging those assumptions could yield real-world solutions.
5. Reports and dialogue 8–10 min per team.

**Timing.** Magic Buttons 30 min; real-world application 30 min; reports 8–10 min/team.

**Keep in mind.** You are inviting participants to "suspend disbelief" — be firm that unexpected solutions truly are possible.

### Model Shop

**What it is.** A Focus exercise that invites participants to use physical modeling materials (foam core, clay, pipe cleaners, Duplos, figurines, craft items) to create a 3-D representation of their end state or Future State Scenario, usually as a surprise add-on after teams have already prepared traditional reports.

**Use it for.** Engaging the full range of hands-on creative faculties; discovering elements not obvious in 2-D; having fun late in the Scan Day to begin shifting energy toward Focus.

**How it works.**
1. Preparation: assemble the kit (glue gun, clay, balloons, Play-Doh, Duplos, foam core 2'x3' per team, figurines, dowels, pipe cleaners, etc.) in a large plastic tub.
2. Arrange materials on a central table from which teams draw ingredients.
3. Give each team their assignment plus a 2'x3' foam-core base.
4. Play bright, upbeat music as teams begin.
5. During report-outs, set the model on a slightly tilted surface and project a real-time video feed to monitors.
6. After the module, collect reusable materials and refresh the kit for next time.

**Timing.** Model-building 30 min; reports and dialogue 8–10 min/team.

**Keep in mind.** Production values matter — quality materials drive serious engagement. Leave the models on team tables overnight. Treat them with great respect during reports — teams have invested real energy in something they may have first viewed as frivolous.

### Prioritization Matrix – Basic

**What it is.** A technique for evaluating options through a systematic comparison of solutions against a small set of criteria, scored High/Medium/Low (3/2/1) and totaled.

**Use it for.** Ranking or prioritizing potential solutions; selecting the best solution.

**How it works.**
1. Identify selection criteria (limit to three or four; too many criteria become tiresome) and agree on a rating scale: High = 3, Medium = 2, Low = 1. Apply weighting if criteria are not equal.
2. Use Multi-Voting if needed to narrow the list to no more than eight solutions.
3. On a flipchart or whiteboard, lay out the Prioritization Matrix.
4. Rate every solution one criterion at a time using the agreed rating system.
5. Sum the rows; the highest total wins.

**Timing.** ~30 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Like all judgmental prioritization, this is a compilation of group opinion — but as Deming said, "From the right people, opinion is data."

### Prioritization Matrix: Head-to-Head Comparisons

**What it is.** A variant of the Prioritization Matrix in which solutions are compared directly head-to-head using a preference scale: 1 = equally preferable, 5 = more preferable, 10 = much more preferable, .2 = less, .1 = much less.

**Use it for.** Ranking or prioritizing solutions; generating a statistically valid comparison when options are compared one to another.

**How it works.**
1. Compare Solution 1 with Solution 2, then 3, etc. — go across each row, then down to the next.
2. For each weight recorded in a row cell (e.g., 5, 10), record its reciprocal (1/5, 1/10) in the corresponding column cell as a check on the result.
3. Add up the row totals; the highest Solution Total is your choice. Confirm it by checking that it has the lowest Reciprocal Total.
4. Optionally compute Relative Value (Solution Total / Grand Total) as a percentage.

**Timing.** ~30–60 minutes depending on number of solutions.

**Keep in mind.** This takes much longer than a basic Prioritization Matrix but gives a much more accurate and valid comparison of the options.

### Problem Statement

**What it is.** A technique for describing a problem, its impact, and the desired state to be gained by solving it.

**Use it for.** Gaining team consensus on a working problem definition; explaining the problem to outsiders; demonstrating the effects of the problem and the benefits of solving it.

**How it works.**
1. Divide a flipchart or whiteboard into three horizontal sections: Current State, Impact, Desired State.
2. In the first, write a concise, objective description of the current state.
3. In the second, describe the impact of the chosen problem.
4. In the third, describe in one or two sentences what it would be like if the problem were solved.
5. Optionally add a short description of the impact of correcting or eliminating the problem.
6. Review the three sections to make sure all team members agree.

**Timing.** ~30–45 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Don't include causes or possible solutions in the Problem Statement — at this point you may not actually know the root causes. Focus impact analysis on the chosen problem only. Express the desired state in realistic, attainable, and where possible measurable terms.

### Process Flowchart

**What it is.** A diagram representing a process or workflow as a series of boxes (ovals for start/stop, rectangles for activities, diamonds for decisions) connected by arrows. A "cross-functional" or swim-lane variant allocates symbols to organizational units.

**Use it for.** Documenting a complex process; understanding systematic information flow; identifying steps to complete a complex task or solve a problem.

**How it works.**
1. Start with the end in mind — identify the final state or output, then identify the start.
2. Decide between standard and swim-lane format (swim lanes typically formatted horizontally).
3. Identify the steps; write each on its own Post-It and organize them on the WorkWall, using arrows to indicate direction.
4. Separate complex processes to avoid having one flowchart become too complex and confusing.

**Timing.** Highly variable depending on process magnitude and team familiarity.

**Keep in mind.** Office tools (Word, Excel macros) can build flowcharts, but for group input use the WorkWalls. If using Post-Its on butcher paper, take photos and tape them down — Rob learned this the hard way when a roll of meticulously designed flowcharts spilled their Post-Its in his hot car trunk.

### Proposal

**What it is.** A module in which teams compete with each other to develop a proposal for the project or new business that is the focus of the wavespace experience, with a smaller team set up as the executives who evaluate the proposals.

**Use it for.** Exploring alternate models for accomplishing a task; injecting the excitement that comes from intramural competition.

**How it works.**
1. With your SDT, write an assignment that captures all the important elements of the task at hand (often framed as a memo from a CEO).
2. Carefully balance teams with a uniform mix of functions or skills.
3. Construct a small Evaluation Team to develop evaluation criteria (metrics, weightings, deal-breakers, plus a separate "style points" scorecard) and rate the proposals.
4. Conduct Report-Outs as a real competition — draw lots in front of everyone to determine presentation order.
5. While the Evaluation Team assesses the winner, reconvene Proposal Teams to identify the good ideas from other teams and rate which team should come second after themselves.
6. Before the Evaluation Team declares the winner, have Proposal Teams report out their second-place picks and good ideas worth incorporating into the final model.

**Timing.** Prep 90 min; presentations 10–15 min/team; debriefs and evaluations 15 min; reports and dialogue 30 min.

**Keep in mind.** Proposal is a simpler form of Simulation — Proposal focuses on content, while Simulation lets teams explore the *process* of collaboratively developing a complex proposal. Consider an award to reinforce bragging rights for the winning team.

### Prototyping

**What it is.** Building an early sample, sketch, or 3-D model to test a concept — "primitive form" or "first impression." Per Matt Taylor, "If you can't build, you can't think." Per Schrage's *Serious Play*, the purpose is "to create or unearth choices" and elicit conversation, not the prototype itself.

**Use it for.** Teaching prototyping in Scan; making different options come alive in Focus (similar to Model Shop but with an explicit assignment); testing ideas and getting feedback during Act.

**How it works.**
1. Identify the role for prototyping (concept-comes-alive, physical product testing, user-experience modeling, or emotional engagement).
2. Choose Low-Resolution materials — Sharpies on paper, iPad stylus sketches, foam core, pipe cleaners — most wavespace prototyping is Low-Res.
3. Frame an assignment, e.g., "Create a prototype of a tool that will help people work in a new way" with ~30 minutes to build.
4. For multi-cycle work, follow Initiative Design Guidance: build first iteration, then a User Feedback round (pair teams, demonstrate prototype with minimal commentary, conduct Empathic Interview), then revise prototype for final report.
5. Plan toward increasingly sophisticated prototypes refined through user feedback and iteration on Act Day.
6. In the field, follow Honda's *sangen shugi*: gen-ba (go to the real location), gen-butsu (observe and formulate), gen-jitsu (support decisions with data and tests at the real location).

**Timing.** Highly variable depending on aim and complexity.

**Keep in mind.** Prototypes should "show, don't tell" and be vehicles for conversation — choice of who you involve (users, suppliers, customers, critics) can matter more than the prototype's details. See Patterns 223 (Iteration and prototyping), 244 (Don't argue. Test), and 245 (Work as play).

### SDT Day Two Evening Circle Up

**What it is.** A very brief Sponsor Design Team circle up — no more than 30 minutes — that prepares the SDT for the Synthesis Conversation at the start of the Act Day.

**Use it for.** Reviewing the Focus Day; identifying preliminary Buckets of Work for the Act Day; beginning to identify the critical few key players who would need to be on each.

**How it works.**
1. Preparation: have a scribe write up the proposed Act Day Strawdog on a WorkWall and identify a scribe to document the SDT Circle Up.
2. Begin by declaring this Circle Up will be no more than 30 minutes so the SDT can take the evening off as promised.
3. Cover this agenda: review the day ("What did you learn? Surprises?"), confirm you doubled Day One's output, review the Day Three Strawdog, describe the Buckets of Work / Vote with Your Feet process, and ask for preliminary Bucket ideas.
4. Close within 30 minutes unless serious unresolved SDT issues require continuing to "good enough for now."
5. Review the SDT outcome in the KreW Circle Up and coordinate chunking of knowledge objects useful to the Buckets teams.
6. Get the KreW out as quickly as possible — they need to be fresh for Act Day.

**Timing.** 30–45 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Keep this Circle Up as short as possible. Get the SDT out so they can relax, help the KreW reset the space and get to sleep — and then it's your turn.

### Second Draft of Our Solution

**What it is.** A module that follows First Draft of the Future, often after a Shift and Share Report. Teams iterate their previous work into a form to be presented to the larger group for feedback. Occasionally new topics not addressed in First Draft are introduced.

**Use it for.** Iterating an important area of the complete solution; refining the model so it can receive feedback from the full group.

**How it works.**
1. Plan the sequence of topics across First and Second Drafts, ensuring full coverage and a build toward a comprehensive solution.
2. Keep the Second Draft assignment short — simply reference the questions and issues from First Round assignments.
3. Allow ample time — usually no less than 90 minutes — and reserve time for detailed report-out and feedback (often a Why It Won't Work exercise).
4. Take a break as a team when necessary.
5. Report-outs 8–10 min/team.

**Timing.** Team working 75–90 min; report-outs 8–10 min/team.

**Keep in mind.** Keep assignments short and focused — by the second half of Focus, groups have exhausted patience for fluffery and lengthy instructions; the locus of control is shifting from facilitator to group. If the final Buckets of Work are becoming clear, mirror them in the Second Draft topic distribution to test the model and get feedback.

### Shift Paper (Paradigm Shift Paper)

**What it is.** A way of illustrating the dimensions of a major transformation by identifying what one is moving *from* and what one is moving *to*, drawn from Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962). Sometimes used in Kuhn's strict sense (the new paradigm is irrevocably better) and sometimes more relativistically (different paradigms suit different contexts).

**Use it for.** Explaining the nature and dynamics of a transformation; identifying what is at stake and what is to be gained; framing a discussion of alternative approaches.

**How it works.**
1. Create a matrix that defines the two states (e.g., Before/After or Current/Future) and the categories that best illustrate the transformation.
2. Fill in the matrix as either an individual or a group activity.
3. Test the outcome with both informed and naïve audiences to be sure the Shift Paper is clear about the nature of the transformation.

**Timing.** Team working 30–45 min; reports 8 min/team.

**Keep in mind.** The most obvious use is as a delivered input, but it is also a great exercise to ask groups to create their own. In practice, it is difficult to avoid a "right column bias" — be explicit about it up front rather than pretending the Shift Paper is neutral.

### Simulation

**What it is.** A direct-experience exercise in which participants live through a new idea, process, way of working, or set of tools — from a software demo to an extended "war game." Simulations make the future "real" in convincing, educational ways.

**Use it for.** Clarifying and learning a new idea, process, or tool through hands-on experience; demonstrating an idea rather than talking about it; comparing the impact of a new process to an existing one.

**How it works.**
1. Identify the learning objectives or elements to test.
2. Design with these critical elements: Make it Realistic; Develop a Surplus of Content; Build It Around a Challenge; Clarify Roles; Write Rules of Interaction and Success; Build in Economic Constraints (e.g., bidding currency); Develop a Time Frame (e.g., 10 minutes = 1 calendar week); Build in Wild Cards.
3. If possible, do a "dry run" of the simulation with the KreW.
4. Build enough time into the schedule — don't rush.
5. Stage all props, handouts, and a behind-the-scenes timeline map for the KreW.
6. Design enough time for participants to debrief their experience.

**Timing.** Highly variable, one to four hours; give it the time it needs.

**Keep in mind.** Production values matter — realistic environment and props help participants immerse themselves. Expect the unexpected and be ready to build on it. See Patterns 245 (Work as play) and 248 (Production Values Matter).

### Sleep on It / Homework for Act Day

**What it is.** A Focus Day closing that sets up Act Day work with a homework assignment inviting the unconscious, dreaming self to engage with the ultimate purpose of the wavespace experience.

**Use it for.** Wrapping up Focus Day; setting psychological conditions for a successful Act Day.

**How it works.**
1. Prepare 3x5 index cards and pens, plus a hypertile with the "sleep on it statement" — usually "What does our organization need from us — from me — on Thursday?"
2. Review the day's flow and outcomes; remind the group you doubled Day One's output and that Act Day typically doubles the first two days combined.
3. Review logistics for tonight and tomorrow.
4. Ask if the group will do absolutely-positively-necessary homework, then play with it until they agree.
5. Give the first assignment: from the moment their feet leave the room until they return, just let it go — no talking about wavespace, no lobbying.
6. Hand out the cards. Have participants write down the statement, take it home, read it before sleep, put it under the pillow, read it again on waking, and write down the answer.
7. Close the day quickly with music as the group exits.
8. In the morning, ask "What did you write down — or what would you have written?" as the kickoff for the Synthesis Conversation.

**Timing.** 5 minutes, delivered as part of Day Two closing.

**Keep in mind.** End-of-Day-Two emotional tone is often fearful ("Holy crap, we have so much to do!"). Combined with the Sleep On It assignment, this trough is often exactly the vehicle for focusing the group. Prepare the SDT in advance for this. See Patterns 229 (Sleep on it twice) and 233 (Allow for Latency).

### Take-a-Stand Matrix

**What it is.** A visual, interactive method for establishing where a group stands — quite literally — on an issue. The 2x2 matrix is laid out in painter's tape on the floor (typically agreement/disagreement on X, misgivings/concerns on Y) so participants can express both support and ambivalence.

**Use it for.** Taking a quick straw poll of participants' positions on an important topic and clarifying why; opening dialogue regarding what would elicit movement.

**How it works.**
1. With the SDT, determine the key question and the axes of the 2x2.
2. Use blue painter's tape on the floor and create a large axis-defining graphic on foam core or flipchart.
3. Conduct: state the question, explain axes and poles, stress this is a tentative straw poll, distribute Post-Its for participants to write their names, invite people to step into their quadrant, open dialogue on reasons for their positions and what would change them, invite movement during dialogue.
4. After 10–15 minutes when people tire of standing, have them transfer their named Post-Its to the graphic and take a seat; continue the dialogue seated.
5. Document the graph as a snapshot.
6. Optionally return to the matrix later to review how positions have shifted.

**Timing.** 30–45 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Stress that positions are not "final and for the record" but a way of deepening dialogue. See Pattern 328 (Take a stand) for the powerful psychological effects of taking a stand rather than a position. Thanks to Stephen Josephs for suggesting this module.

### Why It Won't Work (Benefits and Concerns)

**What it is.** A feedback exercise that typically follows a comprehensive set of solution presentations (e.g., Second Draft of Our Solution). The intentionally provocative phrase encourages candid, honest responses. Each "why it won't work" becomes a specification for success of the final solution.

**Use it for.** Getting the full list of obstacles, objections, and concerns out quickly; identifying details that need to be worked out before solutions are "Ready for Prime Time."

**How it works.**
1. Prepare stacks of green and yellow 2"x2" Post-Its on large index cards — one card per participant.
2. At the start of the Report-Out, explain the Post-It intent: green for what they like, yellow for concerns, placed on the specific part of the report-out (hypertiles, WorkWalls) the note refers to. Encourage signed notes.
3. During Report-Outs, restrict questions to clarifying questions; encourage objections to be written on Post-Its.
4. As each report concludes, label it clearly and move walls/hypertiles to the side; take digital photos of completed work before they get papered with Post-Its.
5. Invite the group to stand, post their Post-Its on the relevant walls, then sit. Teams may read feedback on their area but not remove notes.
6. Close Focus Day by telling the group the KreW will photograph and document the Post-Its since resolving these concerns is an important part of Act Day.

**Timing.** 15–30 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** There's usually a lot of energy followed by an "Oh, Crap!" moment as teams see clouds of yellow Post-Its — this is normal and conducive to a positive outcome. Take photos at high enough resolution that Post-Its are legible. Stage the documented feedback by topic for distribution to Act Day teams (printed hypertiles, retained Post-Its, or typed lists with comments and signatures).

## Act Modules and Tools

### Action Plan

**What it is.** A template describing what must be done to achieve a particular objective and how — at minimum identifying specific actions, owners, and timeframes, and often including resources, barriers, risks, and mitigation. It is a "delicious alphabetical accident" that Action Plan is first in the Act-Phase tools, since aligned action is the ultimate aim of any wavespace experience.

**Use it for.** Helping Act-Day teams identify all the important planning elements for accomplishing their objectives; helping the SDT articulate what is important to them in implementing wavespace outcomes.

**How it works.**
1. Development: start with a draft Action Plan template outlined on a whiteboard.
2. Review with your SDT and iterate to incorporate their edits.
3. With the SDT, create a sample completed Action Plan for use when you introduce the template to Act Day teams.
4. Execution: introduce the template during check-in reports, typically halfway through Act Day after teams have had a chance to work on solution elements.
5. If Action Plans will be rolled up into a Transformation Roadmap or other coordinating device, introduce that connection process at the same time.

**Timing.** Generally 30–45 minutes depending on amount of information.

**Keep in mind.** Always create a completed example to indicate what completeness looks like. Be ready to adjust the template when the SDT discovers a better way to organize the data. Store previous templates so you can revise and reuse them. See Pattern 261 (Make the solution last past Act Day).

### Buckets of Work / Vote with Your Feet

**What it is.** The transition module that chunks the overall Act-Day work into "buckets" of roughly similar size, then has participants Vote with Their Feet by signing up for the bucket that is their first choice — followed by SDT rebalancing.

**Use it for.** Developing the team topics that, worked in parallel, will produce a complete design solution; building ownership at both the collective and individual level.

**How it works.**
1. While the facilitator runs the Synthesis Conversation, the Process Facilitator prepares a hypertile for each Bucket of Work, with overall topic plus a few bullet points of the work to be done.
2. Facilitator unveils the buckets one by one, starting with those with high consensus, and invites questions to surface what is unclear or missing.
3. The PF puts up sign-up hypertiles (7–8 bullets each) and sets out markers; facilitator asks each person to sign up for one and only one first choice, then take a break — music starts.
4. Quickly convene the SDT, review teams one by one, and ask specific sponsors to request individuals to switch teams to balance the load.
5. Bring the group back to the Radiant Room and give them final instructions for getting to work (see Launching the Act Day Teams).

**Timing.** Typically 20–30 minutes unless significant discussion of the buckets themselves.

**Keep in mind.** Be prepared for serious pushback when revealing the model — resolve confusion before sign-up. The common "we can't do X until we agree on A" objection (where A is a vision/mission statement) can usually be resolved by delegating wordsmithing to one team. If not — extremely rare — you moved too quickly and may need to return to Synthesis. See Pattern 262 (Responsibility is contagious).

### Buckets of Work – Open Ended / Vote with Your Feet

**What it is.** A variant of Buckets of Work that relies entirely on group suggestions for what teams will work on, more common where a community has been brought together for a common purpose without a central coordinating sponsor group.

**Use it for.** Developing team topics for parallel work that together address the wavespace objectives; building ownership for the work at collective and individual levels.

**How it works.**
1. At the end of Synthesis, describe the process: any individual with an idea writes a headline plus ≤20 words on a hypertile, one idea per person, with 30 seconds of pitch air time.
2. Give individuals five minutes to write up ideas; allow collaboration on jointly supported ideas.
3. Go around the group following the pitch process.
4. Combine similar ideas, but only if both authors agree.
5. Review all Buckets so everyone understands intent before Vote with Your Feet.
6. Identify "Rules" for go-forward — rule of thumb: if three or fewer sign up, those people join a different group.
7. PF posts sign-up hypertiles; facilitator asks each person to sign up for one first choice, then sit. Music starts. Review which teams go forward, rebalance, and launch.

**Timing.** 30–60 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** It is important that members define the work in their own voice and even their own handwriting. (Rob notes a mistake of trying to write everything himself rather than turning it over to the group.) See Pattern 262 (Responsibility is contagious).

### Collaboration Café / Job Jar

**What it is.** A central area set up in IT-system-oriented or other Act-Day workshops where ad-hoc teams resolve issues that arise between work teams. The Job Jar is a formal process for tracking issues using two templates: a Master List of all open items and an Issue Detail Template completed in two halves — by the issue identifier and by the resolution team.

**Use it for.** Creating a venue and process for teams to resolve open issues identified in the course of doing other work.

**How it works.**
1. Set up the Collaboration Café area with the Master List on a hypertile and copies of the Issue Detail Template.
2. With the SDT or engagement team, design the Issue Detail Template and create a completed example.
3. Explain to the group how the Café and Job Jar process work.
4. As the Integration Team or work teams identify issues, log each onto the Master List with a number, name, and identifier.
5. Integration Team works with the identifier to complete the first boxes of the Issue Detail Template (Questions to be answered, Who We Need).
6. Integration Team organizes a small team to resolve the issue; that team completes the rest of the template (Musts, Wants, Outcome).
7. Integration Team reviews the resolution and checks off the Master List; the completed template is posted on a dedicated Knowledge Wall area with Post-Its for feedback.

**Timing.** Ongoing over the course of the Act Day.

**Keep in mind.** Extremely important during technology-oriented experiences — ensures all issues in a comprehensive solution are addressed. The Job Jar gives early-finishing work streams important additional work to do, even if they're not the "Dream Team" the issue identifier proposed.

### Commitment Statement / Closing Ritual

**What it is.** A ritualized way for participants to publicly sign up for the ongoing work after the wavespace, often paired with a closing ritual (burying a hatchet, burning index cards, throwing a suitcase of old behaviors over the Radiant Wall) that marks the session's successful close.

**Use it for.** Giving participants a public, symbolic way to express support for the outcomes and the work to follow; ritualizing successful closure of the wavespace experience.

**How it works.**
1. Plant the seed at the SDT meetings on the Focus-Day evening and Act-Day morning, while keeping primary focus on Synthesis and Buckets.
2. By early afternoon on Act Day, raise the closing ritual question with the Integration Team and discuss options in light of the organization's culture.
3. If they choose a Commitment Statement, draft it on a Work Wall and bring in a scribe with at least an hour or two to create the final version on a Work Wall or foam core.
4. The usual end-of-Act-Day flow: drafting communications participants want to send; facilitator describes the Executive Summary the KreW will prepare; sponsor closing remarks; sponsor introduces the Commitment Statement (stressing it's strictly voluntary), signs first, invites others to sign and join the celebration.

**Timing.** 10–15 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Possibilities are almost endless — and warn people on the other side of the Radiant Wall before heaving a suitcase (Rob learned this the hard way). See Symbolic Action; Patterns 128 (Symbolic action) and 263 (Rituals of closure).

### Communication Plan – Immediate

**What it is.** A 15-minute end-of-Act-Day exercise to draft talking points for participants to take back to their coworkers — written by the group itself rather than by the Integration Team alone, so the messages are *their* messages and are more likely to be used.

**Use it for.** Summarizing wavespace outcomes; preparing the "official" report from the session; getting feedback on participants' personal takeaways.

**How it works.**
1. Discuss the immediate-communications strategy with the Integration Team — confirm any constraints on messaging.
2. Schedule ~15 minutes between Final Reports and Sponsor Closing Remarks; prep a scribe to capture group input on the Radiant Wall and a documenter to type into a pre-prepared template in real time. Distribute index cards/hypertiles and pens.
3. Put the large group into Kotter Groups; ask them to take a few minutes to craft the message they would like to see go forward to the rest of the organization, sharing any constraints first.
4. After 6–7 minutes, ask each group's spokesperson to share their message; scribe captures verbatim, documenter adds to template, runner takes written copies back.
5. During Sponsor's Closing Remarks, double-check the messages against the Radiant Wall/cards. Print all messages onto one sheet for participants to carry out the door.

**Timing.** 15 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** If the SDT prefers more control, they can solicit input as above and edit it into their own Key Messages. A variation is Let the Tweets Go Forth, framing key messages as 140-character Tweets. See Pattern 262 (Response-ability is contagious).

### Gantt Chart

**What it is.** A type of bar chart developed by Henry Gantt (1910–1915) that illustrates a project schedule by showing tasks against time. Each activity is a bar whose position and length reflect start, duration, and end date; dependencies and overlaps are visible at a glance.

**Use it for.** Tracking project schedules; showing what has to be done and when.

**How it works.**
1. Develop a detailed project plan listing interdependent tasks in their required order.
2. Identify tasks dependent on completion of prior tasks.
3. Identify likely duration of each task.
4. Define the project start date.
5. Create a timeline with reasonable increments (days, weeks, months).
6. List project tasks down the left axis.
7. Draw horizontal bars to indicate start and stop times for each activity.

**Timing.** Usually created over the course of the Act Day.

**Keep in mind.** Many variations exist, but the basic chart suffices for most workshop situations. Many software apps are available, from simple to extremely complex — worth experimenting to find one that works for you.

### Integration Team

**What it is.** A small group of client sponsors and project leaders established as one of the Act-Day teams, working with the facilitator to guide and coordinate the work of all other teams.

**Use it for.** Providing sponsor oversight and coordination across the Act Day; resolving difficult issues or decisions that arise during the work.

**How it works.**
1. Introduce the idea at the SDT Day-Two Evening Circle Up — it needs at least the primary executive sponsor, the project leader, and perhaps one or two others. Stress this is not an honorary position; other SDT members work the other Act Day teams.
2. Finalize composition at the SDT Act-Day Morning Circle Up.
3. When presenting Buckets of Work, identify the Integration Team's role and declare its membership closed.
4. Set up a centrally located team area for the Integration Team.
5. As Act Day teams Scan and Focus their assignments, send one rep (not the whole team) to the Integration Team within ~15 minutes to clarify deliverables.
6. After all teams have reported in, members may walk about and listen to teams — but must not become enmeshed in any one team's work.
7. Discuss Commitment Statement / Closing Ritual / Symbolic Action by mid-afternoon, giving the KreW ample time to prepare artifacts.
8. After the final walk-about review, set the Final Report-Out order so it makes both logical and emotional sense — start strong and end strong.

**Timing.** Ongoing over the course of the Act Day.

**Keep in mind.** Stress to the SDT that this is not honorary — most members need to work the other teams. Let the Act Day teams do their work without interference; they need to undergo whatever "storming" is necessary to take ownership.

### Launching the Act Day Teams

**What it is.** The facilitator's comments after Vote-with-Your-Feet sign-up that set the tone of focused activity for the rest of the day.

**Use it for.** Giving clear direction to teams about what is expected and how to use the wavespace resources; injecting energy and confidence into the group.

**How it works.**
1. After sign-up and SDT rebalancing, reassemble the group in the Radiant Room.
2. Review any team list changes and acknowledge those who agreed to move from their first choice for the good of the group.
3. Drive home four design principles: (1) "Deliver the goods — there are no redundant assignments. Play your position!"; (2) "Communicate with other teams — just walk over and make the connection"; (3) "Use the work we have already done — the KreW have begun staging it"; (4) "No surprises! — if your work will produce heartburn for someone, go let that person know."
4. Identify team areas; ask the first sign-up to take the hypertile and team list to that area.
5. Give the first assignment: within 20 minutes, create a hypertile listing the deliverables your team will absolutely produce, then send one rep to the Integration Team to review.
6. Tell the group their work window (e.g., until 3:30) and that there may be a brief Check-In Report after lunch.
7. Meet your Integration Team and let them know they have 10–15 minutes of downtime — the last they will have today.

**Timing.** 10 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Keep your own energy upbeat and manifest personal confidence that the group is going to achieve everything they came here for — and more.

### Let the Tweets Go Forth

**What it is.** An empowering closing alternative in which Kotter Groups compose 140-character Tweet messages capturing the outcomes they believe most important.

**Use it for.** Bringing closure by having participants sum up outcomes in their own words; creating the "official" communication in a distributed, empowering way.

**How it works.**
1. Print pages with a 140-character grid; put them on clipboards with pens.
2. Invite the group to form Kotter Groups — "three or four to a group. Not two! Not five!"
3. Give the assignment to compose a Tweet (max 140 characters).
4. When complete, have a spokesperson read the group's Tweet.
5. Collect each Tweet as it is read; give to a documenter at the back to type into a single document.
6. Print the compiled Tweets and have them available as participants leave.

**Timing.** 15–20 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Trust the group to capture the essence — and people support what they help create, so buy-in is much better. Keep your energy high during the readings; it's been a long day. See Pattern 262 (Response-ability is contagious).

### PERT Chart

**What it is.** Program Evaluation and Review Technique — a project management tool developed by the US Navy in 1957 for the Polaris submarine project. A PERT Chart focuses on the time required to complete tasks and identifies the minimum time needed for the entire project (the critical path), preferred over a Gantt Chart when time matters more than cost.

**Use it for.** Determining the tasks and sequences needed to complete a complex project; identifying dependencies between tasks.

**How it works.**
1. Identify the different tasks and write each on its own Post-It Note.
2. Estimate time for each task — optionally using PERT's Optimistic (O), Pessimistic (P), Most Likely (M), and Expected Time formula: TE = (O + 4M + P) ÷ 6. Track Float/Slack and the Critical Path.
3. Organize tasks in a network diagram of numbered nodes (rectangles) representing events/milestones, linked by labeled vectors representing tasks.
4. Use arrows to indicate which tasks are sequential (dependent/serial) and which are parallel/concurrent. Use dashed arrows for "dummy activities" — sequential constraints that require no resources or completion time.
5. Show task time on the opposite sides of vectors.

**Timing.** Developed over the course of the Act Day.

**Keep in mind.** The simple form usually needs further refinement post-wavespace. PERT is preferred over Gantt when dependencies matter most. If working at this level of detail, find out what project management software your client uses and adjust to match — but still work BIG on the WorkWalls to keep the team in the process.

### RACI Chart

**What it is.** A chart for clearly identifying roles and responsibilities — Responsible (Doer), Accountable (Buck Stops Here, only one A per activity), Consulted (In the Loop, two-way), Informed (FYI, one-way).

**Use it for.** Clarifying role expectations in a business process; clarifying team roles at project start; troubleshooting confusion and poor performance; formalizing decisions about who is R, A, C, or I.

**How it works.**
1. Determine the major activities or decisions; plot them along the vertical axis.
2. Identify participants by name, role, or organization; plot along the horizontal axis.
3. Use dialogue and consensus to assign R, A, C, and I in each cell.
4. Share the results with everyone on the chart so they understand their roles and responsibilities.

**Timing.** 30–45 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** You can use a RACI Chart in conjunction with a Process Flow Chart to clarify responsibilities at different process steps — locate activities on the horizontal axis beneath the corresponding process step, with individuals/organizations on the vertical axis.

### SDT Act Day Morning Circle Up

**What it is.** A ≤60-minute circle up that prepares the SDT for a successful Act Day. One of the real moments of truth for a facilitator — your trusting relationship with the SDT sets expectations for what will happen.

**Use it for.** Reviewing the flow and internal logic of the Act Day; confirming the Buckets of Work to propose; preparing the SDT for the Synthesis Conversation and the Act Day work rounds.

**How it works.**
1. Preparation: scribe the Act Day Strawdog and proposed Buckets on a Work Wall; prep a breakfast table; identify a KnowledgeWorker as runner to the Process Facilitator.
2. Cover this agenda with the SDT: review the Strawdog; review last night's Buckets and incorporate amendments; confirm the one or two SDT members on the Integration Team and assign others to specific Buckets; review how Vote-with-Your-Feet works; identify the one or two key "anchors" wanted on each Bucket (write names on index cards).
3. Coach SDT behavior in Synthesis: spend the first half hour just listening; wait for the "leadership moment" when the group is ready for the head sponsor to point the way forward; trust the facilitator to call when to bring out the Buckets — looking for three things: (1) all voices heard including introverts and non-native speakers, (2) clear view of remaining work, (3) energy to burst into action.
4. When the Circle Up concludes, share outcomes with the Process Facilitator and agree who creates the Buckets hypertiles.

**Timing.** 30–59 minutes — must start Act Day on time.

**Keep in mind.** Keep an eye on the clock and budget time firmly. See Patterns 249 (Stepping up to the Leadership Moment), 305 (Trust the Process and the KreW), 308 (Tell the truth with courage and compassion), 327 (Give the gift of detachment).

### Symbolic Action

**What it is.** Taking steps — burning index cards, burying a hatchet, signing a declaration, donning a new badge, throwing a brick through a barrier — that represent new cultural commitments, understandings, and behaviors. Cortez burning his boats had practical impact, but the symbolic value was also clear.

**Use it for.** Breaking an old pattern and symbolically declaring a new one; reinforcing a common commitment of intent.

**How it works.**
1. Discuss any possible Symbolic Action thoroughly with your SDT — never assume you understand the organizational culture well enough to predict how an action will land.
2. Production values matter — don't be half-hearted in designing the action or its objects/rituals; the quality of creation gives the group their initial sense of how seriously to take it.
3. Stage the action in service of the moment — at the start (commitment to see the process through), in Focus (confirmation of intent, e.g., throwing a brick through a barrier), or at the end (committing to a new course, e.g., burying a hatchet, burning the rulebook).
4. Example: a paper shredder outfitted with bright orange and red cardboard flames as a "bonfire" of behavior-change cards in a hotel ballroom.

**Timing.** 10–15 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Production values really do matter. Have fun with the problem. Careful preparation with the SDT and thoughtful presentation overcome the inherent strangeness factor. See Patterns 128 (Symbolic action) and 263 (Rituals of closure).

### Synthesis Conversation

**What it is.** The pivotal large-group conversation — usually at the start of Act Day — that marks the shift from generating and assessing options to defining and choosing an overall strategy. Synthesis means "putting together the parts so as to form a whole." This is the "Insight" phase in the Creative Process Model.

**Use it for.** Wrapping up exploratory Focus and preparing the group to Act and build their solution(s); putting the parts together into a common picture of the whole; identifying which solution elements still need work.

**How it works.**
1. Precede with a Sleep on It / Focus Day Homework assignment asking participants to sleep on "What does our organization need from us tomorrow?"
2. Welcome participants back into a reconfigured Radiant Room circle with a serious demeanor; briefly review objectives and assure them all are within reach.
3. Refer to the Sleep On It hypertile and invite people to share what they wrote.
4. Use questions to keep the conversation moving: "What do we know for sure about our solution?" / "What are the most important decisions still to make today?" / "What is on your mind or your heart as you move this forward?" / "What is hard about this?"
5. Well into the conversation, deliberately make space for quieter voices — introverts, non-native speakers — by asking those who have spoken to remain silent for a few minutes.
6. Use the archery metaphor: hit the bullseye by holding both a clear view of the target and enough tension on the bowstring (group energy) — both content and intent matter.
7. Decide when the group is ready to move to Buckets of Work — not too early.

**Timing.** 45 minutes to several hours; this module cannot be rushed.

**Keep in mind.** No one learns this from instructions — observe several and have a coach in the room your first time. The Radiant Room circle setup signals something different — even intimate — is going to happen. Prep the SDT to remain silent at first but be ready for the leadership moment. Multiple scribes capture the conversation; videos and monitors stay off.

### Templates

**What it is.** A large-format, visual guide to a group dialogue. The point is not filling out the template — the real point is the dialogue, which the template then records.

**Use it for.** Focusing group discussion around a complex topic; facilitating workshops repeated across groups, projects, and companies; ensuring all elements of the deliverable are completed; creating group memory of outcomes.

**How it works.**
1. Preparation: clearly identify the outcomes the template should support.
2. Brainstorm the elements that must be completed.
3. On a whiteboard, lay out elements in a logical 2-D format with appropriately sized fields, including a field naming the team that filled in the template.
4. Pre-label templates by team and develop a filing system (mailing tubes, large flat file) to track completed work.
5. Create clear instructions and a model of a completed template at the level of detail you require.
6. Execution: place or create the template in front of the group; work through it as designed; photograph the completed template for the digital Journal.

**Timing.** Templates are completed during the work round in which they occur — add only 10–15 minutes to your normal time.

**Keep in mind.** Always provide a completed example so teams know the level of detail expected. Time distribution carefully — wait until any open dialogue is nearing its end, because once a template appears, filling it out becomes the team's primary focus. Knowledge management ("knowledge wrangling") is critical — plan how you will deliver the right templates to the right teams at the right time, then label and store completed templates.

### Transformation Roadmap

**What it is.** A visual template depicting the major milestones along a major organizational change program — typically one of the outputs of teams working in parallel during the Act Phase, captured on a large wall chart.

**Use it for.** Communicating the overall change journey in one picture; developing implementation plans and milestones; identifying conflicting initiatives and interdependencies across functions and work streams; tracking progress over time.

**How it works.**
1. Lay out the Roadmap on the Radiant Wall or other large format with the Future State Vision at upper left and a timeframe across the top.
2. Develop statements for the Future State Vision and the "As Is" Current State (work of one Act-Day team or a summary of prior work).
3. Identify the short list (no more than 7 or 8) of organizational units, project work streams, or major initiatives radiating from the Future State Vision.
4. Give clear verbal instructions to working groups on how to contribute: detail goes on a separate Action Plan Template; only summary information (major milestones and deliverables) goes on the Roadmap itself.
5. Identify a KnowledgeWorker as the Roadmap "wrangler" to help teams contribute correctly and flag inconsistencies or misaligned interdependencies.
6. Digitize the completed Roadmap in a form the client can amend and work with.

**Timing.** Elements developed across Act Day; final form requires at least 30–45 minutes of dedicated focus by a small group.

**Keep in mind.** "Test drive" the Roadmap format and supporting Action Plans with your SDT for completeness and clarity. Avoid arcane software when digitizing — be sure the version you turn over is one the client can work in. See Patterns 248 (Use the power of compelling visuals) and 261 (Make the solution last past Act Day).

### What Has to Be True?

**What it is.** A tool for testing emergent strategies against each other by articulating which assumptions need to prove true for each strategy to succeed. Based on Discovery-Driven Planning (Ian MacMillan and Rita McGrath, *Harvard Business Review*, 1995): in areas of significant uncertainty, ask "What are the most important assumptions that have to be proven true — and how will we track them?" rather than treating projections as facts.

**Use it for.** Testing new, unconventional options against each other; modeling how to fund pilots based on achieving milestones; avoiding the time-honored methods for arbitrarily "cooking the books" to generate the numbers management expects.

**How it works.**
1. Identify what success looks like for the pilot or initiative — quantifiable measures (revenues, volume, unit selling price, profit margin) that define "goodness."
2. Compile a list of all assumptions made in the initial projections.
3. Ask which assumptions need to prove true; rate each on importance (1–5) and uncertainty (1–5), add the scores, and reorder so the most-important-and-most-uncertain rise to the top.
4. For the top 5–7, identify ways to test each as quickly and cheaply as possible — start with the most unlikely/most important "deal breakers"; if they fail, no need to test further.
5. Map a plan over the next 4–6 months to test those assumptions.
6. Identify milestones or stage gates for reviewing assumption sets, capturing lessons learned, and deciding whether to advance the project.

**Timing.** 75 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** This is a complicated concept — generally don't introduce new complicated concepts on Act Day. Introduce Discovery-Driven Planning during a Syntopical Reading on Scan Day (using Lafley & Martin's *Playing to Win* and the McGrath/MacMillan *HBR* article). Create a Template for teams to record their final list of assumptions with their tests.

## Reporting and Report Formats

### Reports – Basic

**What it is.** A team gets a set period of time at the front of the Radiant Room to present its work — providing context, representing an iteration (not a summary) of the work that preceded it, presenting clear and succinct conclusions, and identifying unfinished business or major disagreements.

**Use it for.** Sharing breakout team output back to the whole group, building Group Memory the room can refer to as the session goes forward, and creating opportunities for feedback or reflection on a group's insights, conclusions, or recommendations.

**How it works.**
1. In the launching assignment, tell groups exactly how much time they have; update if it changes.
2. Prepare a report-out assignment specifying what should be in the report and how long it can run.
3. Distribute the assignment with enough lead time to prepare.
4. Decide whether there's a logical reporting order — otherwise open the floor or draw names.
5. During the report, use an iPad countdown clock or timing hypertiles so reporters know time remaining.
6. Decide whether to allow questions during the report or hold them until the end.

**Timing.** 5–10 minutes per team plus questions and dialogue.

**Keep in mind.** Hold groups to time, especially the first reporters, to set the pattern. Keep your own energy high and don't run more than about eight standard reports — even eight can feel tedious; build in stretch breaks and energizing music between reports 4–5 or 5–6. Run a Plus/Delta after the first round to refine reporting practice.

### Reports – Dominoes

**What it is.** A reporting format used to track and cluster detailed inputs from different groups or individuals — structurally similar to an Affinity Diagram, with participants themselves organizing outputs as they report them.

**Use it for.** Tracking a range of outputs (e.g., Top Five take-aways from Trade Shows) and surfacing patterns and connections across teams who may have developed similar outputs.

**How it works.**
1. Distribute the Dominoes Report Out assignment along with the materials to create individual "Domino" hypertiles.
2. Introduce it with the dominoes-as-a-kid framing: someone comes up, plays a tile, and others with connecting tiles place theirs nearby.
3. When connections in one area are exhausted, someone else starts a new cluster on a different part of the wall.
4. Allow on-deck participants to wait at the side of the Radiant Wall.
5. Once all tiles are up, redo the clusters into more logical groups — either move them yourself based on group feedback, or invite the group to come down front in silence to re-cluster.

**Timing.** About 1 minute per item to report, plus roughly 30 minutes for dialogue and clustering.

**Keep in mind.** It's common for tiles to form one connected "archipelago" rather than distinct "islands" — that may be fine unless you need distinct clusters to drive follow-on small-group work. There is rarely an objectively correct pattern; what matters is the group making its own connections.

### Reports – Gallery Walk

**What it is.** A way to present information by posting it on WorkWalls — often the Knowledge Wall — and giving participants time to walk through and review it. Less structured than a Scouting Report.

**Use it for.** Establishing common context, reporting detailed information quickly and interactively, and getting participants up, on their feet, and engaging with the environment and each other.

**How it works.**
1. Prepare Report Out instructions and distribute with about 15 minutes lead time.
2. Announce the start clearly, stating the time. Use energetic music to get people up, then fade to active-but-contemplative wallpaper music. Set countdown timers in view.
3. Schedule a short break to follow. If energy dissipates early, simply switch to break music and start the break.

**Timing.** Usually 10–20 minutes.

**Keep in mind.** Watch the engagement of participants and bring the Gallery Walk to a close before focused energy dissipates. Distribute Post-It Notes (yellow and green) so participants can leave feedback directly on flipcharts as they walk.

### Reports – Round Robin

**What it is.** A reporting format following a detailed work round in which participants rotate around the room visiting every other team to view their work. Especially useful when work is template-driven (e.g., system design) and best discussed against the templates on the walls.

**Use it for.** Giving participants a chance to conduct a detailed review of every other team's work and to leave detailed feedback for another iteration. Round Robins differ from Scouting Reports in that participants visit every team — so this format only works with three or four concurrent teams.

**How it works.**
1. Hand out Round Robin Reporting instructions at least 15 minutes before reports begin, along with green and yellow Post-Its and pens.
2. Use a countdown timer on an iPad to show time remaining in each round.
3. With a few minutes left, remind participants to fill out their Post-Its (yellow for concerns, green for things especially good) and to sign them.
4. Use loud music to cue movement; participants rotate clockwise.
5. After all rounds, teams return to their original area, review the feedback, and use the Integration Café to resolve significant cross-team issues.

**Timing.** 15–20 minutes for teams to prepare reports, 12–15 minutes per round, and 15–30 minutes for teams to absorb feedback.

**Keep in mind.** Each team needs to leave one or two people behind to host each round. Sign feedback so reporting teams can follow up with questions.

### Reports – Scouting (Walk-About Reports)

**What it is.** A Trade-Show-like reporting format following a detailed work round where participants choose which other team reports to attend — visiting only some, not all. Especially useful for template-driven work where the output is best discussed using the templates still on the walls.

**Use it for.** Detailed review of other teams' work, giving detailed feedback for another iteration, and integrating teams' work on an Act day before final reports. Differs from Round Robin in that participants visit only some other teams; sometimes the original team assigns members to specific teams to ensure coverage.

**How it works.**
1. Hand out Scouting Report instructions at least 15 minutes before reports begin, with yellow and green Post-Its and pens.
2. Use an iPad countdown timer during reporting rounds.
3. With a few minutes remaining, remind participants to leave Post-It feedback.
4. Use loud music to signal time to move on.
5. When teams return, give them a debrief assignment — scouts share "news you can use" from the teams they visited, and the team reviews Post-It feedback and decides how to incorporate it.

**Timing.** 15–20 minutes to prepare, 12–15 minutes per reporting round, 15–30 minutes to absorb and incorporate feedback.

**Keep in mind.** Scouting Reports can break up almost any extended work round, in either Focus or Act phases.

### Reports – Shift and Share

**What it is.** A format that "mixes and matches" participants between groups from one Focus Day work round to the next — typically between First Draft of the Future and Second Draft. Participants carry hypertiles or printouts summarizing first-round work into the new team and build on them.

**Use it for.** Guiding iteration of work teams, creating detailed conversation as ideas combine across rounds, and avoiding too many long large-group Basic Reports on a Focus Day.

**How it works.**
1. At the previous evening's SDT meeting, set up first-round and second-round team topics on a WorkWall and a list of participant name strips.
2. Confirm both topic sets with the SDT, then explain the Shift and Share process — final tonight, no morning changes.
3. Populate first-round teams with name strips; assign each team a color and draw a vertical color line down the strips so you can track movement. Photograph the result.
4. Populate second-round teams using rules: move people into rounds that most need their first-round work; cap each second-round team at representatives from at most 4 first-round teams. Photograph again.
5. Execute: confirm with KreW how reporting documents will be copied (photocopy hypertiles or photograph and print), map out KreW responsibilities, distribute Shift and Share instructions ~15 minutes ahead, photograph all first-round WorkWalls, copy the report documents, and distribute new team lists just before the music cue to move.
6. In new teams, start with the report from the team that worked in that area first round; circle up with the KreW afterward to celebrate that they actually pulled it off.

**Timing.** 15–20 minutes for teams to prepare reports; 45–60 minutes for the new-team reports after the shift.

**Keep in mind.** Lots of moving parts — early KnowledgeWorker teams dubbed it "Sh*t and Scare!" Preparation of both the SDT and the KreW is the key. Hold the line on no team-composition changes during the Focus morning SDT meeting.

### Reports – Video

**What it is.** A reporting format that uses smartphones, iPads, and other devices to let teams record their work — either using their stage time to present an edited video, or creating a detailed video posted to the On-Line Journal alongside a shorter live summary report.

**Use it for.** Documenting detailed team reports, and accelerating large-group reports while making the full story available to anyone especially interested.

**How it works.**
1. Prepare simple instructions on how to shoot a "good-enough" video.
2. Test the technology for projecting the finished video and uploading to Box.com or whatever site hosts the On-Line Journal.
3. Test the technology again, with a range of devices.
4. Review video instructions with the KreW; let people self-identify as coaches.
5. Provide a brief tutorial to the group before giving the video report-out option.
6. Make KreW video coaches available to teams that need them.

**Timing.** 10 minutes for the tutorial; 10–20 minutes at the end of the work round for teams to create their videos.

**Keep in mind.** Seriously, test your technology at least twice — especially if broadcasting to the group. Don't spring this option on teams on Act Day; introduce it earlier so there's some comfort with it before "crunch time."

### Act Day Deliverable Wall

**What it is.** A WorkWall or foam-core wall on the exterior of a team's breakout area where the team posts its deliverables in "final" form as they're completed.

**Use it for.** Maintaining momentum across a 5+ hour Act Day work round, encouraging teams to complete deliverables early enough for feedback and iteration, tracking progress, and injecting fun and celebration into the day.

**How it works.**
1. As teams head into their first work round, the KnowledgeWorker sets up or labels the exterior walls that will serve as Deliverable Walls.
2. The KreW sets up a sound cue — a bell, gong, or fun digital sound (Ah-OOO-gah!) — to broadcast when a deliverable is posted.
3. The Integration Team or facilitator introduces the process to each team's reporter: check it off the list, post the deliverable in final form on the Wall, and ring the bell so everyone can celebrate.
4. When the first few deliverables go up, the Integration Team and KreW lead a loud cheer and rally everyone to join in.
5. Use the Wall to invite other teams to check on those deliverables and map work across teams.
6. The Wall forms the basis for the team's Final Report.

**Timing.** Ongoing over the course of the Act Day.

**Keep in mind.** Avoid Final Report Out surprises with a "sign-off" process in which the Integration Team or Lead Sponsor formally approves deliverables on the Wall — graphics could even create a fun "seal of approval." Have fun with the celebrations; they speed the day and push laggard teams forward.

### Act Day Tracking Wall

**What it is.** A wall built to monitor the progress of teams working independently in technically oriented wavespaces (RSWs / Collaboration Hubs), where dozens of parallel processes may be in play during the Act phase.

**Use it for.** Tracking progress of independent teams working multiple deliverables, redistributing work if a team gets stuck, budgeting remaining time, and injecting fun and celebration as completions are noted.

**How it works.**
1. Construct a Tracking Wall that captures the spirit or theme of the session.
2. Set up a sound cue — bell, gong, or fun digital sound (Ah-OOO-gah!) — to broadcast when a completion is noted on the Wall.
3. When the first few deliverables are noted and the bell rung, the Integration Team and KreW lead a loud cheer and rally everyone in.

**Timing.** Ongoing over the course of the Act Day.

**Keep in mind.** Have fun with the celebrations — they make a long day go faster and push laggard teams to move so they get their own moment.

### Reports – ACT Day Check-In

**What it is.** A mid-day Act Day progress check that can take three forms: a Basic Report from each team, a Scouting Report, or — described here — each team sending one representative to check in with the Integration Team and other team reps. Standard questions: deliverables and what's complete, what's at risk, who else you need to connect with.

**Use it for.** Getting a progress check on all teams while letting most members keep working, facilitating cross-team integration, and assessing problems early enough to mitigate them.

**How it works.**
1. When you launch teams into their work round, let them know there will be a mid-day check-in.
2. With the Integration Team, decide the level of common knowledge needed; usually a representative-only Check-In is adequate.
3. Send Check-In Report instructions to teams 15 minutes ahead.
4. Call representatives to the Radiant Room and strictly time their reports; provide pens and hypertiles for note-taking back to teams.

**Timing.** 30 minutes total. Reports capped at ~3 minutes and 2 hypertiles each.

**Keep in mind.** Have a Scribe track which teams need to connect about what. Keep it brief so reps can rejoin their teams and get back to work.

### Act Day – Final Report Out

**What it is.** The denouement of the entire event — the chance for the whole group to understand the final, complete, integrated solution they and their colleagues created across the entire wavespace, not just the Act Day. Reports are typically much longer than Basic Reports — often 10–15 minutes plus Q&A.

**Use it for.** Giving the entire group a comprehensive view of the outcome of their work.

**How it works.**
1. As the Act Day work round nears completion, confer with the Integration Team to set the report order — both logical (so it tells the integrated story) and emotional (start and end with strong teams; sandwich less strong ones in the middle). Write it on the Radiant Wall.
2. Send out FRO instructions at least 30 minutes before reports begin; consider invoking a "Time Warp."
3. Alert the first two teams to stage their materials.
4. Keep reports strictly on schedule — a tracking matrix helps regulate Q&A time.
5. KreW takes careful annotated photos of report documents, WorkWalls, and the unreported team-area walls.
6. Consider a "Thumb-o-Meter" for fast feedback: clarifying questions only, then thumbs from up (100% approval) to down (complete disagreement); collect what would push <75% supporters higher and what >=75% supporters liked.
7. Take a team photo after each report; close with applause; turn over to the main sponsor for closing rituals.

**Timing.** 10–20 minutes per team including Q&A.

**Keep in mind.** Assigning a KnowledgeWorker to one or two teams for the entire Act Day helps both the work and the Executive Summary process. Ask each team to write a 25-word-or-less summary of their work for the Executive Summary writers. Teams often hit peak productivity in the last hour — use a "Time Warp" by polling teams about whether 30 extra minutes would meaningfully improve their deliverables, then publicly extend the FRO start time.

## Deliverables

### Executive Summary

**What it is.** The high-level summary of the most important work accomplished during the session — designed to memorialize the key decisions, models, and action plans created during the Act phase, presented in an attractive and engaging way that aids post-event implementation and change management.

**Use it for.** A printed, high-production-value artifact distributed to all participants — magazine or spiral-bound PowerPoint — that participants return to both for the information and to relive the spirit of the session.

**How it works.**
1. As early as reasonable, decide with the SDT on format — magazine or PowerPoint.
2. The Graphics Lead creates a template — color palette, landscape vs. portrait, font styles and sizes, bullet format, photo dpi, conventions for quotes and dates.
3. The Graphics Lead creates a starting Storyboard and a mocked-up page to run by the SDT for approval, usually on Act Day.
4. Before the Post-Session SDT meeting, the team leader puts a draft Storyboard on a WorkWall along with key questions for the SDT: audience, print run size (1/participant + how many others?), publishing software compatibility, voice (We vs. They?), role of scribing and participant photos, photos vs. digitized graphics, importance of quotes, text density, delivery address.
5. The PF stages all Act Day output documents on WorkWalls with green and red Post-Its; the team leader walks the SDT through the Storyboard, gets answers, and invites the SDT to identify the most important elements to include.
6. The PF identifies who on the SDT is authorized to approve drafts over the next 12–48 hours and schedules review calls.
7. The Executive Summary team circles up: post current versions on the wall hourly, edit in red pen, keep both old and new rows in play; every third hour the entire team reads the whole document for overlaps, omissions, and consistency.
8. The team leader sends drafts at least an hour before the review call; "Approved" means final approval and release for printing. With sign-off, the team arranges printing and shipping. Celebrate.

**Timing.** Aim to have the printed Executive Summary in participants' hands anywhere in the world no later than one week from the end of the session.

**Keep in mind.** Always create a one-page summary of the Executive Summary, prominently placed, so a reader who only takes in that page still grasps the essential outcomes. If the print copy is delayed, distribute an electronic PDF until the magazine catches up. Schedule reviewer calls together — sequential calls invite rework. Even with a magazine-style deliverable, agree in advance how reusable images or graphics will be made available to the client in PowerPoint.

### On-Line Journal

**What it is.** An online repository (Box.com or DropBox) that contains all inputs and outputs of the clients' work — based on the same On-Line File Structure that served as the cloud server for the KreW during the event, then separated from KreW-specific documents and reorganized as a chronological summary of the activities and outputs from the session.

**Use it for.** The detailed record of all inputs, activities, and outputs — backup and support to the Executive Summary.

**How it works.**
1. Preferably on Act Day, but no later than the Post Day SDT meeting, walk the Integration team or the full SDT through the structure of their section of the On-Line Journal — the logic of its organization, who should have access, and at what level.
2. After the event, the PF or designee cleans up the client deliverable section of the On-Line File Structure and confirms it is complete.
3. When complete and clean, the PF sends invitations to those identified by the SDT, granting the agreed-upon access level.

**Timing.** Built throughout the event; finalized and shared shortly after.

**Keep in mind.** As soon as you've populated client work into the On-Line File Structure (as early as the opening of Focus Day), demonstrate it to participants so they see the documentation work the KreW is doing in the background. Have a detailed conversation with the clients most directly involved with implementation about the structure, contents, and how the material can support post-session work. And yes — capture everything.

### Participant Photo Directory

**What it is.** An alphabetized directory of participants with photos and contact information, usually distributed after a session and often based on the Facebook Pages introductory exercise.

**Use it for.** Giving participants a way to remember and connect with new colleagues, and giving the group a "little something extra."

**How it works.**
1. The simplest form: PDF the Facebook Tiles and organize them into one document with a Table of Contents listing names and page numbers.
2. A more elaborate version digitizes the handwriting on the Facebook Pages.
3. Confirm with the SDT whether they want a separate printable directory or just the digital version included in the On-Line Journal.
4. In its simplest form, it can often be printed and bound in time to send home with participants at the end of the session.

**Keep in mind.** As with all unexpected, value-added deliverables, enjoy giving this gift to the client.

### Single Frame Graphics

**What it is.** A depiction of a complex topic, idea, or outcome on a single page — illustrating elements along with their context and relationships. Usually too detailed to project; intended to be printed in poster-sized or larger format. Builds on the field of information design / infographics.

**Use it for.** Conveying a large, complex idea or concept in an organized visual way; catching the viewer's eye and imagination.

**How it works.**
1. Bring your Graphics team into the process from the very beginning.
2. Gather all data or information you aim to present and display it in one place — Post-Its or directly on a WorkWall.
3. Identify the unique intent of the Single Frame: clarifying complex data, explaining a process, highlighting a trend, supporting an argument, or even lifting morale by placing current challenges in a more heroic or hopeful context.
4. Explore different formats and metaphors. Allow yourselves to think big, bold, and even weird.
5. Sketch out the Single Frame and get feedback on the early version to clarify questions or design problems.
6. Iterate into final form.

**Keep in mind.** As infographics expand, so do digital tools — a simple web search will surface many. But great work can still be done freehand as long as the thinking behind the single frame is clear. Menard's depiction of Napoleon's invasion of Russia, made famous by Edward Tufte, sets the bar.

### SDT Post Day Circle Up

**What it is.** The final Circle Up that marks the formal close of the wavespace experience with the Sponsor Design Team — typically the morning after the Act Day concludes.

**Use it for.** Reviewing outcomes against session objectives, ensuring the SDT has a clear plan to move outputs forward, and getting input into the Executive Summary plus the SDT's role in executing it.

**How it works.**
1. Preparation: arrange Act Day team outputs on WorkWalls around the circle-up area with red and yellow Post-Its; scribe the agenda, an Action Plan, and the proposed Executive Summary Storyboard on a WorkWall; stage a computer or iPad to demonstrate the On-Line Journal on a video monitor; identify a scribe.
2. Cover the agenda with the SDT: review the day's agenda, ask for "morning thoughts" following the session, review Objectives and progress, review next steps and create an Action Plan clarifying who on the SDT is responsible for what, and present the proposed Executive Summary outline.
3. Invite the SDT to join the KreW for the "Let It Go!" closing ritual to formally close the file that has been open since the initial SDT Planning Meeting — "And when our hands clap in unison, this wavespace is …. history!"

**Keep in mind.** The completion of a wavespace experience is an emotional thing. The Symbolic Action of "Let It Go" is a great tension release.

## Additional Reference Material

### Axioms

**What it is.** Principles developed by MG Taylor to guide groups on the mindsets that help them get the most out of a collaborative experience. An axiom can't be proven true — it simply is — and its value is found through applying it to real situations.

**Use it for.** Subtly guiding groups about how to collaborate, and describing what works in this process and environment.

**The axioms (MG Taylor original set, 1986).**
- The future is rational only in hindsight.
- You can't get THERE from HERE, but you can get HERE from THERE.
- Discovering you don't know something is the first step to knowing it.
- Everything that someone tells you is true; they are reporting their experience of reality.
- To argue with someone else's experience is a waste of time.
- To add someone's experience to your experience, to create a new experience, is possibly valuable.
- The only valid test of an idea, concept, or theory is what it enables you to do.
- You understand the instructions only after you have assembled the red wagon.
- If you can't have fun with a problem, you will never solve it.
- Every individual in this room already possesses an answer; the purpose of this intensive interaction is to stimulate one, several, or all of us to remember and extract what we already know.
- In every adverse condition, there are hundreds of good solutions.
- Creativity is the process of eliminating options.
- You fail until you succeed.
- Nothing fails like success.

**Additional axioms proven over time.** No model is perfect, but some models are useful. Never open on Broadway. You can't dig a new well by digging the same hole deeper. Everyone is the star of their own movie. If you want to clean your house, throw a party. The best way to have a good idea is to have lots of ideas (Linus Pauling).

**How to use them.** Deliver via the facilitator's opening remarks, as exhibits on the Knowledge Wall, in prefatory remarks before a module, or in the introduction to a written assignment.

**Keep in mind.** Develop your own deep understanding and bring examples to make them come alive when introducing them to a group. The author often adds: "All of us are smarter than any one of us."

### Guidelines for Trade Show Hosts

**What it is.** A handout sent to Trade Show Hosts that gives them background on the intent and process of the Trade Shows module and prepares them to play their part — leading three consecutive 30-minute chat-room discussions with three different participant groups in their own pre-set breakout space.

**Use it for.** Prepping non-facilitator subject experts so they show up able to lead a "show and ASK" conversation rather than a one-way "show and tell" PowerPoint pitch to inert groups.

**Key guidance for hosts.**
1. **Be very clear about the 3 or 4 major points** you want people to leave with — what do they need to remember, and what's a memorable way to get it across?
2. **Engaging visuals matter, but you cannot project.** The wavespace team will display visuals three ways: have artists draw your content onto the walls; print your PowerPoint slides on 11"x17" paper and post them; or print extra-large slides at FedEx Office (extra time and notice required).
3. **Use no more than 5 PowerPoint slides** if you go that route, and submit them by close of business the day before Prep Day.
4. **Wall-posted slides must be readable from ten feet away** — text no smaller than 24pt. Evocative statements, pictures, and large charts/graphs work much better than dense text.
5. **For dense content** (detailed slides, brochures), give them to the team to print as handouts (or bring two dozen brochures); hand pages out one at a time, give people time to read, then lead dialogue before moving on.
6. **Allot at least 60% of your time for interaction.** Prepare a few questions to start dialogue. Trade Shows are conversations, not presentations — "show-and-ask," not "show-and-tell."

**Keep in mind.** Submit any reproduction-bound materials by the specified close-of-business deadline. Direct host questions about format or role to the wavespace facilitator.

### How to Piss Off Your Client at a wavespace

**What it is.** A memo distributed to consulting engagement teams a day or two before a wavespace experience — discussed first with the engagement team leader. It "inoculates" engagement teams against unconscious or habitual behaviors that subvert the chance to deepen client relationships during the session. Anyone on any KreW (in an increasingly mobile-device-obsessed world) can benefit too.

**Use it for.** Naming the unhelpful behaviors consultants typically display so they can be avoided.

**The seven behaviors that piss off clients.**
1. **The Rules Don't Apply to Me** — We set rules in the first hour about smartphones, distractions, returning from breaks on time, etc. Clients expect you to play by the same rules. When you don't … it pisses them off.
2. **Hanging Out at the Back of the Room** — Refusing to sit with the group during report-outs, prowling the back, hanging with other engagement team members instead of participating fully. When you act like you're too special to sit with the group … it pisses them off.
3. **Driving for MY Answer at the Expense of the Client's** — Engagement teams often arrive with strong views about the outcome. Get those views in play through the SDT — but don't steamroll the group. When you do … it pisses them off.
4. **Let Me Show You How It's Done** — You may be more comfortable at the whiteboard or presenting than client participants are. Initiate, but create space for clients to step up to facilitating and presenting, especially in later stages when the client needs to own the outcome. If the process becomes all about you … it pisses them off.
5. **Let's Project My Favorite Eye Chart** — PowerPoint is for evocative images supporting a presentation, or for desktop-publishing materials meant to be held and read. Confuse the two and you project unreadable spreadsheets, process maps, etc. onto a wall. Print, hand out, give people time to read, then discuss. If you project an eye chart onto a wall … it pisses them off.
6. **Don't Listen to My Colleague — Listen to ME** — Consultants tolerate intellectual conflict; making one-upmanship public in front of the client erodes confidence. If we keep it up … it pisses them off.
7. **The Facilitation Team Can Take It from Here** — Some teams have such confidence in the facilitators that they disappear for key parts of the process. Clients notice and wonder why. When they feel you ducked the heavy lifting … it pisses them off.

**Keep in mind.** Timing matters. First discuss this document with the engagement team leader. Then, a day or two before the session starts, distribute it to every member of the engagement team who will attend. The memo is framed as a gift "with affection and respect" from the wavespace facilitation team.
