# Collaboration Code — full models catalog

Per-model digests for the ~33 models in Matt Taylor, Rob Evans, and Kelvy Bird's *Collaboration Code: Models* (EY wavespace edition, 2021). Each entry: what the model is, where it came from, components, how to use it, and connections.

For the high-level book digest, see [`book-collaboration-code-models.md`](../../../references/book-collaboration-code-models.md).

## How models are organized

The models tell a story in a particular sequence (Matt Taylor's "Glass Bead Game"). Below they're grouped thematically for navigation:

- **Foundations and the designer's mindset** — Ten Design Assumptions, 22 Habits of Creative People, Rate of Change, Feedback of a Complex Kind, Valid Criticism, Net Vector, Mosquito Strategy, You-Me-Us
- **Possibility and perspective** — Spoze, Vantage Points, Best Case Worst Case, Transforming Organization, ValueWeb / Business of Enterprise
- **Knowledge work and memory** — Seven Domains, Ten-Step Knowledge Work Process, 22 Aspects of Memory, Brain/Mind/Body, Being the There
- **Models about models** — What Is a Model?, Three Cat, Black Box Method
- **The creative process** — Creating the Problem, Creative Process, Zone of Emergence, The Art of Making, The Curve of Action, Design Formation
- **Learning** — Five Es of Education, Levels of Competency, 2know2act2know, Appropriate Response, Path of Learning, Cathedral Builders
- **Enterprise and system** — Stages of an Enterprise, Four-Step ReCreative Process, Design-Build-Use, Scan-Focus-Act, Solution Box, Right Livelihood, The Transition Manager's Creed, The Field
- **Closing** — Models in Conversation as a Language

Notation conventions: terms of art are marked with superscript "ToA" in the source. Glyphs (visual shorthand) accompany most models; we describe them textually below.

## Models — Part 1

### Ten Design Assumptions

**The model.** Ten blunt premises that frame the problem the entire MG Taylor System and Method is designed to address: humanity is generating change and complexity faster than its capacity to respond, and our default operating system — built on hierarchy, scarcity, and "absence-of-failure" definitions of success — is producing the very crises it cannot resolve. The closing line is the diagnosis: "We are failing at success."

**Origin.** The Ten Assumptions open the *Models* book and are the design constraints that Matt and Gail Taylor have worked from for over forty years. Rob Evans co-authored this articulation; the EY wavespace edition of *The Collaboration Code* carries them forward unchanged.

**Components.** (1) Change and complexity exceed our response capacity. (2) Humanity is now the primary force shaping its own environment — the Anthropocene. (3) We are in a hundred-year global civil war over resources the planet cannot replace. (4) We have almost no practice acting as a whole; local fixes generate emergent global harms. (5) The condition is not beyond our knowledge; we are individually capable but collectively "clinically insane." (6) People without power, animals, and the planet itself are treated as commodities; the monetary system measures price, not value. (7) We do not remember, know, or use our own history. (8) Technology lacks educated, self-aware users with century-long horizons. (9) Philosophy, religion, science, and politics have become weapons of influence rather than guides. (10) We define success as the absence of failure, health as the absence of disease, freedom as the absence of tyranny — clinging to old myths instead of designing futures worth building.

**How to use it.** Read as a stand. The Assumptions are not propositions to debate but design constraints to accept before doing the work. They tell a wavespace facilitator what the work is *for* and license the disruption a real collaborative session demands. They also frame why the rest of the Modeling Language exists: every subsequent model is a tool for designing humanity's response to these conditions.

**Connects to.** The whole *Collaboration Code* — *Patterns*, *Tools*, *Models*, *Casebook* — is the response architecture. Most directly to Rate of Change (the engine driving the Assumptions), Feedback of a Complex Kind (what we lack), Transforming Organization and ValueWeb (what we must build), and Being the There (the design posture).

### 22 Habits of Creative People

**The model.** A field-tested rule set describing what people who actually bring new things into the world do — across disciplines and centuries. Matt Taylor's claim is that creativity is not a personality trait but a *practice* with embedded design rules; if you install those rules in an environment and a process, you generate a Zone of Emergence.

**Origin.** Drawn from Matt Taylor's years of observing and studying creators in many fields, then translated into rules for the environment and process of a wavespace. The habits have been tested and refined through repeated Design-Build-Use iterations. Kelly Adams created the graphic images for the 22.

**Components.** The full list: 1. Love the work. 2. Find a teacher; learn a technical discipline at the state-of-the-art level. 3. Organize your workplace so it works for you. 4. Let passion drive you (and express it carefully). 5. Execute your process with control and deliberation. 6. Let your learning range widely. 7. Follow your intuition. 8. Focus intensively during the incubation of an idea. 9. Combine high-energy work with periods of re-creation. 10. Document your progress. 11. Rule out failure as an option; know when to retire from the field. 12. Use feedback with skepticism, craft, and deliberation. 13. Develop and use your "reset" button. 14. Employ a hands-on approach. 15. Challenge convention. 16. Work in rapid iterations. 17. Create a language around your field. 18. Observe ritual to maintain focus over a lifetime. 19. Invest everything. 20. Message broadly. 21. Serve a higher purpose. 22. Create community. The habits are quoted alongside Mozart, Steve Jobs, Faraday, Picasso, Mark Twain, the Japanese Dogu (carpenters' "instruments of the way"), and Eugene Tsui's evolutionary architecture.

**How to use it.** As a portal *into* the System and Method, and as a design checklist for the environment and operating system of a creative workplace. As you read each habit, recall a creative success or failure of your own and identify the rule set that was running. Translated into rules during the Program-to-Schematic-Design phase, they become an OS for an individual, team, or group doing creative work.

**Connects to.** Patterns 101 (A place to be fully alive), 104 (Everything speaks), 124 (Time and concentration), 212 (GroupGenius), 223 (Iteration and rapid prototyping), 246 (Build Strong Memory). Tools: Creating a Proper Collaborative Environment, This Approach to the Art of Facilitation. Models: 22 Aspects of Memory, Creative Process, Design Formation, Design-Build-Use, Five Es of Education, Scan-Focus-Act, Zone of Emergence.

### Rate of Change

**The model.** The foundational premise of the entire MG Taylor Method: complexity in human systems is rising, and the *rate* at which the rate of change is accelerating is itself increasing. Three outcomes are possible — catastrophic collapse, an increase in humanity's response capacity, or a deliberate change of trajectory as we learn better ways to respond.

**Origin.** One of the very first models in the System. Matt developed it in spring 1961 in New York City, where he was a 33-story-construction superintendent; an exchange with the building's owner about a 10-year capital payback horizon made him realize a building is conceived in one economic era and delivered in another. Combined with later exposure to Buckminster Fuller, this was the seed of Anticipatory Design (Pattern 230).

**Components.** Five glyphs: *Complexity* (systems grow more complex as they evolve); *Rate of Change* (the rate itself accelerates as systems interconnect); *Ability of System to Respond* (a possible upward path of human capacity); *Partial Attempts to Deal with Change* (looping — partial fixes paradoxically increase change); *Collapse* (catastrophic failure of life-support systems). The companion *Jet Planes model* — two military jets closing at 3,600 mph — illustrates the scale of the response problem and points to the solution: instruments that scan farther than the naked eye.

**How to use it.** As a "create a sense of urgency" device (Kotter), as a diagnosis of why competent organizations with successful histories are failing now, and as the rationale for systemic over linear responses. Three levers Matt names for keeping requisite: see better (modeling and simulation), build faster (radically reduce time to value, "from lust to dust"), and approach challenges on their own systemic level. Reactions to human-driven change are themselves drivers of more change — a positive feedback loop in the cybernetic sense.

**Connects to.** Patterns 203 (Don't meet — MOBILIZE), 224 (It's not safe to play it safe), 230 (Anticipatory Design), 262 (Response-ability is contagious), 328 (Take a stand). Tools: History of the Future. Models: Appropriate Response, Creative Process, Feedback of a Complex Kind, Four-Step ReCreative Process, Spoze, Zone of Emergence.

### Feedback of a Complex Kind

**The model.** A recovery of the technical, cybernetic definition of feedback — "the message, from a sensor of a system to the controller of a system, about the difference between expectation and performance" — and Norbert Wiener's deeper insight that complex systems require *feedback on the feedback itself*. In multi-controller, multi-level systems you cannot tell whether your simple feedback loops are even working without a meta-loop assessing them.

**Origin.** Wiener's term, developed during the founding of cybernetics. Matt traces it to a single overlooked paragraph at the bottom of a page in one of Wiener's books — the most profound thing Wiener ever said, in Matt's view. Cybernetics' early entanglement with WWII and with the politicized reading of "control" caused the field to fall out of favor; this model reclaims it. Influences include Ross Ashby and Michael Arbib.

**Components.** *Positive feedback* amplifies current behavior, leading to oscillation and breakdown when unchecked. *Negative feedback* attenuates behavior, dampening it back toward thresholds. The driving country-road metaphor: lane markers as thresholds, "hunting" as the constant left-right oscillation around an imaginary centerline that no system ever holds exactly. *Simple feedback* (engine governor, thermostat) is straightforward. *Feedback of a complex kind* is recursive, multi-loop, multi-controller, and inherently collaborative — feedback designed to help a system "learn how to learn better."

**How to use it.** As the engineering theory underneath wavespace. The session itself is engineered as a complex system mirroring requisite variety of the system it intends to affect — multi-loop feedback among Sponsor Design Team, KreW, facilitators, participants, experts. The facilitator's job is to discern what feedback matters and to redirect feedback that is "from us to us" (changing how the work flows) rather than "from us to them" (trying to change the participants). Living systems frequently go to the edge of instability, where creativity, evolution, and transformation occur; homeostasis is not always the goal.

**Connects to.** Patterns 121 (Free space to discover the truth), 127 (There is no "out there"), 205 (Sponsor Design Team as microcosm), 212 (GroupGenius), 213 (Careful use of experts), 221 (Requisite variety of design). Tools: Take-a-Stand Matrix, Why It Won't Work. Models: 22 Habits of Creative People, Net Vector, Rate of Change, You-Me-Us, Zone of Emergence.

### Valid Criticism

**The model.** A four-step discipline for criticism that is honest, neutral, and useful — and a refusal of the throwing-rocks behavior that masquerades as criticism in modern discourse. Without these rules you cannot establish a standard, compare alternatives, or propose improvement; you only get the noise that has come to be called fake news.

**Origin.** Inspired by T. S. Eliot's "The Use of Poetry and the Use of Criticism" (1933), which Matt encountered late and recognized as Eliot's *criticism of criticism* — the rules required for criticism to be valid. Augmented by Jacques Barzun's argument in *The House of Intellect* (1959) that only the very best examples of a type are worth criticizing; tearing down the obviously incompetent wastes time.

**Components.** Eliot's four steps: (1) State the ideal or the good — the measure by which you assess. (2) Cite examples from history — works that have stood the test of time. (3) Analyze the work in relation to both the ideal and the historical examples; deliver both positive and negative judgments. (4) Show how the work in question can be improved. Add Barzun: only criticize what is basically good.

**How to use it.** As an embedded discipline in design teams so they can vet ideas through aggressive but legitimate feedback without emotion overwhelming intellect. The model's *Practice* application is a long worked critique of modern hierarchical organizations: a 12-point list of how hierarchies fail (they don't support knowledge work; their C3 cannot match the rate, scale, and complexity of their environment; brain-to-actuator distance is too great; "the language of management does not describe the technical, systemic elements"; passive-aggressive relationships among components; lack of requisite variety; suppression of emergence; non-scalability; energy sink; over-efficient at preserving themselves; breeds dichotomies). The remedy is not to abolish hierarchy but to demote it to one mode among many, replaced as the dominant architecture by a ValueWeb.

**Connects to.** Patterns 211 (Get the whole system in the room), 212 (GroupGenius), 221 (Requisite variety of design), 222 (Recursion). Tools: Twelve Laws of Collaborative Design Spaces. Models: Ten-Step Knowledge Work Process, Transforming Organization, ValueWeb / Business of Enterprise, Zone of Emergence.

### Net Vector

**The model.** A picture of what an organization actually needs to move — not consensus, not unanimity, not alignment-as-uniformity, but a *net vector*: the resultant of many individual force-vectors that, while not perfectly parallel, sum to a meaningful direction and magnitude. The opposite is a *stochastic state* — random aimlessness with no coherent movement.

**Origin.** Matt picked up the word *stochastic* from an inventor friend in exchange for industrial design work and saw it as the perfect description of a wavespace before facilitation begins: 50 to 150 people walk in carrying entirely different vectors of mood, opinion, and concern. The model became Matt's standard answer to sponsors who ask for "agreement": "I don't do agreement — I do alignment."

**Components.** Two glyphs: *Stochastic State* (forces scattered, no coherent movement) and *Net Vector* (forces compiled into directional movement). The mathematical line carries three properties — thickness (mass), length (speed), and direction. Drawn from physics: a vector has direction *and* magnitude. The line is never perfectly straight and the mass is never 100% — that level of agreement would itself be unhealthy.

**How to use it.** With sponsors and Sponsor Design Teams, to reset the goal of the collaborative process from "everyone agrees" to "the differences make a difference but combine into directional force." Backed by Ashby's law of requisite variety: a system's control variety must be greater than or equal to the variety of what it controls. Monocultures of viewpoint are as dangerous as agricultural monocultures. The design process invites divergent viewpoints and radical challenges, then carefully reduces a wide range of options to a critical few — an inclusive process generates organizational support for its outcome without forcing a conclusion. Healthy debate continues during implementation; otherwise feedback degenerates into self-congratulation.

**Connects to.** Patterns 121 (Free space to discover the truth), 211 (Get the whole system in the room), 216 (Diversity and inclusion), 221 (Requisite variety of design), 224 (It's not safe to play it safe), 242 (Sapiential leadership), 308 (Tell the truth with courage and compassion). Tools: Force-Field Analysis, Mobilization, PatchWorks Architecture, Rational-Emotional-Political Model, Synthesis Conversation, What Has to Be True?, Why It Won't Work. Models: Appropriate Response, You-Me-Us, Zone of Emergence.

### Mosquito Strategy

**The model.** A diagnosis of *impedance mismatch* between the frequency/magnitude profile of a problem and the frequency/magnitude profile of the response. High-frequency/low-magnitude problems (mosquitoes, bacteria) cannot be solved by low-frequency/high-magnitude responses (DDT, broad-spectrum antibiotics) without amplifying the problem through positive feedback — the surviving fraction breeds resistance and the response itself accelerates the threat.

**Origin.** Matt grew up at Clark Air Force Base in the Philippines in 1947, where the military crop-dusted DDT over everything; he and the other kids would run out and "get totally soaked." Later, recognizing why mosquitoes were still around, he tracked the Panama Canal's mosquito eradication — accomplished long before DDT by covering every glass of water — and generalized the lesson into a rule about *requisite* responses: not too little, not too much, but at one with what you are responding to.

**Components.** Four glyphs: *Low Frequency*, *High Frequency*, *Low Magnitude*, *High Magnitude*. The mechanical analogue is the automobile transmission — the impedance mismatch between high-rpm crankshaft and stationary wheels resolved by graduated gears. The job of good design is to mitigate impedance mismatches between means and ends.

**How to use it.** As a diagnostic for any "grand sweeping solution." In hospitals, it points away from more antibiotics toward hand-washing protocols and packaging discipline. In mosquito control, toward sterilizing and releasing male mosquitoes. In management: high-frequency/low-magnitude feedback for someone new to a task; low-frequency/high-magnitude observation for veterans (the "pulling up the carrots" problem). In facilitation: a Scan opens with high-frequency/low-magnitude module changes; an Act phase moves with high activity but slower module change. In driving: oversteering is a frequency/magnitude mismatch.

**Connects to.** Patterns 221 (Requisite variety of design), 261 (Make the solution last past Act Day). Tools: This Approach to the Art of Facilitation. Models: Appropriate Response, Design-Build-Use, Feedback of a Complex Kind, Net Vector.

### You-Me-Us

**The model.** A Venn diagram defining the minimum conditions for information exchange. Two circles entirely separate: nothing in common, no basis for communication. Two circles entirely overlapping: nothing different, no information to exchange. Only when there is enough overlap to communicate *and* enough difference to have something to say does information — Bateson's "difference that makes a difference" — actually flow.

**Origin.** Emerged in the very first DesignShops in Boulder in the early 1980s — drawn on the fly in response to recurring sponsor concerns: "Does that mean I have to give up my identity?" "Does that mean we have to agree on everything?" "Does that mean no one is in charge?" Matt initially shipped the model as a Venn diagram and a complementary "flowers in mulch" illustration to a regional FAA director who delivered the first MG Taylor DesignShop without Matt and Gail.

**Components.** The two circles. The companion image: flowers (individual expression) growing in mulch (commonwealth of shared experience). Too much mulch and the flower smothers; too little and it starves. The Venn diagram operates on ideas, paradigms, personalities; the flower image operates on resources.

**How to use it.** As the design rule for populating a wavespace participant set — enough difference to make collaboration worthwhile, enough commonality to make it possible. Operationally informs Pattern 210 (Participants) and Pattern 218 (People Science). Watch the circles in real time during a session: if they drift apart, choose modules that pull people together; if they over-overlap into false agreement, push them apart. The model is also a corrective for Facebook-style filter bubbles and expert-reinforcement loops where common opinions get mistaken for objective truth. It is *not* about compromise — it is about playing Spoze with someone else's idea and letting the design process sort it out.

**Connects to.** Patterns 121 (Free space to discover the truth), 211 (Get the whole system in the room), 212 (GroupGenius), 214 (Send them in two-by-two), 216 (Diversity and inclusion), 218 (People Science), 221 (Requisite variety of design), 308 (Tell the truth with courage and compassion). Tools: Bus Exercise, Deepening Conversation, History of the Future, Quaker Conversation, Speed Dating, Sponsor Design Team Planning Session, Synthesis Conversation. Models: 2know2act2know, 22 Habits of Creative People, Four-Step ReCreative Process, Scan-Focus-Act, Vantage Points, Zone of Emergence.

### Spoze

**The model.** A discipline for handling new information that challenges an existing paradigm without either rejecting it outright or force-fitting it into the old model. The Spoze stage holds new information as a thought experiment — "Suppose this is true, what would that allow us to do?" — testing it through simulations and what-ifs before deciding whether to incorporate it.

**Origin.** Embedded in the process from the very beginning of MG Taylor's work, but not formalized as a model until 1995–96, when Bryan Coffman drew it up for the Modeling Language. The contraction "Spoze" preserves the casual playfulness of the move it names.

**Components.** Five stages. *Paradigm* — the internal model, partly inherited (instinct), partly cultural, that interprets sensory input below conscious awareness. *New Information* — a message whose surprise content is high enough that it cannot be filtered out (drawing on the formal information-theoretic definition: information is proportional to uncertainty). *Encounter* — when New Information presents a strong challenge of sufficient magnitude to bring the disconnect to conscious awareness. *Spoze* — the thought-experiment stage that minimizes immediate disruption while assessing the value of the new information. *Incorporate* — paradigm revision if the truth claims hold up. James Grier Miller's *Living Systems* trio is invoked: Associator, Memory, Decider as the learning loop.

**How to use it.** As both a daily habit ("before you judge something, suppose it's true") and a structural feature of the design process. Many Scan modules are built on Spoze — "this is true, this is true, and this other thing is true whether you believe it or not, now solve for it." Spoze always carries real risk: paradigm-disruption is genuine. The most complete way for an enterprise to play Spoze is to create offspring through "recombination of DNA" — two organizations sharing strengths to form a separate test venture with requisite variety drawn from the full ValueWeb (customers, producers, investors, sometimes competitors). Spoze resembles Scan-Focus-Act but allows for situations where the system did not voluntarily enter Scan; the new information has agency.

**Connects to.** Patterns 211 (Get the whole system in the room), 216 (Diversity and inclusion), 224 (It's not safe to play it safe), 226 (Answers from unlikely places), 244 (Work as play), 310 (Shifting group energy). Tools: Elephant in the Room, Future State Scenarios, Simulations, Spoze. Models: Creative Process, Metaphors, Scan-Focus-Act, Simulation, Strategic Modeling, Syntopical Reading, Ten-Step Knowledge Work Process, ValueWeb / Business of Enterprise.

### Vantage Points

**The model.** A topographic-map-like depiction of seven nested shells of context — from Philosophy down to Tasks — that every enterprise must keep coherent to maintain homeostasis. The deliberate ambiguity of the contour lines (is Task the peak or the valley?) makes the model's central point: you can never know an organization's actual operating philosophy until you watch how its most junior associate performs daily tasks.

**Origin.** Adapted from Thomas Gilbert's *Human Competence: Engineering Worthy Performance* (1978), which proposed an early version to help identify the "scope" within which to understand human performance. Matt and Gail found one element missing — Tasks, the level at which real work happens — and added it.

**Components.** Seven shells. *Philosophy* — the usually hidden beliefs uniting the components, enabling innovation and stability. *Culture* — the values, norms, and behaviors manifesting Philosophy. *Policy* — purpose, intent, goals; the rules of the game. *Strategy* — disposition of large-scale forces over space and time; "patterns of play." *Tactics* — matching Logistics to Strategy in specific situations. *Logistics* — coordination of resources, energy, and knowledge (with the Col. "Jeep" Robinson epigraph: "Amateurs talk about military strategy. Professionals talk about logistics.") *Tasks* — chopping wood and carrying water; the concrete manifestation of Philosophy.

**How to use it.** As both a design template and an audit tool. Three managerial clusters: Philosophy/Culture/Policy to steer the enterprise's evolution; Policy/Strategy/Tactics to steer its operational structure; Tactics/Logistics/Tasks to steer the actual work. Most companies are competent at Strategy through Tasks but weak at Philosophy through Policy. The model is also a syntopical-reading design tool: it lets a team learn to talk *at a different vantage point* than the one they typically argue at, giving them productive engagement before they go after the difficult issues. Major change at any one level requires addressing the others; Philosophy and Culture pervade everything below.

**Connects to.** Patterns 103 (Form and function as one), 127 (There is no "out there"), 211 (Get the whole system in the room). Tools: Bus Exercise (Organizational Culture), Symbolic Action. Models: Five Es of Education.

### Best Case Worst Case

**The model.** A way to create an "envelope of possibility" around a decision that has already been made — bracketing the range of outcomes between best case and worst case, plotting a most probable path, and managing the *Trunk* of resources that brought you to the present. It is *not* a way to make the original decision or build a quantitative business case; it is a discipline for navigating once you have committed to move forward.

**Origin.** Developed in a session with the Independent Bankers Association during the deregulation that allowed Citicorp into local banking markets. The bankers wanted a DesignShop on stopping the deregulation; Matt told them they would lose that fight and proposed instead designing how to compete with Citicorp. The model emerged in the room as Matt drew the first loop and asked, "Forget how, forget where, forget when — what's your best case?" The session produced the bankers' bank (a shared services entity) as the strategic answer.

**Components.** Four glyphs. *Best Case* — what happens if everything goes as planned. *Worst Case* — what happens if everything goes wrong. *Most Probable Case* — the dynamic estimate of likely outcome. *The Trunk* — the past trajectory of events and resources brought to the present moment, "the mouth of the trunk" being where history opens up to possibility. The dynamic version layers cybernetic hunting (positive feedback as vicious cycle, virtuous cycle when toward goal), with new current conditions becoming the next Trunk.

**How to use it.** Brackets the field of action, lets you measure your actual path against your projected paths, and forces *Trunk management* — deciding what to keep, what to lose to lighten the load, and what new resources to acquire. Three key questions at the mouth of the Trunk: do best/worst case accurately bracket the risk/opportunity equation? Is the Trunk model-driven or just old habits and organizational leftovers? Does the plan provide a method for spotting cusp points and dropping redundancy? Trunk management resists the common mistake of throwing out everything that doesn't seem to fit the future vision; preserve requisite variety in resources and options for as long as possible until something "pops."

**Connects to.** Patterns 221 (Requisite variety of design), 261 (Make the solution last past Act Day), 262 (Response-ability is contagious). Tools: Action Plan, Contingency Diagram, Force-Field Analysis, Future State Scenarios, History of the Future, Planning for Uncertainty, Simulation, Transformation Roadmap, What Has to Be True, Why It Won't Work. Models: Feedback of a Complex Kind.

### Transforming Organization

**The model.** A design strategy for moving a traditional hierarchy toward a fluid, networked structure *without* triggering the hierarchy's immune system. The core rule: **never directly attack the hierarchy.** Instead, set up an alternative organization alongside or inside it — sponsored from the top, staffed initially with insiders, with permeable boundaries and a clear, valued mission — and let it demonstrate its way of working until the energy of the enterprise shifts.

**Origin.** Developed across many Matt and Gail Taylor engagements; the chapter is dominated by Col. William Rutley's USAF Memo for Record from 2004 documenting the model's application at Arnold Engineering Development Center (AEDC) and the F-15 Eagle program — driving an order-of-magnitude (X10) improvement over conventional tools (Six Sigma, TQM, Theory of Constraints, GE Workouts). The closing example is General Motors' late-1970s rationalization, which destroyed the "green goo" of horizontal relationships and contributed to GM losing its dominant position.

**Components.** Five phases drawn as glyphs. *Phase 1: Project* — a sponsored, viable-system project drawing most resources from inside. *Phase 2: Initial Success* — public success makes it safe for middle managers to engage and brings outside resources in. *Phase 3: Expanding Network* — the project's sphere grows; elements adopt the new ways back into the hierarchy. *Phase 4: Established Success* — engine of growth, attracting most new resources; mini-hierarchies form within. *Phase 5: Pre-eminence* — the new networked, ValueWeb-like structure becomes the predominant form; the original hierarchy can persist but the energy has clearly shifted. Underneath the model sits Stafford Beer's Viable System Model: every entity needs Systems 1 (operations), 2 (information channels), 3 (structures and controls), 4 (environmental scanning and adaptation), 5 (policy/executive). The chapter introduces *green goo* — the informal, organic relationships that make any hierarchy actually work, analogous to synaptic resistance in the brain. Hierarchy's strength: a stable form designed for predictable conditions; hierarchies are composed of *people*, and competent people add the green goo.

**How to use it.** As the master design strategy whenever an organization needs to evolve and the hierarchy is the obstacle. Identify a project worth sponsoring, give it top-level air cover, staff it from the inside ("our people working on something we need"), build it as a viable system from day one, and let it grow. Adhocracies (Bennis, Toffler, Mintzberg) failed because they lacked C3; the new entity must have it. Yuval Noah Harari, Mark Twain, and Matt are quoted on hierarchy: it lets strangers cooperate; good judgment comes from bad-judgment experience; "Hierarchy becomes corrupted when people at the top think their job is running the company, when their real job is designing and maintaining the system that runs the company."

**Connects to.** Patterns 221 (Requisite variety of design), 222 (Recursion), 230 (Anticipatory design), 325 (Create a space for transformation), 328 (Take a stand). Tools: PatchWorks Architecture, Rational/Emotional/Political Model, Twelve Laws of Collaborative Design Spaces. Models: Rate of Change, Seven Domains, Stages of an Enterprise.

### ValueWeb / Business of Enterprise

**The model.** A network architecture for the knowledge enterprise, answering the question "What is the business of business?" The old model put management in the middle of conflicting demands from investors, producers, and customers — a zero-sum game where one constituent's win was another's loss. The new model arranges the same elements as a web of clamshells around a Commissioner-of-Baseball-style management function, connected by nodes through which value flows back and forth, transforming the zero-sum game into an infinite one.

**Origin.** About three years into MG Taylor (mid-1980s) the firm was acquired by Acacia Group in Washington, D.C. — an insurance company chartered by Andrew Jackson, then in a period of rapid growth. Matt designed an on-site NavCenter and, while preparing a DesignShop on a customer relationship, asked himself a question he had never heard before: *What is the business of business?* The Commissioner of Baseball role, modeled on Peter Ueberroth, became Matt's metaphor for management's real function — serve the *game*, not the owners or any single party. A few years later in an Ernst & Young DesignShop with Digital Corporation (Boston), Matt and Rob named the radiating structures "clamshells."

**Components.** Four roles, redefined. *Investor* still provides capital but is increasingly also producer and customer. *Producer* still makes the product but is more involved in financial and operational understanding and innovation. *Customer* still buys and uses the product but votes with investments and is often actively involved in production. *Management* still balances the whole, but is distributed and information-augmented; "more management going on, but fewer formal managers." The architecture: clamshells radiating from a Commissioner-of-Baseball core, with nodes connecting clamshells; the farther from the center, the more transactional the relationships.

**How to use it.** Two design principles drive the new model's outperformance: (1) the players are tightly enough connected that each recognizes their fate is bound to the others' — knowledge of how each part works flows along the links, and each constituent acts in a cybernetic management role; (2) individuals can play more than one role at once, holding a stake in every facet. Greater transparency makes management a shared responsibility. The model is the architecture toward which Transforming Organization migrates, and it corrects the 12 systemic failures of hierarchy enumerated in Valid Criticism. Carolyn Buck Luce's framing of business model — "the way an organization combines its tangible and intangible assets to provide value to customers and keep its fair share of that value" — anchors the chapter.

**Connects to.** Patterns 262 (Response-ability is contagious). Tools: Business Model Canvas. Models: Net Vector, You-Me-Us.

### Seven Domains

**The model.** Seven interconnected functional areas that must be managed in any enterprise to maintain capability over time. Drawn as a network rather than a hierarchy, the domains together create the conditions for creativity and productivity without requiring command-and-control.

**Origin.** Developed when Matt and Gail moved to Colorado, formulating the DesignShop and "Solution Box Workshops." Sponsors kept asking them to make the system coherent — "you can't just put a bunch of different things together and declare them a system." Matt scribbled until he identified the essential elements that, if attended to, would express the system's design intent. The Seven Domains Audit® is a registered review process applying the model to an organization.

**Components.** Seven glyphs. *Body of Knowledge* — total information (and information about how to get more) the system needs to remain viable. *Process Facilitation* — philosophy and methods for flow; "negative space." *Education* — how the system explores, experiences, explains, sets expectations, gathers feedback (the Five Es). *Environment* — the physical, emotional, and metaphysical field; dynamic, bidirectional. *Technical Systems* — the tools and artifacts that amplify, modulate, or attenuate the other domains. *Project Management* — efficient resource allocation toward finite, temporal objectives. *Venture Management* — establishing and managing homeostasis with the larger environment, exploring new opportunities. The model is silent on "people" by design: people are not resources to be managed; they manage the domains together.

**How to use it.** As both a design template for building enterprise capability and an audit tool for diagnosing it. Most teaching introduces the domains one at a time, but the deeper use is examining them in relation to each other ("Environment of Process Facilitation," "Technical Systems of the Body of Knowledge," "Venture Management of Education"). Used at three recursion levels — individual, organization, society. The Body of Knowledge example is canonical: a 25-year employee walking out the door takes huge institutional knowledge with no documentation left behind; Wikipedia gives the illusion of solved knowledge management without the discipline. Also one of the three elements of the Solution Box model.

**Connects to.** Patterns 101 (A place to be fully alive), 106 (It matters what you take away), 108 (Knowledge-rich environment), 129 (No licking the Knowledge Workers), 212 (GroupGenius), 248 (Production values matter), 305 (Trust the process). Tools: Creating a Proper Collaborative Environment, Twelve Laws of Collaborative Design Spaces. Models: Design Formation, Five Es of Education, Vantage Points.

### Ten-Step Knowledge Work Process

**The model.** A ten-step workflow for processing information through ongoing knowledge work — from event through documentation, knowledge base, distribution, tracking, feedback, design of the next event, and read-ahead, looping back to the next event. It is engineering, not metaphor: the wiring and work protocol of GroupGenius, designed to scale to environments of extreme complexity, high variety, and high rate of change.

**Origin.** Designed in 1987 for a senior manager who had left Acacia and asked Matt to design a mini-NavCenter at his new company. Matt had been studying systems and cybernetics and had a vision of a computer system called CyberCon that would automate knowledge management — capabilities beyond the technology of the day (and in some ways beyond today's). Using the run-walk-run pattern (automate what you can, do the rest by hand, automate again as technology catches up), Matt designed a process that could give a senior executive CyberCon-level support entirely with 1980s tools. The first executive's CEO declared, "Executives do not type!" — three years later the entire executive floor was a NavCenter and KnowledgeWorkers occupied the old executive offices.

**Components.** Ten steps. (1) *Event* — a meeting or session involving "knodes" (knowledge nodes). (2) *Documentation* — objectives, premises, ideas-in-progress, decisions, action plans. (3) *Knowledge Base (K-Base)* — repository and router. (4) *Distribution* — repackaged and transduced to those who need it on a need-to-know basis. (5) *Tracking* — records who got what, when, why. (6) *Feedback* — from recipients, calibrating the value of the information. (7) *K-Base* — organizes and stores the feedback. (8) *Design* — project leader and facilitator design the next event. (9) *Read-Ahead* — distributed to participants of the next event. (10) *Event* — the cycle ends as it began. The chapter includes a role table mapping each step to Project Manager, Project Support Staff, and Project Members.

**How to use it.** As the engine of the knowledge organization. Use meetings only for what the group can do together; avoid them for pure information exchange. All meetings are documented; documentation is central. The K-Base needs librarians. The bias is toward finding and creating relationships, discovering new links between humans, processes, systems, and computers — not just efficient storage. Like any skill, it must be learned and practiced throughout the organization, including at senior levels.

**Connects to.** Patterns 201 (It starts before it "starts"), 203 (Don't meet — MOBILIZE), 246 (Build Strong Memory), 261 (Make the solution last past Act Day), 264 (Engaging deliverables). Tools: Action Plan, Executive Summary, Knowledge Worker Roles, On-Line Journal, On-Line File Structure, Participant Invitation, Participant Pre-Work, Single Frame Graphics, Transformation Roadmap. Models: 2know2act2know.

### 22 Aspects of Memory

**The model.** A radical redefinition of memory as the *state of the entire system at any given moment* rather than retrievable storage. Together with the 22 Habits of Creative People, the 22 Aspects form the conceptual foundation of the Taylor System and Method. They are stated as engineering design assumptions — not science — useful enough to build "mind-like" environments, processes, and artifacts.

**Origin.** Elements of the 22 Aspects were held as trade secrets and granted U.S. patent protection. The reconceptualization began in 1996 with MG Taylor's first work with EY in Hilton Head, while Matt was writing the first provisional patent. The Black Box Method (a half-century-old cybernetic technique) drove the work: Matt observed how memory actually operates across recursion levels and triangulated with neuroscience, engineering, psychology, and decades of DesignShop, NavCenter, and PatchWorks practice. Influenced strongly by John Lilly's *Programming and Metaprogramming in the Human Biocomputer*. Citations across the chapter include Larry Dossey, Norman Doidge, Thomas Kuhn, Noam Chomsky, Anil Seth, Julian Jaynes, Max Tegmark, Benedict Carey, David Bohm, and Michael Pollan.

**Components.** The 22 Aspects: (1) Memory is the state of the entire system. (2) Memory is not frozen storage. (3) Memory is active. (4) Memory develops through sensation and is inextricably emotional. (5) Memory is distributed. (6) The architecture of memory is a network. (7) The architecture changes with use. (8) Memory utilizes reuse. (9) Memory is agent-based. (10) Components vote. (11) Memory is context-sensitive. (12) Memory is both digital and analog. (13) Components are rule-based. (14) Memory employs and generates field effects. (15) Signal strength increases through proximity, repetition, and reuse. (16) Geometry has content. (17) Memory makes use of both open and closed systems. (18) Memory chunks into self-organizing, clustered, cascading hierarchies. (19) Memory employs language. (20) Dialogue delivers instructions consciously and unconsciously. (21) Awareness of architectural patterns enables conscious learning and metaprogramming. (22) Self-awareness opens up new possibilities of BEing. The chapter introduces the *Default Mode Network* (DMN) as the brain's autobiographical-self hub and uncertainty-reduction screen, connecting Aspect 19 to neuroscience. The brain reassembles memories rather than retrieving them; recall is itself an event that changes the system.

**How to use it.** Stated using the *Principle of Minimum Claim* — minimum certainty needed to ground each design assumption. The aspects function as *metaprogramming* in design processes. Apply them as active design principles when building real environments and systems — wavespaces, NavCenters, knowledge workflows. Examples: isolating participants from distracting environments and replacing them with engaging content (Aspect 4); building distributed StrongMemory across many media so single-point-of-failure designs are eliminated (Aspect 5); using Baroque music as a parallel-processing agent (Aspect 17); designing latency intentionally as a feature, not a bug (Aspect 7). The model gives the System and Method the ability to make disparate components of an organization act in mind-like ways.

**Connects to.** Patterns 108 (Knowledge-Rich Environment), 222 (Recursion), 246 (Build Strong Memory), 247 (Use the power of compelling visuals), 264 (Engaging deliverables). Tools: Act Day Deliverable Wall, Act Day Tracking Wall, Executive Summary, Knowledge Wall, On-line File Structure, On-line Journal, Strategic Modeling. ### Brain/Mind/Body

**The model.** A multi-decade Spoze thought experiment that has become the technical basis of the Taylor System and Method. It supposes that the human brain/mind/body is a network architecture of multiple chunked, recursive functions distributed across body and environment; that memory is active reassembly, not storage; that consciousness is an *executive routine*, not a controller; and that a wavespace is the physical instantiation of the same architecture at a higher level of recursion.

**Origin.** Matt first developed the model in 1966 while working with an inventor and steeped in cybernetics and systems theory. He has revisited and iterated it about every decade, with this volume's version the most recent. Influences include Stafford Beer (Viable Systems), Stuart Kauffman (patches and nodes — though Matt anticipated the concepts in 1966), John Lilly (metaprogramming), and Frank R. Wilson's *The Hand* ("brain is hand and hand is brain"). Bessel van der Kolk, Julian Jaynes, and Moshe Feldenkrais are cited on trauma, consciousness, and embodied memory.

**Components.** A chain of *Suppose* statements rather than discrete glyphs. Suppose the brain/mind/body is a redundant, distributed network of nodes and patches, with embedded logic, context-sensitive synaptic resistance, and digital firing on analog substrate. Suppose successful transmission requires a quorum of neurons firing with adequate relaxation time — explaining why the 22 Habits emphasize alternating focus with re-creation. Suppose patches can be semi-autonomous and explain unconscious associations. Suppose the body itself is brain/mind-like and the architecture is recursive across teams, families, organizations, societies. Suppose physical environments are extensions of the brain/mind/body — "Everything speaks." Suppose memory is associative, not stored; recall is reassembly that itself changes the system. Suppose the *executive routine* (ER) is a learned, programmable function — Lilly's metaprogramming — that is *not* at the top of any cognitive hierarchy; it is one rotating function that we experience as "I." Suppose the ER also functions as System Integrator (SI) and as a Commissioner of Baseball — balancing legitimate stakeholders rather than ruling. Suppose brain/mind/bodies generate fields, sync via *entrainment*, and become *entangled* — connected across time and distance like subatomic particles. Suppose growth and transformation come from engagement with complexity that forces reorganization; fatigue is a necessary precondition for breakthrough. *StrongMemory* lives in well-traveled channels and dense, connected ganglia.

**How to use it.** As the underlying theory of why the System and Method works. Wavespace is engineered to mimic the brain/mind/body at the group-recursion level: participants temporarily hand over elements of their executive routine to the facilitation team; the Sponsor Design Team and KnowledgeWorkers create an environment that disrupts existing node-patch systems; Scan introduces relevant content with rapid exercises; premature closure is actively prevented until critical mass emerges; the group eventually reclaims its executive routine and breaks out of the Zone of Emergence as its own. The model passes the System governance tests: useful results reliably, rule-based, handles immense complexity, jumps the change curve, adapts across cultures, governed by Feedback of a Complex Kind, points to its own improvement, expressible as art, benign and sustainable, and "feels good to work this way."

**Connects to.** Patterns 106 (It matters what you take away), 107 (Entry space of transition), 121 (Free space to discover the truth), 201 (It starts before it "starts"), 246 (Build Strong Memory), 304 (Magic is hard work), 325 (Create a space for transformation). Tools: Sponsor Design Team Planning Session, Participant Invitation, wavespace Opening, Spoze. Models: 22 Aspects of Memory, 22 Habits of Creative People, Black Box Theory, Solution Box, Spoze.

### Being the There

**The model.** A koan-as-model built on Matt's axiom, "You can't get there from here, but you can get here from there." A compelling future state — *the There* — is not reached by incremental travel from the present; it is reached by fully imagining and prototyping it, then porting as much of it as possible into today's reality, day after day. To bring the There to Here, you must *be* the There.

**Origin.** Matt traces the source phrase to an unnamed economist on the IEIS computer-conferencing system (created by Starr Roxanne Hiltz and Murray Turoff, authors of *The Network Nation*) during the Renascence Project in the early Boulder years — a brilliant futurist who delivered the line as a frustrated cynic. Rob traced the phrase further back through Marshall Dodge and Robert Bryan's Maine "Down East" *Bert and I* humor and the 19th-century Arkansas Traveler tradition. Matt's response to "you can't get there from here" was always "yes, but you can get *here from there*" — and the conversation with Rob in writing this book added the missing piece: you have to *Be* the There.

**Components.** No formal glyphs — the model lives as the koan and a set of design and facilitation moves. Sources for the There can be individual (meditation, mindfulness, body practices), or — much more commonly — relational, between two deeply entrained partners (Lennon/McCartney, Watson/Crick/Franklin, Buffett/Munger, Van Gogh and his brother Theo, Matt and Gail's drives between the Boulder Management Center and home). Or it emerges through GroupGenius via Kotter's "guiding coalition." Edison's "1 percent inspiration, 99 percent perspiration" sets the discipline. The *adjacent possible* is the experiential frame: participants experience the There as something that could not have existed without their intense interaction and commitment.

**How to use it.** Build the future at the scale and detail necessary to engage the whole system; invite people in; let them experience a low-resolution prototype; design the There in its full integrity, not a streamlined version. Modules that enable Being the There: Simulation, Prototyping, Legends, Future State Scenarios, Create the Problem, Model Shop, First Draft of the Solution (Build-the-There), Create the Game of X, Spoze. The Knowledge Wall, the dressing of the space, real-time reconfiguration of the environment all carry the There. Two cautions: tearing down old structures too quickly activates their immune system with violent force — hospice the death of the old order (see Transforming Organization); and don't accept the low-resolution prototype as the Minimal Viable Product of the Future — design for difficulty. "Allow no compromises to the intent and you will attract people who accept the challenge to be at the very top of their game."

### What Is a Model?

**The model.** A meta-model: a Spoze dialogue *about* models and the process of modeling, opening the entire MG Taylor Modeling Language to healthy skepticism. All models are wrong by their nature — they involve simplification, abstraction, generalization, deletion, idealization. The aim is not truth but *usefulness*: useful for what, under what conditions, with what limitations. Models give us access to reality but are not reality; "believing is seeing," not the reverse.

**Origin.** A long-standing facilitation ritual: Matt and Rob have for decades opened collaborative experiences with "the Model Talk," holding up a wooden model airplane and engaging participants in a Socratic dialogue. Citations include Jorge Luis Borges, George E. P. Box ("Essentially, all models are wrong, but some are useful"), Georg Rasch, John von Neumann, Robert Anton Wilson, R. Buckminster Fuller (sunsight/sunclipse, out-and-in), and the standing MG Taylor Axiom: "The only value of an idea, concept, or theory is what it allows you to do."

**Components.** The dialogue itself, scripted in the source. The facilitator holds up a model airplane and asks: What is this? Is it real? What's the difference between this and a real airplane? — eliciting smaller, simplified, no working parts. "All models are subject to simplification, generalization, omission — distortions of one kind or another." Are larger-than-life models also legitimate? — yes, DNA, molecular models. Is this a good model or a bad model? — "It depends on what you are going to use it for." Good for showing a child what an airplane is; bad for wind-tunnel testing. What models do we use every day? — calendars, spreadsheets, maps, models of social interaction. Are models ever dangerous? — economic models of the market, social models of beauty/gender, religious or political certainty. How much of what is happening here can our five senses actually pick up? — fraction. Are there ways to apprehend reality outside models? — mystical/peak experiences, in-the-zone performance. What does this tell us about the truth or value of our models? — never 100% correct, always room for iteration, but without them we have nothing.

**How to use it.** As a wavespace opening. Done well, the Model Talk both reflects on modeling and instills a healthy skepticism that participants carry into the rest of the session — they hold their convictions and beliefs about both their problem and its solution more tentatively. The facilitator closes by making explicit connections between the Model Talk and the objectives of the session at hand. The "Connects to" entry for this model declares the meta-stance: *you can open this volume anywhere and pick up the thread of this dialogue on models and the process of modeling.*

**Connects to.** Patterns 227 (Metaphorical modes of discovery), 244 (Don't argue, test), 307 (Make the models useful). Tools: wavespace Opening, Strategic Modeling, This Approach to the Art of Facilitation. Models: every model in the Modeling Language.

## Models — Part 2

### Levels of Competency

**The model.** A social architecture for distributing capability across a group, distinguishing the levels at which an individual must hold a discipline (familiarity, journeyperson, mastery) so that knowledge does not bottleneck on a single specialist. The core insight is that resilient teams cross-train every member in every role, with mastery in a few — "specialization is for insects."

**Origin.** Matt Taylor adapted the model in 1983 from the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, who used three explicit levels of competency in life-or-death engineering work: every soldier had to have at least familiarity with everything the unit could do, broad competency across many jobs, and mastery in a few. He encountered this during a one-week DesignShop with the Army Chief of Staff to compress the rollout of a new battle doctrine; the doctrine got formal approval in 48 hours and rolled out in months instead of years.

**Components.** Three nested levels — Familiarity, Journeyperson, Mastery — applied to every role in a KnowledgeWorker (KreW) crew. The model is illustrated in the book by Robert A. Heinlein's *Time Enough for Love* litany ("change a diaper, plan an invasion, write a sonnet…") and by Stewart Emery's wise-counsel passage on the discipline of going beyond one's limits.

**How to use it.** A Process Facilitator (PF) must hold journeyperson-level grasp of every KreW role and be able to perform and teach each one. The model audits a team before an engagement: who has familiarity, who can carry the work as a journeyperson, who is the master and exemplar. It also frames cross-training plans inside an organization that wants to escape rigid job classifications, and it organizes the curriculum for training new wavespace KreW.

**Connects to.** 2know2act2know (knowledge as an active practice), 22 Aspects of Memory, 22 Habits of Creative People, Path of Learning: Five Points of Mastery (the lifelong learning roles), Right Livelihood (mastery of craft as a precondition).

### 2know2act2know

**The model.** A re-envisioning of the older Pyramid of Information model. It distinguishes Data, Information, Knowledge, Wisdom, Memory, and Intuition, and locks in the central claim that you only have knowledge when you are in action — applying information in a particular situation for a definite purpose. Knowledge is "a quality of an agent-in-action aiming to actually do something"; when the action stops, what remains is memory, which only becomes knowledge again when re-created in new action.

**Origin.** Matt Taylor and Rob Evans rebuilt this during the writing of *Collaboration Code* itself, partly because Matt felt the original Pyramid of Information model had degraded in the retelling and "important nuances got lost." The name signals the loop: you have to know in order to act intelligently, and you only know far more once you have experienced the consequences of the act.

**Components.**
- **Data** — the raw atoms of experience; facts and statistics for reference.
- **Information** — Data organized in a context, framework, or sequence that gives it meaning ("the difference that makes a difference," per Bateson).
- **Knowledge** — the application of Information in action for some purpose; "true" only insofar as it produces the intended result.
- **Wisdom** — discernment about what action is appropriate, including the meta-discernment to choose which models to use.
- **Memory** — the state of the entire system at any given moment (linked to the 22 Aspects of Memory). Memory is active and seeks patterns like itself; it is not mere storage.
- **Intuition** — apprehension of the gestalt across the brain/mind/body and the larger Field over eons.

**How to use it.** It diagnoses why an information-rich organization can still be knowledge-poor: nobody is acting on the information against a real purpose and feeding the results back. It justifies the disciplines that the rest of the language demands — keeping a notebook, documenting after action, comparing past beliefs to actual outcomes years later. It's also the philosophical brake against confirmation bias and against treating data, information, and knowledge as interchangeable. The Yuval Noah Harari epigraph ("Nothing should be taken for granted, even if everybody believes it") and Ira Progoff on intuition ("Man does indeed know intuitively more than he rationally understands") sit in the margin.

**Connects to.** Appropriate Response, Five Es of Education, Four-Step ReCreative Process, Ten-Step Knowledge Work Process, 22 Aspects of Memory, The Field (intuition), 246. Build StrongMemory.

### Appropriate Response

**The model.** A six-element filter that asks whether a candidate design or solution is fit to be released into the world. The first three elements are functional qualities of the artifact; the second three are capabilities of a living system, demanding that the solution behave like an organism in its environment.

**Origin.** Matt Taylor and Bryan Coffman (credited as effective co-author) developed the model in response to Matt's reflection on the post-WWII explosion in society's complexity and rate of change. He worried that organizations facing complexity develop "target fixation" — fixing the small thing they can fix while creating a whole new generation of problems (pulling the feathers off the bird without asking whether it should fly).

**Components.**
- **Efficacious** — has the inherent power or capacity to reliably and repeatably produce the desired effect (note the distinction from merely "effective," which only happens to produce it).
- **Proper Scope** — the power is contained within appropriate limits; the design is fit for purpose, not doing too much or too little.
- **True to Nature** — the parts support each other and are mutually requisite, like the roots, stem, and leaves of a seedling — no "impossible physics."
- **Anticipatory** — uses past experience and current data to predict the behavior of other systems and reach homeostasis with them.
- **Self-Correcting** — compares predictions with actual outcomes and adjusts.
- **Sustainable** — survives birth, grows to maturity without depleting the systems that support it.

**How to use it.** It is the filtering tool used in the Engineering stage of the Creative Process to test designs before one is chosen for implementation. Because the Creative Process is recursive and fractal, the model is used between every stage — most powerfully as an audit on the output of the Identity stage's environmental scan. A facilitator typically does not teach the model directly; she designs questions and tests that let the group encounter all six aspects on their own. The first three questions are easy and mostly linear; the second three force a living-systems perspective.

**Connects to.** Creative Process, Vantage Points, Rate of Change, Patterns 221 (Requisite variety of design), 222 (Recursion), 230 (Anticipatory Design), tool: What Has to Be True?

### Path of Learning: The Five Points of Mastery

**The model.** A lifelong-learning model in which every person rotates through five roles over the arc of a life, achieving mastery in each as appropriate. Learning is not a phase but a state, and the same person is constantly cycling among roles depending on the situation.

**Origin.** Gail Taylor created this with the Knowledge Work Consortium in the early 1990s while partnering on a proposal to the Clinton administration on re-creating U.S. education. The proposal lost, but the winning judges later told Gail that her group's work was "too advanced" — they used it as the rubric to judge the others. At a recent PwC workshop in Sydney the team used Velcro to attach the five-pointed star to their badges and rotated it to whichever role a question came from.

**Components.**
- **Learner** — explorer, innovator, model-builder, action-taker, receptive to ideas and guidance, curating an evolving learning network.
- **Facilitator-Guide** — frames others' experience, supplies concepts and challenges, removes blocks, co-owns the learning contract.
- **Sponsor-Advocate-Advisor** — provides feedback and boundaries, vouches for the learner, opens doors, ensures breadth and depth.
- **Expert** — develops a high degree of mastery in a specialized body of knowledge and applies it with a light touch (per Pattern 213).
- **Steward** — applies talents in service to community and world; holds vision; works in a cybernetic, whole-systems way; "all life is sacred" rather than "everything is a commodity."

**How to use it.** A facilitator-guide monitors others' role shifts and consciously re-positions a learner into the role appropriate to her current need. The roles cannot be co-mingled — you must be in one or the other for it to work, and the most effective educational plans are designed with this rotation in mind. The model rests on a long set of design assumptions: learning is interdependent, requires education *and* training (education means "to lead forward," training means "to draw or drag behind"), is optimized in trusting environments, and embeds responsibility for one's own learning and the learning of others.

**Connects to.** 2know2act2know, Five Es of Education, Right Livelihood, Patterns 212 (GroupGenius), 213 (Careful use of experts), 230 (Anticipatory Design), 327 (Give the gift of detachment).

### Cathedral Builders

**The model.** Less a process model than a piece of design poetry — a manifesto-essay that names the moral stance behind the entire System and Method. Its core claim is that one cannot make money; one makes a building, and in doing so makes value, which then allows money. To build is to reveal one's soul — to engage, act, touch, love. The closing question is "Why would you ever build anything less than a cathedral?"

**Origin.** Matt wrote the piece in 1992 or 1993 at the Sea Loft in Hilton Head while listening in on Gail's dialogue with the Knowledge Work Consortium, which was producing the Five Points of Mastery model. He was thinking about the medieval citizens who built cathedrals at the edge of their knowledge — inventing the flying buttress so light could enter — even while life was lived at the margins of survival. The question that drove the piece: how could we educate children to live their lives as works of art?

**Components.** This is a prose model rather than a glyph-based one. Its propositions:
- Uncommon results cannot be achieved by common means; nature does not allow it.
- Life must be lived, not managed.
- Trees become boards only when we have already given up on meaning.
- Value allows money, never the reverse.
- A cathedral is not ordinary and cannot be had by ordinary means.
- One must stand in bright light and be counted; one cannot hide in mists of mediocrity, safety, or normalcy.

**How to use it.** Cathedral Builders is the piece a facilitator reads at the start of an engagement to reset the stakes — to push a client beyond "reasonable limits" and into the aspiration that the work being attempted matters. It is also a gut-check for the KreW: are we educating the next generation for the end of the 20th century or for the 21st? It works as the moral overture before designing a wavespace whose ambition you do not yet know how to achieve.

**Connects to.** Five Points of Mastery (the model that prompted it), Right Livelihood, Cathedral as the controlling metaphor for Xanadu, the Patterns on holding a stand and creating a space for transformation.

### Stages of an Enterprise

**The model.** A life-cycle model for a commercial entity that is recursive across scales — it applies equally to a single human life, a relationship, a project, an idea in your mind, a sport team, or an entire society. At each stage there are developmental challenges that, when resolved, set up the conditions for the next. It is the model Matt says he uses and reflects on personally more than any other.

**Origin.** Gail and Matt developed it in their early consulting work after leaving the Acacia Group in Washington, D.C. They paired it with a Seven Domains audit as a diagnostic before almost every early DesignShop. The "graceful failure" stage was added after Amory Lovins shared the engineering concept with them in 1998–2001 during the two HyperCar DesignShops. Clayton Christensen's *The Innovator's Dilemma* is cited for the dynamics of the Maturity stage.

**Components.**
- **Conception** — born in scribbles on napkins, garage tinkering, persistent push from the rate of change. Most startups die here.
- **Looping** — peaks of hope and valleys of near-collapse; "pivots." This is where the environment delivers its most severe negative feedback. Graceful withdrawal here is honorable.
- **Success** — recognition and resources to be viable; metabolism sustainable. Not a time to coast.
- **Overshoot and Collapse** (the dotted line) — the "Hollywood Star" or "Dot-Com Bubble" syndrome. Recovery requires major redesign and usually a different management style.
- **Maturity** — longest, most stable, most profitable stage; also the most favorable time to spawn new enterprises.
- **Entrepreneurial Button** — the deliberate creation of a Zone of Emergence inside a mature enterprise to test and deploy a new business model.
- **Turnaround** — challenging previously successful models when external rate of change outpaces internal.
- **Graceful Failure** — designing for non-catastrophic end so resources are preserved for the next cycle.
- **Death** — sometimes the old form must die so new viable ones can take its place.

Two MGT axioms anchor the model: "You fail until you succeed" and "Nothing fails like success."

**How to use it.** As a diagnostic — large organizations contain parts in every stage simultaneously (General Motors has start-ups, dying units, and Hollywood-Star units all at once). The model is highly recursive, so it is used both to read the whole and to read each component. It is the framing document when a client is contemplating pushing the Entrepreneurial Button — either by spawning an unencumbered subsidiary or by attempting a regeneration of the parent. The biological intuition behind it is the surface-area-to-volume ratio: you never see a ten-pound amoeba because it would boil; nature has to invent backbones and heat-shedding systems to allow growth.

**Connects to.** Scan-Focus-Act, ValueWeb / Business of Enterprise, Zone of Emergence, Transforming Organization, Patterns 211 (Get the whole system in the room), 222 (Recursion), 223 (Iteration and rapid prototyping), 230 (Anticipatory Design).

### Four-Step ReCreative Process

**The model.** This model, in Matt Taylor's words, "is the essence of the modeling language" and "the most essential expression of what a completed work is and the moral obligation of why completion is a necessary act." Its central insight is that re-creation, not transmission, is what happens between every step of any creative process. The hand-off from one stage or one team to the next is not a transfer of a fixed artifact but a deliberate re-creation of the original vision, with feedback flowing back to the vision itself.

**Origin.** It arose from intimate experiences Matt had around 1982, after he and Gail had already produced most of the major models. He was trying to explain how teams with radically different backgrounds and competencies could collaborate on long projects without the quality slope crashing and without inter-team friction grinding the work down. He had spent thirty years inside conventional architecture watching exactly that happen.

**Components.**
- **Create Vision** — a mental concept of what you want to create, in the highest-variety environment in the system (the human mind, with ~10^14–10^15 neuronal connections).
- **Design Template** — a physical representation in words, symbols, pictures, or some other medium. Choosing a medium is itself part of the creative work; the medium pushes back.
- **Work** — bringing the vision into full material form. Variety increases enormously here, in physics' formal sense (W = F × d), as the realities of time, space, materials, and team dynamics enter.
- **Evaluate** — collect information on how the product performs in the world, in the user's hands.
- **ReCreate** (between every step) — the loop that re-imagines the vision in light of what each step has revealed. Documentation accompanies every cycle.

**How to use it.** It is invoked whenever a project will pass through multiple hands or multiple stages. The model legitimizes the changes that the next team will make ("don't change anything" is impossible with complex work), while protecting the integrity of the original vision through deliberate iteration against it. In a wavespace, the Four-Step is embedded in every module: KreW create a vision of what the module should accomplish, draft the assignment template, write it, take Sponsor Design Team feedback, re-create, and ship to the small group — who then re-create the assignment for themselves, agree on a template, work it, and report out. The model also fits inside Creative Process, Design Formation, Scan-Focus-Act, and Design-Build-Use as the engine that handles transitions between their stages. Stafford Beer's Law of Requisite Variety underwrites it — Beer's *Designing Freedom* is quoted directly: "Without a model, you cannot have a regulator. That's the point."

**Connects to.** Creative Process, Design Formation, Scan-Focus-Act, Design-Build-Use, Three Cat, Appropriate Response, Patterns 223 (Iteration and rapid prototyping), 244 (Don't argue. Test.), 246 (Build StrongMemory), 326 (Anything worth doing is worth doing badly).

### Design-Build-Use

**The model.** A correction to the conventional linear hand-off from architect to builder to occupant. It depicts an interactive, iterative game among Designer, Builder, and User in which no built environment is ever truly finished. The interconnecting feedback loops keep the building alive throughout its lifespan — loose enough not to inhibit each role, tight enough that design-and-build capability is always available when the user needs it.

**Origin.** Matt traces it to two roots: a 1958 visit to Mrs. Pew's Frank Lloyd Wright house (she had hated it at first, then grew to love it after realizing Wright had designed it for "the person that I could become" — Pattern 230, Anticipatory Design), and his apprenticeship with Wright himself, who was almost 92 and would still throw his coat down and demonstrate how to mix mortar. The model crystallized in the early Boulder years of MG Taylor, just after Scan-Focus-Act, sharpened by his General Motors stories — the leaking-Cadillac gasket Ross Perot couldn't get fixed, the two-dollar-a-car cheaper paint, the GM executive whose company-washed and company-serviced car had never had a service issue.

**Components.**
- **Design** — sketches, models, plans, schedules, budgets — continuous over the life of the structure, factoring in uncertainty.
- **Build** — rapid execution that stays close to the origin of the project; expects to adjust to real-world build-out conditions.
- **Use** — the user only understands a building by occupying it. Real processes always differ from initial projections; the external environment changes too. Users need ongoing access to design-and-build capability.

**How to use it.** Design-Build-Use is the architecture for collaborative environments themselves. Wavespace rooms are designed to be radically reconfigured in minutes — walls, workstations, and technology move so the same area can host clusters of teams one minute and a hundred-person theater the next. Inside a wavespace module, every output is itself designed to feed the next activity, so participants and KreW take turns being designer, builder, and user. The model also reframes the ValueWeb among architect, builder, and user — they share equity, debt, and cash flow because they share lifelong responsibility for the artifact. "Things that are 'finished' in a rapidly changing world are dead."

**Connects to.** Creative Process, Design Formation, Four-Step ReCreative Process, Scan-Focus-Act, ValueWeb / Business of Enterprise, Patterns 100 (The Physical Environment), 101 (A place to be fully alive), 230 (Anticipatory Design), 250 (Real-time RE-design).

### Scan-Focus-Act

**The model.** The macro-process model of any creative effort, and the visible spine of a typical wavespace. It identifies three distinct phases, each with its own requirements and success criteria, plus a continuous Feedback element. Its great strength — and its great trap — is intuitive appeal: facilitators absorb it so deeply they begin to use it unconsciously, which is exactly when its subtler dimensions are lost.

**Origin.** Frank Burns, a lieutenant colonel who headed Task Force Delta at the Army War College and is credited with the recruiting slogan "Be All That You Can Be," created Scan-Focus-Act inside Metasystems Design Group, the firm he founded with Linda Nelson after retiring. Burns and his colleague Jim Channon (author of the 1979 *First Earth Battalion Operations Manual*) introduced the model to MG Taylor at the Boulder Center during the very first DesignShops in 1979–1980. Channon also brought the Zone of Emergence concept and wall scribing. What MG Taylor added was operationalization: attaching the model to a three-day process and creating distinct modalities for each day. Channon famously argued the First Earth Battalion was better as a myth than as an actual unit — better as "a model, an image that could pervade the entire Army."

**Components.**
- **Scan** — etymologically "to climb or to mount." Build a vantage point from which to view the phenomenon; gather information broadly; build the conceptual mental models, language, and effective relationships for the work ahead.
- **Focus** — formulate and test alternatives. The conceptual models from Scan become tangible; the broad option set is pruned and the survivors are scrutinized rigorously until a decision is made.
- **Act** — work to build out the chosen solution as a viable system that can deliver an Appropriate Response to the conditions that prompted the wavespace.
- **Feedback** — present at every juncture; the result of any creation is fed back into the creating system as learning that reshapes it.

**How to use it.** The model has several practice subtleties that distinguish a journeyperson from a master:

- **Variable pace.** Scan may take an hour; Focus and Act might take weeks. Or it may invert. A card move is one cycle in seconds; a chess move is a cycle in an hour; a product is a cycle in years; some ideas take generations. The facilitator deliberately slows or accelerates the pace, sometimes insisting on more scanning when sponsors are eager to close — "go slow to go fast" (Pattern 225).
- **Fractal.** Each phase contains its own complete cycle. Even the simplest Scan involves an Act (calling the meeting was the Act of a prior cycle whose intent was to set time, date, location). A wildebeest scans, focuses, and acts in one bite — chewing while watching for the next morsel and the lurking lion. Every wavespace module is itself a fractal SFA cycle.
- **Nonlinear.** A real card play might run Scan-Focus-Feedback-Scan-Scan-Scan-Focus-Act. Skilled facilitation teams recognize when more scanning is needed even inside Focus or Act.
- **Six possible orderings.** The components can be sequenced six ways, each a strategy for a different situation. Act-Scan-Focus when agents must act before they can learn. Focus-Scan-Act when a model must filter the perception. "Ready, Fire, Aim" — Scan-Act-Focus — generates rapid alternatives, then folds the learning into a more strategic plan, with the risk of unintended consequences. The point is not to get the model in the "right" order but to use it to understand your process.
- **Transitions.** What triggers movement from one stage to another, or what blocks it, often matters more than the content of the stages. The facilitator and Sponsor Design Team prepare the energy, mood, and influential participants for each shift, and the facilitator must consciously make the shift in herself first.
- **Diagnostic.** The model can be used in after-action reviews to find holes or gaps in the planning and implementation process.
- **Design template.** Most wavespaces dedicate day 1 to Scan, day 2 to Focus, day 3 to Act — but each day still contains a complete fractal cycle. Many enterprises are uncomfortable in one stage (often Scan, dismissed as not "serious"); using the model as a template forces a proper Scan and disciplines the work.

**Connects to.** Appropriate Response (the criterion the facilitator constantly tests Act against), Four-Step ReCreative Process (the engine of transition between phases), Stages of an Enterprise, Design-Build-Use, Patterns 203 (Don't meet — MOBILIZE), 225 (Go slow to go fast), 226 (Answers from unlikely places), 261 (Make the solution act past Act Day), 310 (Shifting group energy).

### Solution Box

**The model.** A 7×7×7 three-dimensional matrix that combines the Creative Process, Vantage Points, and Design Formation models into 343 cells. Each cell is a distinct "location" in any creative endeavor with its own language, criteria for veracity, standard of completion, and appropriate utility. The Solution Box is an architecture of the path that an idea takes from concept to realization and use — it tells you where you are in any creative process. It is one of the most complex models in the language, and the chapter opens with a half-page warning to stop reading if you don't yet have a working command of the three constituent models.

**Origin.** Stewart Brand (founder of *The Whole Earth Catalog* and the Long Now Foundation) made an off-hand remark in the early 1980s that hit Matt hard: "People keep going to the problem box to find solutions when they need to go to the solution box to find solutions." As Matt and Gail were starting MG Taylor and designing a week-long workshop for individuals and families on planning their futures, they called the work "creating the solution box." It struck Matt that since Creative Process, Vantage Points, and Design Formation each had seven elements, combining them into a 7×7×7 matrix would yield a finely-grained map of the design journey — a high-frequency, structured space in which every step has a unique voice, history, viewpoint, and completion criteria.

**Components.** Three orthogonal axes:
- **Creative Process** (the most detailed view of bringing an idea into full realization) — Identity, Vision, Intent, Insight, Engineering, Building, Using.
- **Vantage Points** — the seven shells of organizational context, from Philosophy down to Task.
- **Design Formation** — borrowed from architects and builders, the documented forms an idea must take across a project: Program, Schematic Design, Design Development, Construction Documents, through Project Management.

Each of the 343 intersections defines what the work product must look like, what voice it speaks in, and what makes it complete enough to move on.

**How to use it.** Three primary uses. First, it gives a comprehensive map of a total creative process so you can locate your current work and know what language and veracity the location demands. Second, it lets you communicate your location through metadata — Matt customarily marks documents with glyphs from the three constituent models so the reader knows the document's degree of completion, voice, and intended use. Third, it diagnoses Solution Box mismatches between collaborators. The book gives two extended worked examples: a furniture-design hand-off where Matt is at Insight / Philosophy / Schematic Design but the client thinks Matt is at Engineering / Task / Design Development, leading to angry Yelp reviews; and a 20-minute new-client phone call where the client assumes she has given enough Insight for you to engineer a workshop at the Task level, while you are still at Identity / Culture / Program. The model also supports self-reflection — participants plot their relative strengths across the three component processes onto a Spider (or Rose Window) Diagram to surface their preferred styles and the stages they avoid.

**Connects to.** Creative Process, Vantage Points, Design Formation, Patterns 201 (It starts before it "starts"), 204 (NO makes a great beginning), 323 (Develop the capacity for awareness), tools: Sponsor Design Team Planning Session, Scoping and Scaling wavespaces, Rational/Emotional/Political Model.

### Right Livelihood

**The model.** A traditional Buddhist teaching, updated as a design model for the modern condition. Right Livelihood is the state in which an individual's work, play, lifestyle, interpersonal relationships, and physical environment are all in harmony with examined inner goals and values. Where the Buddha's eightfold-path teaching could rest on the simple test of not doing unnecessary harm, modern life requires the harmonization of three domains that easily fragment — Personal, Corporate, and Craft — and the conscious overlap among them, which the model names as Art.

**Origin.** Matt first sketched the Right Livelihood concept in his 1974–75 *ReDesigning the Future* course in Kansas City, then developed it in 1978 with Richard Goering of the Renascence Project newsletter. He didn't draw the physical model until 1981, when he and Gail used it in the "Constructing the Solution Box" workshops in Boulder. The model grew up alongside Rate of Change, Creative Process, and 2know2act2know. Matt's own background — twenty years in intentional, mission-driven communities (Air Force bases, private, military, and Jesuit schools) — fed the design assumption that disciplined community can be a positive forcing function, not an abusive one.

**Components.**
- **Personal** — the individual / self domain.
- **Corporate** — organized ventures that augment the individual; small enterprises through governments, the larger ValueWeb.
- **Craft** — serious craftsmanship, scholarship, dexterity, the learning of the schools.
- **And** — the conjunctive overlay; the field of constructive interactions among the three domains.
- **Art** — the greatest expression of human values and potential, taking three sub-forms: governance (Personal ∩ Corporate), personal living and inner development (Personal ∩ Craft), social and corporate organization and tooling (Craft ∩ Corporate).
- **Right Livelihood** — the state at the center, when all three domains are in harmony.

The model rests on six design assumptions: all life is sacred; a life or society can be designed (today it is mostly designed by default and badly); art is the greatest expression of human values; our society is buried in crisis and too human-centric; this path is unsustainable; and design — both individual and collaborative — is required for survival and prosperity.

**How to use it.** Right Livelihood is an aspiration, a destination, and a means. It is the philosophical anchor a transition manager returns to when a client is being asked to compromise — to make "unworthy and not fully understood design tradeoffs" that exchange a greater value for a lesser one in the hope of unearned advantage. It is also the practitioner's own check: am I in the BEing state of knowing/doing, or am I, in subtle ways, destroying my life and my home planet? Mary Oliver's instructions for living a life ("Pay attention. Be astonished. Tell about it.") and D. T. Suzuki's "Very funny religion!" sit in the margin, and Yuval Harari's *Sapiens* passage on the trap young graduates fall into is included as the cautionary tale.

**Connects to.** 2know2act2know, 22 Habits of Creative People, ValueWeb / Business of Enterprise, Levels of Competency, Path of Learning: Five Points of Mastery, Transition Manager's Creed, Patterns 101 (A place to be fully alive), 127 (There is no "out there"), 308 (Tell the truth), 320 (Mastering Facilitation), 321 (On becoming a great lover).

### The Transition Manager's Creed

**The model.** A model in the form of a written creed — a recitable code of conduct for the role MG Taylor calls the transition manager, the person who facilitates an organization, city, or country through the abyss between one paradigm and another. The creed names the role's specific duties, its required dual citizenship in old and new environments, and its highest-order ethical framework, anchored in fiduciary responsibility.

**Origin.** Matt wrote the creed after a six-week 1981 trial in which he and others were sued — twenty-two charges including breach of fiduciary duty — by the owner of a bed-and-breakfast he had helped find investors and operators for. Representing himself, Matt walked through the case law with the Superior Court judge and discovered that fiduciary duty is one of the few areas of law where one is presumed guilty unless one can prove having acted to the highest standard of care. He emerged believing that "if the law were fully enforced, 75 percent of all regulations would be unnecessary." Out of that came the recognition that wavespace work — where participants share private information across competing interests — carries a duty far beyond an NDA. Gail and Matt named the role transition manager, and in the early days Matt would read the creed aloud to the KreW before every event.

**Components.** The creed asserts:
- Transition management is a specific kind of management for specific historical moments.
- The transition manager combines strategic-planning and project-management vantage points and forges an environment in which a group's creative energies can function for mutual and planetary advancement.
- The transition manager's duties are specific; the ethical framework is of the highest order.
- The transition manager works for no agency alone but pledges allegiance to life, planet Earth, humankind, and the local community.
- The transition manager is responsible to life's quest for a higher order of being, manifested in specific accomplishments.
- Authority is sapiential, not derived from position or recognition.
- The transition manager must operate in two often-hostile environments — old and new — and stay free of entrapment by either; the correct vantage point is from both, with no commitment to a predetermined outcome.
- The transition manager must gain no undue advantage from the experience.

**How to use it.** It is recited at the start of an engagement. It is the document the KreW reviews before stepping into a high-vulnerability client situation. It is also the disciplinary standard a facilitator holds herself to throughout — particularly the prohibition on pre-committed outcomes, which protects the emergent quality of the wavespace and the client's autonomy.

**Connects to.** Right Livelihood, Patterns 125 (Fiduciary responsibility), 324 (Cultivate your self-transforming mind), 328 (Take a stand), tool: Sponsor Design Team Planning Session, This Approach to the Art of Facilitation.

### The Field

**The model.** The Field is the interconnecting cosmic energy at the root of reality that conserves and conveys information — the constant and enduring record of all that has happened and will happen, the state of the system not just at any given moment (per the 22 Aspects of Memory) but at all moments taken as one Eternal Now. The model points at the suggestion that the primary stuff of reality is consciousness, not matter, while acknowledging the relationship between the two is complicated. The Field reminds us of our connectedness to all things and of the folly of selfish, merely local solutions.

**Origin.** Matt's awareness developed across the 1950s and '60s as he read in older esoteric traditions and tracked the way quantum mechanics was challenging the materialistic paradigm. The breakthrough came through direct experience as a construction engineer running 800 people across two New York City buildings: when all was well the site was musical, in harmony; when there was friction he could hear the dissonance, follow his ear up to the floor where workers were arguing, and resolve the conflict by asking questions rather than yelling. Gail observed it as a teacher whose young students jumped several grades in one year because she created a space for them to follow their curiosity. Jean Houston is quoted on the early-twentieth-century convergence of two phenomena — the harvest of the world's spiritual traditions and the quantum revolution in physics — that opened the door to talking about the Field again.

**Components.** The Field is not decomposed into parts in the usual sense. The model instead identifies:
- The architecture of the Field is the same "inside" and "outside" simultaneously; the brain/mind/body is the receiver.
- Tuning practices: pay attention to your inner dialogue, notice what you are attending to, notice ideas that come "out of the blue," notice synchronicities, work with meditation or artistic discipline, set a vision for an outcome you do not yet know how to achieve.
- The Field is always there; "the wind of God's grace is always blowing, but you must raise your sail" (Vivekananda).
- A reading list as an enabling technology: Mortimer Adler & Charles van Doren, *How to Read a Book*; David Bohm, *Thought as a System*; Norman Friedman, *Bridging Science and Spirit*; Laszlo / Houston / Dossey, *What Is Consciousness?*; John Lilly, *Simulations of God*; Marvin Minsky, *The Society of Mind*; David Peat, *Infinite Potential*; Ira Progoff, *At a Journal Workshop*; Otto Scharmer & Peter Senge, *Theory U*; Michael Schneider, *A Beginner's Guide to Constructing the Universe*; Schwaller de Lubicz, *The Egyptian Miracle*; Evan Thompson, *Mind in Life*; Maryanne Wolf, *Reader Come Home*. Huston Smith's "Everything is a gift, but nothing is free" sits in the margin.

**How to use it.** The Field is the ground state behind GroupGenius, breakthrough sports performance, the moment a wavespace clicks. The model provides practical entry points: study yourself patiently as the complete system at the human level of recursion; collect physical books (not screens); use the Mosquito Strategy of high frequency and low magnitude (ten minutes a night beats waiting for the perfect window); read syntopically so the books act as agents in your inner dialogue; keep a journal blending mind and hand; engage deeply, then let it go and let the brain/mind/body sort the material that matters. The closing note: "Some rabbit holes are much more useful to you than others."

**Connects to.** 2know2act2know, 22 Aspects of Memory, 22 Habits of Creative People, Mosquito Strategy, Patterns 126 (Create a space for not knowing), 127 (There's no "out there"), 212 (GroupGenius), 246 (Build StrongMemory), 310 (Shifting group energy), 325 (Create a space for transformation), 329 (There's no transformation in the information).

### Models in Conversation as a Language

**The model.** The closing meta-model of the volume. Its claim is that the modeling language is exactly that — a meta-language for creativity and design across disciplines, not a fixed taxonomy. The models are to be understood individually but also as a system. When you put yourself in conversation with the models — and let them be in conversation with each other — you open possibilities for deeper understanding and more effective action than any single model can deliver. The example given is the worked dialogue between the Appropriate Response and Creative Process models earlier in the book.

**Origin.** This is a Spoze Dialogue written by Matt and Rob to close the *Models* volume. It exists because Matt and Rob's "greatest concern" is that practitioners will apply the models too superficially or dogmatically — which is how learning has to start (you must do exactly what the golf instructor says, or you have no chance of hitting the ball straight) but is a terrible place to stop. The aim of the entire *Collaboration Code* series is to capture the current state of the art so this generation can build on it.

**Components.** The model is delivered as a set of practitioner injunctions:
- **You cannot work solely within your mind.** The mind is a high-variety, high-media environment; paper is a severely restricted, lower-resolution medium. Copying brain-to-paper without re-creation produces a chalked-up sketch, not the concept. This is why the Four-Step ReCreative Process is so central — and why doing the work in a wavespace, which replicates the variety of the system being designed for, dramatically increases the chances of success.
- **Keep a notebook.** A separate physical notebook, not a computer file. Use this volume itself as adjunct — Rob laid out the white space deliberately to invite annotation. Date every entry. Add diagrams. Make the notebook a work of art, blending mind and hand. Notebooking is the entry point to the Four-Step.
- **Let your intuition invite models into the work.** Models are ways of chunking ideas you have tried and that you know work; in material form their images carry far more information than words.
- **Treat insight as a gift that has to be worked into form.** Creative people across art, craft, and science describe ideas arriving as flashes — they have to figure out what the gift means. Matt didn't draw Xanadu when the concept first came because he didn't yet know what it was for. The Elizabeth Gilbert TED-talk story about the poet Ruth Stone is included verbatim — a poem barreling across the Virginia landscape that Stone must run to catch on paper, sometimes catching it by the tail and pulling it back through her so it lands on the page intact but backwards, last word to first.
- **Engage! Engage! Engage!** Don't read mechanically, picking what you agree with. Read closely, in the Adler sense. Add your experience to the authors' experience to create a new experience. The point is not to absorb the modeling language but to put it in dialogue with your own language and become part of a global community of practice taking the work to its next level.

**How to use it.** This is the model a senior practitioner reaches for when teaching the others, and the model the reader uses to convert the volume from a reference book into a working partnership. It frames the right relationship to the rest of the language: faithful enough to learn the muscle memory, irreverent enough to keep re-creating it.

**Connects to.** 22 Aspects of Memory, 22 Habits of Creative People, Creative Process, Four-Step ReCreative Process, The Art of Making, Patterns 121 (Free space to discover the truth), 246 (Build StrongMemory), 307 (Make the models useful), 322 (The more you learn, the more you learn), tool: How to Read a Book in an Hour.
