# Sponsor Design Team (SDT) playbook

The SDT is the small group that co-designs the workshop with you. It is the most leveraged investment in any multi-day event. From the wavespace tradition.

## Why an SDT exists

Reading from Pattern 205 (*Sponsor Design Team as microcosm*): the SDT is the workshop in miniature. If you can design a working session for the SDT first, you can design one for the full room.

Beyond the design value, the SDT serves three functions during the event:

1. **Trusted co-pilots** — they read the room with you and help you adjust mid-stream
2. **Distributed authority** — sponsor signal is in the room without the sponsor being in the chair
3. **Continuity** — they own the work after the event ends; they are the living memory

A workshop without an SDT is a workshop run by a facilitator alone — possible at half-day scale, fragile at multi-day scale.

## Who's on the SDT

Composition varies; the constants:

- **Senior sponsor** (or their authorized proxy) — the executive whose signature is on the engagement
- **Operational owner** — the person who will live with the outcomes
- **2–3 cross-functional representatives** — bridges to the constituencies in the room
- **Lead facilitator** (you) — co-designer, not service provider

For Slalom Composable DXP work, a typical client-side SDT is the executive sponsor's chief of staff plus two functional leads from the constituencies that will be most affected. Slalom-side: lead facilitator (Bermon or designate) plus engagement lead.

Size: 5–8 people. Smaller than 5 lacks redundancy; larger than 8 stops being a working team.

## SDT cadence

### T-3 weeks: SDT identification and kickoff
- Sponsor names the SDT
- 60-minute SDT kickoff meeting:
  - Why we're meeting (the workshop's purpose)
  - SDT working agreements (cadence, decision rules, confidentiality)
  - Initial scoping conversation
  - First read of the participant list

### T-2 weeks: First design meeting
- 90 minutes
- Walk the agenda v1 you've drafted
- Identify the riskiest design choices and discuss alternatives
- Refine outcomes
- Identify gaps in pre-work

### T-1 week: Pre-work brief sent
- Pre-work brief signed by sponsor (not you) — Pattern 201 (*It starts before it "starts"*)
- 30-minute SDT check-in: confirm logistics, surface anything that's shifted

### T-3 days: Design walk
- 60-minute SDT meeting
- Walk the final agenda; rehearse openings
- Identify potential friction points and how each SDT member will help

### T-1 day: Prep day circle-up
- 60–90 minutes, in person if possible
- The night-before walk-through
- Each SDT member states what they'll be watching for
- Logistical final check
- Closing ritual: "we're ready"

### Each morning of multi-day events: Morning circle-up
- 30 minutes before participants arrive
- Read the previous day's signals
- Adjust today's agenda if needed (Pattern 250 *Real-time re-design*)
- Reconfirm roles for the day

### Each evening of multi-day events: Evening circle-up
- 60 minutes after participants leave
- What worked today; what didn't
- What needs to change for tomorrow
- Sleep-on-it design (Pattern 229 *Sleep on it twice!*)

### T+1: Post-event SDT debrief
- 60 minutes
- What did the workshop produce?
- What's the SDT's role in carrying it forward?
- What did we learn for next time?

## SDT working agreements

Establish in the kickoff. These compound across the engagement:

1. **Co-design, not approval.** SDT members bring ideas, not just thumbs-up.
2. **One conversation, one truth.** What's said in SDT stays in SDT until publicly aligned.
3. **Surface friction, don't smooth it.** If something feels wrong about the design, name it now.
4. **Show up to every meeting.** SDT continuity is the value.
5. **The lead facilitator owns the design call.** SDT advises; the facilitator integrates.

## SDT roles during the event

Each member typically takes a role:

- **Greeter** — owns the entry experience; sets tone before the formal opening
- **Energy reader** — watches the room's pulse, signals to facilitator
- **Stakeholder bridge** — sits with their constituency; helps them lean in
- **Time/logistics** — owns breaks, food, room reset
- **Documentarian** — captures decisions and artifacts during the session
- **Sponsor proxy** — speaks for the sponsor's authority when needed

You assign these in the prep day circle-up.

## SDT failure modes

- **Sponsor delegates to a junior who has no authority.** Push back; if sponsor is not in the SDT or properly delegated, the SDT has no air. Pattern 205 fails.
- **SDT becomes an audience.** Watch for "we're just here to learn." The SDT must work; if it can't, replace members.
- **SDT cancels meetings.** First sign of a sponsor relationship in trouble. Re-contract or escalate.
- **Lead facilitator over-delegates to SDT.** The design call is yours. SDT advises; you decide.

## Slalom context

For Slalom engagements:

- The Slalom-side SDT is usually the engagement lead, the senior architect, and the practice lead (or designate).
- The client-side SDT is the executive sponsor's chief of staff plus 2–3 functional leads.
- For pursuit-stage workshops, the SDT is the win team plus the executive sponsor.
- For internal practice events (quarterly planning, off-sites), the SDT is the practice leadership team.

The cadence above is calibrated to high-stakes (multi-day, contracted-outcome) events. For half-day workshops, compress to a single design meeting + a 30-minute prep call.
