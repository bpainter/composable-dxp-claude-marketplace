## 100 — The Physical Environment

### 101 — A place to be fully alive

**The pattern.** Collaborative environments must be intellectually stimulating, sensorially rich, and beautiful — designed for people to come fully alive at work, not merely to be functionally efficient.

**Why it matters.** Buckminster Fuller's axiom — "Environment is stronger than willpower" — sets the frame: where you think shapes how and what you think. Most workplaces are "prairie dog towns of cubicles" optimized by accountants who measure occupancy per square foot but cannot assess effectiveness. We humans evolved in environments that were alive, dangerous, and engaging; controlled low-stimulus offices make us shut down. Oliver Sacks wrote in the NYT that, in 40 years of medical practice, only two non-pharmaceutical "therapies" proved vital for chronic neurological patients: music and gardens. Matt Taylor's design for UniCredit's management center in Turin runs a self-circulating stream through a Renaissance building — an extreme expression of the principle that biophilia is essential, not optional.

**When to use.** Whenever you are designing or refreshing a wavespace, fixed center, or mobile pop-up — and especially when budget pressure tempts you to treat plants, art, music, or beauty as nice-to-haves.

**The move.** Stock the room with intriguing books and a Knowledge Wall; bring stuffed animals, toys, games. Treat plants as a line item — large greenery to anchor, smaller flowers to delight; on remote setups, point cameras at plants on flat screens. Curate music that leads the group between energetic states. Use the "bread and roses" test: does the space lift the spirit?

**Related.** Patterns 104, 105, 122, 228, 230; Tools Creating a Proper Collaborative Environment, Twelve Laws of Collaborative Design Spaces; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Brain/Mind/Body, Seven Domains.

### 102 — Room for the whole system

**The pattern.** Build spaces large enough to gather representatives of the entire system in one place at one time to work together — not as audience, but as a parallel collaborating whole.

**Why it matters.** Ordinary offices reflect hierarchy and the division of labor: floor space gets quantified and assigned to subunits, communication channels track architecture, and people end up clustered with others most like them in level and function. The only large-group spaces tend to be "sit and get" auditoriums; the only cross-functional contact happens around the coffee machine. The result is intellectual and emotional separation into tribes whose viewpoints are inevitably partial and parochial. Wavespace environments deliberately scale up almost infinitely so the whole system can engage with itself, with breakouts continuous with plenary so the boundary stays porous.

**When to use.** Any wicked problem that crosses functions or levels and demands more than a task force or sequential meetings — when "getting the whole system in the room" is the unlock.

**The move.** Plan ~100 square feet (≈9 sq m) per participant — clever floor plans can squeeze in more, but resist the urge. Use rolling WorkWalls or other flexible subdivisions so plenary and breakout share a continuous footprint. Equip for remote inclusion via video and even roaming telepresence robots so distant colleagues are physically represented.

**Related.** Patterns 103, 211, 213; Tools Creating a Proper Collaborative Environment; Models ValueWeb / Business of Enterprise.

### 103 — Form and function as one

**The pattern.** Collaboration spaces should not be rabbit warrens of single-purpose rooms or cool-looking "innovation" theatres — form and function should join, in Frank Lloyd Wright's phrase, "in a spiritual union."

**Why it matters.** Companies that invest in collaboration venues often hire a designer who doesn't understand process, producing small isolated rooms with mismatched, uncomfortable, ostentatiously "creative" furniture. The more explicitly creative the aim, the more ridiculous the furniture. Wright (Matt Taylor's teacher) celebrated organic, fluid form/function relationships; the MG Taylor DesignShop and navCenter brought that principle into collaboration. Stewart Brand's How Buildings Learn reinforces the point: spaces evolve through use, so don't fully outfit until you've worked in the space.

**When to use.** When commissioning, retrofitting, or evaluating a fixed collaboration center or mobile environment — and any time the temptation arises to lock in a fancy permanent fit-out before you've used the space.

**The move.** Start with a large open footprint, unobstructed by columns. Standard kit: an entry/transition area; large rolling WorkWalls for sub-dividing; comfortable stackable chairs; video monitors; a Knowledge Wall; movable KreW workstations; a full, often-visible production area; and humanizing elements (plants, books, toys, games, music, art). Refrain from fully outfitting until patterns of use emerge.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 104; Tools Creating a Proper Collaborative Environment; Models Design-Build-Use.

### 104 — Everything speaks

**The pattern.** Every detail of the environment communicates — align all of it to the work, and remove anything that subtly undermines it. "Everything speaks. Listen."

**Why it matters.** Humans evolved to be exquisitely context-sensitive; our lizard brain is constantly asking "is that food, or am I food?" Rob's first collaborative experience (Tampa, Q4 1995, EY) hit him precisely because the environment had embedded instructions for how to work — chair configurations made it obvious that you were going to think big, write and erase, share to hypertiles. The Disney parable underscores it: visible sweepers near the entrance signal "we care about cleanliness" and invite reciprocity; no signs telling people what not to do — just make the right thing easy; no gum sold (because gum is the hardest thing to clean up). Disordered space spawns disordered thinking.

**When to use.** Continuously, before/during/after every session — and as a personal check on your own home or office.

**The move.** Use "Ninja" KnowledgeWorkers to silently refresh breakouts during plenary. Match chair arrangements to the work (no tables unless the table has an explicit purpose). Have the *exact* number of chairs — empty chairs subliminally ask "Who's missing?" Pens face the same way in the marker tray; all pens work. Refresh the Radiant Room every time the group is in breakout. Walk the space with a newcomer's eyes.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 122, 128, 246, 247, 248; Tools Executive Summary, Symbolic Action; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Net Vector, Vantage Points, Zone of Emergence.

### 105 — Fresh reusability

**The pattern.** Tools, toys, room layouts, and assignments are inherently reusable — but you have to fight the autopilot and make every session feel custom-made. Mechanization, said Frank Lloyd Wright, best serves mediocrity.

**Why it matters.** Books go out of date (especially on tech and social trends); toys break; Knowledge Wall graphics linger; KreWs cut and paste old assignments. Each client must feel the road was made for them, even when they're converging on solutions common to many organizations. Both things — common roads to unique solutions and unique roads to common ones — operate simultaneously. Participants take unconscious cues from the KreW: when you're fresh and on the edge, they will be too; when you're going through the motions, they get the message.

**When to use.** Every time the same environment hosts back-to-back sessions, every Act Day morning, and any time the team has fallen into "we always do it this way."

**The move.** Schedule "fresh eyes audits" — walk entryway to production area with beginner's mind. Move walls, furniture, books, toys, graphics, displays. Set creative constraints: one new module per session; five minutes before Opening, challenge scribes to work an unusual phrase or image in; ban five facilitator words chosen by the KreW one minute before start. Identify a session theme with the SDT and weave it into space, scribing, assignments. Use serendipity — accidents, misunderstandings — creatively.

**Related.** Patterns 104, 107, 323, 331; Tools wavespace Opening, Synthesis Conversation; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Four-Step ReCreative Process.

### 106 — It matters what you take away

**The pattern.** A great environment, like a great product, often succeeds by what it takes away — strip out everything that does not serve collaborative work. Per Saint-Exupéry: "Perfection is achieved when there is nothing left to take away."

**Why it matters.** The modern office's tools and habits are toxic to sustained dialogue. One EY partner described organizational life as "being nibbled to death by ducks." Multitasking is a myth — "you're not multitasking, you're ignoring me," as one client SVP put it; drivers who multitask are roughly as impaired as drunk drivers. Behavioral scientists (per Cal Newport's Deep Work and Gloria Mark's research) show even short interruptions disproportionately delay completion. Sony's Walkman and the Apple iPod won by what they didn't do — and so do wavespace environments.

**When to use.** When designing the room, the rules of engagement, and the participant agreements — especially around devices, agendas, breaks, and dress.

**The move.** Remove desks/tables in plenary; jackets and ties; last names and titles on badges (first names large, no affiliations); overhead "death ray" projectors; offices/breakout rooms with doors; public minute-by-minute agendas; pre-scheduled breaks (informal break ~every 75 min); the crisis du jour (have participants set away-messages). Replace with open WorkWall-based space, a Radiant Room, mobile tech, and a trained KreW. For participant devices, lean on education, agreements, insight, and recognition of success.

**Related.** Patterns 103, 122, 124; Tools Creating a Proper Facilitation Environment, Plus/Delta; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, 22 Aspects of Memory, Brain/Mind/Body.

### 107 — Entry space of transition

**The pattern.** Build a transition space at the entrance — with rituals of welcome — so participants can shed "street behavior" and arrive in a different kind of place. "This is different, and the difference starts now."

**Why it matters.** Christopher Alexander, in Pattern 112 of A Pattern Language ("Entrance Transition"), argues that without a meaningful threshold, "the inside of the building fails to be an inner sanctum" — people cannot drop street behavior unless something destroys its momentum. Moments of arrival or leaving "swell" relative to surrounding minutes; the space must swell to match. Aim for positive cognitive shock: "We are definitely not in Kansas anymore, Toto." Hotels and ballrooms make this *more* important, not less, because a registration table alone just signals "another one of those workshops."

**When to use.** Every session opening, especially in temporary venues like ballrooms — and as the very first designed module of the day.

**The move.** Stand up a Graphic Welcome Wall with the client's name and theme. Carve out a discrete Transactions of Joining space (badge, briefcase drop, photo, Facebook Page template). Set a Breakfast Café bordered by a Knowledge Wall, books, plants. Have the Radiant Room visibly ready before the first client walks in — no last-minute bustling. Then quiet yourself, watch participants arrive, and let what you see shape the Opening.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 104, 106, 122; Tools Creating a Proper Facilitation Environment, wavespace Opening, Facebook Pages; Models Being the There, Seven Domains, The Transition Manager's Creed, Zone of Emergence.

### 108 — Knowledge-rich environment

**The pattern.** Engineer serendipity by surrounding the group with a wide, frequently-refreshed assortment of books, articles, visuals, and knowledge objects that invite unexpected connections.

**Why it matters.** Most workplaces are knowledge deserts — hot-desking, "decoration" by process maps and work plans, retreat into headphones. But humans evolved to recognize patterns across divergent inputs and delight in new insights. Horace Walpole coined "serendipity" in 1754 from the Persian fable "The Three Princes of Serendip," whose protagonists made discoveries "by accident and sagacity." Steven Johnson (Where Good Ideas Come From) makes the case directly: "The best innovation labs are always a bit contaminated" — noise-free environments become sterile and predictable.

**When to use.** When stocking and curating any collaboration space, and as a continuous practice during a session.

**The move.** Build a library that goes beyond business — history, biology, philosophy, psychology, sociology — and weed it like a garden, moving titles around mid-session. Stand up a Knowledge Wall pre-planned with the SDT, then add articles in real time as new topics arise, and populate it with photos of participants. Use kiosks and electronic media for awe-inducing visuals. Bring living plants and flowers. Choose music that demonstrably listens to and comments on the dialogue. Run modules like Authors, Metaphors, Syntopical Reading, TED Talks, Terms of Art.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 104, 226, 229, 241; Tools SDT Planning Session, KnowledgeWorker Roles, Metaphors, Multi-Faith Reflections, Syntopical Reading, TED Talks, Terms of Art, Walk in the Park; Models 22 Aspects of Memory, 22 Habits of Creative People, Brain/Mind/Body.

### 109 — It takes a center to create a center

**The pattern.** Capabilities are learned only from people who already have them. You cannot stand up a management center from a manual — it takes immersion in an existing one.

**Why it matters.** Michael Polanyi coined "tacit knowledge" in 1958 — the unwritten storehouse acquired through joint activity, "the bulk of what one knows." Richard Davidson (On Being, June 2011) cites feral children who never develop normal language without a linguistic community; compassion, he says, is taught not by explaining but "simply to be it." Alan Robinson's village softball team in England had read all the books — and was still flummoxed when the Air Force outfield shifted for a left-handed batter. Intel's "Copy Exactly!" method, profiled in HBR's "Getting It Right the Second Time," prevented engineers from selectively copying parts of what worked. The EY ASE in Chicago was midwifed at Hilton Head's knOwhere store with Matt and Gail Taylor and rotating veteran KnowledgeWorkers — only after Chicago matured could it train other centers.

**When to use.** Whenever you launch a new center or KreW, recruit a new facilitator, or are tempted to "rationalize" the process before you've fully practiced it.

**The move.** Apprentice new practitioners at an existing center for at least a month before opening. Send experienced KnowledgeWorkers to a new site to set the habits. Forbid "improvements" until newcomers can perform at the level of those teaching them. Apply this at the wavespace level too — bring in a master Process Facilitator (Svenja Ruger is the named exemplar) to coach a green KreW.

**Related.** Patterns 103, 104, 246, 325; Tools Creating a Proper Collaborative Environment, Twelve Laws of Collaborative Design Spaces; Models 2know2act2know, 22 Habits of Creative People, Design Formation, Four-Step ReCreative Process, Levels of Competency, Seven Domains, Vantage Points.

### 110 — Space, Place, and Grace

**The pattern.** Turn the space you are given into a Place — rooted, safe, beautiful — and hold it with intention until Grace (or, if you prefer, emergence) appears. Carolyn Buck Luce's Auburn Seminary vision: "Holding the Space, Place, and Grace with intention for tough conversations and warm relationships until a future that we can all trust emerges."

**Why it matters.** Our nervous systems still scan environments the way our savannah ancestors did — Kahneman's System 1, always on, asking "is this safe? is this in order?" Think of the harried huddle-room handover and how badly your nervous system is set up to do good work. Wright's "organic architecture" — buildings of the hill, not on it — is the foundation. The pattern crystallized through conversation with Carolyn after she'd been in Auburn's design space for the Nobel Women's Initiative gathering; Rob had just worked with William Ury and ten people from a country in conflict in an Airbnb that Jac Howard converted into a design environment. An office and a private home, both transformed into Places. Grace, in this usage, is the "spirit of bravery, humility, awe, and trust that emerges" when conditions are right.

**When to use.** Any environment you inherit and wish to make worthy of difficult work — a conference room, a hotel ballroom, a borrowed home.

**The move.** Don't accept the space as you find it. Use natural elements — light, plants, water — and let it look "of a piece." Bring a carefully chosen group, not a random one, and ease them into connection (alignment is an outcome of process, not its precondition). Hold radical hospitality. Then allow room for Grace.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 109, 131, 231, 325; Tools Creating a Proper Collaborative Environment, Twelve Laws of Collaborative Design Spaces; Models The Art of Making, Cathedral Builders, Net Vector, Seven Domains, Zone of Emergence.

## 120 — The Energetic Environment

### 121 — Free space to discover the truth

**The pattern.** Create a "time out of time" where the normal rules of hierarchy and function are temporarily suspended, so people can look at the whole system honestly and discover the truth together.

**Why it matters.** To be in any sizable organization is to be in a box: hierarchy, function, role. F.W. Taylor's scientific management drew a sharp line between thinkers and doers — "you do your job and let me do the thinking" — and though the ideology has fallen out of favor, it lingers structurally and has been re-energized by reengineering and right-sizing that engineer slack out of work. Even the cliché of "outside the box" survives because some part of us knows we are constrained by psychological and organizational structures. Holacracy promises liberation but is, by its own description, "more structured than conventional management." A free space is the antidote.

**When to use.** Any wavespace addressing strategy, organizational change, culture, or any topic where rank and function would normally distort the conversation.

**The move.** Strip status signals: first-name-prominent badges with no titles or affiliations; jeans-allowed dress; relieve people of briefcases and laptops; mix functions and levels on teams (but not someone with their boss's boss). Tell people to "leave your rank at the door" knowing they remember exactly where they parked it. Begin with a holistic Scan phase. Set the openness tone with the SDT in advance; offer them "plausible deniability" so ideas can be tested on merit. "You may not always get your way, but you will definitely have a chance to have your say." Use the Twelve Laws of Collaborative Design Spaces to keep the parent organization out.

**Related.** Patterns 126, 227; Tools SDT Planning Session, Twelve Laws of Collaborative Design Spaces; Models Creative Process, Transforming Organizations, ValueWeb / Business of Enterprise.

### 122 — Energetic container

**The pattern.** The KreW creates an energetic field — of intention, focus, and being-on-purpose — that acts as a containment vessel, channeling group energy in productive directions much like a reactor pressure vessel channels nuclear reaction.

**Why it matters.** Drucker's "culture eats strategy for breakfast" plus Newton's First Law: organizational energy has inertia, and groups carry their cultures with them and re-create them in new surroundings. If left alone, they'd reproduce their normal energy in your design session. Christian Huygens discovered the law of entrainment in 1665: two nearby rhythms will lock into synchrony. When the KreW's energy is tightly coherent, participants will, over time, match it. Even small punctures of a reactor pressure vessel — a stray phone, a missing person — can be serious.

**When to use.** Throughout every wavespace, and especially on Day 3 (Act Day) when the Synthesis Conversation peaks the focused energy.

**The move.** Get the KreW into energetic alignment first — team dinners, breakfasts, Circle Ups, morning rituals, agreed work roles, shared humor. Take the role of welcoming hosts (when you host, participants become guests, and "my house, my rules" lowers their resistance to the unusual ground rules). Hold the boundaries: no visitors, everyone stays for the entire session, minimize Weapons of Mass Distraction. On Act Day, seat participants in a circle inside WorkWalls; position the KreW alongside the walls to add their concentration to the field.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 104, 123, 203, 243, 310; Tools wavespace Opening, SDT Planning Session; Models 22 Aspects of Memory, Brain/Mind/Body, The Field.

### 123 — Mirror neurons

**The pattern.** Participants entering an unfamiliar wavespace unconsciously search for cues about how to behave — the KreW and facilitator must model the behaviors they want, because participants' own neurology will pick them up.

**Why it matters.** Italian scientists in the early 1990s identified mirror neurons in the primate premotor cortex — neurons that fire both when an animal acts and when it observes the same action in another. Maryanne Wolf (Reader, Come Home) notes that even reading about Anna Karenina jumping in front of the train activates motor neurons in your legs and trunk. A.N. Meltzoff calls human infants *homo imitans*. MIT's Alex Pentland writes in Social Physics that mimicry, even unconscious, increases empathy and trust — "negotiations with lots of mimicry tend to be more successful." The science may be overhyped, but the human capacity for empathy and pacing is unquestioned, and people are even more open to taking cues when they're in novel or uncomfortable situations — which is exactly the wavespace entry experience.

**When to use.** From the moment the first participant enters — and as the explicit reason to bring the SDT into the space on Prep Day so they too can model.

**The move.** Run a tight, visibly cooperative KreW that participants observe out of the corners of their eyes — focused on task, quick to help. Use the facilitator as the front-of-room model (but remember Dan Newman's line: "the facilitator is just the decoy; most of the important work happens in the back of the room"). Make explicit agreements with the SDT about modeling expected behavior. Trust that the new behaviors will set in within the first opening window.

**Related.** Patterns 122, 127, 305, 330; Tools KnowledgeWorker Roles, Facebook Pages; Models 22 Aspects of Memory, Brain/Mind/Body, The Field.

### 124 — Time and concentration

**The pattern.** Block out the outside world for several days and concentrate the right people on the matter at hand — the alternative is "an hour a week, forever."

**Why it matters.** Modern executives have little undistracted time; their days are carved into smaller and smaller bits, either by others' calendar invites or by their own multitasking. Three days seems long — until you compare it to months of meetings, revisions, and missed alignment, all interrupted by the crisis du jour. Cal Newport's Deep Work documents the cost; Tom DeMarco's Slack documents what gets engineered out when efficiency is the only god. Clients consistently underestimate both how much time the work needs and how much acceleration concentrated time delivers.

**When to use.** Whenever scoping a wavespace — and any time a sponsor proposes to compress the agenda or allow late arrivals, early departures, or executive fly-bys.

**The move.** No agendas distributed (keeps the group on outcomes, lets the KreW retime in real time). Everyone arrives at the start and stays till the end — high-status people playing by special rules disrupt the most. No visitors, no tourists. No executive fly-bys (a brief drop-in misreads any single module; brief executives in a separate session a day or two later). Create distance from the office; if the venue is on-site, get explicit no-disturbance agreements. Minimize phone and computer use — there is no effective multitasking, only ignoring.

**Related.** Patterns 106, 203; Tools Scoping and Scaling wavespace experiences, SDT Planning Session; Models 22 Aspects of Creative People, Net Vector, Rate of Change, Zone of Emergence.

### 125 — Fiduciary responsibility

**The pattern.** The facilitation team owes a fiduciary duty to *all* participants — not just to the client paying the bills. We are not "Have Gun — Will Travel"; we are "Have solutions — will travel."

**Why it matters.** Clients sometimes think they're hiring a hired gun to deliver a predetermined outcome, and facilitators get confused too. The wavespace environment is psychologically powerful: charming, disarming, asking participants to take risks and be vulnerable. If those tools serve only the check-writer, the result is a rigged game. Courts impose fiduciary duties on lawyers, doctors, clergy, directors — but not on facilitators, so we impose it on ourselves. Rob's contrast: the EY/Capgemini ASE documented that every dollar a client spent generated $5 of follow-on consulting within three months — *not* because of pressure (pressure tactics were expressly forbidden) but because integrity built trust. Echoing Hippocrates and Elie Wiesel's "we must always take sides — neutrality helps the oppressor."

**When to use.** Any engagement with a paying client — and especially when a consultancy hires you to facilitate their client.

**The move.** Sign a written contract clarifying mutual obligations, scope, cancellation. Then accept ethical obligations beyond the contract: fully engaged but neutral; no special advantage from the relationship; no manipulation of the process toward a precooked outcome. Honor the agreed givens (budget, scope, success criteria) but don't engineer outcomes. Forbid up-selling of next-tier workshops or your own follow-on services from the room. Let value drive the next sale.

**Related.** Patterns 211, 213, 231, 303; Tools This Approach to the Art of Facilitation, Twelve Laws of Collaborative Design Spaces, SDT Planning Session; Models Business of Enterprise, The Transition Manager's Creed.

### 126 — Create a space for not knowing

**The pattern.** Collaborative sessions are zones of emergence where *not knowing* is a precondition for innovation — actively prevent the rush to a premature solution and hold the space for something better.

**Why it matters.** Modern management style is "often in error, but never in doubt" (Acheson on the Supreme Court). Subordinates suffer for not knowing things they have no way of knowing; consultants are paid to know. A Fortune 500 CEO survey once found their biggest fear was that someone would discover they didn't know what they were doing. Participants drag this in: they will do anything to avoid appearing not to know. Keats coined "negative capability" for artists who can sit in mystery and doubt; the more mundane cognate is *ambiguity tolerance*, key in entrepreneurs. MG Taylor axiom: "Discovering you don't know something is the first step to knowing it."

**When to use.** From the SDT planning session through Scan and Focus phases — and any time the group lunges toward a too-easy answer.

**The move.** Introduce design thinking, "failing fast," and "rapid iteration" as evidence we don't yet know the answer. Ask the SDT for hypotheses but offer plausible deniability so ideas can be tested on merit. Frame Scan-Focus-Act so participants understand we will not be rushing. Return to the theme at the end of Scan Day (much done, almost nothing concluded) and reassure the SDT in Focus prep that Act will deliver decisions. Model focused patience as a KreW. Then — equally important — when the group lands a fit-for-purpose solution, declare the space *closed* and help them celebrate; humans love and need closure.

**Related.** Patterns 121, 127, 263, 329; Tools Spoze, Synthesis Conversation, What Has to Be True?; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Black Box, Creative Process, Spoze, Three Cat.

### 127 — There is no "out there"

**The pattern.** When the KreW is in deep rapport with the group, the team's inner experience mirrors the group's. Use what's happening in the KreW to diagnose — and treat — what's happening in the group.

**Why it matters.** Rob discovered this as a psychotherapist and clinical director of substance-abuse programs: he'd be with an upbeat client and notice he himself was feeling depressed for no personal reason — opening dialogue toward those feelings would help the client express them. He learned hypnotherapy and NLP techniques for building rapport and used them in his first collaborative experience in 1995. The TomTom GPS line captures it: "You are not stuck in traffic. You ARE traffic." When the group is dysfunctional, the KreW is dysfunctional in roughly the same ways; when key subgroups feel distrustful, KnowledgeWorkers start holding back from each other; when the group is stuck, the KreW is stuck. In hypnosis it's called pacing and leading.

**When to use.** During every Circle Up — especially end-of-day — and any time the KreW is mysteriously off.

**The move.** Use the Opening to develop rapport: hold eye contact briefly with each person; envision throwing a "soft sheet of connective energy" over the group to the back wall. Once paced, you can lead — raising your own energy to lift a tired group, slowing yourself to slow a rushing group. In Circle Ups, ask the KreW to identify quirks or weirdness in how *they* are working — that surfaces both KreW improvements and clues about what the client group needs. Get the KreW unstuck and the group often follows uncannily.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 122, 123, 303, 304; Tools KnowledgeWorker Roles; Models Brain/Mind/Body, The Field.

### 128 — Symbolic action

**The pattern.** Design symbolic actions that invite participants to *embody* their shift into a different game — myths and symbols have a gravitational pull on reality.

**Why it matters.** Deal and Kennedy's Corporate Cultures lists the elements that determine culture: business environment, values, heroes, rites and rituals, cultural network. Barry Schwartz: "We want work that is meaningful — that makes a difference to other people and thus ennobles us." Gallup finds ~90% of workers not engaged or actively disengaged; when work changes, the symbols must change too or they become a drag. The set-piece anecdote is "Burying the Hatchet at AEDC" — Arnold Engineering Development Complex, after a brutal 1989 Christmas strike. At the end of Scan Day, a union boilermaker and a young general manager exchanged "we'll never do that again" / "then we will never strike again" — Matt said the room temperature dropped sixty degrees. Overnight, the KreW built a casket with a velvet-lined hatchet; next morning a hooded Death figure entered to "Phantom of the Opera," each person signed a "Death of the 1989 Strike" certificate, a Jesuit-clad KnowledgeWorker preached the eulogy, a "widow" wept, every participant hammered a nail, pall bearers from both sides processed across the base behind a hearse, the casket was buried, and everyone sang "Amazing Grace." The tombstone is still there; for fifteen years people gathered on the anniversary.

**When to use.** When a group needs to symbolically close the past, mark a transition, or commit to a future they have not yet fully chosen.

**The move.** Don't discuss or explain the action before or after — let it land. Don't rush; gravitas requires time. Production values matter enormously. Tell the KreW the result you want and leave the design to them (Matt's AEDC instruction — they worked nearly all night). Use well-known cultural symbols. Pair entry symbols (Welcome Wall, badges, music, Knowledge Wall) with mid-session set pieces (the eVerest 12-foot mountain, Tibetan prayer flags, candlelight when the lights went out) and Rituals of Closure.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 104, 107, 123, 263, 329; Tools Rational/Emotional/Political Model, Symbolic Action, Commitment Statement / Closing Ritual; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, 2know2act2know.

### 129 — Protect the team

**The pattern.** Create a team environment that respects and empowers KnowledgeWorkers — and actively protect them from disrespect. The work depends on it.

**Why it matters.** The KnowledgeWorker role doesn't exist in most organizations, so participants — especially consultants steeped in their firm's hierarchy — see KreW as "go-fers," or as one Ivy League junior faculty member sniffed, "We call them staff." The most public KreW tasks (clearing dishes, helping a stalled team) look menial; the more complex work happens behind the scenes. Many KreW have more event experience than the facilitator. Most firms that tried to copy the EY/Capgemini ASE failed, primarily because they staffed the back of the room with junior consultants whom they treated as staff. The Heinlein epigraph — "Specialization is for insects" — frames the alternative.

**When to use.** Always — but escalate dramatically when a participant crosses the line.

**The move.** Don't try to "manage" KnowledgeWorkers — use the Seven Domains model to give them context, then trust them to step up and self-manage. As facilitator (highest-status to clients), model the tone of respect you want others to give. Refuse to use them in menial, non-value-adding ways. When assault on the KreW environment happens — *step up*. The legendary moment: ASE facilitator Chip Saltsman at PollyEsthers in Chicago, when a drunk client decided his next tequila shot would have its salt licked from a female KnowledgeWorker's neck — Chip got in his face and bellowed: "*Don't! Lick! The! KNOWLEDGE WORKERS!!*" The offender left immediately and faced serious discipline. Chip = our hero.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 140, 303, 308, 328; Tools wavespace Opening, KnowledgeWorker Roles, Commitment Statement / Closing Ritual; Models Path of Learning: The Five Points of Mastery, Right Livelihood, Seven Domains, The Transition Manager's Creed.

### 130 — More than a soundtrack

**The pattern.** Use music as *information* — to inform and direct participants, deepen their experience, and build StrongMemory. Never as mere wallpaper, never by default.

**Why it matters.** Music is, with scent, the most direct route into emotion and memory; it has been weaponized in elevators, stores, restaurants, and films, and Rob has watched music facilitation degrade in workshops as new generations lose touch with how (and why) to use it. Two scenes from his first collaborative experience in 1995 fixed the standard: after Matt showed the seven-step Creative Process model, the team sent everyone into breakouts to Dave Brubeck's "Unsquare Dance" — written in 7/8 time. ("Somebody is really paying attention back there!") And after partners debated whether to launch the ASE — "If the firm is not smart enough to do this, we should just leave and do it on our own" — KnowledgeWorker Patsy Martel sent them out to Warren Zevon's "Lawyers, Guns and Money." Perfect. Dr. Georgi Lozanov's Accelerated Learning research (baroque at largo) underwrites the use of "wallpaper music" during breakouts.

**When to use.** During KreW working sessions, SDT Prep Day, participant arrival and breakfast, working breakouts (wallpaper music), transitions to/from the Radiant Room, end of each day, and end of session.

**The move.** Match music to the demographics of the group — meet them at their musical home before going eclectic. Take requests from Day 2 onward. Think through tone, tempo, energy *and* lyrics (Rob nearly dove across five rows when "Freeway of Love" — pink Cadillac — opened a Honda Motor Company session). Two iron rules: never kill a song mid-stream — always fade. Invest in the best sound system you can afford (Ryan Romsey's Imaginal Labs kit uses studio monitors). Use it to build StrongMemory — conscious, unconscious, and somatic.

### 131 — Stepping up

**The pattern.** Allow KreW members to choose their own work areas and invite participants to volunteer for breakout tasks — engagement is intrinsic. "Stepping up is just what we do."

**Why it matters.** Gallup's 2018 employee-engagement poll celebrated 34% engaged as the highest ever — meaning ~66% were not engaged or actively disengaged. Going through the motions feels lousy and translates into worse productivity, retention, customer satisfaction, and safety. Rob's ODI background trained him in Herzberg's distinction: extrinsic rewards work up to a point (and lavish rewards for naturally enjoyable tasks actually *reduce* later performance — the Lepper/Greene/Nisbett "overjustification" finding). The motivators that stick are intrinsic. Gail Taylor's elementary school students taught her this when she invited them to identify their own questions. Drucker: "Accept the fact that we have to treat almost anybody as a volunteer." MG Taylor KnowledgeWorkers were free agents responding to a "bird call," cohering into GroupGenius around a project, then disbanding.

**When to use.** Every KreW Circle Up before a session, and every Act Day handoff to participants.

**The move.** During the first Circle Up, the PF lists needed roles on a WorkWall and invites the KreW to sign up for what they volunteer to play. Stretch repertoires session over session ("Who wants to step up to figure this out?"). Frame participant facilitation and scribing of their own breakouts as stepping up. On Act Day, invite participants to "vote with their feet" toward the topic that most calls them.

### 132 — Brave space

**The pattern.** You must do your utmost to create a safe space — and you will fail. So commit instead to a *brave* space. As the tagline has it: "It's not safe to play it safe."

**Why it matters.** Amy Edmondson's The Fearless Organization shows that psychological safety is the underpinning of group performance — Google's Aristotle research (Julia Rozovsky, who is Carolyn's niece) confirmed it as the most important of five team dynamics. But "safe space" has become a cultural battleground, with trigger-warning advocates and free-speech defenders (Lukianoff & Haidt, "The Coddling of the American Mind") talking past each other. Trauma is real and ubiquitous: van der Kolk's The Body Keeps the Score lays out the work of recovery; Robert Gass's "trauma with a small t" reminds us that almost everyone carries embedded painful memories. In any decent-sized group, somebody is being triggered by something. Upset is inevitable.

**When to use.** Especially in diversity and inclusion work, social-justice convenings, multi-faith gatherings, and any session touching deep beliefs, identity, or institutional violence — but the principle scales down to ordinary sessions too.

**The move.** Keep doing the foundational work — warm hospitality, participant agreements in the Opening, ground rules to stop ad hominem attacks. Then read aloud Micky Scottbey Jones's "Invitation to Brave Space" (written for the Faith Matters Network in Nashville and shared at Auburn after a 15-year-old was killed by police in Dallas before the G4J training): "Together we will create brave space / Because there is no such thing as a 'safe space.' / We exist in the real world / We all carry scars and we have all caused wounds…" Attribute it. Pair humility with honesty; cultivate humus — soil that can be cultivated.

**Related.** Patterns 121, 122, 127, 216, 224, 306, 323, 325; Tools wavespace Opening, Quaker Conversation; Models 22 Aspects of Memory, Brain/Mind/Body.

### 133 — Practice what you sell

**The pattern.** This System and Method is more than what we sell; it's also how we run our own businesses. Personal and organizational congruence is the foundation of trust.

**Why it matters.** Edgar Schein's three levels of culture — artifacts, espoused values, underlying assumptions — make it easy to spot the gap between what an organization preaches and does. Wells Fargo's 37-page "Vision and Values" brochure used "trust" 24 times and exhorted "watch what they do" — while thousands of employees were defrauding customers by opening accounts to hit cross-selling targets. At the personal level, the same thing: a friend says "yes, that's a good idea" while shaking his head and avoiding eye contact. Ultra-high-speed photography of two people in dialogue reveals a constant nonverbal dance; you cannot help but communicate when your body and words disagree. The early EY ASE learned this the hard way — a one-day "dog and pony show" left partners thinking the ASE was a place where you do dog-and-pony shows; only when they brought real EY problems did the message land.

**When to use.** As an ongoing audit of your own practice — solo, team, or firm — and especially when bringing new clients in via a Discovery Day.

**The move.** Apply the *22 Habits of Creative People* (love the work; find a teacher; organize your workplace; let passion drive; execute with control; learn widely; follow intuition; focus during incubation; combine high-energy work with re-creation; document; rule out failure; use feedback skeptically; develop a reset button; hands-on production; challenge convention; rapid iteration; create a language for your field; observe ritual; invest everything; message broadly; serve a higher purpose; create community). Run a Seven Domains Audit, paying as much attention to connections between domains as to each domain. Use the Ten-Step Knowledge Work Process for distributed work. Sell via Discovery Day — "use the process to sell the process (but don't 'sell')." Get a trusted colleague to score you straight — everyone is a hero in their own mind.

## 200 — Preparation

### 201 — It starts before it "starts"

**The pattern.** Eighty percent of success may be showing up, but the real leverage in a wavespace is in four kinds of preparation done long before the room fills.

**Why it matters.** Sun Tzu's "every battle is won before it ever starts" is the framing. Evans has watched rookie consulting teams burn enormous client money preparing things that never get used — overpacking for the wrong vacation. The four areas that actually matter, in increasing order of importance, are Context, Content, Collaborators, and Curiosity. Context spans three nested rings: internal (use the Rational/Emotional/Political model, visit clients on their home ground, "breathe their air"), external (the ValueWeb of producers, customers, investors, and social/political stakeholders, citing Kotter and Sisodia on stakeholder-balanced firms), and global (an infected bat in a Wuhan wet market shut down the planet — there are no externalities anymore). Content must be in a form usable for collaborative work; predigested PowerPoint decks violate Requisite Variety. Collaborators' wavespace begins the moment they're invited — never send a detailed agenda (Woody Hayes: "three things can happen when you throw the ball, and two of them are bad"). Curiosity is the antidote to defensiveness; planting open questions induces "unconscious search."

**When to use.** Every wavespace, from the first sponsor conversation forward — especially when an inexperienced consulting engagement team is preparing materials.

**The move.** Visit the client site in person; map stakeholders with the SDT using ValueWeb; build a Fact Book rather than a slide deck; replace the agenda with overall aim plus a pre-work assignment (Carolyn Buck Luce's story of partners scrambling on the plane to read the books they each got in the mail); plant questions and walk away.

### 202 — Prepare for success

**The pattern.** Shift the client's focus from "will the event succeed?" to "will the implementation succeed?" — the wavespace itself is just the start.

**Why it matters.** First-time clients fixate on the logistics and political risk of the event, but a wavespace is only validated by what gets implemented afterward — and the popular statistic is that 85 percent of change efforts fail. Evans's own line ("People support what they help create") is the load-bearing principle. The wavespace process is engineered for implementation success: the SDT becomes Kotter's "guiding coalition"; the wavespace itself extends that alignment to a much larger group; the Scan instills contextual understanding so the solution is requisite with its environment; Focus surfaces what the group has chosen *not* to do (which speeds execution); Act ratifies plans through Symbolic Action.

**When to use.** Especially with first-time clients and high-stakes, change-heavy engagements where anxiety about the event itself is crowding out attention to the "problem after next."

**The move.** Frame implementation as the "problem after next" from the start; vet the participant list specifically for people with change-management and communications responsibilities; commit the Action Plan as the real deal (only lightly vetted afterwards by SDT on Post Day); refuse to let business leaders "invent" change-management plans (get specialists); build mid-course correction points into the plan; reconnect with the client on a regular cadence after the wavespace to nourish their drive.

### 203 — Don't meet — MOBILIZE!

**The pattern.** Don't design a meeting; design a mobilization — assemble and marshal the group's resources for action and decisive results.

**Why it matters.** Dave Barry's quip — that the one word for why humanity will never reach its potential is "meetings" — is the launch pad. Most organizational meetings are one-way "sit and get" sessions powered by the "death ray machine" (PowerPoint), and as one EY partner ruefully put it, "the purpose of meetings is to have more meetings." The dictionary definition of mobilize matters here: to put assets into circulation, to assemble for war duty, to marshal resources for action. Five strategies create the conditions for mobilization: (1) co-create the story of a clear and worthy goal — "people support what they help create," and the leader should "start with why" (Sinek); (2) engage with the full complexity of the issue using Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety rather than artificially simple problem framing; (3) explore the full range of options through an open, transparent emergent process that elicits Roger Martin's "integrative thinking"; (4) decide by design, not by default — no voting, instead emergence into GroupGenius; (5) plan to act now, not plan to plan.

**When to use.** Whenever decisive action is required — when resources must be aligned and the whole point is delivering results, not exchanging information.

**The move.** Co-create (don't deliver) the story of the goal with sponsors; refuse the "no-fly zones" sponsors want to draw around tough topics; structure honest feedback opportunities; eliminate voting in favor of decision-by-design; produce real plans on the day, not commitments to plan later.

**Related.** Patterns 202 Prepare for success, 261 Make the solution last past Act Day; Tools Action Plan, Mobilization: Moving a Group to Action, Scan-Focus-Act, Templates, This Approach to the Art of Facilitation; Models 2know2act2know, Feedback of a Complex Kind, Four-Step ReCreative Process, Scan-Focus-Act.

### 205 — Sponsor Design Team as microcosm

**The pattern.** The SDT is a living scale model of the larger system — how they show up with each other forecasts how the whole group will behave at the wavespace.

**Why it matters.** The Rational/Emotional/Political model frames the work: rational elements (objectives, business case, processes) are easy to discuss; emotional and political ones are not. A good SDT Planning Session uses questions like "what has made it hard to tackle this in the past?", "where are people likely to have trouble talking to each other?", "have efforts like this failed before — why?", "where will the future state feel threatening?" to surface the hidden material. But the deeper insight is that the SDT's behavior *is* the diagnostic — if the SDT can't give you proper planning time, expect distraction at the workshop; if they interrupt and put each other down, expect the same; if they avoid the elephants in the room, prepare to drag the elephants out yourself. Direct conflicts between SDT members translate into difficulties for their reports — why would those reports cooperate when it's politically unsafe? Carolyn Buck Luce's line: the SDT should be your "lead steers, but not your bulls." The "gatekeeper problem" — risk-averse intermediaries who claim to speak for the boss but rarely do — must be managed by gently insisting on direct contact with senior leadership.

**When to use.** Every engagement where political dynamics are non-trivial — which is most of them.

**The move.** Cast a wide net for the SDT, deliberately including known opponents; run a Take-A-Panel exercise with the SDT to surface differences indirectly; serve adult beverages halfway through the Scan Day Evening Circle Up; use the SDT as the place to rehearse the conflicts that will erupt at the larger session; "earn your big facilitator bucks" by transforming SDT interactions, since when part of any system moves the whole system moves.

### 206 — Creature comforts

**The pattern.** To run collaborative workshops is to be in the hospitality business — and participants will complain about logistics long before they complain about your design.

**Why it matters.** Frederick Herzberg's 1968 motivation-hygiene theory is the underlying frame: motivators (achievement, intrinsic interest, responsibility) drive satisfaction; hygiene factors (working conditions, supervision, environment) only prevent dissatisfaction. Getting hygiene right doesn't guarantee success, but failing it digs a hole. A highly engaged group can have low hygiene and high motivation — they're motivated *and* full of complaints. As Twain put it, "the mind can only absorb what the seat can endure." A shabby hotel, three pasta meals in a row, no afternoon snacks, or the fourth complaint about a cold room will sour an otherwise great session. Process Facilitator Robin Brooking apparently maintains a "deeply disturbing" photo collection of hideous hotel carpets they've worked on. Hotel thermostats, by the way, are often there for "decorative purposes only" — engineers have personally confirmed this to Evans.

**When to use.** Every session, in-person or virtual.

**The move.** Specify your full requirements (lighting, ceiling height >=10ft, 100 sq.ft./person, sound, seating, dietary needs) to client meeting planners up front; scout the space in person or via photos before signing; set the room to 68F and warn participants to bring a layer; befriend the venue support staff; for virtual sessions, send amenity kits (good chocolate, screen wipes, pipe cleaners, a personal whiteboard) and schedule "Have Lunch Together" or At-Home Happy Hours.

### 207 — It's not a cost; it's an investment

**The pattern.** Sell on value, not price — clients aren't buying three-day meetings, they're buying breakthrough solutions, acceleration, and alignment.

**Why it matters.** EY's first three-day collaborative experiences in 1995 ran upwards of $250,000 plus expenses — a ridiculous price for a meeting, but a fair one for solutions to mission-critical problems that would have taken three to six months otherwise. Evans tells the Kellogg story: their president walked up mid-Focus Day and asked when the next one could be — they ended up doing more than half a dozen, and Carlos Gutierrez so distinguished himself in those sessions that he was named CEO and later went on to be U.S. Secretary of Commerce. Edgar Schein's Humble Inquiry is the operating manual: "getting questions right was more important than giving recommendations." Ken Martin's line: "consider time a variable, not a constant; if time is the constant, you reduce your objectives to fit it."

**When to use.** Discovery Day and SDT Planning Session conversations with new sponsors, especially senior executives who are used to vendor pitches.

**The move.** Do real homework on the company *and* the person (annual report, what they've said in the press); meet alone, no PowerPoint or brochure; sketch the Rational/Emotional/Political model on a whiteboard to open the "tricky bits"; prepare short "What We Founds" but be ready to dump them; act as a trusted advisor from minute one by finding something to disagree with; document a 10X ROI (and reframe as ROE — return on engagement, or ROA — return on attention); anchor with a large standard fee; make a small promise within the meeting and keep it; never unbundle costs when negotiating price down; if you must lower the price, omit elements instead.

## 210 — Participants

### 211 — Get the whole system in the room

**The pattern.** Bring all the key stakeholders — often the entire system — into one well-designed collaborative process, instead of running parallel small-group efforts that cycle endlessly.

**Why it matters.** Most large-group assemblies are PowerPoint fests where everyone is in "sit and get" and the breakouts ask "what did you think of that last presentation?" — there's no way to pool ideas. The alternative — working subgroups in isolation — feels like painting a bridge: by the time you finish, you have to start over because each group's "improvements" need re-vetting by every other group. The MG Taylor axiom: "Every person in this room already possesses an answer. The purpose of this intensive interaction is to stimulate one, several, or all of us to remember and extract what we already know." Clayton Christensen: great new ideas emerge "at the intersection of functions or people that have never met before." When SDTs balk at inviting customers ("we don't want them to see how screwed up we are"), Evans's experience is (1) customers already know in greater detail than the leaders, and (2) they're often willing to help because it's in their self-interest.

**When to use.** Complex, multi-stakeholder problems where knowledge is distributed across geographies, functions, and disciplines that lack formal relationships.

**The move.** Start the inclusion process with the SDT itself (3-8 people, gender-balanced, including healthy skeptics, capped to keep evening Circle Ups manageable); use SDT organizational knowledge to identify everyone affected by, contributing to, deciding on, or implementing the solution; explicitly include outsiders — customers, suppliers, regulators, recently-departed competitors, free thinkers; bring guests in for just Chat Rooms or Panels if they can't stay; exploit virtual sessions to multiply far-flung participation and unbundle the rigid 2-3-4 day format with more soak time.

### 212 — GroupGenius

**The pattern.** Design the process so diverse voices — especially the ones without "recognized credentials" — are heard, and a higher-order collective intelligence emerges.

**Why it matters.** Knowledge is widely distributed, but typical group dynamics suppress it. Two archetypes do the most damage: the "loud dummy" whose combative certainty shuts everyone down, and the "quiet smarty" whose insight needs the right social conditions to surface at all. Cass Sunstein's catalogue of failure modes is invoked: planning fallacy, availability bias, optimism bias, self-serving bias, informational pressure, reputational pressure, common-view bias — without careful attention, "groups, including firms, become unified, confident, and wrong." Gail Taylor's definition of GroupGenius: the ability of a group working iteratively and collaboratively to seek, model, and put in place higher-order solutions, with time compression, flow state, dynamic feedback, and individual + collective creativity at the core. Surowiecki's four characteristics of wise crowds (diversity, independence, decentralization, aggregation), MIT's "Factor C" (collective intelligence — driven by social perception, evenness of participation, and number of women on the team), and Amy Edmondson's psychological safety (Google's "Project Aristotle" finding) all converge.

**When to use.** Any session where the answer is genuinely distributed among the participants and groupthink is a real risk.

**The move.** Use Take-A-Panel, Balanced Brainstorming, and Multi-Voting to enable simultaneous rather than sequential contribution ("get people to pay much less attention to what everyone else is saying"); run parallel teams on similar or identical assignments; deploy Red Team Future State Scenarios and friendly competitions; use ThinkTank-style polling tools; in your opening, model and hold the boundaries that make psychological safety real.

### 213 — Careful use of experts

**The pattern.** Experts are useful as long as no one — including the experts — believes they have a corner on the truth.

**Why it matters.** Phil Tetlock's research at Penn showed that political and economic experts' predictions were terrible — less accurate than a crude algorithm extrapolating recent trends — and the only factor that worsened performance was *more* media exposure. Stewart Emery: "the phenomenon of punditry is antithetical to the spirit of Yankee ingenuity." The Heaths in *Decisive* lower the bar to "an expert is simply someone who has more experience than you do." Niels Bohr is quoted: "an expert is a person who has made all the mistakes that can be made in a very narrow field." Sunstein distinguishes three kinds of expertise: scientific (data-backed), experiential cue-based (apprenticeship-trained pattern recognition), and experiential case-based (mastery of thousands of examples) — each with different reliability. Consulting subject-matter experts present a particular risk: they slip into sales mode and lose credibility with their own clients. George Bernard Shaw: "every profession is a conspiracy against the laity."

**When to use.** Any wavespace where outside experts or internal SMEs will speak — which is most of them.

**The move.** Bring in more than one expert, ideally from different fields (Nate Silver's "poll of polls"); brief experts that they're collaborative co-designers, not "possessors of the answer"; have them do their own Take-A-Panel to surface their views and absorb others'; encourage them to discuss countervailing positions and the typical blind spots of groups they advise; remind them practical stories beat theoretical lecturettes; help them accept the final decision is not theirs; for the group, design Chat Rooms and Panels so participants can judge expert opinions for themselves; design real-world tests (a Gladwell-style "Blink" answer is not validation).

### 214 — Send them two-by-two (at least)

**The pattern.** Never let any stakeholder group be represented by a single person — invite at least two from every constituency.

**Why it matters.** SDTs commonly try a false economy: one person per group to keep numbers down. Carolyn Buck Luce calls this "being one hundred percent half right." Three reasons matter. (1) Diversity of representation: there's no human group with uniform views, so a single delegate truncates the actual range — and delegate selection may itself be political. The stakes rise sharply for underrepresented groups; Konrad and Kramer's Harvard Business Review research found that even on corporate boards it takes *three* women directors before group dynamics shift to be "much more conversational and less hierarchical." (2) Parallel processing: when 50 participants split into 7 teams of 7-8 to explore Future State Scenarios simultaneously, you can't put your one Asia-region person or your one HR rep into seven teams at once. (3) Mutual support afterwards: participants must go home and sell the outcomes; two voices working the same pitch are far more effective, and "yes, that did really happen" is easier to defend with a second witness. Cited: Mark 6:7 — "he called the twelve and began to send them out two by two."

**When to use.** Earliest SDT planning meetings, when invitee lists are forming and people are still on calendars.

**The move.** Surface the question in week-one SDT meetings; give participants 4-6 weeks of lead time to commit; map planned breakout topics against required representation so you have enough of each "kind" to cover concurrent teams; make doubling-up a core SDT job, not an afterthought.

### 215 — The right sizes for breakout groups

**The pattern.** For in-person breakouts: seven plus or minus two. For ad hoc small-group dives: three or four (not two, not five). For online breakouts: five or six.

**Why it matters.** As a "trained and licensed consultant," Evans's first answer is "it depends" — but experience gives concrete numbers. The "magical number seven" comes from George Miller's 1956 paper on short-term memory limits — which, Evans admits, has nothing to do with breakout team size, but the title is sticky and the number happens to work. A team of seven plus or minus two can work together 45 minutes to several hours, gets diverse views, lets everyone contribute, and fits comfortably around the standard hotel eight-foot round table. The "three or four" number comes from John Kotter, who introduced "Kotter Groups" while running EY partner leadership training in the Cambridge ASE — he'd say "three or four... not two... not five" with a twinkle in his eye until it became a partner running joke. Kotter Groups are what Evans calls "the best facilitator trick in the book": when a plenary hits a difficult, emotionally charged issue, breaking into small groups gives the facilitator space to figure out what to do next. Bezos's "two pizza rule" is referenced; Yogi Berra: "okay you guys — pair up in threes." The 5-6 online number emerged post-pandemic from the entire practitioner community's mad re-creation of the work over Zoom and tools like Mural and Miro, with a dedicated facilitator/recorder built in.

**When to use.** Whenever you're sizing a breakout — or buying yourself thinking time mid-plenary.

**The move.** Default to 7±2 for substantive design work; deploy Kotter Groups (3-4) for fast emotional defusion; size online breakouts at 5-6 with a designated facilitator; use PatchWorks Architecture to scale total participation past traditional limits.

**Related.** Patterns 211 Get the whole system in the room, 242 Sapiential leadership; Tools Kotter Groups, PatchWorks Architecture; Models Zone of Emergence.

### 216 — Diversity and inclusion

**The pattern.** Demographic diversity isn't enough — actively recruit for it *and* build a "speak-up culture" so the diverse voices in the room actually shape the outcome.

**Why it matters.** Most organizations are far more monochromatic than the world; one pharmaceutical client estimated, by their own internal math, that at their current rate it would take *70 years* to reach gender balance in senior ranks. The Center for Talent Innovation (co-founded by Carolyn Buck Luce, now Coqual) distinguished two forms in their 2013 research: *inherent diversity* (gender, race, age, background, disability, nationality) and *acquired diversity* (cultural fluency, generational savvy, gender smarts, military experience, language skills). The benefits to innovation and growth don't kick in until teams show *at least three* elements of each kind. Sylvia Hewlett's data: in non-2D-diverse organizations, 40% of team members see groupthink as a problem (vs. 25% elsewhere), 62% of leaders don't see value in ideas they don't personally need (vs. 37%), and 48% of new ideas rarely reach market (vs. 26%). One pharma exec quoted: "it is hard for members of the dominant culture to recognize what's missing or who's missing." Carolyn Buck Luce: "beware the cluelessness of privilege." An African American pharmaceutical executive: "when I walk into a room, my job is to make everyone else comfortable with the fact that I'm Black. Nobody else has that job."

**When to use.** Every session — but especially when an SDT defaults to "the usual suspects."

**The move.** Educate the SDT on diversity-as-innovation-driver as soon as the invitee list is on the table; track diversity characteristics alongside org affiliation as the list forms; refuse token representation — ensure no one is the "only one of their kind" in a breakout (echoing Pattern 214); explicitly remind native English speakers to make space for non-native speakers in inclusive-dialogue prep.

**Related.** Patterns 205 Sponsor Design Team as microcosm, 211 Get the whole system in the room, 212 GroupGenius, 214 Send them two-by-two (at least), 242 Sapiential leadership, 330 Masculine and feminine in harmony; Tools SDT Planning Session; Models The Field, The Transition Manager's Creed.

### 217 — Virtual participation

**The pattern.** Virtual participants can engage across the full range of workshop activities, but only if you handle People, Process, and Technology with discipline.

**Why it matters.** Cost and hassle of travel were already pushing executives toward telepresence — Evans recounts healthcare execs at one session falling for the Double Robot (an iPad on a mini-Segway base) so hard they bought their own and FedExed them to meetings. Then Covid-19 sent everyone "to their rooms" and virtual became the only game in town, now permanent. The People dimension: vPAX (virtual participants) need the same focus as inPAX (in-person), so phone-only is banned and cameras stay on; remote participants need training in Mural, Miro, Google Docs; targeted participation (Panels, Chat Rooms, Reports) often works better than full-day attendance. Process: schedule modules with virtual participants tightly so they don't sit waiting; in breakouts the facilitator must build relationships, direct conversation actively, describe what's on the WorkWall for the unsighted, and add 10-20% time for tech overhead. Technology: use the simplest tech that works; bandwidth thrice; have a dedicated tech person each for video, virtual whiteboard, and participant rescue; "good video is mostly good audio" (Ryan Romsey); cool isn't enough. Case study: in January 2012, EY imposed a travel ban midway through planning a trans-Atlantic Life Sciences session, so Evans led from Jersey City and Matt Taylor flew to London, using flat-screen TVs with Apple TV boxes and iPads/iPod Touches over Skype; they "played the time float" handing work back and forth across time zones. When the U.K. audio failed at the final report-out, Evans rubber-banded his iPhone to a microphone in what they called "The Great MicKludge."

**When to use.** Any session with remote participants, hybrid format, or budget/travel constraints — i.e., almost everything now.

**The move.** Prefer video to audio-only ("auditory-only conference calls are just meetings without the donuts"); train participants on collaboration tools before the session; schedule virtual modules with specificity; over-provision bandwidth and tech support; rehearse for failure (Finagle's Corollary); design hybrid audio carefully (multi-channel headphones for inPAX is one experiment underway).

**Related.** Patterns 211 Get the whole system in the room, 213 Careful use of experts, 301 Preparation is the antidote to fear; Tools KnowledgeWorker Roles; Models 22 Habits of Creative People.

### 218 — People Science

**The pattern.** Use People Analytics ("Big Data for People") to identify the right participants and the right team configurations, instead of relying entirely on the SDT's existing connections.

**Why it matters.** A World Economic Forum study found that in an average industry-specific gathering of 100 people, only *four* can meaningfully help you. SDTs are limited by who they already know. Evans points to a precedent from the early ASE days: librarian-trained researchers would scour databases live during a session, and when a participant mentioned a company in a report-out the researcher would have an article posted on the Knowledge Wall by the time the group left the Radiant Room. That same real-time intelligence access is now in everyone's smart phone, and Big Data scientists have moved well past it. Sandy Pentland is quoted: "the intelligence of individuals doesn't matter. The content doesn't matter. The pattern of how we share with each other is all that matters... the flow of ideas between people makes all the difference." With People Science (PS), options run from simple pre-session questionnaires to mapping participants' email and social media traffic to surface real influencers, knowledge sharers, "political backstabbers," and people about to leave the company — information not even the SDT possesses. Evans flags that this same data is presumably available about you too — "welcome to the brave new world of Big Data!"

**When to use.** When invitee selection is critical and the SDT's network is too limited to identify the right talent — increasingly, anywhere across a large organization or global NGO.

**The move.** Start small with structured pre-session questionnaires; partner with People Science specialists (Brandon Klein and others) for organization-wide data approaches; use algorithmic team formation to break out of habitual political groupings; treat this pattern as "more promise than demonstrated performance" — initial trials with major companies and NGOs have been impressive but the field is early.

**Related.** Patterns 211 Get the whole system in the room, 212 GroupGenius, 221 Requisite variety of design; Tools KnowledgeWorker Roles; Models ValueWeb/Business of Enterprise. (Resources include Brandon Klein's peoplescience.info, Valdis Krebs of OrgNet, Jeremy Howard at Singularity University, and Alex Pentland's *Social Physics*.)

## 220 — Workshop Design

### 221 — Requisite variety of design

**The pattern.** A well-designed collaborative process must be at least as varied and complex as the problem it is meant to solve — counter to nearly every "normal managerial" instinct.

**Why it matters.** Evans frames the pattern through a side-by-side table of eight explicit reversals between the "Normal Managerial Approach" and the "wavespace Alternative." Where managers want a clear solution before convening, small groups, urgency, linear progress, and serious focus, the wavespace tests preconceptions, gets the whole system in the room, allows iteration, embraces "going slow to go fast," and treats humor and fun as engagement fuel. The pattern surfaces "no-fly zones" — issues too dangerous to raise — and asserts "most secrets in organizations are really open secrets." Decades of experience, Evans claims, show these methods are "faster, less expensive, and exponentially more effective" than conventional management.

**When to use.** When sponsors arrive with a complex, multistakeholder problem and the urgency-fueled instinct to compress the design or pre-determine the answer.

**The move.** Walk the SDT through the eight reversals explicitly. Insist on enough variety in participants, time, and process to match the problem's complexity. Use Frances Wickes ("To the rationally minded, the mental processes of the intuitive appear to work backwards") to give permission for what feels backwards. Close with the punch line: "It's not safe to play it safe."

**Related.** Patterns 126 Create a space for not knowing, 131 Brave Space, 211 Get the whole system in the room, 225 Go slow to go fast, 226 Answers from unlikely places, 245 Work as play; Tools Shift Paper, Spoze; Models Being the There, The Curve of Action, Spoze, Ten Design Assumptions; Resources Kevin Kelly *Out of Control*, John Kotter *Leading Change*.

### 222 — Recursion

**The pattern.** Re-create the conditions that produced the most complex organisms in nature: give every organizational unit the maximum autonomy to respond to its immediate environment, and repeat that pattern at every level of scale.

**Why it matters.** Traditional command-and-control hierarchies "are not designed to create emergent, transformative innovation." Hierarchies attenuate variety; bad news travels too slowly. Evans cites Jack Welch ("If the rate of change on the outside exceeds the rate of change on the inside, the end is near"), then reaches for Stafford Beer's Viable System Model, derived from cybernetics (Norbert Wiener) and the human nervous system. Beer identified five subsystems — Operations, Coordination, Direction (audit), Planning, Identity — and the central insight that viable systems are recursive: any viable system is composed of smaller viable systems and embedded in a larger one; all five subsystems must repeat at every level. Kevin Kelly: "Distributed control has to be grown from simple local control."

**When to use.** When designing a workshop or organization where local responsiveness, fast adaptation, and emergent innovation matter — particularly when the client's structure is over-stratified.

**The move.** Apply the VSM lens: give operating units clarity of mission, budget, internal autonomy, clear interfaces with other units, and safeguards against jeopardizing the larger system. Design the workshop itself recursively — small teams that mirror the structure of the whole, each carrying the full Scan-Focus-Act dynamic.

**Related.** (Related-To list not in extract.) Body references VSM, cybernetics (Wiener, Beer), Kevin Kelly *Out of Control*.

### 223 — Iteration and rapid prototyping

**The pattern.** It is practically impossible to develop a complex solution on the first try; build fast cycles of make–show–critique–remake into every part of the workshop.

**Why it matters.** All modern design thinking values fast-cycle iteration, but organizations overvalue out-of-the-gate perfection — Evans attributes this partly to a misreading of Six Sigma's "do it right the first time" (the *it* in that sentence is a tightly-engineered repetitive step, not novel work). The lone manager polishing a PowerPoint to imaginary perfection before showing anyone is the figure he wants to retire. Evans cites Fred Brooks (*The Mythical Man-Month*), David Kelley of IDEO ("Fail fast to succeed sooner"), Luke Williams ("Never review the results of an iteration cycle by yourself"), and Steve Jobs ("Real artists ship"). Customers can't articulate requirements abstractly — they need to see it, touch it, drive it.

**When to use.** Throughout any session involving complex problem-solving, prototyping, or anything where the deliverable is novel.

**The move.** Compress work rounds to two hours or less (until Act Day). Require a report-out at the end of every module so teams develop the discipline of shippable form. Force iteration by changing the medium — teams build on WorkWalls, then 15 minutes before report-out the KreW hands them a different format (hypertiles, single-panel WorkWall, flipchart, iPad) with a tight spec. Use Why It Won't Work and Walk-About so teams collect Post-It feedback for the next iteration.

**Related.** Patterns 203 Don't meet — MOBILIZE!, 221 Requisite variety of design, 222 Recursion, 244 Don't argue. ### 224 — It's not safe to play it safe

**The pattern.** When major change is required, the very disciplines that smoothly run an enterprise — directing, budgeting, controlling — become impediments to improvement; the leader must reverse-wire those instincts.

**Why it matters.** John Kotter's distinction is the spine: managing (directing, budgeting, controlling) versus leading change (visioning, aligning, motivating). Managers and engagement directors who got their authority by mastering the first set try to apply that toolkit to a collaborative session — restricting the Scan, truncating exploration, engineering a predetermined outcome — with "dramatically counterproductive" results. A wavespace looks to the untrained eye as if it might spin out of control; in fact it is a "well-engineered and highly controlled production environment" for emergent knowledge work, but only when the boundary conditions are firmly in place. Shirley Mount Hufstedler: "If you play it safe, you've decided that you don't want to grow anymore."

**When to use.** Whenever a senior sponsor or engagement lead pushes to constrain the design out of risk-aversion, and any time real transformation (not optimization) is the goal.

**The move.** Walk leaders through the eight reversals from Pattern 221. Hold the line on the Scan; resist the pressure to engineer a predetermined answer. Make the case that the process is *more* controlled, not less, than the alternative — the boundary conditions are simply set in different places.

**Related.** Pattern 221 Requisite variety of design (paired); the closing line of 221 itself: "It's not safe to play it safe."

### 225 — Go slow to go fast

**The pattern.** Use the Scan-Focus-Act discipline to invest the time required for a group to build true GroupGenius and breakthrough solutions, rather than collapsing the agenda to satisfy a "sense of urgency."

**Why it matters.** Evans leans on John Kotter's *A Sense of Urgency* and its concept of *false urgency* — "almost always the product of failures or some form of intense pressure put on a group," fueled by anger and anxiety, rarely productive and often destructive. False urgency short-circuits the creative process: teams collapse problem space into solution space, grasp at the first doable option, fall into "groupthink" or "boss think." Lao Tzu: "Do you have the patience to wait until your mud settles and the water is clear?" A hastily engineered technical answer also lacks political support and emotional commitment. Evans cites Garvin and Roberto's HBR on shifting from advocacy to inquiry, and gives explicit completion criteria for each phase (Scan: complexity understood, assumptions tested; Focus: alternatives explored through the Insight phase; Act: solution, plan, motivation).

**When to use.** Every time a sponsor says "We don't have time for a three-day session — this is a crisis!" Especially for transformational sessions.

**The move.** Educate the SDT on the logic of the sequence; the more inexperienced the SDT, the more education they need. Defend the full three days; the Act phase is "usually" what suffers from unwise abbreviation, leaving Action Plans too general and groups not fully committed. Use the MG Taylor models to "locate where a group is at any given moment and where it needs to go next."

**Related.** Patterns 121 Free space to discover the truth, 126 Create space for not knowing, 212 GroupGenius, 229 Sleep on it (twice!); Tools Scan-Focus-Act, Mobilization, Rational/Emotional/Political Model, Scoping and Scaling wavespace experiences, SDT Planning Session; Models Creating the Problem, Creative Process, Design Formation; Resources Garvin & Roberto HBR Sept 2001, Kotter *A Sense of Urgency*.

### 226 — Answers from unlikely places

**The pattern.** Expose participants to ways of thinking about their issues from outside their field so that "Aha!" experiences can occur — and to do that, the facilitator must "read widely and weirdly."

**Why it matters.** When facing a mission-critical challenge, organizations restrict their search for solutions to their own experience or to benchmarking inside their industry — producing inbred or out-of-date ideas, or "received wisdom" from recognized experts. Evans frames looking outside as "crop rotation for the mind." He draws on Alan G. Robinson and Sam Stern's *Corporate Creativity* and its six elements that foster creativity in large organizations: Alignment, Self-initiated activity, Unofficial activity, Serendipity (a fortunate accident plus sagacity), Diverse stimuli, and Within-company communication. Henry Ford anchors it: "If you always do what you've always done, you'll always get what you've always got."

**When to use.** Designing the Scan phase, especially for organizations stuck in inbred thinking or facing a problem that resembles others they've already failed to solve.

**The move.** Mine the SDT for parallel situations, metaphors, and analogies. Use "In the shoes of…" to put participants in roles they don't usually inhabit (customers, regulators, competitors). Introduce the HBR move "staple yourself to an order." Source material from science, nature, history, technology, military tactics, music, theater, sports, mythology — "the list is almost endless." Reach into the global facilitator network for what others are reading.

**Related.** Patterns 218 People Science, 227 Metaphorical modes of discovery, 241 Lead (and learn) with experience, 245 Work as play; Tools Contingency Diagram, In the Shoes of…, It's Just Lunch, Metaphors, Scan-Focus-Act, SDT Planning Session, Syntopical Readings, TED Talks; Models Creative Process, Zone of Emergence; Resources Edward De Bono *Lateral Thinking*, Robinson & Stern *Corporate Creativity*, Shapiro/Rangan/Sviokla "Staple Yourself to an Order" HBR.

### 227 — Metaphorical modes of discovery

**The pattern.** Provide fresh metaphors, patterns, analogues, and analogies for the work; metaphors juxtapose two unrelated situations and let the mind play with one to find solutions for the other.

**Why it matters.** Organizations meet their problems "head-on in an intellectual frontal assault," and the framing they choose silently constrains the solution set — they end up working "within a box whose sides are no longer there." Evans quotes Dietrich Dorner (*The Logic of Failure*: "Perhaps the most important method for expanding a problem sector is thinking by analogy") and John Holland on cross-disciplinary work as "a rich source of metaphor." Worked example: how to attract more customers becomes how to catch more fish — different bait, sonar, think like a fish, drain the lake — then translate the strategies back.

**When to use.** During Scan and Focus when a group is stuck in the literal language of their problem.

**The move.** Begin the metaphor hunt in dialogue with the SDT. Match metaphors to the deep structure of the client's challenge. Examples Evans cites: Sherpas evolving from pack animals to esteemed guides (used to help IT shift its relationship to business partners), ants and bees (complex behavior without command-and-control), Mongol cavalry (sales acceleration), tidal marshes (productive boundaries with suppliers). Design enough "soak time" — small-group reading or video-watching, then dialogue on the metaphor itself, before applying. In one Walmart session about selling food, the story of how the French government introduced the potato by ostentatiously guarding it became the icon of the entire engagement.

### 228 — Rhythms of the body and the day

**The pattern.** Pay attention to the group's natural biorhythms over a day and build in a range of activities, including down time and regular breaks; the human experience of participants directly affects their ability to solve the problem.

**Why it matters.** wavespace experiences are immersive and demanding — clients arrive anxious, biorhythms are scrambled by travel, and Carl Jung's introverts (a third to half of the population) initially read a workshop as "another, more relentless form of the open-plan office they have come to loathe." When Evans first heard he'd be working twelve-hour days without breaks, his reaction was "Just shoot me now." The MG Taylor axiom anchors it: "If you can't have fun with a problem, you will never solve it." James Joyce's "Mr. Duffy lived a short distance from his body" frames the disembodied-participant problem.

**When to use.** Every session — and Evans says "triply true" of virtual sessions where people are slumped at screens.

**The move.** Design first impressions to be unexpected (plants, music, art being made in front of guests). Keep opening sit-and-get under 15 minutes. Grant explicit "rights of passage" to leave chairs. Schedule breaks every 75–90 minutes (research-based) but don't advertise them — that just invites people to schedule calls. Schedule introvert-friendly activities (Take-A-Panel, Syntopical Reading, meditation). Run breakouts during meals; clear plates as a host. Lead stretching at every report-out. Take a Walk in the Park (silent, no email). Hourly bell for personal reflection on Act Day. For virtual: stand-up desks, Speaker view (not Gallery), led stretching, outdoor breaks where PAX return with a photo.

### 229 — Sleep on it (twice!)

**The pattern.** Build an "incubation" phase — the literal opportunity to sleep on it — into the session; the unconscious processes complex problems more effectively than the conscious mind, and unfinished tasks stay alive in the background.

**Why it matters.** The Scan throws huge information at groups conditioned for rapid action ("A full day together and we haven't solved something?!"). Evans cites Maarten Bos in HBR: during distraction, "our unconscious can process large amounts of information — as long as we give it time." Russell Foster's TED talk: those who sleep on a problem have a threefold advantage. Then the Zeigarnik Effect — Bluma Zeigarnik's 1920s discovery (under Kurt Lewin) that waiters remembered unpaid orders better than paid ones; interrupted tasks generate intrusive thoughts that pull the mind back. And Milton H. Erickson's "unconscious search" — paradoxical instructions that get the controlling conscious mind out of the way long enough for new responses to emerge "as if automatically."

**When to use.** Multi-day sessions where deep choices and commitment are required, especially the Focus-to-Act transition.

**The move.** End the Scan Day with a serious investigation of possibilities (a Future State Scenario, Model Shop physical model, a Legend skit) — but *don't close it down*. Report-outs happen in the *morning*, not at the end of the day, forcing overnight incubation. For the Focus-to-Act transition, give participants a 3x5 card with a question like "What does our company need from us — from me — on our last day?" Have them sleep on it and write the answer on the reverse when they wake. Those answers seed the morning Synthesis Conversation. "The results can be magical."

### 230 — Anticipatory design

**The pattern.** Design solutions that anticipate the likely evolution of the situation beyond any initial solution and build in a way to keep designing downstream responses; wicked problems have no final fix, so the design must include the next design.

**Why it matters.** Evans grounds the pattern in Horst Rittel and Melvin Webber's 1973 paper on wicked problems — no definitive formulation, no stopping rule, solutions are good-or-bad rather than true-or-false, addressing them is "a component of politics. There is no escaping that truism." The MG Taylor work was conceived for wicked problems; the Taylors saw existential global problems "each one of which is capable of crashing human civilization." Matt learned anticipatory design from Frank Lloyd Wright. The Mrs. Pew story is the heart: she initially hated her Wright house, ready to sell at financial loss, then realized "Mr. Wright had not built a house for who I was — but for the person that I could become." Buckminster Fuller's "Comprehensive Anticipatory Design Science" expanded the idea. Evans laments that today the phrase has been "de-geniused" by UX designers (Aaron Shapiro, HUGE) into algorithmic choice-elimination — "we are not going to be able to consume our way out of wicked problems."

**When to use.** Whenever the problem is wicked (multi-stakeholder, value-laden, recursive) — which is most engagements worth taking on.

**The move.** Anticipate "the problem after next" with the SDT — what challenges arise once we successfully solve the immediate one? Design the Scan to include the larger context and downstream effects of success. Use Best Case Worst Case. Build "collateral learning" into the outcomes so the group can keep evolving.

Less Choice."

### 231 — If you're not part of the problem…

**The pattern.** Invert the Sixties slogan: if you are *not* part of the problem, you cannot be part of the solution. Solutions arise out of a common, shared humanity — and only by taking responsibility, refusing the Drama Triangle, do groups escape self-defeating polarization.

**Why it matters.** The original Sixties slogan framed politics as us-versus-them — abandon your side and join ours. Evans uses Buffalo Springfield's "For What It's Worth" ("nobody's right if everybody's wrong") to mark the cost. The core model is Stephen Karpman's Drama Triangle: Victim ("Poor me!"), Persecutor ("It's all your fault!"), Rescuer ("Let me help you!"). Roles switch fluidly; each player gets unspoken needs met without acknowledging the damage. Rescuing keeps Victims one-down and lets Rescuers duck their own responsibility. Evans distinguishes true victims from the seductive *role* of Victim, which blocks decision. The Idries Shah Mulla Nasrudin story — the magistrate who needed a week to stop eating sugar himself before forbidding it to a child — anchors the move toward personal responsibility.

**When to use.** Sessions involving conflict between factions, blame-laden organizational change, or any situation where stakeholders are casting each other as villains.

**The move.** Surface the Drama Triangle as a shared frame; invite participants to notice when they're slipping into Victim/Rescuer/Persecutor positions. Insist on personal responsibility as the only exit. Use the Lilla Watson framing: "If you have come here to help me, you are wasting your time. But if you have come because your liberation is bound up with mine, then let us work together." Bring even-antagonistic stakeholders into productive dialogue rather than separating them.

### 232 — Adjacent possible

**The pattern.** Like evolution in the natural world, practical innovations almost always proceed by recombining and extending what already exists; design helps groups explore the possibilities reachable from the current state, not the entire space of imagination.

**Why it matters.** Evans pulls from Stuart Kauffman's Santa Fe Institute complexity work (the ASE built ties to SFI through the EY Center for Business Innovation in the 1990s). Kauffman calls pre-life self-organization "order for free" and shows autonomous agents "push their way into novelty" by moving into the *adjacent possible* — combinations one step away from the current state. He proves mathematically that only a tiny fraction of imaginable forms are realizable. Steven Johnson translates: "The adjacent possible is a kind of shadow future, hovering on the edges of the present state of things, a map of all the ways in which the present can reinvent itself. Yet it is not an infinite space." Babbage is illustrative — the difference engine was buildable in Victorian mechanical technology, the analytical engine had to wait for vacuum tubes. We cannot debate our way to solutions for wicked problems — we can only run the simulation.

**When to use.** When a group is tempted by either utopian leaps or stuck-in-the-rut incrementalism — Scan and Focus phases especially.

**The move.** Include the adjacent possible as a Scan topic — read about ants and bees, complex adaptive systems, evolutionary dynamics. Take constraints seriously rather than happy-talking past them ("In every adverse condition, there are hundreds of good solutions"). Design Zones of Emergence using Johnson's three levers — physical environment, social network, information habits. Insist on prototyping, feedback, and iteration as the only reliable strategies.

### 233 — Only go out as far as you know you can get back

**The pattern.** A facilitator has fiduciary responsibility for participants who have temporarily entrusted their executive function to the KreW; only take them out as far in the Scan as you know you can get them back.

**Why it matters.** The Scan invites a "turtles all the way down" infinite regression (Stephen Hawking's anecdote opens the pattern). The pattern is fundamentally ethical. During the early hours of a session, participants entrust their schedule and agenda to the facilitator. A good Scan will challenge organizational culture and individuals' worldviews; Evans is direct that there's a word for using temporary authority to break or disrupt core belief principles — "the word is brainwashing." Creatives have a higher appetite for operating outside the norm. Evans warns equally against the opposite failure: holding back to avoid pushback, protecting the sponsor relationship. "That is not doing your job either." Matt Taylor: "I can't act if I do not have a model of where I am, what I am doing, and what I hope to achieve by doing it."

**When to use.** Every Scan design conversation with the SDT, especially when the facilitator is tempted to push hard on the group's worldview.

**The move.** Run *everything* by the SDT — agenda, topics, exact assignments, team composition. Expect pushback and push back on the pushback. Use the Prep Day Circle Up and especially the Scan Day Evening Circle Up — "we will be a lot smarter then than we are now after all that we will have learned during the Scan." The Focus phase is where you really challenge and break things; let the Scan-day conversation calibrate how far. Honor the limits of your relationship with this group rather than confusing it with personal-evolution work.

### 234 — Graceful failure

**The pattern.** Plan for graceful failure to avoid the worst and to set the conditions for feedback, learning, and improvement; design so that when something breaks, core function survives.

**Why it matters.** Evans opens with the 2018 Camp Fire that destroyed Paradise, California — 85 dead, 18,800 structures gone, foreseeable risks (downed lines, inadequate evacuation routes, warned by Cal Fire in 2005) ignored. "Failure to imagine the possibility of failure is the most profound mistake engineers can make" (Henry Petroski). Definition: graceful failure is "the ability to maintain core functionality when portions of a system break down" — the shear pin in a snow thrower, the fuse, the sprinkler that melts to release water. Positive feedback loops (Madoff's Ponzi scheme) cause cascading failure; graceful failure builds *negative* feedback (cruise control) for stability. Five blame-game pathologies: narrative fallacy, hindsight bias, safety-vs-performance tension, single-root-cause fiction (Toyota's Five Whys works for ordinary failures but not complex ones), and cultures of blame that drive facts underground. Richard Cook: "The surprise is not that there are so many accidents. It is that there are so few." People are the ultimate source of resilience.

**When to use.** Throughout solution design — especially Focus and Act — and any time the group is converging on a recommendation.

**The move.** Build a Brave Space (Pattern 132) so people will tell the truth about failure. Include the organization's past mistakes in the Scan; don't sugarcoat. Run Why It Won't Work to legitimize tough feedback. In Focus, design crash-and-burn Future State Scenarios and use the Contingency Diagram. Use What Has to Be True? and the Appropriate Response model. Action Plans must address barriers, risks, and mitigation.

### 235 — Designing Virtual Sessions

**The pattern.** A virtual DesignSession is an opportunity to combine the best of both the physical and the virtual worlds; the designer must build on the true strengths of each. Start by assuming you can design a session that is *better* than an in-person session.

**Why it matters.** Evans wrote this in late 2020 as a companion to Pattern 312, drawing on roughly 25 weeks of post-pandemic experience rather than the 25-year base behind every other pattern. Zoom went from 10 million to 300 million daily participants between December 2019 and March 2020. The original sin was that knowledge workers simply imported old, bad meeting habits to the new medium. The pivot: more effective but not more efficient — prep effort scales 2X to 4X. The underlying patterns, tools, and models still hold; only technology and tactics shift.

**When to use.** Any virtual or hybrid session — Evans flags hybrid as "the problem after next" once in-person becomes safe again.

**The move.** Table stakes: precise agenda; a back-channel like iChat or WhatsApp (never the video app's chat); invisible technology (Zoom, Miro, Mural, Google Drive); KreW practiced until "we couldn't get it wrong"; middle-of-the-room facilitation — deploy a KW into each breakout for light facilitation and live-scribing, since "voice of God" doesn't reach breakouts; explicit agreements (cameras on, mute, raise-hand). Leverage virtual strengths: unbundle time and space — Carolyn and Evans split global cohorts into eleven three-hour weekly sessions; Dan Newman of Matter Solutions has moved away from the "event" structure entirely toward weeks-or-months journeys on a Collaboration Zone. Send physical pre-work, chocolate, pipe cleaners; use Junk Drawer Challenge or the Desky Award. Open and close with connection (Ceremony of Appreciation). Break every 75 minutes; Bill Hoogterp of Own the Room: actively involve PAX every 8 minutes. Treat digital whiteboards as the *start* of documentation (VRchitect Ryan Romsey's skeumorphic Mural environments, the upCreation Nebula on Miro).

## 240 — Process

### 241 — Lead (and learn) with experience

**The pattern.** Design learning experiences that let participants discover new information for themselves and immediately put it into practice.

**Why it matters.** People learn visually, auditorily, and kinesthetically — most learn best when all three channels are engaged — yet most organizational learning is PowerPoint-and-podium, with the learner expected to sit still and stay receptive. The MG Taylor Five Es of Education model (explain, exemplify, expectation, experience, exploration) treats the last two Es as the move that turns a learner into an active participant, not a passive recipient. Rob also rejects separating problem-solving from learning: "What is problem-solving if not a way of learning what works?" The Chinese proverb in the margin sums it up — "I hear, I forget. I see, I remember. I do, I understand."

**When to use.** Throughout the entire arc, starting before participants even arrive — the invitation should teach something new, the entry space should surprise — and especially when running experiential modules where the meaning has to be discovered, not pre-explained.

**The move.** Start the discovery in the SDT meeting and the invitation. Run experiential modules (Marshmallow Challenge, Inventions, Masterpiece, Model Shop, Legends, Simulations, Win as Much as You Can) by jumping into the activity, emphasizing how over why so the insight isn't given away. Then design debrief questions in advance and let participants name and claim their own takeaways — make them the authors.

### 242 — Sapiential leadership (self-facilitating teams)

**The pattern.** Rely on teams to facilitate themselves, and expect the emergence of sapiential leadership — wisdom and direction-setting that surface from inside the group rather than being imposed by a designated facilitator.

**Why it matters.** Conventional facilitation assigns a designated facilitator to each working group, usually one of the "usual suspects" — internal or external consultants who have skin in the game and quietly steer toward their preferred answer. Engagement teams will even gerrymander team rosters to engineer the outcomes they want. But "people support what they help create": a group that feels engineered toward a predetermined result will not own the outcome. *Sapiential* means having wisdom or discernment, and the bet is that with a few prompts groups already know what good facilitation looks like — they're just rarely asked to do it themselves.

**When to use.** Whenever you assign breakout work, and from the very first SDT meeting where the default expectation is "expert" facilitation by the consultants.

**The move.** Make three expectations explicit: the answers are already in the group; whoever sees the next step most clearly is responsible for naming it and leading the group through it; each team is responsible for making space for all voices. Reinforce this with carefully written assignments (purpose, context, specific questions, timing, report-out instructions); set the expectation with the SDT in the first meeting; review what works with the group mid-session; share the GroupGenius research; and stack working teams with good gender diversity, since research shows mixed teams perform better.

### 243 — Move the group

**The pattern.** Move the group physically — you cannot push the boundaries of the mind without moving the body.

**Why it matters.** "Sitting is the new smoking." We don't have bodies; we are bodies, and the word *stuck* names both a group's progress and the literal condition of their bodies in the chair. Walk past the breakout teams that are making no progress and have nothing on their WorkWalls and you will find them glued to their seats. Rob recalls producing Tony Robbins' first New York Firewalk Experience, where Robbins told him, "If what you are doing isn't working, try anything else!" Movement creates fresh perspective, unlocks energy, and breaks stuckness.

**When to use.** When energy sags, when groups go static in breakouts, on transitions back to the Radiant Room, and proactively as a built-in rhythm rather than a rescue.

**The move.** Locate the workshop far enough from the office that participants won't get pulled into normal work. Build "rights of passage" into ground rules (stand in back, walk to snacks) — suspend them only during the Synthesis Conversation. Cue movement with energetic music between plenary and breakouts. Encourage standing during breakouts, take-turns scribing, and use modules that put people on their feet (Take-A-Panel, Simulations) or on the floor (Inventions, Model Shop). Stretch and breathe when returning to plenary. In virtual sessions, build movement into the agenda explicitly — adjustable desks, group stretches, dance breaks — it is "an absolute necessity."

### 244 — Don't argue. Test!

**The pattern.** Arguments based on opinion and conjecture are never a sound basis for decision and design — bring data, prototype, test.

**Why it matters.** The title was a challenge from Lufthansa's VP of marketing during Rob's TQM work in the 1990s; he was alarmed his department had become "one of the best-paid debating societies in Germany." Business meetings devolve into zero-sum debates where winning matters more than truth. Testing refines solutions, surfaces unexpected insights, lets us learn from reality as it is rather than as we imagine it, and gives a controlled way to course-correct early. But data alone aren't enough. W. Edwards Deming insisted on a System of Profound Knowledge with four lenses: Appreciation of Systems, Knowledge of Variation, Theory of Knowledge, and Knowledge of Psychology. "Experience by itself teaches nothing" and "data has no meaning apart from its context" — data only become useful when located inside a theory of the whole system.

**When to use.** Anytime a group is stuck arguing competing opinions, and structurally throughout the Scan-Focus-Act process so that ideas get tested, not just defended.

**The move.** Get the whole system in the room and give it Scan time to share what it knows. Work with the SDT to anticipate the data needed to challenge future-state proposals. Build prototypes that visualize new solutions for critique, and test them rigorously against the data, the models, and the judgment of others. Design real-world tests using What Has to Be True? Borrow the Stanford d.school axiom: "Prototype as if you know you're right, but test as if you know you're wrong."

### 245 — Work as play

**The pattern.** Infuse the work with the spirit of play to engage the whole person in the design process.

**Why it matters.** The wicked problems addressed in wavespaces require everyone's best creativity, but Gallup data shows only 32 percent of workers are engaged, 50.8 percent are "not engaged," and 17.2 percent are "actively disengaged" — numbers that haven't moved in years. Meanwhile, since 2004 humans have spent 50 billion collective hours on World of Warcraft (5.93 million person-years, roughly the time since our ancestors stood upright) and contribute massive volunteer effort to game wikis. As Jane McGonigal argues in *Reality Is Broken*, games meet human needs reality currently doesn't. Play boosts brain function and memory, reduces stress, releases endorphins, builds trust through shared laughter, and — per the research on convergent vs divergent problems — increases the range and quality of solutions to divergent (multi-answer) problems, which is exactly what wavespaces address. The MG Taylor axiom: "If you can't have fun with the problem, you will never solve it."

**When to use.** From the KreW's first communications, through the SDT prep, and throughout the session — especially during long Act-phase work rounds when energy needs maintenance.

**The move.** Start with the KreW: team meals, "bring it in" and "shake it out" rituals, energetic Circle Ups. Stage a physical environment that reads as play — toys, stuffed animals, art on the Knowledge Wall, music. Let the wavespace Opening signal eager anticipation. Use humor strategically — Willi Railo's advice: "Don't waste your humor. When you tell a joke, you distract the ego long enough to create an opening to make a serious point. Be sure you really land it." Run game-like modules (Masterpiece, Inventions, Metaphors, Future State Scenarios, Simulations) and inject Act-phase energizers (Rock-Paper-Scissors tournaments, paper airplane contests with "valuable prizes!").

### 247 — Use the power of compelling visuals

**The pattern.** Use spontaneously created, compelling, real-time visuals to inspire and stimulate thinking and feeling.

**Why it matters.** Sight carries by far the most perceptual bandwidth — Norretranders gives 10,000,000 bits/second for seeing versus 1,000,000 for touch, 100,000 each for hearing and smell, and a paltry 1,000 for taste. Edward Tufte points out we read four to five times faster than we listen, which is precisely why standard PowerPoint is so painful: by the time the presenter reads line one, the audience has finished the slide and is wondering why the speaker is so slow. Tufte's quip: "Power corrupts. PowerPoint corrupts absolutely." In wavespaces, projectors are called "death-ray machines." The standard business meeting starves a perceptual system designed to process rich, complex visual information.

**When to use.** Throughout — from a Welcome Wall at the entrance to the live scribing of every plenary conversation, and especially in virtual sessions where physical cues are absent and visuals must compensate.

**The move.** Use live scribing — talented scribes whose markers create the visual richness that produces StrongMemory. Build the room around visual richness: Welcome Wall, Knowledge Wall augmented in real time with photos and quotes, plants, toys, books chosen intentionally. Train participants to use WorkWalls and Strategic Modeling for their own graphic representations. Add mind mapping (Tony Buzan) and digital graphics on iPads. Online, invest heavily in the whiteboard environment and have a good scribe — visuals matter more virtually, not less.

### 248 — Production values matter

**The pattern.** Create the highest production values you can — they signal to participants that the work is worth doing.

**Why it matters.** Organizations are in a race to the budgetary bottom, but cost-cutting easily slides from frugal to cheap, and cheap reads as both inexpensive and poorly made. *Production values* — borrowed from theater and film — are the combined technical qualities of methods, materials, and stagecraft, and they aren't a function of budget; Hollywood proves you can waste enormous sums on lousy films. High production values bring believability: in a wavespace, they make the work feel actually worth doing. As theater impresario Craig Evans put it, "It's a paradox, but the more realistic a set is, the more we forget it's make-believe." For the KreW, the standard is the price of admission. For participants used to "the usual crappy conference room equipped with a single, sad, dried-out orange marker," the contrast triggers a shift in their *discretionary effort* — the gap between maximum sustainable effort and the minimum needed to avoid being fired.

**When to use.** Across every visible surface of the session — and continuously, since it's the loving maintenance of the space, not the initial purchase, that creates the effect.

**The move.** Sweat the layout (logical, symmetrical, orderly breakouts; a map of the whole space). Format assignments consistently and attractively with custom headers and footers. Keep technology, furniture, and tools current and in good repair — apply the no-broken-toys-in-a-wavespace rule everywhere, with budget for maintenance and replacement. Outfit experiential modules (Inventions, Model Shop, Legends, Day in the Life) with abundant, panache-laden materials so participants suspend disbelief. Make the Executive Summary so well produced that clients display it in their offices years after the Action Plan is implemented.

### 249 — Stepping up to the leadership moment

**The pattern.** Prepare a group's formal leader both to step back and let the group find its own style of leadership, and to be ready to step up to the leadership moment when necessary.

**Why it matters.** Humans are social animals — some say herd animals — wired to follow alphas who exhibit leadership, yet public confidence in formal authority is at historic lows while collaborative decision-making rises. (Rob notes that as recently as 2004, when he wrote the Capgemini "Collaborative Business Experience" position paper, the word *collaboration* was new enough in French to carry connotations of wartime disloyalty.) John Kotter's distinction is the load-bearing frame: *management* is "a set of processes that can keep a complicated system of people and technology running smoothly" — planning/budgeting, organizing/staffing, controlling/problem-solving. *Leadership* is "a set of processes that creates organizations in the first place or adapts them to significantly changing circumstances" — establishing direction, aligning people, motivating and inspiring. Most organizations are over-managed and under-led, and the term *change management* is itself an oxymoron — transformation requires change *leadership*. The Tao Te Ching captures the goal: of a good leader who talks little, "they will say, 'We did this ourselves.'"

**When to use.** Whenever you brief a formal leader before a session — especially one whose authority depends on hierarchical structures — and at any moment in the session where leadership rather than management is required.

**The move.** Coach the formal leader explicitly on when to step back (during emergent design and self-facilitation) and when to step up (when direction-setting, alignment, or motivation is required). Use Kotter's distinction to give them language for both modes. Lean on management practices for logistics and Action Plan rollout, and on leadership behaviors for vision, alignment, and emotional energy. Bobby Kennedy's line — "There go my people. I must catch up with them, for I am their leader" — captures the spirit.

### 250 — Real-time re-design

**The pattern.** No plan survives contact with the room — when something goes wrong, redesign in real time using the KreW and your own flexibility.

**Why it matters.** "No battle plan survives contact with the enemy" — Helmuth von Moltke. Printers fail. Guests are delayed. Pre-work is undone. Modules run long or short. Cultural emergencies hijack attention. Or, as Rob puts it, "the universe just decides to mess with you." He conceived the pattern after a workshop where the office staff printed hundreds of pages of pre-publication patterns single-sided after being asked to print double-sided — discovered fifteen minutes before start. Carolyn Buck Luce's line captures the alternative: "That wasn't an agenda. That was a 'pretenda'! We had everything scheduled down to the minute, but by the first morning we were hours behind and had to rethink everything."

**When to use.** Continuously — built into the basic design (preparation), embedded in every assignment (setting expectations), monitored throughout (recognition), and triggered the moment you realize you're off (recovery).

**The move.** Four elements. *Preparation:* give each exercise the time it actually needs plus a little more, schedule a 7–8 minute break every 75–80 minutes (Rob keeps a collection of 7-minute songs), end at the appointed time as a sacrosanct contract, and align meals with breakout work to prevent energy dissipation ("table-by-table, just like at a wedding"). *Setting expectations:* put time limits on every assignment, deputize participants with a visible iPad timer, and use the Final Report-Out matrix on Act Day. *Recognition:* track actuals against the strawdog with the PF the whole way. *Recovery:* the cardinal rule — if you're in a time crunch, decide what to *delete*. Don't try to do everything faster; rushing leaves groups feeling out of control and produces unsatisfactory output. Huddle with the PF, Circle Up the KreW, and check in with the SDT. For virtual sessions, have a back-channel (iChat, WhatsApp) and contingency phone numbers for total platform failure.

### 251 — Allow for latency

**The pattern.** Exercise patience and discernment as you assess the rates of response latency your groups exhibit — ideas need time to land.

**Why it matters.** Latency is the lag between stimulus and response, and it's built into the human operating system in measurable ways: it takes about 20 minutes for new dendrites to form in the brain (and they vanish within hours if not reinforced); the *dream lag effect* shows images appearing in dreams about a week after the events, suggesting that long for permanent neocortical storage; introverts may show longer response times because they analyze incoming stimuli more carefully. Gardeners know not to pull up carrots to see if they're growing. Anxious clients drive toward premature closure — "let's get something decided" — which ignores that the unconscious needs unknowable time to integrate. Piet Hein's grook nails it: "When you feel how depressingly slowly you climb, it's well to remember that *Things Take Time*." Robert Caro's writing instructor R. P. Blackmur warned him, "You're never going to achieve what you want to, Mr. Caro, if you don't stop thinking with your fingers" — Caro now drafts in longhand to force depth. Matt Taylor remembers Frank Lloyd Wright's version: "All you will get is a confusion of half-baked ideas. Build it totally in your mind before you commit to paper, then draw it out."

**When to use.** Continuously through SDT design (don't show the strawdog first even though it's requested first), and across the arc of the session as ideas planted in early modules don't land until later ones.

**The move.** *Cook before you commit:* work ideas out internally before externalizing them. *Plant seeds:* in the first SDT planning session and the wavespace Opening, drop "embedded invitations" — Rob promises "Scan Day = one unit of work, Focus Day = double, Act Day = double the first two combined" — then return to harvest them later. Schedule the Why It Won't Work exercise to deliberately raise discomfort at the end of Day 2, then circle back to the original promise to set up Sleep on It homework. Get your impatient executive routine out of the way and trust the process.

### 252 — Human technology (humans first)

**The pattern.** Use any and all means to augment the human ability to solve problems — and aggressively avoid technologies that merely distract from the experience of collaboration.

**Why it matters.** Blame *Minority Report* and CNN's Magic Wall. Blame the consulting tech-arms race that fills "innovation centers" and "maker spaces" with whiz-bang gear (3-D printers used mainly to make game pieces and party favors) intended to validate the parent firm's tech credentials more than to facilitate process. Knowledge workers have to fight off floor-to-ceiling Radiant Walls in rooms too small to focus on, breakout areas dominated by always-on screens that sap energy, and report-outs produced by a few "technology high priests." The real point: the human interaction must remain front and center. Allan Willie's quote captures the counter-truth — even at his software company, "the low-tech whiteboard with markers and sticky notes is a very easy medium for brainstorming. There are basically no constraints. It's effortless." Vannevar Bush's 1945 *Atlantic* essay "As We May Think" — read by Doug Engelbart, who then invented the mouse, word processor, and hyperlink — was always about technology in service of all humanity, not technology for its own sake.

**When to use.** Whenever a new tool or platform is being considered, and aggressively whenever expensive or showy technology threatens to crowd out human interaction.

**The move.** Default to technologies that let people work simply, elegantly, accessibly. Hard to improve on the whiteboard. Use what genuinely accelerates a human process or eliminates tedium — digital photography for documentation, projection that enlarges report-outs, multi-vote apps, iPad timers in place of hypertiles, TED Talks as a Scan tool, video Chat Rooms. Push back hard on tech for its own sake. For virtual sessions accelerated by the 2020 pandemic, evaluate every Zoom/Miro/Mural/polling app against the same questions: Does it truly serve the group? What does it accelerate, what are its hidden costs, does it support GroupGenius, can the whole group contribute directly without mediation?

### 253 — Honor resistance

**The pattern.** Welcome resistance as a gift — it is not the obstacle, it is free troubleshooting.

**Why it matters.** People don't resist change — we love change, which is why most movies make their money in the first weeks even if inferior to the ones we love most (Rob's example: *The Princess Bride*). What people resist is *failure*. Daniel Kahneman in *Thinking, Fast and Slow* describes the *negativity bias*: the brain is built to give priority to bad news because shaving hundredths of a second off predator detection improves the odds of living long enough to reproduce. So when faced with an organizational change, most people are running an unconscious calculation of every way it could hurt them — which advocates, having already convinced themselves of the benefits, dismiss as ornery. Milton Erickson reframes it clinically: resistance is "an erroneous mental set that gets in the way of new experience." Carl Jung added the warning: "What you resist not only persists, but will grow in size." And the Borg's "Resistance is futile" in *Star Trek: First Contact* — the truth being that when there is no resistance to the resistance, the resistance changes character.

**When to use.** Whenever critics or naysayers push back — in SDT meetings, during plenary, in breakouts — and especially when your own first instinct is to defend.

**The move.** Shift your energy to listen with different ears, not to prepare a rebuttal. Carolyn and Rob remind each other: "This isn't happening to *you*, it's happening *for* you." It doesn't have to be objectively true — it's useful, and the MG Taylor Axiom is that "the only value of an idea, concept, or theory is what it allows you to do." Invite dissent and alternative viewpoints actively in the session; your acceptance of and curiosity about challenges sets the tone for the whole group to open up. Look for a co-designer in every critic.

**Related.** Patterns 121 Free space to discover the truth, 132 Brave Space, 306 Move toward the fear; Tools Contingency Diagram, Question Storming; Models Feedback of a Complex Kind, Valid Criticism.

## 260 — Outcomes and Deliverables

### 261 — Make the solution last past Act Day

**The pattern.** Address implementation problems directly as part of solution development — anticipate resistance and interdependence and design for them from the first SDT meeting.

**Why it matters.** Even well-designed solutions are useless if poorly implemented, and as Dietrich Dorner writes in *The Logic of Failure*, failure "develops gradually according to its own logic" once complicated situations elicit habits of thought that "set failure in motion from the beginning." John Kotter in *Leading Change* names two implementation killers: *resistance*, always waiting to reassert itself, and *interdependence* — the modern problem that everything is connected to everything else, so any change creates ripples and resistance far and wide. INSEAD's Kim and Mauborgne identify three universal elements of *fair process* that mitigate resistance: Engagement, Explanation, and Expectation Clarity. Kotter's cardinal rule: "Whenever you let up before the job is done, critical momentum can be lost and regression may follow… Once regression begins, building momentum can be a daunting task, not unlike asking people to throw their bodies in front of a huge boulder that has begun to roll back down the hill."

**When to use.** From the first SDT meeting onward, throughout the Act phase, in the lead sponsor's closing comments, and in the post-session SDT cadence.

**The move.** Use the Rational/Emotional/Political Model in the SDT to scope all the issues. Build the Scan to introduce successful implementation approaches. Use the Action Plan to identify mitigation strategies. Turn the dozens of wavespace participants into ambassadors who can explain in the larger ecosystem which alternatives were rejected and why. Apply Kotter's interdependence moves: invite more change not less, get more help, exercise leadership from above, empower project leadership from below, reduce unnecessary dependencies. Mark celebration at the end as the *beginning* of the real work. Keep the SDT alive past Act Day with check-in calls at one week, one month, and three months. Do not allow a name on any Action Plan unless someone in the room takes responsibility for personally communicating that assignment.

### 262 — Response-ability is contagious

**The pattern.** Build the organization's capacity to respond — its *response-ability* — and the habit of proactive engagement becomes contagious.

**Why it matters.** In most traditional organizations authority is sharply curtailed and exceeding it is punished — hence "It is better to seek forgiveness than permission." Martin Seligman's research on *learned helplessness* in the 1960s and '70s showed that dogs given unavoidable shocks (and humans given unavoidable noise) failed to take action even when escape later became possible. Even "empowerment" is hollow, as Scott Adams skewers it via Dilbert: the boss admits empowerment is "just a way to blame employees for not doing things we tell them not to do." A wavespace inverts this: over the Scan-Focus-Act sequence, the locus of control deliberately shifts from facilitator to participants — Scan is tightly time-boxed and directed; by the time participants confirm Buckets of Work and self-select Act teams, it is clearly *their* show. Kotter in *The Heart of Change*: it is a change in *emotions* that drives change in an organization. The undermining emotions are anger, false pride, pessimism, arrogance, cynicism, panic, exhaustion, insecurity, anxiety; the facilitating ones are faith, trust, optimism, urgency, reality-based pride, passion, excitement, hope, enthusiasm.

**When to use.** Throughout the session, by deliberately calibrating the loose-tight handoff with the SDT and watching for the moments groups step into agency.

**The move.** Design the Scan-Focus-Act sequence so the facilitation team progressively releases control. Calibrate the pace jointly with the SDT — some groups take responsibility early, others only after a breakdown or breakthrough. Watch and name the shift as response-ability spreads from one team to the next. Send participants out as living proof that transformation is possible.

**Related.** Patterns 123 Mirror neurons, 203 Don't meet — MOBILIZE!, 224 It's not safe to play it safe, 242 Sapiential leadership (self-facilitating teams), 245 Work as play, 249 Stepping up to the leadership moment, 264 Engaging deliverables; Tools Rational/Emotional/Political Model, Scan-Focus-Act, Action Plan, Transformation Roadmap, Executive Summary, Synthesis Conversation, Buckets of Work, Launching the Act Day Teams; Models Being the There, Levels of Competency, Ten Design Principles, Valid Criticism.

### 263 — Rituals of closure

**The pattern.** Build in multiple rituals of closure at the end of a session to mark completion and release the tension that has accumulated.

**Why it matters.** The human mind is like a computer operating system — every unfinished task drains energy from the whole system. Cal Newport in *Deep Work* notes that without finishing one task, attention can't fully transition to the next, and performance suffers. Humans crave the tension release of closure — the last paragraph, the closing scene, the final chord. A wavespace generates psychological tension from the moment planning begins; the process actively prevents premature closure until the group has done its work, so by Act Day the group has built up considerable accumulated charge. It isn't enough that they reached the objectives; they must *feel* they reached them — emotionally and physically — to release energy for what comes next.

**When to use.** At the literal end of the session — final report-outs, Act Day close, the post-session SDT meeting — and proportional to the size and intensity of the group.

**The move.** Give final report-outs ample time; don't rush them even after three or four long days. Distribute a high-production-values Executive Summary. Stage Symbolic Action: bury an actual hatchet, write old behaviors on cards and shred them in a paper shredder outfitted with gold and orange flames (so the hotel sprinklers don't go off), tweet intentions publicly, sign a Commitment Statement, take and distribute a group photo printed with the Statement, end with champagne and dancing. For smaller groups (twenty or fewer), use William Ury's Circle of Appreciation — chant each person's name in unison, two-minute timer of popcorn-style positive feedback, chant the name to close. The pandemic-era version is the Ceremony of Appreciation and Honorable Closure (described in Pattern 235). Close the post-wavespace SDT meeting by teaching the clients the "Let It Go" ritual.

**Related.** Patterns 126 Create a space of not knowing, 128 Symbolic action; Tools Commitment Statement / Closing Ritual, Reports — Act Day Final Report-Out, Executive Summary; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Creative Process, Design Formation.

### 264 — Engaging deliverables

**The pattern.** Create comprehensive deliverables that document the outcomes of a session and memorialize them in an engaging physical form.

**Why it matters.** It isn't enough to get to a solution — the group has to *know* they got there and be able to prove it. The word *virtual* originally meant something with physical substance and consequence (from Latin *virtus*, "excellence, potency"); now it means the opposite, and we record everything in ephemeral digital media on platforms that won't be readable in five years. Jeff Rothenberg of MIT Media Lab: "Digital lasts forever, or five years, whichever comes first." Walter Isaacson, working on his Leonardo biography, noted Leonardo's 7,000 paper notebook pages are easier to access today than Steve Jobs' 1990s emails were even to Jobs' own tech people — and that a university librarian told him the best way to preserve emails was to print them out and put them in a box. After a long Act Day with parallel teams working on pieces of a complex problem, participants don't see how the whole solution fits together until the final report-out — and skepticism can seep back in. The deliverable is the synthesis that gives them the Big Picture and confirms the achievement.

**When to use.** Designed from the first SDT meeting, drafted from the SDT Post Day Circle Up, and delivered within a week of session end.

**The move.** Produce three kinds of deliverables. (1) An **On-line Journal** — the complete record, all assignments, high-resolution photos of WorkWalls and final reports — for completeness. (2) An **Executive Summary** — printed, with high production values, heavy on participant photos and visuals over blocks of text, in the voice of the group, finalized within a week and distributed widely. (3) A **lagniappe** (New Orleans for "a small bonus gift") — a framed group photo, a Facebook collection, a session playlist — given from the KreW to the participants. The Executive Summary tells the group what it achieved, speaks for the SDT, and travels to people not in the room. Printed Executive Summaries get displayed in clients' offices for years. Get permission to use them with future prospective clients. Make it wonderful.

**Related.** Patterns 246 Build StrongMemory, 247 Use the power of compelling visuals, 261 Make the solution last past Act Day; Tools Act Day Deliverable Wall, Act Day Check-In, Act Day Final Report-Out, Executive Summary, On-line Journal, PatchWorks Architecture, Scouting Reports, SDT Post Day Circle Up, Single Frame Graphics, Transformation Roadmap; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, The Art of Making, Design Formation.

## 300 — Learning Facilitation

### 301 — Preparation is the antidote to fear

**The pattern.** For the facilitator-in-training, there is no substitute for thorough preparation; it is the only honest way to handle the dread that comes with leading a wavespace.

**Why it matters.** The wavespace context loads a beginner with stressors at once: a mission-critical problem, competing agendas, an anxious Sponsor Design Team, a KreW that may be more experienced than the facilitator, the pressure to design and run multi-day sessions with countless moving parts, plus the physical demands of the schedule. Evans cites Roger Ailes' *You Are the Message* — "There are only four things people you communicate with won't forgive you for: not being prepared, comfortable, committed, and interesting" — and Chuck Close's "Inspiration is for amateurs. The rest of us just show up and get to work." Preparation is what lets the rest of the patterns work; without it, fear runs the room.

**When to use.** In the weeks and days before any session you lead — and especially before your first, second, and third wavespace.

**The move.** Master four bodies of knowledge before you stand up: the client's situation across Rational/Emotional/Political dimensions; the SDT's hopes and what's personally at stake for each member; what is actually hard about this session; what veteran facilitators have done with similar challenges; and the skills and talents of your KreW. Build process knowledge by working as a KnowledgeWorker first. Protect calendar time for prep and for taking care of yourself physically — don't overschedule to look like a good team player.

**Related.** Patterns 302, 304, 306; Tools This Approach to the Art of Facilitation; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Levels of Competency, Path of Learning: The Five Points of Mastery.

### 302 — Fake it 'til you make it

**The pattern.** Once you are prepared, act as if you are already the facilitator your client needs — you are representing a tradition, not just yourself.

**Why it matters.** New facilitators know intellectually what should happen next but lack the muscle memory; clients, like lions smelling fear, pick up on it. "Acting as if" is grounded in research — Amy Cuddy's power-posing work shows posture changes cortisol and testosterone, and neuroscientists tell us the brain doesn't distinguish vivid visualization from lived experience. Evans recalls his colleague Willi Railo coaching Scandinavian sports stars like Mats Wilander and Grete Waitz on visualization. The word courage comes from *coeur/cor* — heart — and means acting with heart in throat. This pattern only works as the partner of 301 — prepare first, then act as if.

**When to use.** In the moment, when you are about to lead and your inexperience is a liability you cannot afford to telegraph.

**The move.** Speak as a member of the larger community of practice — "what we do in instances like this." Maintain self-possession; your emotional tone will powerfully influence participants. Watch yourself on tape and critique yourself as if a stranger. Co-facilitate with a more experienced partner, or use a back-of-room conversation partner. One ASE facilitator-in-training developed by mastering three areas in order: Process Knowledge (KreWing as a KnowledgeWorker), Design Knowledge (strawdog development), and Transition Knowledge (smooth handoffs while letting go).

**Related.** Patterns 301, 309; Tools Graphical Strawdog, KnowledgeWorker Roles; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Levels of Competency, Path of Learning: The Five Points of Mastery.

### 303 — It's not about YOU

**The pattern.** Self-conscious focus on your own performance is the rookie's trap; the real measure is whether your clients reach their objectives.

**Why it matters.** Quoting Reverend Forrest Church — "Consciousness and self-consciousness are opposites." Beginners naturally fixate on "How am I doing?" while juggling voice, presence, SDT communication, on-the-fly redesign, assignment writing, and energy management — all real skills, none of them ends in themselves. Bill Hoogterp's *Own the Room* gives you the public-speaking foundation; servant-leadership thinkers like Robert Greenleaf give you the orientation. Your real job is to work with the KreW to create the conditions for GroupGenius (Pattern 212) to emerge.

**When to use.** Continuously, but especially when you catch yourself measuring the day by your own performance highlights instead of the group's outcomes.

**The move.** Get over yourself. Put everything you know and can do at the service of your client. Cultivate your "observing self" — the part that watches your own performance more or less objectively — and create the kind of environment in which your PFs and KreW will give you honest, corrective feedback. Celebrate strong performances as performances, then return to the only valid measure: their results.

**Related.** Patterns 301, 306, 309, 323, 324, 326; Tools Mobilization, Rational/Emotional/Political Model, SDT Prep Day Circle Up, This Approach to the Art of Facilitation; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Levels of Competency, Path of Learning: The Five Points of Mastery.

### 304 — Magic is hard work

**The pattern.** What participants experience as "magic" is in fact a learnable craft — and like stage magic, it depends on the active (often unconscious) participation of the audience.

**Why it matters.** Mark Pinto, an early member of EY's Chicago ASE and a practicing stage magician for twelve years, taught the team how magic actually works. His core insight: there is no such thing as real physical magic — the magician creates magic *inside the heads of the audience*. Almost every trick is in the magic section of any library, freely available, because "people don't want to do magic; they want magic done to them." No magic occurs without the subject giving permission, whether they know it or not. The wavespace facilitator is doing the same thing: constructing collaborative environments, sequencing activities, writing assignments, guiding groups through nonlinear discovery — none of which is magic, all of which is craft. Evans explicitly addresses whether virtual sessions can be magical and concludes yes, with the same insights and hard work.

**When to use.** Whenever participants (or you yourself) start mistaking the wavespace for sorcery rather than craft.

**The move.** Treat your skills as learnable. Get clients to suspend disbelief and consent to a nonlinear path — that consent is what lets the "magic" occur. Use the audience's own permission, focus, and imagination as part of your medium. Remember that learning the approach is hard work for the whole KreW, and executing it is hard work for the participants too.

**Related.** Patterns 226, 235, 301, 312; Tools wavespace Opening, Sleep on It / Homework for Act Day; Models Feedback of a Complex Kind, The Field, Zone of Emergence.

### 305 — Trust in the process (and the KreW)

**The pattern.** Trust the wavespace process to do its work, and trust the process itself to build the trust your clients need to have in you.

**Why it matters.** The nonlinear wavespace doesn't match anyone's mental model of a sound (linear) path to a solution, which causes anxiety in sponsors, participants, and even the beginning facilitator. Evans recounts a Harvard School of Education session where a world-renowned psychologist tried to seize control halfway through Focus Day to make it more "respectable" and "how we do things at Hah-vahd" — his colleagues were having none of it. Trust is a function of Competence + Connection + Character, divided by Risk. Every relationship has a "trust account" with deposits and withdrawals; surprise is the enemy of trust, predictability its ally. The trust the SDT, KreW, and group develop in you is built incrementally through Preparation, Launch, and Delivery.

**When to use.** From first SDT contact through the Synthesis Conversation — but especially when angst (yours or theirs) tempts you to grab the wheel.

**The move.** In SDT meetings, unpack the logic behind each process step and respond fully to concerns; send an updated Workshop Design Document after every meeting as concrete proof of listening. Empower the KreW to challenge you — they have effective veto power over rookie progress. In your opening, make it relational: get nonverbal response from every member, and seed predictions ("On day two we'll double the output of day one...") so the group's experience confirms your competence. Use the Scan-Focus-Act formula as a visible reliable guide.

**Related.** Patterns 202, 212, 309; Tools Scan-Focus-Act, SDT Planning Session, Workshop Design Document, wavespace Opening, Synthesis Conversation; Models Brain/Mind/Body, Feedback of a Complex Kind, Levels of Competency, Valid Criticism.

### 306 — Move toward the fear

**The pattern.** The best way to manage fear — your clients' and your own — is to move toward it, exploring it openly so it stops running the session from underground.

**Why it matters.** Even after decades and tens of thousands of sessions, the wavespace is so different from a normal meeting that it inspires fear in everyone with skin in the game. Luis Vargas of the Achuar Nation: "When you encounter a jaguar, in dreams or rainforest, you must move toward the jaguar. Touch it and turn it to be your friend." Cass Sunstein and Reid Hastie in *Wise* divide leaders into the complacent and the anxious, and conclude groups do well precisely when they have anxious leaders who aggregate dispersed information. Willi Railo, before he was Scandinavia's leading management consultant, was a sports psychologist whose main job was keeping athletes in the optimal anxiety range — Goldilocks, not too much, not too little.

**When to use.** In SDT planning when fears are unspoken, and in-session when a group stalls without explanation.

**The move.** In the SDT Planning Session, ask directly: "What's hard about this? What are you worried about?" Evans recounts a bank-merger session that ground to a halt until an IT executive admitted his real fear — that the team supporting the losing system would walk out — at which point a solution became designable. Pay attention to your own anxiety as facilitator information; let it tell you where to spend extra attention; test whether it's tapping into a real threat.

**Related.** Patterns 301, 305, 308, 323, 324, 328; Tools SDT Planning Session, Workshop Design Document; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Best Case Worst Case, Brain/Mind/Body, Path of Learning: The Five Points of Mastery.

### 307 — Make the models useful

**The pattern.** All models are wrong; hold yours lightly, take them seriously, and judge them only by what they let you and the group do.

**Why it matters.** Quoting Korzybski ("the map is not the territory"), George Box ("essentially, all models are wrong, but some are useful"), and Georg Rasch (a model is "never accepted finally, only on trial"), Evans grounds the facilitator's relationship to the MG Taylor Modeling Language in epistemic humility. Our perception itself is model-bound — we see what we have a concept for. The (probably apocryphal but useful) story of Caribbean natives unable to "see" the European galleons because they had no concept for them; Buckminster Fuller arguing "sunrise" should be "sunsight" given a heliocentric universe. Models are real things in their own right with substance, and experts are particularly prone to over-privilege their current expertise against contrary feedback.

**When to use.** When opening a session (Model Talk), when introducing any framework mid-session, and when an expert in the room mistakes their model for the territory.

**The move.** Run the Model Talk in the wavespace Opening — Socratic dialogue holding up a model airplane: What is this? Is it real? Is it good or bad? (You can't say until you know how it'll be used.) When are models dangerous? Use the MG Taylor models like a coach — introduce them only when the group is ready to use them, present each one as an aid to thinking *now*, in the present instance. Do your homework on the modeling language and return to it; a once-through is not enough.

**Related.** Patterns 227; Tools wavespace Opening; Models What Is a Model?

### 308 — Tell the truth with courage and compassion

**The pattern.** Most organizations don't practice truth-telling; the facilitator is uniquely positioned to install that norm — with both fierce honesty and felt care.

**Why it matters.** Stephen M.R. Covey's "trust tax" — low-trust organizations pay it right off the top. Hierarchy, status, conventions of politeness, and the experience of underrepresented groups all suppress candor. Courage comes from Latin *cor*, heart — to act with your heart in your throat. Compassion comes from ecclesiastical Latin *com-pati*, to suffer with. Evans distinguishes judgment (moral, anointed-to-reward-and-punish, communicates disdain) from evaluation (relative value in this context, accepting the way things are now). The image he uses is "finger in the chest plus an embrace," learned in his early prison and drug-treatment work — a hard truth offered out of love and respect.

**When to use.** From the very first SDT meeting through the Synthesis Conversation; whenever the elephant in the room is calcifying the group.

**The move.** With the SDT, ask: What's hard? Where do people have trouble facing reality? What topics are off-limits? Is there an open secret no one will name? If they don't bring it up, you should. Use the *ludic* path (Legends, the literal stuffed Elephant in the Room covered with hard-topic cards) and the *via negativa* (Contingency Diagram, Why It Won't Work, Future State Red Team) to climb the candor gradient. Model courageous feedback yourself in report-out dialogue.

**Related.** Patterns 121, 205, 224, 306, 328; Tools Contingency Diagram, Elephant in the Room, Future State Scenarios, Legends, Rational/Emotional/Political, SDT Planning Session, Synthesis Conversation, Why It Won't Work; Models Feedback of a Complex Kind, Valid Criticism, The Transition Manager's Creed.

### 309 — Conscious co-facilitation

**The pattern.** When two facilitators co-lead, work intentionally, explicitly, and in alignment about who owns what — otherwise unconscious hierarchy will assign roles for you.

**Why it matters.** Humans are hierarchical creatures and project assumptions onto situations — "usually it's the old straight white guy" who gets perceived as in charge (says Evans, a self-described card-carrying old straight white guy). When one facilitator drifts into an "alpha" role, the other gets cast as subservient, which damages the second facilitator's credibility for future sessions and over-privileges one reputation over another, harming individual and system development.

**When to use.** Whenever two facilitators jointly lead any session, and especially when a newer facilitator is paired with a more experienced one.

**The move.** Introduce the conscious co-facilitation concept to the client and any engagement team at the earliest opportunity — set the expectation of a facilitation team. Agree explicitly on roles; the more design and planning you do together before the session, the more you become a team. Inform the SDT and KreW of how responsibilities are divided. Announce the dual approach to participants early. Develop nonverbal signals for switching the lead. See yourselves as a tag team — cover each other's mistakes, build on each other's comments, focus on making your partner look good.

**Related.** Patterns 216, 303, 305, 330; Tools SDT Planning Session, wavespace Opening; Models Feedback of a Complex Kind, Path of Learning: The Five Points of Mastery.

### 310 — Shifting group energy

**The pattern.** Read the energy of the group like a sailor reads winds and tides, and use everything available — including your own personal energy — to move it where the work requires.

**Why it matters.** Bill Hoogterp's *Own the Room* notes the average adult attention span is 20 minutes; without periodic relief, focus drifts. Different phases of work need different energies — reflective, quietly confident, physical, humorous, attentively focused, anxious, even braked. Evans cites Anthony Robbins ("If what you are doing isn't working, try anything else!"), recalling that he produced Robbins' first Firewalk Experience workshops in Boston and New York at the start of Robbins' career. The deeper craft is Milton H. Erickson's "accepting and utilizing" — meeting people where they are, then leading — popularized by Bandler and Grinder as pacing and leading. Evans warns these are powerful techniques requiring real ethical discipline.

**When to use.** Continuously throughout the session, in micro-decisions about what to do next.

**The move.** Become a student of body language and facial expressions — let those, not the agenda or the clock, tell you when to move on. Use Chip Saltsman's "Standing Factor" — the ratio of standing to sitting in a breakout group — as an engagement gauge. Trust KreW reports. Manage your own energy first ("if you don't have energy, you don't have an audience"); visualize your personal energy field extending throughout the venue. Use a "humor reverse" — Willi Railo taught Evans that when a group laughs, their guard drops, and that's the moment to land the serious point. Use Circle Ups to align KreW interventions. Deploy KreW as silent witnesses around a difficult Synthesis Conversation, or into breakout groups.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 122, 127, 228, 243; Tools Chat Rooms, Gallery Walk, Knowledge Wall, Legends, Panel Discussions, Synthesis Conversation, Take-A-Panel, TED Talks; Models Being the There, Brain/Mind/Body, The Field, Net Vector.

### 311 — Don't open on Broadway

**The pattern.** Build your facilitation skills somewhere other than the geography or organizational context where you'll eventually be working as a lead.

**Why it matters.** First impressions persist. Daniel Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* describes System One — the automatic, near-uncontrollable mode that hands out halo effects and snap judgments. A facilitator-in-training stumbling visibly in front of home-turf colleagues, sponsors, or engagement partners may be permanently cast as "not ready for prime time." Old colleagues find it especially hard to update mental models of someone they knew in a different role. The metaphor is from Broadway — new productions tour Hartford, Kansas City, Philadelphia to work out problems before facing the New York Times critics. The EY/Capgemini ASE's geographic spread made this strategy feasible: facilitators could be deployed away from their home center for early sessions.

**When to use.** During on-the-job training as a new facilitator, and again whenever you're trying out genuinely new material.

**The move.** Wherever possible, get assigned to early lead sessions outside your home center, industry segment, or known peer group. In smaller organizations where geographic distance isn't available, find friendly small groups for early at-bats. Once you've made the Big Time, keep working on your craft — the only way to stay on Broadway is to keep making better mistakes.

**Related.** Patterns 301, 304; Tools This Approach to the Art of Facilitation; Models Feedback of a Complex Kind, Levels of Competency.

### 312 — Facilitating Online DesignSessions

**The pattern.** Everything you know about good facilitation transposes online — only more so — but the medium demands new technology, new tactics, and a real home production studio.

**Why it matters.** Written in December 2020, this is Evans' partner pattern to 235 Designing Virtual Sessions, drawing on roughly 25 weeks of pandemic-era work versus the 25-year base of every other pattern. His finding: the patterns, tools, and models are as true and useful as ever — the transposition is real but the essentials persist (design, empathy, engagement, creativity, iteration, documentation). The musical metaphor: transposing a key requires knowing how the original mood is produced. Online drains the subtle multimodal cues humans evolved to read; everyone's home/work focus is impaired; and yet brains are still brains and groups still need someone to facilitate.

**When to use.** Any virtual session — and Evans expects virtual to remain pervasive even after in-person returns.

**The move.** Build a real home studio: dedicated camera, professional mic (not optional), proper lighting, multiple monitors, separate back-channel device (iChat/WhatsApp, never the platform's "private" chat), adjustable desk, bandwidth, props within reach. Frame your face filling the screen, top of head cropped. Apply Bill Hoogterp's Own the Room rules: start precisely on time, start hot (no preambles), give them a problem every few minutes, use Chat with summarization, always call on people (Evans uses a Rainbow Burning Man Top Hat for fairness), use thumb-o-meters and finger polls, "Imagine..." as a magic word, Kotter Groups in breakouts. Use 4D communication — strong language (7%), tone (38%), face/gestures (55%), and your full physical space. Build larger KreWs with new roles (VPF, VRchitect, PAX Tech Support, scribes, breakout facilitator/recorders). Document with an On-Line Journal and Executive Summary — a Miro board is not a record. Above all, bring spirit and humanity to a medium that defaults to perfunctory.

## 320 — Mastering Facilitation

### 321 — On becoming a great lover

**The pattern.** Mastery of facilitation is impossible without genuine love for the work — and love is a choice you renew, not a chemistry that lasts on its own.

**Why it matters.** Citing Malcolm Gladwell's Outliers and Daniel Levitin's research, Evans grounds mastery in the ten-thousand-hour rule — and notes that the dedication required is unsustainable without love. He distinguishes infatuation (chemistry, the spark, the "falling in love") from love (quantum physics, a matter of choice). The human nervous system is built to crave novelty and habituate to persistent stimuli — look at any vivid color long enough and the eye sees gray. Lasting love — for a person or for the work — requires applying mind and will to keep the heart open as initial chemistry fades. Stewart Emery: "Passion and love are internal resources that you choose to invest in the world." Evans leans on Steven Pressfield's *The War of Art* and *Do the Work* on resistance — the inevitable internal foe of any creative commitment.

**When to use.** At the start of your facilitation journey, and at every plateau, slump, or temptation to quit thereafter.

**The move.** Make the choice — and remake it again and again. Expect resistance (internal more than external) and prepare to fight it. Do the work. Remember W.N. Murray's lines from *The Scottish Himalayan Expedition*: "Until one is committed there is hesitancy.... The moment one definitely commits oneself, then Providence moves, too." Then, on the other side of recommitment, expect to fall in love with the work all over again.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 245, 303; Tools Multi-Faith Reflections, SDT Planning Session; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, The Art of Making, Right Livelihood.

### 322 — The more you learn, the more you learn

**The pattern.** Mastery is not a destination but a commitment to continuous learning — staying on the edge to make new and finer distinctions.

**Why it matters.** Evans' graduation ritual for ASE facilitators paired two figurines: Mickey Mouse as the sorcerer's apprentice (activating powers beyond his control) and Yoda (ancient Jedi master). We are always somewhere on the continuum between them. Star Wars also offers Darth Vader as the dark option; Evans and Matt Taylor explicitly withheld certain powerful techniques from immature trainees and worked to remove untrustworthy actors. The Levels of Competency model — unconscious incompetence to conscious incompetence to conscious competence to unconscious competence — describes the path. Csikszentmihalyi's *Flow* gives the inner experience: set overall and sub-goals, measure progress, make finer distinctions, develop skills, raise the stakes when bored. Carolyn Buck Luce's "Decade Game" reframes it: each decade has 87,600 hours, minus 30,000 for sleep — enough time to become world-class in five entirely new areas of mastery, every decade.

**When to use.** After every formal "graduation" or recognition — and, really, at every plateau where the fantasy of having arrived starts to seduce you.

**The move.** Commit to continuous learning. Establish conditions for satisfaction for your work and assess hard-nosed. Train yourself to observe minute differences. Record new distinctions as you discover them; review regularly. Formulate hypotheses about what works and test them next time. Bring KreW and colleagues into the conversation for fresh perspectives. Beware of the "weak clones" trap — adopting external trappings (scribes, whiteboards, toys) without the deep structure.

**Related.** Patterns 208, 301, 302, 305; Tools This Approach to the Art of Facilitation; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Five Es of Education, Levels of Competency, Right Livelihood.

### 323 — Develop your capacity for awareness

**The pattern.** Master facilitators cultivate the ability to observe themselves and their own perceptual lens — without judgment, grounded in the body, without trying to change what they observe.

**Why it matters.** Humans are born early and unfinished, "completed" by circles of care that teach us what to attend to and what matters — at the cost of teaching us *one particular* way to perceive. Quoting Buddha ("we are what we think"), José Ortega y Gasset ("Tell me what you pay attention to and I will tell you who you are"), and Csikszentmihalyi (attention is psychic energy, finite, that determines what enters consciousness), Evans argues that attention is the foundational tool. Daniel Goleman's *Emotional Intelligence* gives the concept of the amygdala hijack — overwhelming emotional responses out of proportion to immediate stimuli, triggered by older, deeper threats. Without self-observation, you stay hostage to inherited reactions; *with* it, you eventually develop the foresight to perceive an incoming impression as it enters your body and choose a response.

**When to use.** As an ongoing daily practice, and especially in the moment when a stimulus is about to provoke an unconscious reaction.

**The move.** Practice three principles of self-observation: (1) observe without judgment — let what is, be as it is; (2) ground observations in the body — breathe, allow energy to flow without directing it; (3) observe without trying to change what you observe (the spiritual schools' "not-doing" or "stopping the world"). Expect to notice in hindsight first — catching yourself in old patterns. With time, you'll catch yourself in the moment, then eventually develop foresight.

**Related.** Patterns 225, 245, 305, 308, 324; Tools wavespace Opening, Synthesis Conversation; Models 22 Aspects of Memory, 22 Habits of Creative People, 2know2act2know, Brain/Mind/Body, The Field, Solution Box.

### 324 — Cultivate your self-transforming mind

**The pattern.** Adult psychological development continues across life through stages of mental complexity; master facilitators actively seek transformational learning to grow into the self-transforming mind.

**Why it matters.** Evans was Bob Kegan's teaching fellow at Harvard in the early 1980s, and draws extensively on Kegan's revolution in adult developmental psychology. Kegan's three adult plateaus from *Immunity to Change*: (1) the Socialized Mind — shaped by group expectations, "groupthink as virtue"; (2) the Self-Authoring Mind — internal seat of judgment, personal code, but stuck inside its own filter; (3) the Self-Transforming Mind — can stand back and look *at* the filter, not just through it, holding any model as incomplete. Kegan's data show most of us operate at lower levels than we believe; the highest stage is rare. "We are in over our heads." Ron Heifetz's distinction between technical challenges (known expertise applied) and adaptive challenges (require transforming the mindset of those involved) maps directly to wavespace work, which is uniquely capable of holding adaptive problems but only when the facilitator can lead at that level. Kegan's "immunity to change" — our hidden commitments that keep us exactly where we are — is the obstacle to overcome.

**When to use.** As a lifelong, never-ending practice; especially when you find yourself reaching for old, technically-comfortable solutions to adaptive problems.

**The move.** Actively seek opportunities and contexts for transformational learning. Map your own immunity to change — the behaviors that undermine your aspirations (Evans gives the example of the facilitator committed to better delegation but unconsciously committed to being the smartest designer in the room). Surround yourself with people on the path of growth. Recognize physical and emotional signals when your immunity is activated. Be prepared to fail BIG. Keep at it.

**Related.** Patterns 303, 306, 307, 323, 326; Tools Rational/Emotional/Political Model, This Approach to the Art of Facilitation; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, 2know2act2know, Being the There, Brain/Mind/Body, The Curve of Action, Feedback of a Complex Kind, Levels of Competency.

### 325 — Create a space for transformation

**The pattern.** You cannot force transformation; you can only create the conditions in which it might emerge — a space the master facilitator holds with King-archetype energy.

**Why it matters.** Change can be imposed; transformation is emergent, organic — more like tending a garden than fixing a clock. Stewart Emery's airplane conversation with a biological-warfare expert became the touchstone: "You create conditions favorable to the explosive growth of bacteria, and you can trust them to do their thing." Stewart's conclusion — "results turn up in the space you create for them to exist in" — is the core of this pattern. Drawing on Robert Moore and Douglas Gillette's *King, Warrior, Magician, Lover*, Evans frames the facilitator's stance as the King archetype — a Jungian energy available to all genders, characterized by self-sufficiency in service to the group, two functions: instilling order and bestowing blessing. Evans closes with the imaginal-cells biology of caterpillar metamorphosis — dormant cells, attacked initially by the immune system, that persist, multiply, resonate together, and become the butterfly — as the metaphor for the work he and Carolyn Buck Luce founded Imaginal Labs to do.

**When to use.** From the first SDT contact through the closing moments of any session where transformation (not mere change) is the goal.

**The move.** Get your ego out of the way — you are not the boss of this process, you are its steward. Express King energy through personal integrity that radiates through the room. *Instill order* by mastering the moving parts and creating a well-ordered environment from the moment participants arrive. *Bestow blessing* by seeing the group at its best — even when there's a gap between behavior and that vision — while remaining willing to be fierce in protecting the boundary conditions. Hold space for the possibility that something astonishing can emerge. Be the first one to know it can.

**Related.** Patterns 204, 205, 211, 221, 222, 227; Tools Best Case/Worst Case Planning, Bus Exercise, Future State Scenarios, Magic Buttons, Metaphors, Question Storming, Spoze, Zwicky Box; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, Being the There, Best Case Worst Case, The Field, Transforming Organization, Zone of Emergence.

### 326 — Anything worth doing is worth doing badly

**The pattern.** The path to mastery in any creative work begins with doing it badly, repeatedly — your taste will outrun your skill for years, and that's the deal.

**Why it matters.** Ira Glass's well-known YouTube talk: people new to creative work go through a phase where their taste exceeds their ability, and the only way out is to do a lot of work. David Bayles' *Art & Fear*: a piano student lamented to his Master, "I can hear the music so much better in my head than I can get out of my fingers" — to which the Master replied, "What makes you think that ever changes?" The book's most-cited story is the ceramics teacher who graded one half of a class on a single perfect pot and the other half on pure poundage — and the best pots came from the quantity group while the quality group "had little more to show for their efforts than grandiose theories and a pile of dead clay." Carol Dweck's research: praising children for intelligence impairs learning because they avoid risks; praising effort sustains motivation. Evans tells SDTs that one difference between him and rookie competitors is that he has already made all the rookie mistakes — when they make mistakes together, his will be world-class.

**When to use.** Whenever fear of looking bad threatens to prevent you from making the next attempt.

**The move.** Be willing to fail forward; learn to forgive yourself for these failures. Do the work, lots of it. Praise effort, not innate talent — including in yourself. Contemplate (without trying to do anything with it): mistakes are not good/bad but unskilled/skilled; uncouple your sense of worth from the quality of any single action; pay close attention to your self-talk, since it's the first lever for making better mistakes. Curiosity, not blame, gives you a real shot at improvement.

**Related.** Patterns 224, 244, 305, 306; Tools Contingency Diagram, Empathic Observation: What? How? Why?; Models 22 Habits of Creative People, The Curve of Action, Feedback of a Complex Kind, Levels of Competency, Valid Criticism.

### 327 — Give the gift of detachment

**The pattern.** Take responsibility for the *space* and the *process*, not for the *outcomes* — your detachment from the specific solution is what frees the group to find theirs.

**Why it matters.** When facilitators take responsibility for outcomes, they infantilize the group, make false promises, derail GroupGenius, and deprive clients of the chance to build their own solutions. Steven Karpman's Drama Triangle (Rescuer/Persecutor/Victim) shows how the rescue impulse becomes a no-win game. Facilitators are not subject-matter experts who can engineer a predictable end-state; their challenges are transformational, not technical (Pattern 324). Evans grounds detachment in many spiritual traditions — Buddhism's Four Noble Truths ("Attachment is the root of suffering"), Bhagavad Gita ("The work is yours, but not the fruits thereof"), Lao Tzu, St. John of the Cross, Pema Chodron. He recounts a global pharma session on continuous bioreactor manufacturing where, halfway through Focus Day, a chief scientist declared "I think we have been addressing the wrong problem" — instead of an "Oh crap" moment, the impromptu Synthesis Conversation pivoted the whole session to a better direction.

**When to use.** Whenever the urge rises to drive the group to a specific result — and especially when you, your engagement team, or the SDT have a preferred answer in mind.

**The move.** Detach from the form of the solution, not the rigor of the process. Be fierce in protecting boundary conditions and in accepting nothing less than a solution that meets session objectives, but stay open to where that solution comes from. Trust yourself to Not Know. Get your ego out of the way; give yourself the psychological freedom of not being the center of the universe. Hold the expectation that the group will find a way forward — that expectation is itself your gift to them.

**Related.** Patterns 212, 262, 303, 305, 325; Tools SDT Prep Day Circle Up, Synthesis Conversation; Models Creating the Problem, Creative Process, The Transition Manager's Creed.

### 328 — Take a stand

**The pattern.** Beginning facilitators are taught neutrality; master facilitators know that as designers there is no such thing as value-neutral design — they take a stand for good outcomes, even as they hold no specific position.

**Why it matters.** Evans recommends Doyle and Straus's classic *How to Make Meetings Work* (1976) for foundational neutral facilitation, but argues we are much more — we are designers, and design is never value-neutral. Lynne Twist's *The Soul of Money*: "Taking a stand is a way of living and being that draws on a place within yourself that is at the very heart of who you are. When you take a stand, it gives you authenticity, power, and clarity." The Pachamama Alliance (Twist co-founder; Evans and Carolyn deeply involved) frames a stand as a creative declaration of a vision in the future. Years before Simon Sinek said "start with why," the ASE was explicit that its purpose was to change how the world deals with wicked problems. Evans introduces the Appropriate Response model — six tests of fitness in two sets of three: Efficacious, Proper Scope, True to Nature (functional); Sustainable, Self-Correcting, Anticipatory (living systems).

**When to use.** In session design, in SDT planning, and at the front of the room when an engineered "agreement" is being mistaken for a sound solution.

**The move.** Distinguish stand from position. Do not advocate for a particular answer, but do hold the design accountable to producing answers that survive the six Appropriate Response tests. Be explicit with clients about what you stand for (humane, collaborative ways of solving critical problems) — clients build trust faster when they know what you stand for and watch you live it. Treat the work as fiduciary duty: do not degrade the process by pretending the topic is too pedestrian to merit integrity.

**Related.** Patterns 121, 125, 221, 222, 230, 308; Tools This Approach to the Art of Facilitation, SDT Planning Session, Synthesis Conversation; Models Appropriate Response, Creative Process, Ten Design Assumptions, Zone of Emergence.

### 330 — Feminine and masculine in harmony

**The pattern.** Master facilitators harmonize the masculine and feminine energies in themselves — and ideally in their co-facilitation partnerships — because the work cannot be done well from a single-gender stereotype.

**Why it matters.** Evans cites the 2016 LeanIn.Org/McKinsey "Women in the Workplace" study (men get more promotions, challenging assignments, access to top leaders; women perceive a steeper trek and pull a disproportionate "second shift" at home) and Coqual's research that the implicit leadership archetype in most organizations is "male, pale, and stale." The masculine realm is conventionally active, hierarchical, rational; the feminine is receptive, connected, intuitive. Carl Jung's anima/animus — every human carries both — and the Shadow as the home of unaccepted parts of the self that Jung also identified as the source of creativity. The hyper-masculinity in our society reflects a woundedness in men that prevents accepting the feminine in themselves. The Taoist yin-yang symbol is the iconic image. Joseph Campbell's hero's journey (and Carolyn Buck Luce's parallel work on the heroine's journey, where the heroine descends to reclaim what she sacrificed in succeeding by male rules) frame the second-half-of-life work of individuation. Evans references his men's group with John Gray and other Transformational Leadership Council members in Marin County.

**When to use.** Continuously as personal development; in every co-facilitation pairing decision; in every SDT participant-selection conversation.

**The move.** Do your own inner work to integrate masculine and feminine energies — recognize and manage your Shadow. Co-facilitate with a partner of the other gender when possible: partnerships of opposite-sex facilitators working visibly well together model harmony in a way clients rarely see. Build gender balance and cultural diversity into the KreW. With the SDT, treat gender diversity in participant selection as essential, not optional.

**Related.** Patterns 131, 214, 216, 306, 323; Tools This Approach to the Art of Facilitation; Models Ten Design Assumptions, Right Livelihood.

### 331 — Lather. Rinse. Repeat

**The pattern.** Mastery is never complete — return regularly to beginner's mind to keep the practice alive and the field fresh.

**Why it matters.** Novices console themselves with the prospect of one day "arriving"; on the path to mastery you discover your peaks are temporary resting places. Jim Collins: "No matter how much you have achieved, you will always be merely good relative to that which you can become." Evans cites Jesko von den Steinen — the Cirque du Soleil performer-turned-facilitator: "It takes 8 years to master a craft and then 8 years to forget it. To become a master, you must learn how to escape the craft you spent so much time learning." The pattern's title comes from the (probably apocryphal) advertising-world tale: a copywriter doubled shampoo sales by adding one word to the bottle — *Repeat*. Japanese Zen Buddhism's *shoshin* — beginner's mind — and Shunryu Suzuki: "In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities; in the expert's mind there are few." Evans notes that compiling these patterns has itself been his way of re-encountering the deep practices of the work.

**When to use.** At the end of every session, after every formal recognition of mastery, and whenever you catch yourself feeling expert.

**The move.** Adopt the discipline of looking at familiar problems with fresh eyes. Use the KreW (especially the newer KnowledgeWorkers) to ask the beginner's-mind questions — they often see what you've stopped seeing. Drop the questions into Circle Ups: What are we learning here? What is just emerging with this group? What does what we are experiencing tell us about this group, given there is no "out there"? Find your own ways of staying fresh — teaching new KnowledgeWorkers, compiling patterns, exploring with master colleagues. Encounter the creative void, that source of everything new — your colleagues are excited to see what you bring into being.

**Related.** Patterns 101, 126, 130, 245, 303, 322, 324 (and, as the source notes, related to everything); Tools Contingency Diagram, Spoze; Models Being the There, Cathedral Builders, Path of Learning: The Five Points of Mastery.
