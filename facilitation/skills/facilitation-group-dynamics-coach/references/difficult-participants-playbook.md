# Difficult participants playbook

Specific scenarios and the moves that work. From Klein & Newman's *Facilitating Collaboration* Chapter 7, Collaboration Code Patterns 250s, and field-tested practitioner moves.

## The principle

Most "difficult" participants are doing rational things in their context. The dominator is filling a vacuum the design left open. The silent one is following cultural or personal rules that have served them. The skeptic is protecting against past organizational pain. Treat behavior as information about the room, not as personal flaw.

## The catalog

### "I tried that already"
**Pattern**: Every idea is met with "we tried X in 2019 and it didn't work."

**What's underneath**: Past organizational pain. Often the person was burned by a previous attempt that didn't have leadership support, or that was poorly executed, or that was the right idea at the wrong time.

**Moves**:
1. **Acknowledge specifically** — "Tell me more about what happened in 2019. What got in the way?"
2. **Use it as data** — "What you're naming is real. Let's design around it."
3. **Reframe** — "We're not doing the same thing. Here's what's different now."
4. **Channel** — "Be our pre-mortem. When we converge, you'll tell us what would kill it."
5. **Name the pattern if it persists** — "I notice you're carrying a lot of past attempts. Can we agree to name them as risks but not let them block exploration?"

### The deflector
**Pattern**: Every conversation routes back to a different topic — usually one they care about.

**What's underneath**: They have a real concern that hasn't been heard, or they're trying to control the agenda.

**Moves**:
1. **Acknowledge and park** — "That's important. Let's add it to the parking lot for the right time."
2. **Make the parking lot real** — visible, owned, revisited later
3. **If repeated** — "I notice we keep coming back to X. Let's spend 5 minutes on it now and then return to our agenda."
4. **If chronic** — pre-meeting conversation: "Help me understand what you need to discuss in this session."

### The interrupter
**Pattern**: Cuts off others. Often well-meaning ("building on what you said...") but silences the original speaker.

**Moves**:
1. **Mid-interrupt** — "Hold that. Sarah, finish your thought." Then: "Mark, your turn."
2. **Norm setting** — "We hear each person fully before responding. I'll enforce this."
3. **Round-robin** — structurally prevents interruption
4. **Coaching** — privately, "I noticed you're building on people's ideas before they finish. I want to make space for them to land first."

### The CEO / authority who's "just here to listen"
**Pattern**: Says they're observing. Their first comment recalibrates the entire room toward their preference.

**Moves**:
1. **Pre-meeting conversation** — "Your speaking pattern shapes what others say. I'd like you to speak last in ideation rounds. Are you OK with that?"
2. **Position physically** — not at the head of the table; not on a stage
3. **Use silent generation first** — everyone writes before any speaks
4. **Round-robin from quietest first**
5. **Name the dynamic if helpful** — "It's hard to ideate freely with the boss in the room. Let's structure around it."

### The expert who knows everything
**Pattern**: Subject matter expertise becomes a way to invalidate other voices. "Actually, that's wrong because..."

**What's underneath**: Identity attachment to expertise; fear that not being expert means not being needed.

**Moves**:
1. **Honor the expertise** — "You know more than the rest of us about X. Help us frame the question right."
2. **Bound the role** — "We need your expertise on the technical feasibility. The customer-need question is for the room."
3. **Pattern 213 (*Careful use of experts*)** — design experts in for specific moments, not whole sessions
4. **Use Fishbowl Conversation** — expert speaks, then steps out; group reflects without expert

### The agenda-saboteur
**Pattern**: Repeatedly questions the design. "Why are we doing this exercise? Couldn't we just discuss it?"

**What's underneath**: Discomfort with structure; preference for free-form discussion (which often serves them); or genuine design concern.

**Moves**:
1. **Acknowledge the question** — design choices are legitimate to question
2. **Explain the why briefly** — "We're doing silent generation so dominant voices don't shape the field. We'll discuss after."
3. **Hold the design** — Pattern 305 (*Trust in the process*); don't abandon under pressure
4. **Re-contract if persistent** — "It seems like the structure isn't working for you. Help me understand what would."
5. **Sponsor escalation if deeper** — sponsor and facilitator together address; don't fight design battles in front of the room

### The withdrawer
**Pattern**: Disengages mid-session — phone, side conversations, slumped posture.

**What's underneath**: Bored, lost, dismissed, anxious, or in another fire.

**Moves**:
1. **Notice without naming publicly** — energy break, mid-session shift
2. **Modality change** — verbal → written, sitting → moving
3. **Direct invitation** — "Raj, I want your read on this. What are you noticing?"
4. **Private check at break** — "How's it landing? Is there something I should adjust?"
5. **Accept gracefully** — sometimes people are dealing with something else; you can't always recover them

### The crier
**Pattern**: Strong emotion surfaces — tears, raised voice, visible distress.

**What's underneath**: The work touched something real. This is not failure; it's data.

**Moves**:
1. **Don't rescue** — silence is OK; let them have the moment
2. **Brief acknowledgment** — "Take your time" or "We're with you"
3. **Offer privacy if needed** — "Want to step out for a minute?"
4. **Don't make it the topic** — the room moves on after a moment of grace
5. **Check in privately at the next break**
6. **Pattern 132 (*Brave space*)** — if the work was designed for brave conversation, real emotion is appropriate

### The sniper from chat (virtual)
**Pattern**: In virtual sessions, side commentary in chat — sarcasm, dismissal, off-topic asides.

**Moves**:
1. **Acknowledge in voice, not chat** — "I see something in chat. Let's talk about it." Brings it into the room.
2. **Ask the chat-sniper to elaborate verbally** — most stop when called on
3. **Norm-set early** — chat is for clarification and contributions, not commentary
4. **Coach co-facilitator** — they monitor chat in real time; you focus on voice

### The drift-into-debate pair
**Pattern**: Two participants engage in extended back-and-forth that excludes the room.

**Moves**:
1. **Park** — "This is a real disagreement. Let's name it as a parking-lot item and come back to it after we've heard from others."
2. **Channel** — "Both of you, take 5 minutes during the break to align and propose to the room."
3. **Use as data** — "The disagreement here probably reflects a wider pattern. Let's check."

### The late arriver / early leaver
**Pattern**: Drops into a multi-day session for "just the part I care about."

**Moves**:
1. **Set expectations in the invite** — full presence or no presence
2. **Don't restart for them** — the meeting doesn't pause to catch them up
3. **Sponsor escalation** — if a key participant is partial-only, the sponsor may need to convert them to full
4. **Accept the limit** — sometimes partial is what's available; design around it

## The meta-rule: every "difficult" person is your teacher

The patterns above teach you what your design is missing. A persistent dominator is teaching you that your design needs more silent generation. A persistent skeptic is teaching you that your sponsor mobilization wasn't sufficient. A withdrawn participant is teaching you that the room isn't safe enough yet.

Real difficulty is rare. What looks like difficulty is usually design feedback.

## When to escalate

Some situations exceed facilitation:

- **Harassment, slurs, sustained aggression** — pause the session; escalate to sponsor and HR
- **Trust breakdown that exceeds session-level work** — recommend mediation
- **Participant in genuine mental health crisis** — pause; offer privacy and resources; do not try to facilitate through it
- **Authority abuse (a senior figure punishing dissent in real time)** — name privately to sponsor; do not engage in real time

In these cases, your role is to make space safe and step out of facilitation mode. The session can wait.
