# Gamestorming — full game catalog

Per-game digests for ~120 games from gamestorming.com (the open companion site to Gray, Brown, and Macanufo's *Gamestorming* book). Each entry: object of play, players, duration, how to play (numbered steps), strategy, and source URL.

For the high-level book digest, see [`book-gamestorming.md`](../../../references/book-gamestorming.md).

## How games are organized

The site organizes games by category. A single game often appears in multiple categories — Empathy Map, Affinity Map, World Café, and Cover Story are workhorses across many session types.

| Category | Use when |
|---|---|
| Games for opening | Establishing context, surfacing what's known, warming up |
| Games for fresh thinking and ideas | Ideation, brainstorming, generative work |
| Games for design | Designing artifacts, services, products, processes |
| Games for team-building and alignment | Building shared understanding and trust |
| Games for vision and strategy meetings | Long-horizon thinking, vision, strategy |
| Games for problem-solving | Diagnosing, framing, and resolving problems |
| Games for any meeting | General-purpose tools that work across formats |
| Games for decision-making | Convergent, narrowing, prioritization, voting |

## The Core Games (the book's "essentials")

If you only know seven games, know these: **7Ps Framework**, **Affinity Map**, **Bodystorming**, **Card Sort**, **Dot Voting**, **Empathy Map**, **Forced Ranking**, **Post-Up**, **Storyboard**, **WhoDo**.

These appear in nearly every Gamestorming-driven workshop. They're listed in this catalog along with all the others.

## The catalog (alphabetical)

The digests below are organized in alphabetical/numerical order across all categories. Each entry follows the standard Gamestorming format.

### 3-12-3 Brainstorm

**Object of play.** Compresses an ideation session into one short format — 3 minutes generating observations, 12 combining them into rough concepts, and 3 presenting back. The "ticking clock" forces spontaneous, quick-fire decisions and prevents overthinking.

**Players.** Up to 10 (working in pairs); beyond 10, switch to triads to keep pace.

**Duration.** 21–30 minutes total, depending on number of participants.

**How to play.**
1. Distill the topic to two words (e.g., "energy efficiency," "tomorrow's television"). Avoid the full challenge question up front so the group thinks about defining aspects first.
2. Distribute index cards and markers; explain the rules and start immediately.
3. **3 minutes — Generate a Pool of Aspects.** Each participant writes as many characteristics, nouns, or verbs about the topic as they can, one per card. No filtering.
4. **12 minutes — Develop Concepts.** Divide into pairs; each pair draws three cards at random from the pool. Using those cards as thought-starters, the pair develops a concept (sketch, prototype, or other media) to present back. Reveal a fuller version of the challenge ("How will we become more energy-efficient next quarter?") if needed.
5. **3 minutes — Make Presentations.** Each pair has up to three minutes to present their concept and may show the cards they drew and how those influenced their thinking. After all pairs present, the group reflects.

**Strategy.** Speed is the whole game — traditional brainstorming gets fouled when time isn't of the essence, and short bursts often capture the essential parts of good ideas. After presentations, teams can dig deeper on a single concept, integrate ideas, or vote on which to develop further. Concepts coming out of this game tend to be unusually memorable because participants are bonded in the time-driven stress of creating together. Credited to James Macanufo.

**Source.** [3-12-3 Brainstorm](https://gamestorming.com/3-12-3-brainstorm/)

### 4Cs

**Object of play.** A simple information-splicing game that disrupts standard ways of breaking down a topic. The 4Cs gather and organize what a group knows about any subject using four common key concepts: Components, Characteristics, Challenges, and Characters.

**Players.** 5–20.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, draw a 2×2 matrix on a large wall surface. Label the boxes Components, Characteristics, Characters, and Challenges, and add a small icon for each.
2. Define the terms for the players: Components are parts of the topic (e.g., responsive tweets in a social commerce strategy); Characteristics are features (e.g., speed of response); Challenges are obstacles; Characters are the people associated with the topic.
3. Divide the group into four teams (5–7 per team is fine) and assign each team one "C." Provide sticky notes and markers.
4. Give them 3 minutes to plan an information-gathering strategy, 5 minutes to interview others in the room and collect information, and 3 minutes to analyze and organize their findings on the matrix.
5. Run the timed phases. Stay out of the way during gathering — players move around and interview each other.
6. Have teams analyze their data and post their notes in the matrix.
7. Close by asking volunteers to present their team's findings; ask clarifying questions and invite the rest of the group to add or refine.

**Strategy.** The deliberate speed and slight chaos prevent the meeting from devolving into a list of pre-known information. Even people who think they know the topic will pick up something new from the brief interviews. You can swap the four Cs for other letters (e.g., four Ds: Discover, Design, Damage, Deliver) as long as the categories give a meaningful lens. Don't shortchange the closing — the group reflection is where the real synthesis happens. Based on Matthew Richter's activity in the March 2004 Thiagi GameLetter.

**Source.** [4Cs](https://gamestorming.com/4cs/)

### 6-8-5s

**Object of play.** (Page rendering issue — see source URL for full delivery instructions.) A rapid sketching exercise that generates many concept ideas under heavy time pressure. Each player produces 6–8 distinct sketches of design ideas in 5 minutes, forcing breadth over polish.

**Players.** Small groups; works well with 3–8 designers or stakeholders.

**Duration.** Roughly 15–30 minutes including review (5 minutes per round, plus debrief).

**How to play.**
1. Frame the design challenge clearly so everyone is working on the same problem (e.g., "the home screen of a mobile banking app," or "a way to onboard new hires").
2. Hand out templates: a sheet of paper folded or pre-printed into 6–8 boxes per player. Provide pens or thin Sharpies (no erasers).
3. Set a timer for 5 minutes. Each player must produce 6 to 8 distinct sketches of ideas in that time — one idea per box. Stick figures, boxes, and arrows are encouraged; perfection is forbidden.
4. When time's up, each player posts their sheet on the wall and walks the group through their sketches in 30–60 seconds each, naming each idea.
5. The group dot-votes or marks the most promising ideas. Run a second round to refine, mash up, or push the favorites further.

**Strategy.** The constraint is the point — by demanding 6–8 ideas in 5 minutes, you force participants past their first obvious answer into the territory of the unusual and the playful. Pair this game with a divergent need (early concept exploration) rather than a convergent one. Quiet sketchers often produce the breakthrough ideas; the timed round levels the floor with verbal extroverts. Multiple rounds work better than one long round.

**Source.** [6-8-5s](https://gamestorming.com/6-8-5s/)

### 7Ps Framework

**Object of play.** Every meeting deserves a plan. A great plan can't guarantee a great outcome, but it lays down the fundamentals from which you can adapt. Sketch out those fundamentals using the 7Ps framework as a checklist.

**Players.** Individual (the meeting leader).

**Duration.** 20 minutes to 2 hours.

**How to play.**
1. **Purpose.** Why are you having this meeting? State it clearly and succinctly. Consider urgency — what's on fire? If you can't articulate it, ask whether the meeting is really necessary.
2. **Product.** What specific artifact will the meeting produce? What will it do, and how does it support the purpose? If meetings feel like "all talk and no follow-through," a defined product changes things.
3. **People.** Who needs to be there, and what role will they play? Think in terms of questions and answers — what questions are you answering, and who can answer them?
4. **Process.** What agenda will you use to create the product? Co-design the agenda with attendees in advance — it's the quickest path to engagement.
5. **Pitfalls.** What are the risks, and how will you address them? These can be ground rules ("no laptops") or topics ruled out of scope.
6. **Prep.** What would be useful in advance — pre-reads, research, "homework"?
7. **Practical Concerns.** Logistics — where, when, who's bringing lunch.

**Strategy.** Each P can affect the others — limited participant time changes your process, for example. Get others involved in the design; their participation is the quickest route to an effective meeting. Revisit "Why are we having this?" for recurring meetings, which drift. Make the 7Ps visible in the room as a refocus tool. Always plan, but expect to adapt — the framework can't run the meeting for you. Designed by James Macanufo.

**Source.** [7Ps Framework](https://gamestorming.com/7ps-framework/)

### $100 Test

**Object of play.** A prioritization method in which participants assign relative value to a list of items by spending an imaginary $100 together. Using the concept of cash captures more attention and engagement than an arbitrary point or ranking system.

**Players.** Small groups of 3–5.

**Duration.** Medium; up to 1.5 hours depending on list length and group size (commenters note 20–30 minutes is often plenty).

**How to play.**
1. Set up a matrix listing each item to be prioritized, with columns for the dollar amount spent and the reasoning.
2. Explain the challenge: the group has a collective $100 to spend across the list. The dollars represent importance, and the group must decide together how to allocate.
3. Give the group time to assign values and ask them to write a brief explanation for each amount. If literal cost or effort comes up, table it as a separate discussion (or run it as its own $100 Test) so it doesn't muddy the importance question.
4. When the matrix is complete, ask the group to walk through their decisions and reasoning.
5. Use the matrix as a guidepost for future decisions — which items rank as higher-priority than the others.

**Strategy.** Common in software for prioritizing user features, but useful any time "false scarcity" would help focus a group's wants and needs — for example, an HR group polling employees on benefit options. Variants include Divide the Dollar and a short-form $10 Test. Source unknown.

**Source.** [$100 Test](https://gamestorming.com/100-test/)

### 20/20 Vision

**Object of play.** Get a group aligned on which projects or initiatives should be a higher priority than others. Defining the "bang" together helps ensure that the prioritization process produces real consensus, not just a ranked list.

**Players.** 5–10.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1.5 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, write each proposed project or initiative on its own sticky note. Shuffle and post them in random order — even blind-post — so there's no implicit prioritization on your part.
2. Introduce the game as forced prioritization based on perceived benefits. Stress that consensus on priorities is what moves the organization forward.
3. Take an initiative and ask players to describe its benefits. Capture the descriptions on adjacent sticky notes. If there's disagreement, write down both views; assume validity to multiple perspectives.
4. Repeat for every initiative until benefits are documented for all.
5. Ask if any initiatives are missing; capture and discuss those too.
6. Move to a fresh wall space. Pull two random initiatives and ask which the group agrees is more important.
7. Post the more-important one above the other.
8. Pull the next initiative and ask whether it's more or less important than the two posted; place it accordingly — top is highest priority, bottom is lowest.
9. Repeat until all initiatives are stacked in priority order.

**Strategy.** Describing benefits first lays groundwork for the harder ranking step. Resist the temptation to let the group waffle — they must make hard decisions. The players who resist ranking the most often offer the deepest insight and ultimately help refine the final order. Adapted from Luke Hohmann's *Innovation Games*.

**Source.** [20/20 Vision](https://gamestorming.com/2020-vision/)

### 35

**Object of play.** A quick, energetic way to prioritize a set of items without endless discussion or social influence — similar to 20/20 Vision but using paired comparisons over multiple rounds.

**Players.** 4–50.

**Duration.** 15–45 minutes depending on group size and number of items.

**How to play.**
1. Run another game first (or use a prepared list) to generate the items needing prioritization.
2. Have everyone place items in the middle of the group, one at a time, briefly explaining each.
3. With all items pooled, each attendee selects their "Top" / "Most Important" item out of the pile, going one person at a time. If their first choice is taken, they pick second, third, etc., so everyone ends up holding one card. (For small groups, allow two cards each.)
4. Have people mingle and pair up. Each pair distributes 7 points between their two cards — write the points on the back of each card.
5. Swap cards with each other and find a new partner for the next round.
6. Repeat for five rounds total (5 × 7 = 35).
7. Sum the weights on the back of each card and sort from highest total down to produce the prioritized list.

**Strategy.** Getting consensus on priorities is hard, and 35 makes it fast and repeatable. Because cards constantly change hands and partners shift, no one can game the outcome — each item is being weighed against many others by many different pairings. Even repeated runs of the same items will produce roughly the same sort order, though raw point counts may shift. A great way to reach fast group consensus on priority.

**Source.** [35](https://gamestorming.com/35/)

### Affinity Map

**Object of play.** Brainstorming gets you a high quantity of information; the Affinity Map (or Affinity Diagram) helps you sort and cluster that language-based information into relationships, revealing categories and meta-categories. Use it when you need to find embedded patterns and see where most thinking is concentrated.

**Players.** Up to 20.

**Duration.** Depends on group size; max 1.5 hours.

**How to play.**
1. On a flip-chart sheet, write a question for the players (paired with a quick visual). Run this only when you know the question will generate at least 20 pieces of information.
2. Give each player 10 minutes to silently generate sticky notes responding to the question. (For four or fewer, use index cards on a table.)
3. Collect the ideas and post them on a flat surface visible to everyone.
4. With guidance from the players — and ideally letting them post directly to save time — sort the notes into columns or clusters based on relationships.
5. Create a sticky-note "parking lot" for ideas that don't fit naturally. Don't discard duplicates; leaving them up shows how many people share the thought. At this stage, focus on grouping by affinity, not naming higher categories.
6. Once content is sorted, ask the group to suggest category names for each column or cluster and write the agreed name at the top. If there's disagreement (e.g., "Facilities" vs. "Infrastructure"), keep both rather than burn time debating.

**Strategy.** Value rises with the volume of good data points and the cleanliness of the sorting. Optional: re-shuffle and re-sort the same notes to surface affinities the group missed the first time. When affinities are nuanced, take a stronger facilitative role — too many categories waters down the data, too few waters down the analysis. Find the sweet spot. Devised by Jiro Kawakita in the 1960s; also called the KJ Method.

**Source.** [Affinity Map](https://gamestorming.com/affinity-map/)

### Agile UX Sketching and Scrum

**Object of play.** An agile sketching session structured for Scrum teams: gather many ideas quickly, build shared understanding across the team, and feed concrete sketches back into the release life cycle. "Sketching for understanding" trades polish for speed and shared meaning.

**Players.** A Scrum team (small, cross-functional).

**Duration.** About 30 minutes total across phases.

**How to play.**
1. **Define Inputs (5 min).** Capture the product/feature vision. On individual sticky notes, write possible scenarios where someone would experience the feature, possible functionality that would enhance those scenarios, and a brief assumption persona (age, gender, job title, interests).
2. **Sketch (5 min).** On blank paper, explore as many ideas as possible using boxes, arrows, callouts, and metaphors. Don't stop at the first idea. If stuck, list ideas as words and recombine; or explore opposites (e.g., richly animated vs. flat 2D). Tools: thin Sharpie, sticky notes, 6up and 1up templates. Rules: fast, not perfect; if you mess up, don't start over; if it really goes wrong, grab fresh paper.
3. **Get Organized (10 min).** Post inputs on the wall. Cut up sketches and add them to the wall using drafting dots. Walk the group through the thinking behind each idea. Group similar ideas; label the groupings with sticky notes (so they can move).
4. **Critique (5 min).** Review ideas individually with sticky notes; ask open questions (HOW, WHERE, WHO) — not closed ones (DOES, SHOULD, COULD). Refer back to inputs. Use red sticky notes to document holes and concerns inclusively. Post next to the relevant idea, then review as a team.
5. **Wrap Up (5 min).** Review overall comments (black-hat notes), photograph the wall for future reference, and thank the team — the results inform upcoming iterations.

**Strategy.** Effective critique is selfless — its purpose is to surface thinking, not judge. Open questions keep the conversation generative. Capturing photos preserves the artifact for the rest of the sprint. Authored by Rick Butler, UX Architect and Agile UX Specialist.

**Source.** [Agile UX Sketching and Scrum](https://gamestorming.com/1269/)

### Air Time Mastermind

**Object of play.** Brainstorm multiple solutions to participants' most pressing problems — while feeling like a 7-year-old again. Uses paper airplanes (or props like inflatable soccer balls) to randomize who answers whose question.

**Players.** 10–50.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1.5 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Hand each participant a piece of construction paper.
2. On one side at the top, each writes their biggest, most pressing problem the group can help with; on the reverse side, their name.
3. Push for specificity — "What low- or no-cost ways can I increase referrals for my service business?" beats vague phrasing.
4. Leave plenty of space below the question for multiple answers.
5. Fold each sheet into a paper airplane with the question on the inside of the folds.
6. Decorate each plane individually so participants can find it later.
7. Everyone stands up and tosses their planes into the air.
8. Each participant grabs an airplane that isn't theirs and unfolds it.
9. Read the question and write the best answer you can. Sign it (name or initials).
10. Allow about 3 minutes per round; call time.
11. Refold and re-launch.
12. Repeat for as many rounds as time allows.
13. If you get the same plane twice, swap with a neighbor.
14. At the end, each person retrieves their own plane.
15. Facilitator asks volunteers to share questions with broad appeal and opens group discussion.
16. Optional: silly prizes for best flier, sorriest looking, best design, etc.

**Strategy.** Different perspectives on the same question reveal angles the asker would never reach alone. Quieter participants get airtime they don't normally get because they have introspective time to write. Signed answers naturally seed private follow-up conversations after the session. Customize the props to fit your event theme (e.g., "Kickin' It!" with inflatable soccer balls and taped index cards).

**Source.** [Air Time Mastermind](https://gamestorming.com/air-time-mastermind/)

### The Anti-Problem

**Object of play.** Helps a team get unstuck when they're at their wit's end on a problem. By asking players to solve the *opposite* problem, it becomes easier to see where a current solution might be going astray or where an obvious solution isn't being applied.

**Players.** 5–20.

**Duration.** 30–45 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, identify a real situation needing resolution.
2. Provide sticky notes, markers, index cards, pipe cleaners, modeling clay — anything players can use to design and describe solutions.
3. Break large groups into small groups of 3–4 and describe the anti-problem: the current problem's opposite. (If the problem is "boost sales conversion," the anti-problem is "get customers to avoid buying.") The more extreme the opposite, the better.
4. Give players 15–20 minutes to generate and display ways to solve the anti-problem. Encourage speed and volume; there are no wrong solutions.
5. When time's up, each group shares their anti-solutions, displaying any visual or physical creations.
6. Discuss insights and discoveries.

**Strategy.** Push the anti-problem more extreme than the real one to dislodge thinking. Don't worry if the group doesn't generate fully viable solutions — the goal is a fresh approach that sparks insight after the meeting, not solving the complex problem in 30 minutes. The exercise often segues naturally into a real conversation about the actual problem; use that energy if time allows. Watch for the "whoops" moment when someone realizes their current solution is contributing to the problem. Optional variation: race through several smaller problems with a tighter clock. Adapted from Donna Spencer's "Reverse It" at designgames.com.au.

**Source.** [The Anti-Problem](https://gamestorming.com/the-anti-problem/)

### Argument Map

**Object of play.** A systematic way to map a debate as a single big visible chart — moving conversation from debate toward mutual understanding. The goal is to surface every issue and argument before zeroing in on any one issue, so you don't end up debating the wrong thing or running out of energy before the most important issue surfaces.

**Players.** 5–30.

**Duration.** 1–3 hours.

**How to play.**
1. **Just the Facts.** Build a facts map: briefly share verifiable facts and figures about the topic. No opinions.
2. **Quiet Brain Dump.** Take ~10 minutes individually to find causes, consequences, pros, and cons. Jot down every argument for or against the case.
3. **Take Turns and Share.** Going around the group, each person shares one argument per turn. Pass when you have nothing more. Capture every argument on the whiteboard, in outline software, or on individual sticky notes.
4. **Prune Your Arguments.** Cross out any argument on your list that someone else brings up first.
5. **Be Terse.** Cut off long discussions, salvos, or storytelling.
6. **Exhaust Yourself.** Whoever passes last "wins." Loop back to step 3 if not yet exhausted.
7. **For or Against.** Take two flip charts labeled "For" and "Against" and sort each captured argument onto the appropriate chart.
8. **Shape, Organize, and Thicken.** Cluster arguments into themes; name each theme with one to three keywords. Within each theme, sub-cluster as theorems, propositions, opinions, or stands. The For and Against sides need not mirror each other.

**Strategy.** Repeat with other stakeholder groups for fuller coverage. Watch for reasoning errors as you go: petitio principii (begging the question), argumentum ad hominem (personal attack), post hoc ergo propter hoc (false cause). If you have time and budget, render the harvest as a colorful tree-structured chart on a single A3 sheet. Originally conceived by the Argumentenfabriek.

**Source.** [Argument map](https://gamestorming.com/argument-map/)

### Atomize

**Object of play.** Break a large but poorly understood structure down into its base components — its "atoms." Useful for unpacking a firm's offering, a technology platform, an enterprise initiative, a supply chain, or an intangible like culture. Smaller components are more discreet, more tangible, and easier to handle in problem solving or brainstorming.

**Players.** Small groups.

**Duration.** 1 hour or more.

**How to play.**
1. Open by writing the name of the system on a sticky note at the top of a large whiteboard. Frame the exercise as a way to understand what the system is made of in tangible terms by breaking it into "atoms."
2. Ask the group to "split" the main system into its components. Generate a short list of three to five large components, captured on sticky notes directly below the main topic.
3. For each item, repeat the splitting process by asking, "What combines to create this?" Build a pyramid of components downward, layer by layer.
4. The resulting map and individual components can feed other activities or be documented as an explanation of the system's parts.

**Strategy.** Around four to five levels deep, items stop becoming more diverse and start becoming more fundamental — that's the atomic level, and it's where the most interesting findings emerge. In atomizing a culture, this is where hidden attitudes and behaviors surface. In atomizing a service or product, this is where elementary characteristics and points of differentiation live. Credited to James Macanufo.

**Source.** [Atomize](https://gamestorming.com/atomize/)

### Back of the Napkin

**Object of play.** Each team comes up with an answer to a provocative question and writes or draws it on the back of a napkin. The format itself — informal, small, associated with "aha" moments — combats people's instinct to worry about whether they can draw or have the perfect answer.

**Players.** Teams of 3 (range 2–6 works well). No limit on the number of teams.

**Duration.** Set by the organizer; minimum about 10 minutes per team per question.

**How to play.**
1. Form teams. Optionally require that each team includes at least two people who have just met.
2. Pose at least one open-ended problem statement or question per session.
3. Each team writes and/or draws their answer on one side of a (folded) napkin.
4. Players write their team's names on the other side.
5. Optional: allow multiple submissions per team, but each must come from a different team configuration.
6. Optional: judges review entries. Play for bragging rights or prizes. To open the field, offer playful categories like Most Practical, Most Out-of-the-Box, Most Whimsical, and Most Visual.

**Strategy.** Use it to introduce people, fuel networking, make brainstorming competitive and fun, or turn a reception into a memorable part of an event. Question design matters: open-ended, short and clear, and connected to something all players share — a speech everyone heard, a book they all read, a company they all worked with, or a common experience. Logistics: roughly 5"x5" folded napkins with smooth surfaces work best; gel pens write well; tape finished napkins to a flip-chart on the wall. At larger events, station a host at each question. Credited to Sheila Kim.

**Source.** [Back of the Napkin](https://gamestorming.com/back-of-the-napkin/)

### The Blind Side

**Object of play.** Every person and every company has blind spots. The goal is to make the team's knowledge — and lack of knowledge — explicit so it can impact organizational success. Inspired by the Johari Window, the game maps what we know we know, what we know we don't, what we don't know we know, and what we don't know we don't know.

**Players.** 5–15.

**Duration.** 30–45 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, choose a topic. Draw a large profile of a person with four arrows out of the head, labeled Know/Know, Know/Don't Know, Don't Know/Know, Don't Know/Don't Know.
2. Provide sticky notes and markers. Tell players the goal is to make explicit what they know and what they don't know but could use.
3. Start with **Know/Know** — generate everything the group knows about the topic. This goes fastest. One bit per sticky, clustered near the right arrow.
4. Move to **Know/Don't Know.** Slower than the first, still productive. Cluster near the arrow.
5. **Don't Know/Know** — surface skills people have that aren't currently used to solve problems, or untapped resources that have been forgotten.
6. **Don't Know/Don't Know** — the group will get stuck here, possibly indefinitely. This is where discovery happens. Ask provocative questions: What does this team know that your team doesn't know it doesn't know? How could you find out?
7. Discuss what to do proactively about each category. Even discovering you have blind spots is a fruitful outcome.

**Strategy.** Works best with familiar teams that span disciplines — diversity is what unlocks the Don't Know quadrants. Because it has a trust-building aspect, start with easy information and move toward more substantive material as comfort grows. Keep it on business or project topics; the self-help applications belong outside the office unless the group is into that. Adapted from the Johari Window by Joseph Luft and Harry Ingham.

**Source.** [The Blind Side](https://gamestorming.com/the-blind-side/)

### Bodystorming

**Object of play.** Brainstorming with the body. Get people to figure things out by trying things out, in the actual environment when possible, breaking the pattern of analyzing ideas around a conference table.

**Players.** Small group (any size that can act and observe together).

**Duration.** Varies by level; allow at least an hour for meaningful Level 2 work.

**How to play.**
1. **Level 1: Go Observe.** Go to the location where the work happens — coffee shop, hospital, campus, mall — and do your work as you would normally, blending in. Don't zero in on a specific analysis; stay open to environmental cues.
2. **Level 2: Try It Out.** Use role play and props to physically act out an experience. Identify and assign critical roles — the customer or user is usually the focal point. Other roles emerge ("Who wants to be the Internet?"). Improvise, building on each other's inputs ("Yes, and..." beats "Yeah, but..."). For more structured sessions, work from a prepared script and build a real environment with props (set up the counter, the chairs, or go to the actual park).
3. **Level 3: Reflect on What Happens, and Why.** Acting out the experience surfaces new possibilities and reveals flawed assumptions. Document the session on video so participants can later "watch the reel" and discuss key moments.

**Strategy.** Choose the right level for the group. Bodystorming asks people to step away from conference-table thinking, so newcomers may need structured sessions with scripts and assigned roles before they can do full improv. The exercise is more memorable than ordinary problem-solving and generates empathy that comes from "embodying" the experience. Term coined by Colin Burns at CHI '94 in Boston.

**Source.** [Bodystorming](https://gamestorming.com/bodystorming/)

### Brainwriting

**Object of play.** A simple way to generate ideas, share them, and build on them within a group — silently. Some of the best ideas are compilations from multiple contributors, and brainwriting taps multiple hands, eyes, and minds without privileging the loudest voices.

**Players.** 5–15.

**Duration.** 30–45 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. In a space visible to the players, write the topic and draw a picture of it (e.g., "Employee Recognition Program").
2. Distribute index cards to each player and ask them to silently generate ideas on the cards, one per card.
3. As they finish each idea, they pass that card to the person on their right.
4. Each player reads the card they receive, treats it as an "idea-stimulation" card, and adds an idea inspired by what they just read or enhances the original. Then pass right again.
5. Continue brainwriting and passing until each card has multiple ideas on it.
6. Collect the cards and tape them to the wall around the topic and its picture.
7. Have the group come to the wall, draw stars next to the most compelling ideas, and discuss.

**Strategy.** Extroverts dominate verbal contributions in most groups; brainwriting is intentionally silent so quieter players can contribute and you're certain you'll hear from everyone. It also lets ideas emerge before being critiqued and creates space for co-creation with shared ownership — increasing the chance of follow-through. Optional variations: fold paper into airplanes and fly between participants, or set up a flip-chart "idea gallery" around the room and have players wander and contribute. Based on Michael Michalko's *Thinkertoys*; the original brainwriting techniques came from Horst Geschke and associates at the Batelle Institute in Frankfurt.

**Source.** [Brainwriting](https://gamestorming.com/brainwriting/)

### Break

**Object of play.** Not a formal game — but unstructured time (a walk, an informal lunch, a short break) is critical to the creative process. Breaks let people process new information, give teams time to gel, let people catch up on outside life so the session is less stressful, and create space for ideas to settle, merge, and collide with prior knowledge.

**Players.** Any.

**Duration.** Any length — even a short break helps.

**How to play.**
1. Schedule breaks deliberately into your session — walks in the park, informal lunches, short pauses between intense working sessions.
2. Invite teams to use the time to take a walk, get to know each other socially, or catch up on outside work.
3. Don't underestimate or skip breaks just because you feel pressure to "cover all the information."

**Strategy.** Thomas Edison and Leonardo Da Vinci were both known for short naps interspersed with intense working sessions. Skipping breaks is dangerous — they renew, refresh, and reinvigorate the team. Research on the "break effect" supports this: a 2010 study, "Idea Generation and the Quality of the Best Idea," found that hybrid approaches combining group work with individual time produced the highest-quality ideas. As facilitators, we tend to overlook breaks; resist the urge to cram and trust that individual processing time produces better results than nonstop group work.

**Source.** [Break](https://gamestorming.com/break/)

### Bring Your Own Dashboard

**Object of play.** Validate the strategic alignment of your team's KPIs, understand the relationships between them, and brainstorm tests to validate both. Most teams have access to data measuring their goals; this game pressure-tests whether those measures actually serve the strategy.

**Players.** 5–15.

**Duration.** 90 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. **OPEN — Option A (existing KPIs).** Ask each team or individual to bring a sample report or dashboard. They briefly present, identifying which strategies the report aligns to (the **O** in OKR), the report KPIs (the **KR**), and any other reporting metrics. Then everyone writes their KPIs and metrics one per sticky note and posts them on the wall.
2. **OPEN — Option B (less defined or exploratory).** Write an organization-wide strategic goal at the top of the whiteboard. Players individually brainstorm for 5 minutes: list all customer behaviors impacting that goal (e.g., signing up for the newsletter, visiting the website, following on Twitter). Post and present sticky notes one by one. Group them into themes organically. Spend 5 minutes per theme on the thinking. For each theme, identify one or two KPIs that best measure the desired behavior.
3. **EXPLORE.** Build a KPI relationship matrix. For each pair of KPIs, decide whether the relationship is Direct (an increase in one drives an increase in the other) or Indirect (an increase in one drives a decrease in the other). Players can write privately first, then call out results. Keep it as a quick gut check. Then discuss: What do you notice? What KPIs should you add or remove? Where should you optimize rather than maximize? Are strategies aligned? What other organizations are implicated? What's uncertain — and how could you test it next week?
4. **CLOSE.** Run a Start-Stop-Continue. Each person individually brainstorms what to Start, Stop, and Continue; share results.

**Strategy.** In a data-rich world, your strategy is what your KPIs say it is. Teams often try to maximize KPIs without understanding their downstream effects — Marketing maximizing visitors can hurt Sales' conversion rate. This game surfaces those ripple effects and the moments when optimization should trump maximization. Inspired by David Kiron and Michael Schrage's MIT Sloan Management Review article *Strategy For and With AI*. Pairs well with Manage What You Measure, Post-Up, Affinity Map, and Start-Stop-Continue.

**Source.** [Bring Your Own Dashboard](https://gamestorming.com/bring-your-own-dashboard/)

### Build The Checklist

**Object of play.** In any reasonably complex work, equally important tasks can overwhelm the human mind — doubly true in knowledge work where tasks are "fuzzy." A checklist is a focusing object that forces a team to discuss the order and importance of tasks, surfacing different perspectives and working through them.

**Players.** A small team with deep experience in the task at hand.

**Duration.** 1 hour or more, depending on the task being analyzed.

**How to play.**
1. Decide whether the checklist will run in order of operation (first to last) or as a ranked priority list. Pick whichever the group will benefit from more.
2. Introduce the topic: "You will be creating a checklist for doing [fill in the blank]." Prime the group with a particular situation or duration — "Getting from A to B," "Dealing with an Angry Customer."
3. Brainstorm tasks on sticky notes. Push for items that are concrete and measurable, like a switch turned on or off — "deploy landing gear" beats "assess arrival readiness."
4. Use Post-Up and affinity mapping to remove duplicates and cluster.
5. Either: order the tasks into a procedure using sticky notes on a wall with a defined start and end, debating ordering as you go. Or: force-rank the tasks by importance, which lets the group cut items from the bottom and produce a shorter, more direct checklist.
6. Capture the checklist as an artifact and share it with others who can test and improve it.

**Strategy.** The discussion and reflection coming out of the brainstorm is where most progress is made — new ideas surface during the debate and get added to the checklist. Treat the first version as a draft to be tested and iterated rather than a final document. Credited to James Macanufo.

**Source.** [Build The Checklist](https://gamestorming.com/build-the-checklist/)

### Building Partnerships

**Object of play.** The partnership canvas enables visualization of current and/or future partnerships and can be used for early testing of the value-creating potential between two partnership candidates. It defines your business priority for partnering and helps you empathize with a partner to explore whether there is a match.

**Players.** Preferably 2 teams of up to 5 people, one representing each side of the partnership. Solo use is also possible.

**Duration.** 60-90 minutes total.

**How to play.**
1. Define intent (15 min): describe the aim of the partnership for your business, list ideal partners and why via a post-up, then form two teams - one representing your business, one representing the potential partner.
2. Design partnership (15 min per sketch): each team identifies the assets they desire in the other partner's business model and sketches a partnership canvas from their own perspective using sticky notes for each building block.
3. Bring teams to the negotiating table (15 min): each team presents its canvas, then mirror the two perspectives - compare value offers of one team to desired value of the other and check that transfer activities are mutually understood. Adapt and iterate until there is clear fit.
4. Evaluate negotiation results (20 min): confirm the created value provides clear added value to each partner, then define next steps to effectuate the partnership.

**Strategy.** Use the canvas either to prepare for a conversation with a potential partner or jointly with a partner once mutual interest exists. The exercise tests technical fit and, through negotiation rivalry, surfaces cultural fit. To get full strategizing value, work it integrally with the business model canvas, since the partnership discussion is a key step in business model innovation.

**Source.** [building-partnerships](https://gamestorming.com/building-partnerships/)

### Button

**Object of play.** A round-robin keeps the rule that everyone speaks once before anyone speaks twice, but it drains energy and lets attention wane while people wait. The Button preserves the rule while avoiding the predictable round-robin trap.

**Players.** Any group small enough for a single round-table.

**Duration.** As long as the prompted question requires.

**How to play.**
1. When the group is asked to report on a question, give a small token - a poker chip or similar - to the first volunteer to respond.
2. After his response, he chooses a person who has yet to speak and passes the button.
3. Continue until everyone has spoken once.
4. Index-card variant: each participant writes a one-word answer plus their name on a card; pass the cards left briskly so the order scrambles, then participants call on each other by reading the words aloud and asking the writers to explain.

**Strategy.** Randomization keeps participants' attention - when you don't know who will be called next, you stay present and focused. The Button also passes control to the participants by giving them the power to nominate the next speaker. The technique is inspired by the Native American "Talking Stick" tradition, in which a ceremonial object representing the right to speak is passed to honor the speaker and avoid interruptions.

**Source.** [button](https://gamestorming.com/button/)

### Campfire

**Object of play.** Most workplace know-how is actually transferred through storytelling rather than formal training. Campfire leverages our natural storytelling tendencies by giving players a format and space to share work stories of trial and error, failure and success, competition, diplomacy, and teamwork - revealing commonalities in employee perception and experience along the way.

**Players.** 8-20.

**Duration.** 30-45 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, brainstorm 10-20 trigger words or phrases (positive or neutral: partnership, venture, first day, work travel, fun project, opportunity) and write them on sticky notes.
2. Post the notes as a "wall of words" visible to all players, give them markers and blank stickies, frame the room as a workplace campfire, and have them spend 1-3 minutes recalling stories tied to the words. Kick off by peeling one word, posting it nearby, and telling your introductory story.
3. Ask a volunteer to peel another word and post it next to yours, starting the sticky-note "story thread."
4. Before the first volunteer begins, instruct other players to listen for words or phrases that remind them of their own work stories and jot them on stickies; if nothing jumps out, they can pull from the original wall.
5. After each story, the next volunteer posts her word, reads it aloud, and shares.
6. Repeat until a snake-like story thread archives the conversation. Before "putting out" the fire, ask for lessons learned or final thoughts.

**Strategy.** Your job as facilitator is simply to encourage sharing - if there's a lull, point back to the wall of words or invite a "wild card" story. You can let stories drift toward less positive topics for catharsis, but be ready to manage what surfaces and don't end on a sour note. Humans want to tell stories; expect players to linger after the meeting.

**Source.** [campfire](https://gamestorming.com/campfire/)

### Card Sort

**Object of play.** Card sorting is used by information architects and designers to gather and structure inputs for many purposes - originally to help create navigation categories for a website, but equally useful for sorting slides, organizing presentations, or any moment information needs sensible structure. Unlike Post-Up or affinity mapping, the cards are usually prepared in advance and act as a semi-permanent artifact that can be reused across exercises to spot patterns.

**Players.** Small groups or individuals.

**Duration.** 30 minutes or more, depending on the number of cards and participants.

**How to play.**
1. Use 3x5 index cards or similar; aim for 30-100 cards total. More overwhelms; fewer isn't worth the effort.
2. On each card put a succinct bit of information - just enough to tell participants what it is. Too much slows sorting; too little causes confusion.
3. Hand the group the shuffled deck and a stack of blanks. Describe the overall organization challenge and ask them to sort the cards into groups that go together.
4. If something is unclear or missing, they may alter a card or create a new one.
5. Once groups are formed, ask the participants to name and describe each group.
6. Variations: rank items most-to-least desirable, sort into "must have" vs. "nice to have," or sort into a predefined set of categories to test their validity.

**Strategy.** Card Sort won't tell you everything about a set of information, but it reveals the thought process of participants - it's more about people than information. Larger patterns appear only after sorting exercises with multiple groups, so plan to repeat the exercise with different participants when you want reliable structural insight.

**Source.** [card-sort](https://gamestorming.com/card-sort/)

### Carousel

**Object of play.** Carousel gathers facts and opinions from participants on different aspects of an issue and helps the group gain and share insight from all points of view, since everyone gets the chance to contribute.

**Players.** Up to 50.

**Duration.** 15 minutes to an hour, depending on the number of participants.

**How to play.**
1. Prepare 5-10 flip-charts, each addressing a different aspect of the topic with a powerful, impersonal question that asks for facts and opinions - focus on "what," "when," and "how" questions.
2. Spread the flip-charts through the room with enough distance between them for group discussion without disturbing other groups.
3. Quickly introduce the topic and walk through the questions on each chart so everyone understands them correctly.
4. Split participants into pairs (or groups up to 5 for larger sessions) so there is one group per flip-chart.
5. Ask each group to answer the question on their chart by adding ideas, facts, and opinions - via writing, images, or sticky-note artifacts - in a way others can interpret.
6. Give each group 2-3 minutes to add their information, then rotate clockwise (or counter-clockwise) to the next chart.
7. Repeat until each group has answered every question.
8. Allow another 5-10 minutes for the entire group to review the generated content, then move on to prioritization or deeper research.

**Strategy.** The time limit per question forces groups to focus on what matters most. The goal is not to gather all information from each participant but to surface meaningful information as a group, which then becomes the basis for prioritization or deeper research.

**Source.** [caroussel](https://gamestorming.com/caroussel/)

### Challenge Cards

**Object of play.** To identify and think through challenges, problems, and potential pitfalls in a product, service, or strategy - turning critique into a collaborative storytelling game so the team engages rather than defends.

**Players.** Small groups of 5-10.

**Duration.** Not specified on the source page.

**How to play.**
1. Divide the group into two teams. The "solution team" silently brainstorms features and strengths of the product or solution; the "challenge team" silently brainstorms potential problems or challenges, writing each on its own index card.
2. When play starts, the two teams collaborate to tell a story. The challenge team picks a card and plays it on the table, describing a scene or event where that issue might realistically arise.
3. The solution team must then play a card from their deck that addresses the challenge. If they have a solution, they get a point; if not, the challenge team gets the point.
4. Together, the teams design a new card that addresses any unanswered challenge.
5. Continue alternating challenge to solution to challenge until the story or scenario reaches a conclusion.

**Strategy.** The goal is to improve a product or strategy by working through scenarios and alternatives. Turning the exercise into a competition and a storytelling game gets players engaged and immersed in the scenarios; keeping it lighthearted and fun raises energy. It shouldn't feel like work. The game emerged spontaneously during a Gamestorming workshop in London in 2010.

**Source.** [challenge-cards](https://gamestorming.com/challenge-cards/)

### Choose Your Words Wisely

**Object of play.** The Semantic Environment Canvas helps you understand the language, rules, and power dynamics that make it possible for people to accomplish their purposes in particular situations - or that hinder them from doing so.

**Players.** 1-6. With more than six, break into groups and assign separate environments to each.

**Duration.** 20-40 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Print the Semantic Environment canvas large (A0 ideal) and tape it on the wall; bring sticky notes, markers, and a camera.
2. Fill the canvas one section at a time - individual brainstorm, then group discussion. If an idea fits a different section better, place it there and return later.
3. Environment: brainstorm the area of discourse, its language (law, commerce, religion), its purposes, and its key terms and metaphors; agree on a clear, compelling name.
4. Actors: brainstorm individuals, roles, or groups; choose Actor A and Actor B, then discuss their relative power and fill the Power Relationship section.
5. Goals: for each actor, brainstorm why they participate, what they expect to get out of it, and how they'll know they've succeeded.
6. Rules: brainstorm spoken and unspoken behavioral expectations for each actor and what happens when rules break.
7. Key Words: capture each actor's technical vocabulary and metaphors; discuss who controls the environmental metaphors and who maintains the definitions.
8. Touchpoints: brainstorm the channels and contexts where communication happens - in person, remote, the surrounding mood.
9. Analyze: draw arrows between stickies, color-code helps (green) vs. hinders (red), and pin related canvases side by side to spot drift.

**Strategy.** Collaborators often take their words for granted; mapping the semantic environment makes hidden assumptions visible - useful when teams use the same word to mean different things, when navigating political tensions, or when designing software whose conceptual model must serve users and stakeholders with differing language.

**Source.** [choose-your-words-wisely](https://gamestorming.com/choose-your-words-wisely/)

### Code of Conduct

**Object of play.** Set the right culture in a group and build mutual trust at the start of a meeting or workshop, empowering participants to act on the agreement they create together.

**Players.** Up to 30.

**Duration.** Around 30 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Write the words "Meaningful" and "Pleasant" in the middle of a flip-chart or whiteboard.
2. Ask everyone to shout out what they believe is necessary to make the meeting or workshop meaningful and pleasant.
3. Record the contributions in a mind-map structure - preferably using images instead of words.
4. Run through each idea quickly and confirm shared understanding; adjust wording where needed. These become the group's values.
5. Return to the first item and ask participants how they would carry it out in practice. Record these as actions attached to each value.
6. End by pointing out that this code is now a working agreement: every participant is responsible for ensuring the group respects it.
7. Optional: ask people to take ownership of specific actions - but be aware this can trigger the response that monitoring is now "their problem, not mine."

**Strategy.** Make sure everybody contributes to the mind-map. If the group isn't strong or comfortable enough for live shouting, substitute silent writing combined with affinity mapping. Building the shared code takes time, but it helps groups with low trust break through initial barriers.

**Source.** [code-of-conduct](https://gamestorming.com/code-of-conduct/)

### Context Map

**Object of play.** We don't truly grasp a situation until we see it in fuller context. The Context Map shows the external factors, trends, and forces at work surrounding an organization so the group can respond proactively to that landscape rather than reactively.

**Players.** 5-25.

**Duration.** 45 minutes to 1.5 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Hang six sheets of flip-chart paper on a wall in a two-row, three-column layout.
2. On the top-middle sheet, draw the organization (an office, a globe) and label it; in that sheet's upper corners write "POLITICAL FACTORS" (left) and "ECONOMIC CLIMATE" (right).
3. On the top-left sheet, draw large arrows pointing right and label it "_____ TRENDS" with the blank for a qualifier.
4. On the top-right sheet, draw arrows pointing left and label it "_____ TRENDS."
5. On the bottom-left sheet, draw arrows pointing up and to the right; label it "TECHNOLOGY FACTORS."
6. On the bottom-middle sheet, draw your customers and label it "CUSTOMER NEEDS."
7. On the bottom-right sheet, draw a thundercloud or person with a question mark; label it "UNCERTAINTIES."
8. Introduce the map and ask the group which category to discuss first - any except TRENDS. Capture their comments in the corresponding space.
9. Continue until every category but TRENDS is filled.
10. Poll the group to qualify the trend types (online, demographic, growth) and write the qualifier in the blanks; then populate.
11. Summarize and ask for observations, insights, "aha's," and concerns.

**Strategy.** As facilitator, draw out content with intelligent, thought-provoking questions; do interviews or research beforehand if needed. Note where the group starts and where they generate the most content - that's where their energy lies. Keep the discussion focused on the external environment; if it drifts internal, redirect. The aim is a holistic external picture that shows where to focus for strategic results.

**Source.** [context-map-2](https://gamestorming.com/context-map-2/)

### Coriolis Effect

**Object of play.** A corporate team-building exercise that offers insight into how different team members consume information and detail, whether they prefer structured or unstructured problem solving, and how quickly or slowly they move ahead based on understanding of the solution.

**Players.** 4-15 per team.

**Duration.** 30 minutes of activity (25 minutes project time plus debrief).

**How to play.**
1. Materials: one plastic pipe (gutter) per person, one ball per team, one team handout, one timer.
2. Form a human circle; each person holds one gutter; the tallest person starts with the ball.
3. Goal: move one ball around the circle as fast as possible using only the gutters, beginning and ending with the tallest person.
4. Constraints: no one's gutter can be skipped; the ball must pass through every team member's gutter; gutters cannot touch each other; your own pinkies must touch each other at all times; you cannot touch any gutter besides your own; people cannot touch the ball in transit.
5. If the ball drops, restart the process. Teams may attempt as many runs as they like in the 25-minute project window.
6. Debrief on the back of the handout: discuss communication forms used, which were most powerful, where communication broke down, what ideas were generated, how the plan was developed and evaluated, what action steps were taken, and where gaps appeared.

**Strategy.** Be a stickler - when teams ask for clarification, repeat that "everything you need to know is on the team handout." This forces self-direction and surfaces team dynamics. The debrief is where the learning lives, so allocate time to the processing questions about communication, idea generation, role-taking, and the team's preferred working style.

**Source.** [coriolis-affect](https://gamestorming.com/coriolis-affect/)

### Cost Benefit

**Object of play.** A simple collaborative cost-benefit analysis useful when a group has expert knowledge about costs and/or benefits - especially valuable when a customer or customer proxy needs to prioritize work items with a development team.

**Players.** Small to medium working group with the relevant cost and benefit knowledge.

**Duration.** Roughly 30-45 minutes once items are listed.

**How to play.**
1. Generate ideas: if no list exists, run a silent post-up - each person writes work items on stickies (one per sticky) for ~10 minutes, then posts them on the wall.
2. Cluster: in silence, the team groups items by subject; park items causing discussion. Consensus on exact scope isn't required - a majority is fine - but everyone must know which cluster each item lives under.
3. Prioritize on cost - sorting: in 5 minutes, the team sorts items top-to-bottom by cost.
4. Prioritize on cost - scaling: hang the lowest sticky much lower and the highest much higher than the rest. Mark scale points (1, 5, 10, 15, 20) and reposition stickies on the scale, preserving the sorted order. Write each sticky's relative position (e.g. 50%) in the bottom-left corner.
5. Prioritize on benefit: repeat the same sort-and-scale process for benefits, ideally with a product owner or customer; write the benefit number in the bottom-right corner.
6. Put it together: draw an X (cost) and Y (benefit) axis and place each sticky according to the coordinates noted on it. Low-hanging fruit and infeasible-expensive items emerge clearly.

**Strategy.** The same sort-and-scale approach works with a Kano diagram or any 2D graph. It's a fun way to clarify priorities through collaborative support. Watch for "discussion starters" - they consume time and can be redirected by re-stating the goal: prioritization, not consensus on scope.

**Source.** [cost-benefit](https://gamestorming.com/cost-benefit/)

### Cover Story

**Object of play.** Cover Story is a game of pure imagination - a visioning exercise. Players suspend disbelief and envision a future state so stellar that it landed the organization on the cover of a well-known magazine, pretending it has already happened and been reported by the mainstream press. The game encourages big thinking and plants seeds for futures that previously felt impossible.

**Players.** Any number, in groups of 4-6 per template.

**Duration.** Maximum 90 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, draw large templates with sections for: Cover (the BIG story of success), Headlines (substance of the cover story), Sidebars (interesting facets), Quotes (from anyone, related to the story), Brainstorm (initial ideas), and Images (illustrations).
2. Explain the object and define each category for the players.
3. Break into groups of 4-6 with markers and one template each; let them choose a scribe or write together.
4. Ask players to imagine the best-case scenario for their company and push it one step further. Give 5 quiet minutes for individual imagining, then 30-45 minutes to agree on one story and populate the template.
5. Reconvene and have each group present its vision in 5-10 minutes.
6. Note common themes and areas of agreement; collect observations, insights, and concerns.
7. Optional: have two players role-play a magazine interview based on a team's cover story.

**Strategy.** As meeting leader, speak about the "successes" with enthusiasm and in the past tense - encourage players to do the same. Don't let the group slip into analysis, logic, or pragmatism, and steer them away from peer "reality checks." Common themes across teams are telling because they reveal shared hopes and seed real possibilities. If this sits inside a longer process, post the visions around the room as ongoing reference points.

**Source.** [cover-story](https://gamestorming.com/cover-story/)

### Customer, Employee, Shareholder

**Object of play.** Imagine possible futures from multiple perspectives, surfacing intuitive knowledge about the business that may not surface otherwise.

**Players.** 1-10.

**Duration.** 1-3 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Divide the group into three roles: Customers, Employees, and Shareholders.
2. Ask players to step into their roles and imagine the business five years from now: What will they value? What will their experience be like? What events or trends will have emerged? What specific, tangible things will be different?
3. Have players draw their visions of the future and share them with the rest of the group.
4. Ask the group to identify themes and new possibilities; capture them and consult the group on next steps.

**Strategy.** This exercise gives the group a chance to relate intuitive knowledge about the business that may not surface in standard discussion. If possible, have the group cycle through multiple roles by reshuffling assignments and repeating the exercise - the perspective-shift each round produces different insights and helps individuals appreciate stakeholder tensions they would otherwise overlook. The game is based on the Stakeholder Framework developed by Max Clarkson in "A Stakeholder Framework for Analyzing and Evaluating Corporate Social Performance" (Academy of Management Review, 1995).

### Design The Box

**Object of play.** Before you begin, focus on the end. Teams create the physical "box" that sells their idea - whether or not it will become a tangible product. By imagining the package, the team commits to important features and other aspects of the vision that are hard to articulate. Popular among software developers capturing the customer view of a new application, the game also works for "our future methodology," "the ideal hire," or any vision-oriented discussion.

**Players.** Works with a small group; better with parallel teams designing different boxes for richer "selling phase" discussion.

**Duration.** 1 hour or more, depending on group count and depth.

**How to play.**
1. Setup: gather paper, markers, and ideally blank white cardboard boxes from an office supply store, plus craft paper, stickers, tape, and scissors. Bring sample boxes (cereal, toy, gift, plain store-label) as inspiration.
2. Phase One - Fill the Box: orient teams with possible names, possible customers/end users/buyers, and possible features or defining details. Give just enough info to feel comfortable starting.
3. Phase Two - Make the Box: give teams 30+ minutes to create a box for their idea, imagining it shrink-wrapped on a retail shelf. Useful prompts: What's it called? Who's it for? What's the tagline? What are the most compelling features and benefits? What imagery makes it stand out?
4. Phase Three - Sell the Box: each team or individual stands up and "sells" their box back to the group; consider a timer and a prize for the best presentation.
5. Listen for the natural breakthrough: when selling, players translate features into benefits - watch for "so that" or "because" bridging mechanical features into living benefits.
6. Optional convergent run: note similarities and differences across boxes, then run a second round to incorporate agreements into a final shared box.

**Strategy.** Keep the boxes and display them prominently - they often outlast and outperform any other documentation from the exercise. Record the presentations if it isn't disruptive. The core "design the box" act can be adapted to many contexts and participants.

**Source.** [design-the-box](https://gamestorming.com/design-the-box/)

### Do, Redo & Undo

**Object of play.** When creating something it's easier to think in the affirmative, but over time undoing decisions matters just as much. Do, Redo & Undo asks a group to think through the implications of dismantling and altering - useful in any human-to-machine or human-to-human system, where users need to change configurations, fix mistakes, and remove things entirely.

**Players.** Small groups.

**Duration.** 1 hour or more, depending on the complexity of the existing concept.

**How to play.**
1. Start from a concept or prototype: a user story, feature list, or a draft process flow. Walk the group through it.
2. Best-Case Scenario: brainstorm answers via Post-Up to "What mistakes can and will be made?" Pool the stickies into a starting set of scenarios. Sharpen with prompts like "What would happen if a group of monkeys tried to use it?" and "What happens if we pull the plug? Where is the plug?"
3. Worst-Case Scenario: tackle each item via three solution types in this order of preference: Do (change the design or plan to avoid the problem altogether, taking the issue off the table), Redo (provide a means for altering the action while it's being taken - course correction or buffering), and Undo (provide a means for completely reversing the action and returning to a previously known state).
4. If there are many worst-case items, prioritize by likelihood and focus on the hot spots.
5. Capture solutions and revisit the original Best-Case Scenario; carry the solution draft alongside the design as it matures, validating in user testing and the real world.

**Strategy.** There's an implied order of preference: a problem entirely eliminated by changing the design avoids needing redo or undo (e.g. dropping a "enter your contact info" field if the data can be fetched elsewhere). The exercise is credited to James Macanufo.

**Source.** [do-redo-undo](https://gamestorming.com/do-redo-undo/)

### Dot Voting

**Object of play.** When a brainstorm has produced too many good ideas, concepts, and possibilities to proceed, dot voting is one of the simplest ways to prioritize and converge on an agreed solution.

**Players.** At least 3 participants; in larger groups, tallying votes will take more time.

**Duration.** Short.

**How to play.**
1. Assemble a set of things to vote on - a wall of sticky notes from a recent brainstorm or a flip-chart list capturing ideas in one place.
2. Ask the group to cast votes by placing dots next to the items they feel most strongly about, using stickers or markers.
3. As a rule of thumb, give each participant five votes.
4. Participants vote all at once and may place more than one vote on a single item if they feel strongly about it.
5. Once all votes are cast, tally them and, if needed, re-list the items by their new rank.
6. Use the prioritized list as the subject of discussion and decision making.
7. Optionally, reflect on the items that received no votes to verify they haven't been left behind without cause.

**Strategy.** Dot voting is used to collaboratively prioritize any set of items - a list of features, discussion topics, or strategic options. Five votes per participant tends to be enough to be meaningful while still forcing individual prioritization, but it isn't a hard rule; adjust to the volume of items and the precision you need from the group.

**Source.** [dot-voting](https://gamestorming.com/dot-voting/)

### Draw Toast

**Object of play.** Use Draw Toast to introduce people to visual thinking, working memory, mental models, and/or systems thinking. It also doubles as a warm-up that gets people engaged with each other and thinking visually - and it's fun.

**Players.** Any number.

**Duration.** 10-15 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Hand out paper or index cards.
2. Ask people to draw "How to make toast."
3. After a couple of minutes, ask people to share their diagrams with each other and discuss similarities and differences.
4. Invite observations and insights about the various drawings - you'll likely hear comments about relative simplicity or complexity, whether the drawings include people, how technical they are, and how similar or different they look.
5. If it serves your purpose, point out that although the drawings are all different they are all fundamentally correct - there are many ways to visualize information and they enrich understanding rather than being right or wrong.
6. Note also that while the drawings differ in content, they tend to be similar in structure - most contain three to seven elements connected by lines or arrows.

**Strategy.** The exercise demonstrates the power of visual thinking. Mental-model visualizations usually contain 5-7 elements linked by lines or arrows, mirroring the limits of working memory (Miller's Magical Number Seven). Tom Wujec's TED talk and DrawToast.com offer ways to extend the exercise into group problem solving - worth watching as prep or sharing with the group afterward as a debrief springboard. Created by Dave Gray.

**Source.** [draw-toast](https://gamestorming.com/draw-toast/)

### Draw the Problem

**Object of play.** (page rendering issue - see source URL) Force a team to externalize how they understand a problem by drawing it. Surfacing each person's mental model side-by-side reveals where assumptions diverge and where the real problem lives, before anyone leaps to solutions.

**Players.** Small group, typically 3-10.

**Duration.** 30-45 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Frame the problem briefly in words, then ask each participant to draw it on a sheet of paper or index card - no words allowed, only images, arrows, and simple shapes.
2. Give 5-10 minutes of silent solo drawing so people commit to a representation before they're influenced by others.
3. Have each person hold up their drawing and explain what it depicts - the actors, the flow, the friction, and where the problem actually sits.
4. Capture similarities and differences across drawings on a flip-chart; note where people drew the same thing, where they drew different things, and where the problem boundary differs.
5. As a group, attempt a single shared drawing that synthesizes the strongest elements from individual versions.
6. Use the shared drawing as the working artifact for the rest of the session and any subsequent solution generation.

**Strategy.** Drawing forces specificity that words let you skate past - you cannot draw a vague problem. Differences between drawings are the real product of the exercise: they expose hidden assumptions, mismatched scope, and conflicting mental models that would otherwise sabotage downstream solutioning. Resist the urge to vote on a "best" drawing; instead synthesize.

### Elevator Pitch

**Object of play.** A time-proven product-development exercise that applies equally to any concept - services, company-wide initiatives, or ideas worth spreading. The group benefits from collaborating on what is and isn't in the pitch: a short, compelling description of the problem, who it's for, and one key benefit that distinguishes it from competitors. It must be unique, believable, and important; the better and bigger the idea, the harder the pitch is to write.

**Players.** Works as well individually as with a small working group.

**Duration.** Long - save at least 90 minutes; consider a break between idea generation and pitch shaping. One person may need follow-up accountability for the final wording.

**How to play.**
1. Setup: write these questions in sequence on flip-charts - Who is the target customer? What is the customer need? What is the product name? What is its market category? What is its key benefit? Who or what is the competition? What is the product's unique differentiator?
2. These elements feed the formula: For (target customer) who has (customer need), (product name) is a (market category) that (one key benefit). Unlike (competition), the product (unique differentiator).
3. Explain each element. Fix the product name in advance to focus the conversation; let the market category give a frame of reference; insist that key benefit be one single memorable reason to buy.
4. Generating Phase: participants brainstorm sticky notes under each header without discussion or analysis, then Post-Up onto the flip-charts.
5. Discuss the hardest areas (do we know enough about competition? do we agree on a target customer?), then dot-vote or affinity-map to cull each category.
6. Forming Phase: in small groups, pairs, or individually, each writes a draft pitch from the surviving ideas in ~15 minutes.
7. Reconvene and present drafts; the group may role-play as target customers and ask questions.
8. End when there's strong direction on what the pitch should and shouldn't contain - possibly several pitches for different target customers.

**Strategy.** Don't aim for final wording with a large group - it's an achievement if you reach it, but not critical. What matters is deciding what is and is not part of the pitch. Role play is the fastest way to test a pitch; assuming a customer's role (or recruiting a real one) filters out jargon and empty terms.

**Source.** [elevator-pitch](https://gamestorming.com/elevator-pitch/)

### Empathy Map

**Object of play.** (page rendering issue - see source URL) Build a simple shared portrait of a target user or stakeholder so a team designs from a real, specific person rather than an abstraction. The map is fast to create, easy to update, and gives every participant the same picture of who they're solving for.

**Players.** Small group, 3-10.

**Duration.** 20-30 minutes per persona.

**How to play.**
1. On a large sheet of flip-chart paper, draw a face in the middle and divide the surrounding space into quadrants labeled "Thinking & Feeling," "Seeing," "Saying & Doing," and "Hearing." Add "Pains" and "Gains" sections at the bottom.
2. Choose one specific target user and give them a name and a few biographical details to make them concrete.
3. As a group, populate each quadrant with sticky notes from the user's perspective: what occupies their thoughts and emotions, what their environment looks like, what they say in public versus do in private, and what they hear from friends, colleagues, and the market.
4. Fill in Pains (frustrations, obstacles, fears) and Gains (wants, measures of success, aspirations).
5. Step back and look for tensions - say-do gaps, mismatches between what the user hears and what they feel, surprising pains.
6. Use the map to anchor downstream design or messaging decisions; revisit and update it as new research lands.

**Strategy.** Empathy maps are a forcing function for shared understanding - two designers with the same map can have a productive disagreement about a feature; two designers without one are usually arguing about different imagined users. Use real research where you have it and explicitly mark guesses so the team knows what still needs validation.

### Empathy Map

**Object of play.** The Empathy Map helps a team build deeper understanding of the people they're designing for by mapping a user or stakeholder's experience along the dimensions of what they think and feel, see, hear, say and do — surfacing pains and gains that drive behavior. (Source page returned a JavaScript-required error; digest written from common Gamestorming knowledge of the Empathy Map.)

**Players.** 3–10, ideally a cross-functional product or design team.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Pick a person or persona to empathize with — give them a name and a goal they're trying to accomplish.
2. Draw a large empathy map template on a wall or whiteboard divided into six quadrants: THINK & FEEL, SEE, HEAR, SAY & DO, PAIN, and GAIN, with the persona at the center.
3. Have players silently generate sticky notes for each quadrant, putting themselves in the persona's shoes — what worries or excites them (Think & Feel), what's in their environment (See), what others tell them (Hear), and how they behave publicly (Say & Do).
4. Cluster the notes inside each quadrant and discuss patterns as a group.
5. Capture PAINS (frustrations, obstacles, fears) and GAINS (wants, measures of success) at the bottom.
6. Step back, read the map as a whole, and identify insights or "aha" moments worth designing for.

**Strategy.** The map's value comes from collaborative interpretation, not solo theorizing. Push the group past surface demographics into the contradictions — what someone says publicly versus what they really think, or pains they wouldn't admit out loud. Use the completed map as a shared artifact to anchor downstream design conversations and to test whether proposed solutions actually relieve a real pain or create a real gain.

### Ethos, Logos, Pathos

**Object of play.** The goal of this game is to channel Aristotle's assessment of your argument, evaluating a piece of communication — like a value proposition or pitch — against the three classical pillars of persuasion.

**Players.** 1–10.

**Duration.** 10 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Choose a communication to evaluate, such as a value proposition, pitch, or message.
2. Role-play as your intended audience rather than as the author of the message.
3. Score the message from 1 to 10 on Ethos (Credibility): who you are and what authority you have on the topic.
4. Score from 1 to 10 on Logos (Logic): how clear and consistent your reasoning is, and how your facts measure up against the audience's facts.
5. Score from 1 to 10 on Pathos (Emotion): how vivid, memorable, and motivating your message is.
6. Look across the three scores for areas of improvement or imbalance, and identify which dimension needs the most work.

**Strategy.** Aristotle laid the groundwork for persuasive communication in the 4th century, and effective communication still rests on the same three legs. Most messages lean too heavily on one pillar — typically logos in business settings — at the expense of the others. The diagnostic value of the game is in spotting that imbalance and forcing yourself to strengthen the weakest leg before sending the message into the world.

**Source.** [ethos-logos-pathos](https://gamestorming.com/ethos-logos-pathos/)

### Fishbowl

**Object of play.** When stakeholders aren't familiar with each other's perspectives or aren't accustomed to listening without offering an immediate response, rich conversation is hard to achieve. The Fishbowl game activates attention — priming natural listening and observing skills so a more substantive conversation can take place.

**Players.** Medium to large groups.

**Duration.** 40–45 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, pick a topic worth a group discussion and write down questions to seed it.
2. Find a room with open space and clear out everything but chairs.
3. Create a handout where observers can capture discussion points and supporting evidence.
4. Arrange chairs in two concentric circles — the inner circle for players in conversation, the outer circle for observers.
5. Introduce the game and assign each person to either "player" or "observer," handing out pens and the handout (used only by observers).
6. Announce the topic and give the inner circle 15 minutes to discuss it, using your seed questions to keep things moving. Players talk; observers silently log everything.
7. Switch seats and roles, then run a second 15-minute discussion on the same or a different topic.
8. Debrief: ask volunteers to share what they observed and to compare the experiences of being inside vs. outside the circle.

**Strategy.** Most people are well-versed in conversation; far fewer are practiced at listening, observing, and being accountable for what they heard. The real action of Fishbowl happens in the outer circle. Frame it explicitly as a listening exercise — if there were points (and there aren't), they'd go to the most accurate observers, not the most talkative players. Use the debrief as a segue into a heightened give-and-take among stakeholders.

**Source.** [fishbowl](https://gamestorming.com/fishbowl/)

### Five-Fingered Consensus

**Object of play.** This is a technique for managing the feedback loop between a facilitator and a large group — a way to periodically gauge perceived consensus on a topic without spending unnecessary time talking about it. A facilitator runs a quick "five-fingers check" to take the room's temperature.

**Players.** Any group size; especially useful with larger groups or multiple breakouts.

**Duration.** Seconds to a couple of minutes per check.

**How to play.**
1. Pose the topic or proposed direction and ask the group to rate their level of consensus from 0 to 5.
2. Five fingers up means "absolute, total agreement"; a fist (zero fingers) means "completely different points of view."
3. Have everyone show their hand at the same time so individual responses are visible.
4. Scan the room for the spread of fingers — uniform fives signal alignment; a mix of ones, twos, and threes signals more work to do.
5. If the spread is wide, drill into the lowest fingers to surface concerns before moving on.
6. Use it repeatedly through a session as a lightweight pulse check, especially when running breakouts on different topics in parallel.

**Strategy.** The trick is gauging how far apart individuals feel from consensus, not just the average. A group where some hold up fives and others hold up twos may need outside support and mediated discussion — that spread is more diagnostic than a uniformly mediocre three. Hand signals are a long-standing element of consensus-based decision making and dispute resolution; the related thumbs-up, thumbs-down, thumbs-sideways technique works the same way at lower resolution.

**Source.** [five-fingered-consensus](https://gamestorming.com/five-fingered-consensus/)

### Flip It

**Object of play.** Often a change in a problem comes simply from a change in our perspectives. Flip It! is a quick game designed to show players that perspectives are made, not born — at its best when players begin to see challenges as opportunities and to make doable suggestions around solving problems rather than just rehashing them.

**Players.** 5–20.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, hang four to eight sheets of flip-chart paper on a wall and write the game name on a top-row sheet.
2. Label the bottom row "FEAR" and the top row "HOPE."
3. Give players 5–10 minutes to silently write concerns, issues, and fears about the chosen topic on sticky notes, then post them on the FEAR sheets and discuss.
4. Ask players to survey the fears and "flip" each one by reframing it in terms of hope; give them 10–15 minutes to generate sticky notes for the HOPE row.
5. Post and discuss the hopes, then dot-vote on the hopes the group can take practical action on.
6. Write "TRACTION" on a fresh sheet, move the winning hopes onto it, and brainstorm actionable next steps for each.
7. Optionally, ask volunteers to initial the actions they'd support, signaling interest without a hard commitment.

**Strategy.** Because Flip It opens with FEARS, reassure the group early that they won't wallow there — fears are just raw material for reframing. Model the flip-it move yourself with a quick personal example. Spend the bulk of the time turning fears into hopes and then into traction; with large groups, sort and cluster sticky notes into themes before voting. Set boundaries upfront if you want to scope concerns to internal or external factors only.

**Source.** [flip-it](https://gamestorming.com/flip-it/)

### Forced Analogy

**Object of play.** We understand things by grouping them with other things of similar type and function. The Forced Analogy game breaks these hard-wired categories and allows us to see things from a different angle, opening new possibilities in problem solving and idea generation.

**Players.** 1–10.

**Duration.** 15 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Generate a random list of things — animals, objects, or people — and write each item on its own index card.
2. For each item, write some of its qualities or attributes on the card (e.g., "An airplane flies through the air, moves along predefined routes, and has an autopilot feature").
3. Shuffle the cards and distribute them randomly among participants.
4. For each card, ask participants to develop analogies to the problem at hand: "How is this problem similar to [random object]?" and "How would I solve this problem with [random object]?"
5. Alternatively, work through one analogy together as a group, e.g., "How would we use a paper clip to solve our data integration problem?"
6. Capture the surprising connections and reframe them as candidate solutions for the real problem.

**Strategy.** A truly random list of objects will push the boundaries of the group's mindset and create new perspectives. If the group keeps reaching for safe, on-topic objects, the technique loses its bite — so consider having an unbiased nonparticipant generate the list in advance to guarantee the items are genuinely unrelated. The strangeness of the analogy is what does the cognitive work.

**Source.** [forced-analogy](https://gamestorming.com/forced-analogy/)

### Forced Ranking

**Object of play.** When prioritizing, a group may need to agree on a single, ranked list of items. Forced ranking obligates the group to make difficult decisions, with each item ranked relative to the others — an important step when a clear, prioritized list is needed for investments, business priorities, features, or requirements.

**Players.** Small group of 3–10 participants.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour, depending on list length, criteria, and group size.

**How to play.**
1. Bring two things to the session: an unranked list of items and the criteria for ranking them. Make the criteria as concrete as possible (e.g., "Most important features for User X" or "Most potential impact over the next year").
2. If there are multiple dimensions, rank separately for each criterion and combine scores at the end — don't ask people to weigh more than one criterion at once.
3. Aim for about 10 items in a small group; longer lists are workable if displayed on a flip chart or whiteboard so everyone can see them at once.
4. Build a matrix with items down one axis and criteria across the other.
5. Each participant assigns a unique number to every item — #1 for most important, #2 for next, and so on down to least important. No ties allowed; the ranking is "forced."
6. Tally the rankings, share the prioritized list, and discuss next steps based on the result.

**Strategy.** Creating a forced ranking can be uncomfortable because it requires clear-cut judgments about a set of items. In many groups, the default mode is to add items and string together broad agreement rather than to rank ruthlessly. Pushing past that resistance, guided by clear criteria, is the entire point — the discomfort is the technique working.

**Source.** [350](https://gamestorming.com/350/)

### Force Field Analysis

**Object of play.** The Force Field Analysis game is a time-tested way to evaluate the forces that affect change — both the drivers pushing for it and the restraints holding it back. Making a deliberate effort to see the system surrounding change can help us steer it in the direction we want it to move.

**Players.** 5–30.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1.5 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, draw the proposed change in the middle of a large sheet, label it, and write "Forces FOR Change" on the top left and "Forces AGAINST Change" on the top right, with big arrows pointing inward.
2. Introduce the change topic and explain that the goal is to evaluate its feasibility.
3. Ask players to spend 5–10 minutes silently generating driving forces on sticky notes (one idea per note), then 5–10 more minutes generating restraining forces.
4. Have players weight each sticky note from 1 (weak) to 5 (strong).
5. Post the FOR notes and have the group cluster them by affinity; create overarching category names for each cluster and write them inside the FOR arrow.
6. Average the numeric weights within each cluster and write that average next to its category.
7. Repeat the clustering and weighting process for the AGAINST notes.
8. Total each side, write the totals at the bottom, and discuss whether and how the change should proceed.

**Strategy.** Players often arrive with preconceived beliefs; balance the conversation by pushing eager groups to consider real obstacles and reluctant groups to imagine their best-case scenario. Don't let fixed perspectives dominate. Treat outliers as signal, not noise — they often expose unforeseen angles. The numeric totals are a discussion gauge, not a verdict. For a deeper read, ask the group to find meta-categories (politics, economics, culture, mid-level management) above the cluster categories. Based on the framework developed by Kurt Lewin.

**Source.** [force-field-analysis](https://gamestorming.com/force-field-analysis/)

### Friend or Foe?

**Object of play.** The object of this game is to create an organizational map of your stakeholders — sometimes resembling the org chart, sometimes a context-specific shape. In addition to mapping organizational relationships, you'll analyze each stakeholder's contextual disposition toward your initiative so you can plan accordingly.

**Players.** 5–15, ideally drawn from across the project's organizational spectrum.

**Duration.** 30–60 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Run a five-minute brainstorm: who are our project stakeholders, inside and outside the org? One stakeholder per sticky note.
2. Have each player present their stickies and place them on a wall, briefly explaining their thinking.
3. As a group, organize the stickies into a rough draft org chart on the wall.
4. Using the draft as a guide, have a scribe redraw a cleaner version on a whiteboard top-to-bottom; abbreviate areas less relevant to the analysis.
5. Label all stakeholders and denote any future roles that don't exist yet but matter; draw a border around the area affected by the change.
6. Use dotted lines or alternate colors to handle matrixed teams without visual clutter, and draw people as circles with upside-down "U" shapes rather than departments-as-boxes.
7. Step 2 — discuss each stakeholder one by one to map disposition (for, neutral, against), level of impact, relationship strength, and participation energy.
8. Layer findings onto the map using tokens — green smiley for supportive, partial battery icon for low-energy, cloud for confused, and so on.

**Strategy.** Org charts are unambiguous; the value of this exercise is in mapping the less obvious things — influence, real (vs. titled) decision power, disposition toward your specific initiative. Defined structures are rarely challenged but offer little insight on their own. The interesting layer is people's actual attitudes and informal influence, which a safe, structured, collaborative setting tends to surface openly and often surprisingly. Pairs well with Who Do, Empathy Map, and Understanding Chain.

**Source.** [fof](https://gamestorming.com/fof/)

### Give-And-Take Matrix

**Object of play.** The goal of this game is to map out the motivations and interactions among actors in a system — from individuals who must collaborate on a task to organizations linked for the long term. A give-and-take matrix is a useful diagnostic tool that helps players explore how value flows through the group.

**Players.** Small group.

**Duration.** 1 hour or more.

**How to play.**
1. Compile a list of all the actors in the system, either in advance or at the start of the exercise.
2. Create a matrix with the same list of actors along both the horizontal and vertical axes — vertical axis as "from," horizontal axis as "to."
3. Fill in the diagonal cells (where each actor intersects with itself) with that actor's primary motivation — a brief phrase capturing what they want from the system.
4. For each off-diagonal cell, ask "What can the row actor offer to the column actor?" and capture the value flowing in that direction.
5. Work through one actor's row at a time so the conversation stays focused.
6. Note the cells that are easy to fill, and pay extra attention to the ones that are hard — they expose disconnections or tensions in the system.
7. Use the completed matrix to identify gaps, asymmetries, and the most complete picture of how each actor can benefit the others.

**Strategy.** Completing the matrix may involve research before and after the initial mapping — surveys or interviews with actual actors can validate the inputs and the assumed value flows. Some intersections will surface previously unconnected actors or pairs that are quietly at odds. Alongside stakeholder analysis and boundary mapping, the Give-and-Take Matrix helps players define both the actors and the interactions inside a system before designing changes to it.

**Source.** [give-and-take-matrix](https://gamestorming.com/give-and-take-matrix/)

### Graphic Gameplan

**Object of play.** Ideas never come to fruition without a plan. The Graphic Gameplan shows a team how it will get where it wants to go with a project — turning a big idea into an executable, visible action plan, which is one of the differences between teams that are merely good and those that are outstanding.

**Players.** Small groups, but can also be done individually.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 2 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, pick one or more projects that need traction.
2. In a large white space (3–4 feet high by 6–12 feet wide), draw a matrix with a column for projects on the left and a timeline across the top.
3. Tell the players the goal is to reach consensus on the specific tasks required to complete each project.
4. Write the first project's name in the top-left cell and add other projects down that column.
5. Either set the timeline upfront in days/weeks/months along the top row, or wait until the steps are mapped.
6. With sticky notes, ask the group to choose a project and agree aloud on the first step required — write it on a sticky and place it in the first cell next to that project.
7. Continue eliciting second, third, fourth steps until each project has a complete sequence.
8. Repeat for every project. Optionally, add owners' names to the stickies and capture needed resources on a side flip chart.

**Strategy.** Building the gameplan as a group breaks big projects into manageable chunks and uses the "group mind" to raise the quality of project flow — important steps are less likely to be missed. Don't accept the first sequence the group lays out. Press: Does this really have to come first? Can these be combined? Do steps in one project block another? Setting the timeline after the steps are mapped often produces a more honest plan, since premature deadlines distort what people are willing to commit to. Based on The Grove Consultants' Graphic Gameplan template.

**Source.** [graphic-gameplan](https://gamestorming.com/graphic-gameplan/)

### Graphic Jam

**Object of play.** Graphic Jam is an all-purpose visualization game that exercises the visual cortex by forcing players to draw abstract concepts. It works as a warm-up before other games or as a standalone exercise for sharpening the skill of visualizing words like "strategy" or "justice" — a skill increasingly useful as business problems get more complex.

**Players.** 5–15.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Set up a large flat white display area and give all players access to sticky notes and index cards.
2. Ask each player to spend 1–2 minutes writing, on index cards, words they have difficulty conceptualizing and drawing — one word or phrase per card.
3. Gather all the cards, shuffle them, draw one, read it aloud, and tape it up in the display area.
4. Give players 2–3 minutes to draw a visual representation of the word on a sticky note.
5. Have everyone post their sticky under the index card with the related word.
6. Repeat steps 3–5 until all (or most) words have been worked through; redraw if you hit a repeat or near-synonym.
7. Step back and let the group walk the gallery of visualizations.
8. Debrief: ask what certain images mean, how artists got from word to image, which words were hardest, and how the group might apply visualization in their daily work.

**Strategy.** Most words people contribute will be on the abstract end, so set timing based on group size, words generated, and concept complexity. When you call last words, ask if there are any burning concepts the group still wants to see drawn. Close by giving players a chance to compare creative processes and techniques. Inspired by the activity created by Leslie Salmon-Zhu, co-founder of the International Forum of Visual Practitioners.

**Source.** [graphic-jam](https://gamestorming.com/graphic-jam/)

### Heart, Hand, Mind

**Object of play.** The object of this game is to examine an issue from another perspective and find significance in it, by looking at a product, course of action, or situation through three complementary lenses that map to emotion, action, and reason.

**Players.** 1–10.

**Duration.** 10 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Pick the issue, product, or course of action you want to examine.
2. Look at it through the Heart lens: what makes it emotionally engaging?
3. Look at it through the Hand lens: what makes it tangible and practical?
4. Look at it through the Mind lens: what makes it logical and sensible?
5. List the characteristics or features that appeal to each lens.
6. Score each category from 1 to 10 to evaluate strengths and weaknesses across the three.
7. Use the score profile to spot which lens is underdeveloped and where the offering needs work.

**Strategy.** Significant products, activities, and experiences appeal to a whole person — they "feed the heart, hand, and mind." Use these three lenses as a way to find, clarify, or diagnose the meaning of any endeavor, and to surface imbalances where something is, say, intellectually compelling but emotionally flat or practically unworkable. Inspired by Swiss educational reformer Heinrich Pestalozzi.

### Help Me Understand

**Object of play.** Help Me Understand assumes employees come to meetings with widely different questions about a topic or change, and that leadership can't possibly anticipate them all. The game gives employees a chance to externalize what's on their minds and have leadership respond in real time — letting some sunshine in on a project so the people implementing change have fewer lingering questions.

**Players.** 5–25.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1.5 hours.

**How to play.**
1. In a large white space, write the meeting topic and post the headers WHO?, WHAT?, WHEN?, WHERE?, and HOW? across the top.
2. Tell players the goal is to help leadership understand and respond to all questions about the topic.
3. Start with WHO? Give players five minutes to silently write as many WHO questions as they can on sticky notes.
4. Have players post their questions under WHO? and ask volunteers to cluster them by topical similarity.
5. Highlight the largest clusters and ask leadership to respond to the most common questions and any interesting outliers.
6. Repeat for WHAT?, WHEN?, WHERE?, and HOW?, with leadership responding to the most salient questions in each round.
7. At the close, gather all the sticky notes so leadership can review and follow up on questions not covered live.

**Strategy.** You can run the five questions back-to-back (employees write all rounds first, then leadership responds in the second half) or interleave (leadership responds after each round). The first approach lets employees write uninterrupted and saves leadership time; the second changes the questions employees ask as they get information. Choose based on the group. During clustering, label emergent themes near each cluster so both leadership and employees can see where attention is concentrating. Adapted from Sam Kaner's "Who What When Where and How," itself inspired by the "Five W's and H" exercise in Van Gundy's Techniques of Structured Problem Solving.

**Source.** [help-me-understand](https://gamestorming.com/help-me-understand/)

### Here, There, Everywhere

**Object of play.** Here, There, Everywhere emerged so that workshop participants could detail — sometimes in front of the room, sometimes just to themselves — how they'll change their behavior once they return to work. It turns interesting content into committed action by forcing reflection at three levels: the specific moment, its application, and the general principle.

**Players.** Everyone in the meeting.

**Duration.** 3–5 minutes for reflection and commitments; +10 minutes for sharing and discussion.

**How to play.**
1. Tell participants you're going to take a moment to reflect on and crystallize a learning from the material just covered.
2. Ask them to go back and review whatever the material is — sticky-note walls, digital canvases, notes, or slides — so the content is fresh again.
3. Have each participant capture three things, one per sticky note: HERE — something that caught their attention or piqued curiosity (a game, a comment, a concept, a framework).
4. THERE — how they might take that specific example and implement it at work or in their personal life, with enough detail to make it easy.
5. EVERYWHERE — a generalized interpretation of that thing, an underlying principle absent context, that allows for more universal application.
6. Optionally, break participants into small groups to discuss their reflections, then have groups share back to the room.

**Strategy.** It's a common meeting pitfall to either rush or skip the closing phase. Closing can mean making decisions and commitments, or reflecting on personal development; Here, There, Everywhere works for either case. However you close, give it the time it deserves — the payoff of a workshop is not the content covered but the behavior change it produces. The HERE/THERE/EVERYWHERE frame gives participants a portable thinking tool they can apply after any meeting. Created by David Mastronardi and Eric Wittenberg.

**Source.** [here-there-everywhere](https://gamestorming.com/here-there-everywhere/)

### Hero's Journey Agenda

**Object of play.** The Hero's Journey Agenda is a unique way to lay out the agenda for a meeting or workshop that creates a sense of adventure and builds anticipation for what's coming. It maps the day onto the structure Joseph Campbell identified in mythology — call to adventure, helpers, threshold, trials, the pit, new powers, and the return — turning an agenda into a story.

**Players.** One, usually the facilitator, drawn live in front of a group.

**Duration.** 10–15 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Draw a large circle on a whiteboard or flip chart and tell people it represents everything you'll do today, starting at the top.
2. Draw a stick figure at the top, then a wavy "threshold" line dividing the known world from the unknown.
3. At ~1 o'clock, write "The Call" — talk about purpose, why this work matters, and what brought everyone here.
4. At ~2 o'clock, draw helpers and mentors — the openers, frameworks, experts, or tools that will guide the group.
5. Mark the threshold crossing with a coffee or tea cup — the first break, where the group leaves ordinary life.
6. At 4–5 o'clock, write "Problems and Pitfalls" with explosions or barbed wire — the brainstorming, mapping, and exploration of the problem space.
7. Draw "The Pit" at the bottom — often lunch — the low point where deep reflection happens before rebuilding.
8. At 7–8 o'clock, write "Powers" with stars or a caped figure — the solutions, prototypes, and customer journey work that emerges after the pit.
9. At ~10 o'clock, write "The Return" — how the group will bring what they learned back to ordinary life and to their teams.
10. Close by inviting additions or questions before launching the meeting.

**Strategy.** This is a powerful frame for relatively large-scale sessions. Spend time upfront drawing it and walking through it with key stakeholders. It also acts as a litmus test for the meeting itself — if you can't articulate the call, the problems to explore, the powers to gain, and what to bring back, you should ask whether the meeting needs to happen at all. The agenda creates positive energy and ensures the major bases are covered. Created by Dave Gray, inspired by Joseph Campbell and James Macanufo's Pie Chart Agenda.

**Source.** [heros-journey-agenda](https://gamestorming.com/heros-journey-agenda/)

### Heuristic Ideation Technique

**Object of play.** The Heuristic Ideation Technique (HIT) generates new product, feature, or concept ideas by systematically combining attributes from two unrelated domains — pairing every element of one set with every element of another to surface combinations the group would never reach by free brainstorming. (Source page returned a JavaScript-required error; digest written from common Gamestorming/innovation knowledge of the HIT method.)

**Players.** 3–10, ideally with mixed backgrounds.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Define the problem or opportunity space — the kind of new idea you want, e.g., new uses for an existing product, or new product concepts in a category.
2. Brainstorm a list of attributes for one dimension — for instance, key features, customer needs, or use occasions related to your domain.
3. Brainstorm a parallel list of attributes for a second, often unrelated, dimension — materials, technologies, contexts, or another product category entirely.
4. Build a matrix with the first list along the rows and the second list along the columns.
5. Walk every cell of the matrix and ask, "What product or idea would result from combining these two attributes?"
6. Capture every plausible combination, even the strange ones, on sticky notes inside or beside the matrix.
7. Cluster, dot-vote, or use a follow-on selection tool (How-Now-Wow works well) to advance the most promising combinations to prototyping.

**Strategy.** The power of HIT is mechanical exhaustiveness — by forcing the group to consider every pairing, you bypass the brain's tendency to prune to familiar combinations. The wider the gap between your two dimensions, the more surprising the output. Avoid editorializing during the matrix walk; capture combinations first, judge them later. The technique pairs well with Forced Analogy as a divergence engine and with How-Now-Wow as a convergence partner.

**Source.** [heuristic-ideation-technique](https://gamestorming.com/heuristic-ideation-technique/)

### History Map

**Object of play.** Organizations naturally look ahead, but the past can be as informative as the future. The History Map game shows you how to map the moments and metrics that shaped your organization, helping a group discover, recognize, and appreciate what got them where they are — and giving new people a fast way to absorb organizational history and culture.

**Players.** 10–50.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1.5 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Draw a continuous timeline along the bottom of several flip-chart pages hung end-to-end on a wall, with year increments matched to the organization's age (5- or 10-year windows for long histories). Leave more space at significant years.
2. Have each player write their name and draw a self-portrait on a sticky note and post it above the year they joined; encourage stories about first impressions and motivations as people approach the wall.
3. Optionally, before the post-up, ask everyone to physically line up by tenure and let them self-organize through conversation.
4. Ask questions about company successes, lessons learned, leadership and vision changes, culture shifts, market trends, structural reorganizations, regulation shifts, revenue and headcount swings, and major projects. Plot answers on the map with text and images.
5. If you don't draw freehand confidently, prepare an icon set in advance — stars for successes, arrows for revenue or headcount changes, a toolbox for projects.
6. Summarize findings and ask players what they learned and why organizational history matters; look for emergent patterns and relate the past to the future.

**Strategy.** Mapping history should be enjoyable — a time for storytelling, reflection, and appreciation. Set a supportive tone and encourage honesty about hard times as well as successes. Always know the level of institutional memory in the room — old-timers are essential, but newcomers' fresh eyes also matter depending on your goal. If the meeting runs long, leave the map up so people can keep adding to it on breaks. Use sticky notes for uncertain dates so you can correct them later. Based on The Grove Consultants' Graphic History template.

**Source.** [history-map](https://gamestorming.com/history-map/)

### Homepage Bingo

**Object of play.** Ranking selections can be quite boring. In this game, participants build the homepage of a product, service, or organization's website as if it were a bingo card — everyone has to make quick, on-the-fly decisions about what goes top, what goes middle, and what gets pushed off entirely.

**Players.** Small groups.

**Duration.** 30–60 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Before the session, prepare a homepage frame template — a simple layout with prioritized zones from top to bottom.
2. Prepare an identical set of cards (sticky notes work well) for every participant, each card naming one attribute or feature you want to rank. Keep one set for yourself as the caller.
3. Seat participants around a table or surface, far enough apart that they can't easily see each other's homepages.
4. Like in bingo, call out the cards in random order, with a brief shared-understanding explanation — but don't over-explain or you'll bias the placement.
5. As you call each card, participants must immediately decide where to place it on their homepage frame.
6. As space fills up, participants will be forced to push lower-priority cards off the page entirely; have them keep these in a separate pile.
7. When the bingo is over, post all completed homepages on the wall as an "exhibition" and open discussion.

**Strategy.** It will look messy at the beginning as participants start pushing down — or out — cards they originally valued. That mess is the point. Forcing quick judgments under time pressure surfaces real priorities that careful ranking would smooth over. Discuss the pushed-out pile after the game; those rejections often hold the most insight. Illustrated cards work especially well because images strip away the prejudice of words and leave more room for interpretation.

**Source.** [homepage-bingo](https://gamestorming.com/homepage-bingo/)

### How-Now-Wow Matrix

**Object of play.** When converging after a brainstorm, people tend to pick the ideas most familiar to them — a "creative paradox" or "creadox." The How-Now-Wow Matrix is an idea selection tool that breaks the creadox by forcing players to weigh each idea on two parameters at once: originality and ease of implementation.

**Players.** 1 to 30.

**Duration.** 10 to 40 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Draw a 2-by-2 matrix on flip-chart paper. The X axis is originality of the idea; the Y axis is ease of implementation.
2. Label the quadrants: NOW (Blue) — normal ideas, easy to implement, the low-hanging fruit; HOW (Yellow) — original ideas, currently impossible given technology or budget; WOW (Green) — original ideas that are also implementable today.
3. List the ideas from your ideation phase on large charts around the room.
4. Give each player 3 sticky dots in each color (3 blue, 3 yellow, 3 green) — typically 9 dots per person, adjust based on time and idea volume.
5. Have each player vote for their 3 best ideas in each category by placing colored dots in front of those ideas.
6. Tally dots on each idea; the highest count of a single color categorizes that idea. Ties: blue=green resolves to blue; yellow=green resolves to green.
7. Walk away with a bucket of WOW/Green ideas to develop, NOW/Blue ideas for immediate implementation, and HOW/Yellow ideas to watch for the future.

**Strategy.** The technique exists specifically to keep groups from washing out their best divergent ideas in convergence — the most familiar choice usually feels safest, which is exactly why it loses. By splitting "original but unworkable" from "original AND workable," the matrix protects the rare WOW ideas that actually deserve to advance. Check that your yellow dots are visible from a distance, and consider flipping the Y-axis (per Martien van Steenbergen) so WOW lands in the upper right. Adapted from the COCD Matrix developed by The Center for Development of Creative Thinking (Vullings, Byttebier, Spaas).

**Source.** [how-now-wow-matrix](https://gamestorming.com/how-now-wow-matrix/)

### Image-ination

**Object of play.** To generate new ideas about a topic you feel stuck on by using random images to spark unexpected associations.

**Players.** 5–7 per group.

**Duration.** 15 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, assemble 3–5 wordless photos or images per person, prioritizing variety over beauty.
2. Lay a flip-chart sheet on the table and write a one- to three-word topic in the center (e.g., "Finding New Customers").
3. Place pictures face down around the edges and give each person sticky notes or index cards.
4. Frame the goal: think as widely as possible, going beyond what the group already knows; demonstrate by showing an image and stating several ways it relates to the topic.
5. Have each participant flip an image and silently write one idea per note for how it could relate to the topic, posting notes around the central topic.
6. Allow about five minutes per round, then have people draw new images and repeat until time or images run out.
7. Cluster the notes into related groups, choose one image per cluster as an illustration, and write a short title under it.
8. If multiple groups played, have each share their photo-titles, then discuss what the images suggest and list possible actions to take.

**Strategy.** Images spark insights and create new associations by forcing rapid switching between visual and verbal modes. Keep time frames short so participants free-associate from a less-considered space rather than reaching for the answers they already have. Reassure people the goal is not a finished design or specific answer; if someone "can't find the right picture," that is itself a creative opening to find a different association.

**Source.** [image-ination](https://gamestorming.com/image-ination/)

### Impact & Effort Matrix

**Object of play.** A decision-making exercise where possible actions are mapped on two axes—effort to implement and potential impact—so contributors can balance and evaluate suggestions before committing.

**Players.** Based on small groups, but scales to any size.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour, depending on group size.

**How to play.**
1. Frame the goal as a "What to do" or "What we need" question (e.g., "What do we need to reach our goal?").
2. Ask each person to generate ideas individually on sticky notes.
3. Using Post-Up, have participants present their ideas back by placing them inside a 2×2 matrix with axes for Impact (potential payoff) and Effort (cost of acting).
4. As notes go up, let the group openly debate placement; ideas often shift up in impact or down in effort as the discussion sharpens them.
5. Read the four quadrants: high impact / low effort holds the best bets, high impact / high effort warrants further study, low impact / high effort should usually be avoided, low impact / low effort warrants further study.
6. Use the populated matrix to commit to next steps, focusing first on the high-impact / low-effort cluster.

**Strategy.** Treat the lower ends of both axes as "not negative" rather than "negative impact" or "negative effort," or participants will pile every note in the upper-right corner. Group dialogue while placing notes is the real value: shared evaluation usually surfaces a tight cluster the group is most committed to. For non-profits, the high-impact / high-effort quadrant often represents the core of the mission rather than a "maybe."

**Source.** [impact-effort-matrix-2](https://gamestorming.com/impact-effort-matrix-2/)

### Impromptu Speed Networking

**Object of play.** An ideal activity for flex points in a gathering—the start, after lunch, end of day—that lets everyone "get there" mentally by engaging the purpose, gives every participant meaningful air time, and energizes the room by getting people standing and moving.

**Players.** Unlimited; scales well from about a dozen to thousands.

**Duration.** 20 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Invite everyone to leave their stuff at their seats and gather in an open space where there is room to stand and move around.
2. Pose a juicy question directly tied to the purpose of the gathering.
3. Ask people to reflect silently on the question for a full minute.
4. Explain the rules: when the chimes sound, find a partner (preferably someone you know less well); raise a hand if you still need a partner; have a five-minute conversation about the question.
5. When the chimes ring again, find a new partner and repeat; when chimes ring continuously, stop.
6. Run three rounds total.
7. Transition into the next segment, or invite pairs to combine into knee-to-knee circles of four to six and discuss what struck them about the conversations.

**Strategy.** Debrief the process as well as the content. Ask participants what it was like: how starting on their feet built rather than drained energy, how repeating the same question with different partners deepened their thinking, and how an open question creates more room for creative thought than a presentation. The point is to introduce the pattern of generative conversation, not just to harvest answers.

**Source.** [impromptu-speed-networking](https://gamestorming.com/impromptu-speed-networking/)

### Innovation Generator

**Object of play.** Help teams identify and address customer needs by combining current or potential inventions with the values they could deliver, fueling fresh innovations.

**Players.** 5–8.

**Duration.** 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Hand out sticky notes and markers and draw three columns on a whiteboard: A) Customers'/Prospective Customers' Problems; B) Invention/Value; C) Innovation.
2. Ask players to brainstorm problems customers in your market may face, post them in column A, and discuss what those issues mean for your company.
3. As a group, choose about five inventions your company has or could create and write each on a different colored sticky note in column B.
4. For each invention, explore values it could deliver beyond its current purpose and post those around the invention notes; consider how each value could resolve problems from column A.
5. Collaborate to develop new innovations in column C by combining the inventions with their values.
6. Capture and prioritize the most promising innovation candidates for follow-up.

**Strategy.** Stay disciplined about anchoring innovations in the column-one problems—this is what keeps the exercise from drifting into clever ideas that nobody actually needs. The combination of value and invention is the fuel for innovation; without a real customer problem in view, you generate features rather than outcomes. Use the cross-column connections to build a defensible case for which innovations to pursue first.

**Source.** [innovation-generator](https://gamestorming.com/innovation-generator/)

### JourneyFolio

**Object of play.** A framework that helps you structure your career journey and portfolio so you can put your own narrative on it, going beyond the limited dimensions of a standard resume or CV.

**Players.** Primarily an individual exercise, but useful to involve others updating their own resumes, plus family, friends, classmates, and colleagues who can help fill out the swim lanes.

**Duration.** Anywhere from a few hours to a few weeks; JourneyFolio is never quite complete.

**How to play.**
1. In Google Slides (or similar software with inter-slide linking), set up a timeline grid with multiple layers such as Personal Highlights, Education and Learning, Side Projects, and Professional Path.
2. Gather artifacts—photos, certificates, badges, report cards, journals—and lay out moments along the timeline by layer.
3. For each moment, build a slide that tells the story with words, images, quotes, and links.
4. Add a "HOME" hyperlink button on every slide that returns to the main timeline grid.
5. Color-code points on the timeline (using spheres, emoji, or squares) to match each layer.
6. Hyperlink each timeline point to its detail slide so reviewers can navigate the story directly.
7. Iterate freely—JourneyFolio is meant to evolve as your story does.

**Strategy.** Recruiters spend an average of about seven seconds on a resume, so a CV may get you an interview but rarely sets you apart. JourneyFolio communicates a fuller story in an elegant, navigable format that leaves an impression. You don't need to be a "capital-C" creative—built on Google Slides, the format is open to anyone. Related games like History Map, Cover Story, and Storyboard can help spark memories for the timeline.

### The Long Table

**Object of play.** Use the format of a dinner party—structured by etiquette, with conversation as the only "course"—as a stylized but open-ended, non-hierarchical stage for public engagement and thought.

**Players.** Group-scale; designed for public participation around a single table.

**Duration.** Open-ended, typically the length of a dinner gathering.

**How to play.**
1. Set up a long dining table at the center of the room as the focal point and invite people to gather around it.
2. Frame the gathering with a theme or question and the etiquette of a shared meal—people speak in turn, listen, and stay present.
3. Anyone may take a seat at the table to join the conversation; others observe from around the room.
4. Participants are free to leave the table when they are done, opening a seat for the next person who wants to contribute.
5. Let conversation rotate organically as new voices replace old ones throughout the session.
6. Close by reflecting on what was said and what the format made possible.

**Strategy.** The Long Table works because it pairs theatrical craft with political and social commitment: the (often-feminized) domestic realm becomes a stage for public thought. The etiquette of the dinner table provides just enough structure to make participation feel safe and patterned, while the open seating keeps the format non-hierarchical. Use it when you want depth of conversation rather than breadth of polling, and when you want the form itself—an invitation to sit down together—to do part of the work.

### Low-Tech Social Network

**Object of play.** Introduce event participants to one another by co-creating a mural-sized, visual network of their connections.

**Players.** Large groups in an event setting.

**Duration.** About 25 minutes to create the first version; the network stays up for the whole event and can be added to throughout.

**How to play.**
1. Give each participant a 5×8 index card and access to markers, and prepare a substantial wall covered in butcher paper.
2. The emcee explains: the group will build the social network in the room, starting with the most fundamental element—who you are.
3. Each person draws an avatar (profile picture) on their card and adds their name at the bottom; optionally, two "tags" representing who they are or what they're interested in.
4. Direct everyone to bring their cards and a marker to the wall and "upload" themselves by sticking their card to the butcher paper.

6. Leave the network up for the duration of the event so people can browse, add new connections, and discover relationships.

**Strategy.** The first pass will be chaotic—a tangle of "spaghetti lines"—and that's fine; the messiness is part of the discovery. Encourage people to revisit the network throughout the event; new conversations spark new connections, and previously unknown links between attendees often surface. The artifact is also a living visual archive of the gathering.

### Make A World

**Object of play.** Create a three-dimensional model of a desired future state, letting players imagine that future and take a first concrete step toward bringing it to life.

### Making The Game Come Alive

**Object of play.** Show how custom-designed games—built specifically for an audience or situation—create more memorable and meaningful sessions than off-the-shelf templates, by walking through one such game (a music-themed strategy session for a career-changing musician).

**Players.** Tailored to fit; the example was a one-on-one session with a custom structure for one player and a facilitator.

**Duration.** Roughly three hours of structured play across five parts, plus an after-session assessment.

**How to play.**
1. Use the audience's identity as the game mechanic—in the example, music: brainstorm what you love in life and work as "riffs" (about 60 minutes).
2. Group ideas into common themes and assign major and minor "chords" to major and supporting groupings (about 30 minutes).
3. Arrange the chords into logical "progressions," using literal guitars to test that the combinations feel right (about 30 minutes).
4. Mix items across progressions to form "songs" and order the progressions by importance or impact (about 30 minutes).
5. Close loops and identify the "hit formula"—use star-shaped sticky notes to distill the intent of each progression into a brand essence or anthem (about 30 minutes plus follow-up).

**Strategy.** Custom games work because they engage the player's identity; build the structure around what the player already loves so the form motivates engagement. Expect the first run of any new game to wobble and adapt on the fly. Most published games skew visual because their authors are visual designers, but games can be auditory, tactile, or kinesthetic—matching the dominant senses of your players makes the session more relevant and memorable.

### Manage What You Measure

**Object of play.** Understand how customer behavior connects to higher-level corporate objectives, and direct organizational efforts to the most influential of those behaviors.

**Players.** 5–15, ideally drawn from across the KPI spectrum—individual contributors, management, and executive leadership.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 3 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Introduce the game: the goal is to focus resources on the customer behaviors that most impact corporate strategy.
2. Write a corporate-wide Strategic Goal at the top of a whiteboard.
3. Below it, write the success measures (KPIs) for that goal on differently colored sticky notes.
4. Give players five minutes to silently brainstorm customer behaviors that influence those KPIs, one per sticky note, color-matched where possible.
5. Have players post notes under the appropriate KPI grouping.
6. Spend 5–10 minutes clarifying confusing notes and flagging behaviors that map to multiple KPIs.
7. Repeat the brainstorm one more time, asking what behaviors drive the previously listed behaviors.
8. Run a Dot Vote (five dots per player) on the most important customer behaviors given the strategic goal.
9. Tally votes, debrief surprises and dismissals, and consider Impact & Effort or the NUF Test if further prioritization is needed.
10. Assign baseline values and target goals to the prioritized behaviors; consider Who-What-When for ownership.

**Strategy.** Different levels of the org understand goals differently. Mapping the relationships between high-level objectives, mid-tier KPIs, and customer behaviors creates a shared map everyone can navigate. Once the map exists, every team's KPI report tells a story executives can connect back to the outcomes they actually care about. Pair with Empathy Map upfront for deeper customer understanding.

**Source.** [manage-what-you-measure](https://gamestorming.com/manage-what-you-measure/)

### Mapping Design Operations

**Object of play.** Build shared understanding of how Design Ops operates within the larger organizational context—visually mapping an existing function, or designing one if none exists.

**Players.** 1–6 depending on objective; for organizational mapping, work in groups including partner organizations and stakeholders to maximize accuracy.

**Duration.** 15 minutes for an individual napkin sketch, half a day for current-state mapping, up to two days for designing a future Design Ops model with vision, mission, and metrics.

**How to play.**
1. Start with the **Stakeholders** in the "Who are we?" circle, using different sticky-note colors per stakeholder type (external vendors, internal support functions, clients).
2. Move to the **What do we do?** section and map the value propositions offered to each stakeholder, color-matched to the related stakeholder.
3. Map the remaining building blocks of the Design Ops model, working clockwise around the canvas and saving "What Constrains Us?" for last.
4. Assess the model with green (strength) and red (weakness) sticky notes—or "+/–" markers—on the strong and weak elements.
5. Use additional canvases to sketch improved or alternative Design Ops org models.

Use it not just to design a productive Design Ops org but to educate the rest of the company on what design can do and how to plug in. Players unfamiliar with design may stay quiet at first, but their participation pays compounding dividends in alignment. Pair with Empathy Map beforehand and Business Model Canvas afterward for a fuller managerial view.

### Mapping Organizational Culture

**Object of play.** Assess, map, and transform organizational culture through deep reflection, producing the intelligence needed to make a business case for change interventions, executive support, and budget.

**Players.** Use individually or with a group; for groups, gather 5–6 people from the same function (IT, HR, finance) who work together and know each other well. The boss should not attend.

**Duration.** 15 minutes for a personal napkin sketch; up to 90 minutes for a group session.

**How to play.**
1. Review the three sections using a garden analogy: **Outcomes** are the fruits, **Behaviors** are the heart of the culture, and **Enablers and Blockers** are what helps or hinders the garden.
2. Start with **Behavior**—the easiest to discuss—using guide questions; write one behavior per sticky note, group similar ones, and remove duplicates. Be specific; ask how the behavior would look as a scene in a movie.
3. Move to **Outcomes**: behavior by behavior, ask what happens to the business because of it; place outcome notes near related behaviors and draw a line connecting them.
4. Move to **Enablers and Blockers**: behavior by behavior, identify the organizational incentives that explain why people behave as they do; place notes near related behaviors with connecting lines.
5. Step back, examine the map, and discuss whether the relationships make sense and the behaviors are concrete enough.

**Strategy.** Roughly 60–70% of change initiatives fail to meet their objectives, and the leading cause is resistance to change—so do not skimp on risk awareness. Culture Mapping surfaces information that no other method can reliably collect, giving executives access to frontline culture in a way they could never achieve directly (the water-cooler talk shuts down when senior leaders walk by). Keep relationships as direct as possible: ideally one outcome and one enabler/blocker per behavior.

**Source.** [mapping-organizational-culture](https://gamestorming.com/mapping-organizational-culture/)

### Memory Wall

**Object of play.** Acknowledge employee contributions, celebrate accomplishments, and build camaraderie by surfacing positive stand-out memories of working together.

**Players.** 10–50.

**Duration.** 45 minutes to 1.5 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Provide markers, paper, tape, and a flat drawing surface; reserve usable wall space for display.
2. Ask players to spend 10–15 minutes surveying others in the room and writing down positive, stand-out memories of working together, learning, or participating in organizational life.
3. Have players spend 20–30 minutes drawing each memory on a separate sheet of letter-sized paper, partnering with anyone involved to fill in details.
4. Tape the drawings on the wall to form a visual "memory cloud."
5. Ask volunteers to approach the wall and share the stories behind their memories; when volunteers run out, pick memories that catch your eye and ask their owners to share.
6. Close by inviting silent appreciation for those who have made a positive contribution—and follow up with a happy hour.
7. (Optional) Have players guess whose memory each drawing belongs to, awarding small prizes for correct guesses.

**Strategy.** This is a game of appreciation, not strategy. Enforce only one rule—memories should be positive and uplifting, not offensive or negative—and discourage judgment of artistic ability. The drawings exist to illustrate stories and provide good-natured humor, not to win a contest. If a player struggles to recall a memory, ask open-ended prompts; you can also tailor the wall to a specific project or milestone by drawing a large representation of it as the backdrop.

**Source.** [memory-wall](https://gamestorming.com/memory-wall/)

### Mission Impossible

**Object of play.** Challenge constraints by taking an existing design, process, or idea and changing one foundational aspect that makes it "impossible"—for example, "How do we build a house in a day?" or "What would a browser be without an Internet connection?"

**Players.** Small groups.

**Duration.** 45 minutes to 1 hour, depending on group size.

**How to play.**
1. Develop a question in advance that engages both rational and emotional parts of the brain (e.g., a mobile device with no battery is both an engineering feat and a make-the-world-better proposition).
2. Write the question for the group and explain the challenge.
3. For about 30 minutes, working in pairs or small teams, develop approaches to accomplishing the "impossible," using prompts such as: What new benefits or features might emerge from this constraint? Why is the constraint considered typical or assumed? What core elements are in conflict? Can they be eliminated, replaced, or altered? What if you change time, space, materials, motion, or environment?
4. Have each group present their concepts to the others.
5. Hold a reflective discussion about common and uncommon approaches, producing a list of possible solutions.
6. Define follow-up work to explore the most promising directions.

**Strategy.** This game is excellent for surfacing unexamined assumptions in a product or process. When a product is languishing and needs to be reimagined, the constraint forces a deeper interrogation of why things are the way they are. For slow or overloaded processes, the "fire drill" question—"How would we do this in a day?"—is a powerful framing device that reveals which steps are genuinely required and which are merely habit.

**Source.** [mission-impossible](https://gamestorming.com/mission-impossible/)

### Mood Board

**Object of play.** Create a poster or collage that captures the overall "feel" of an idea, useful as a frame of reference and inspiration throughout development.

**Players.** 1–10.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 2 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Choose a theme to interpret—e.g., "Our Culture," "Next Year," "The New Product."
2. Gather visual material from magazines, the web, and corporate presentations, plus scissors, tape, blank paper, and flip charts.
3. Bring the group together around the materials and the theme.
4. For small teams, co-create a single mood board from individual contributions; for larger groups, interpret the theme separately and then share boards with each other.

### Navigate Your Market Opportunities

**Object of play.** Step back from current product and customer assumptions to identify, evaluate, and prioritize potential markets for innovation, providing a shared language for strategic discussion using the Market Opportunity Navigator.

**Players.** 1–6, depending on objective; a diverse team is recommended for broader, more accurate mapping.

**Duration.** Two hours for a quick pass to two days for a thorough discussion, across three steps.

**How to play.**
1. **Step 1—Identify a Market Opportunity Set:** Define market opportunity as "any application of your abilities for a specific set of customers." Brainstorm the firm's core technologies and unique abilities (one per sticky), then sort with an Affinity Map and summarize on Worksheet 1.
2. Repeat the brainstorm for customer problems those abilities could address; sort and discuss what categories mean for the company.
3. Discuss potential applications and customer types stemming from those abilities, picking a handful of interesting opportunities and placing them on the Navigator.
4. **Step 2—Evaluate Opportunity Attractiveness:** Define an attractive option as offering high potential for value creation and limited challenge in capturing it. 5. **Step 3—Depict Your Agile Focus Dartboard:** Using Worksheet 3, assess each attractive opportunity's relatedness to currently pursued markets. Pick at least one backup option and one growth option to keep open, and depict the decision on the Navigator's right side.
6. Discuss implications for technology, patents, and marketing messages.

### NUF Test

**Object of play.** Run a quick "reality check" on brainstormed ideas by rating each on three criteria: how New, how Useful, and how Feasible it is.

**Players.** Small group.

**Duration.** 15–30 minutes, depending on group size and discussion depth.

**How to play.**
1. Create a matrix listing the candidate ideas down one axis and the three criteria—New, Useful, Feasible—across the other.
2. Define each criterion: New (significantly different from prior approaches and attention-grabbing), Useful (actually solves the problem without creating new ones), Feasible (can be done with available resources and effort).
3. Have each player rate every idea from 1 to 10 on each criterion as a quick gut check.
4. Optionally, have players write scores individually first, then call out results to create the tally.
5. Tally the scores per idea and per criterion.
6. Discuss the results, surfacing uncertainties and previously underrated ideas; consider strengthening weaker ideas (e.g., "How do we make this idea more feasible with fewer resources?").

**Strategy.** The NUF Test is meant to surface weak points so ideas can be strengthened before facing the realities outside the meeting room—not to kill good ideas. Treat low scores as design challenges rather than verdicts; an idea that scores low on Feasible may improve dramatically with a small reframing. The test is adapted from a process used for evaluating patents.

**Source.** [nuf-test](https://gamestorming.com/nuf-test/)

### Object Brainstorm

**Object of play.** Use tangible objects to externalize thinking—objects suggest stories about how they might be used, making them a starting point for free association and exploration.

**Players.** Any.

**Duration.** 30 minutes or more.

**How to play.**
1. Curate a collection of objects ahead of time—favoring "things that do things," such as kitchen gadgets, hand tools, instruction manuals, functional packaging and dispensers, containers, sports equipment, toys, and games.
2. Frame the brainstorm with a question (e.g., "How will the next generation of [X] work?") that asks participants to reimagine an existing product or invent a new one.
3. Direct the group to explore the objects and play with them, letting their physical features, uses, and personalities suggest ideas (e.g., a stapler's hinged jaw might inspire a new way to bend steel).
4. Allow time for free association; participants are likely to stumble onto fully formed ideas as objects "explain themselves."
5. After a set time, have participants share their ideas and document them.
6. Decide on next steps—voting on a concept to pursue, or moving into another brainstorming exercise.

**Strategy.** Decide in advance whether to use a set of objects or a single object: a set produces wider, more divergent ideas, while a single item forces deeper study and association along the lines of forced analogy. Use sets for larger groups and divergent brainstorms; use a single object for smaller groups and focused inquiry. A good collection evolves over time as other contributors add to the cache.

### Open Space

**Object of play.** Host large events—retreats and conferences—without a prepared agenda. Participants are gathered around a guiding purpose and create the agenda themselves on a bulletin-board "marketplace," then "vote with their feet" by moving between breakout sessions.

**Players.** 5–2,000.

**Duration.** A day or longer.

**How to play.**
1. **Setup—An Open Invitation:** Craft a compelling invitation framing a challenge that is urgent, important, and complex enough to need diverse perspectives (e.g., "How can we revitalize our city's schools?").
2. **Create the Marketplace:** Seat participants in a circle (or concentric circles), invite anyone to come to the center and write an issue they care about, and post it on the marketplace wall with a time and place to host the discussion. Allow 60–90 minutes.
3. **Run the breakouts:** Sessions typically last 90 minutes; hosts record the discussion so others can join later.
4. **Honor the Law of Two Feet:** If you are neither learning nor contributing, use your two feet to go somewhere else—participants own their learning.
5. **Continue for a day or more,** depending on the scope of the event.
6. **Close the space:** Return to the original circle and reopen the floor for reflection on what was discovered and what comes next; avoid a formal report-out from each group.

**Strategy.** Hold the four principles in mind: whoever comes are the right people; whenever it starts is the right time; whatever happens is the only thing that could have; when it is over, it is over. Open Space requires the organizer to "let go" and trust that participants will produce a richer outcome than any pre-planned agenda. The format has been popularized as BarCamps and Unconferences and was developed by Harrison Owen.

**Source.** [open-space](https://gamestorming.com/open-space/)

### Pain-Gain Map

**Object of play.** Develop an understanding of a key person's motivations and decisions—their pains and gains—so you can craft the most relevant arguments to influence them.

**Players.** 3–10.

**Duration.** 10–15 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Write the key person's name or sketch them on a wall; this person may be the ultimate user of a product or a leader whose approval is sought.
2. Step inside the person's mind and capture their pains on one side, prompted by questions such as: What does a bad day look like for them? What are they afraid of? What keeps them awake at night? What are they responsible for? What obstacles stand in their way?
3. Capture gains on the opposite side—either as inversions of the pains or beyond them—prompted by: What does this person want and aspire to? How do they measure success? Given the subject at hand, how could this person benefit? What can we offer this person?
4. Summarize the most important pains and gains.
5. Prioritize the top items the group believes are most influential.
6. Carry the prioritized pains and gains into presentations, value propositions, or any other artifact aimed at influencing this person's decision.

**Strategy.** The map's value is in shifting the group's perspective from "what we want to say" to "what this person actually feels." Use the prioritized list to anchor every persuasion artifact—pitch decks, sales scripts, product pitches, executive memos—so messaging speaks directly to the person's lived pressures and ambitions rather than to your own assumptions about them.

**Source.** [pain-gain-map](https://gamestorming.com/pain-gain-map/)

### Party Invitations

**Object of play.** Improve the onboarding process of a product or service by reframing it as a party guests are being invited to.

**Players.** 5–30

**Duration.** 30–60 minutes

**How to play.**
1. Hand each participant a piece of paper and a marker.
2. Ask them to imagine the product or service is a party or event and to write an invitation to it.
3. Invitations should be detailed and realistic — an inviting statement, what to bring, what the host will provide, time, dress code, directions, RSVP info, or a Who/What/Where/When/Why structure.
4. Allow at least 10–15 minutes for writing and refining invitations through several iterations.
5. The facilitator calls for ideas on each element in turn: what did you call the party, dress code, refreshments, what to bring, what the party is for, how guests will get there.
6. Each participant reads their invitation aloud while the facilitator captures themes on a whiteboard.
7. The group narrows and refines the ideas, looking for emergent metaphors, crucial elements, and the right feel for the brand.
8. Sketch or otherwise generate ideas to translate the refined invitation into a draft onboarding experience.

**Strategy.** This is a metaphor-generation game that lets participants imagine how they want to engage their audience. Detail is good — players who go whole-hog with a white-tie gala or potluck succeed as long as they carry the metaphor through. Interesting tensions surface between "come as you are" and "festive dress code," or between RSVP-required and just-show-up. Strong technology products are not just friendly but literally inviting, asking the audience in and rewarding them when they accept.

### Pecha Kucha / Ignite

**Object of play.** Fast, structured talks that share ideas quickly with minimal distraction, forcing presenters to be concise and compelling.

**Players.** Any size, from a small working group to an auditorium full of people.

**Duration.** One to four hours, varying widely with the number of presenters.

**How to play.**
1. Set the format up front: Pecha Kucha is 20 x 20 — 20 slides, 20 seconds per slide. Ignite is similar (20 slides at 15 seconds each).
2. Recruit a slate of presenters and have them prepare slide decks that conform to the constraints.
3. Configure slides to advance automatically; presenters do not control the pace.
4. Pick a venue with good acoustics and arrange reliable audio/video equipment, especially for larger crowds.
5. Have an emcee introduce each speaker and keep the energy up between talks.
6. Run the talks back-to-back, advancing slides automatically. Speakers must keep up with the timer.
7. Optionally make it an informal evening event with food and drinks; the format also works inside a work team.

**Strategy.** The goal is to constrain presenters while keeping things fun. The hard time-box prevents the usual "show up and throw up" presentation and forces every slide to earn its place. The right emcee and venue make a meaningful difference; never give presenters control of their own laptops or the discipline of the format collapses. The Pecha Kucha format originated in Tokyo in 2003, devised by Astrid Klein and Mark Dytham of Klein Dytham architecture as a way for designers to share ideas. Variations include Talk20 and Ignite.

**Source.** [pecha-kuchaignite](https://gamestorming.com/pecha-kuchaignite/)

### Personal Kanban

**Object of play.** Organize your work visually so you can be more efficient and productive by seeing the whole pipeline and limiting work-in-progress.

**Players.** Any number of people can play this game.

**Duration.** 10–15 minutes

**How to play.**
1. Divide a whiteboard or sheet of paper into four columns: Backlog, Ready, Doing, and Done.
2. Using sticky notes, fill the Backlog column with all the work that needs to get done.
3. Move the highest-priority jobs into the Ready column, then rank the items in Ready from highest to lowest priority.
4. Take the top one to three jobs from Ready and move them into the Doing column — these are the things you will work on right away.
5. As you finish each job, move it to the Done column.
6. Regularly re-prioritize the Backlog, dropping items that are no longer important and promoting things that have become urgent.
7. Limit work-in-progress in the Doing column to keep yourself focused.

**Strategy.** The key is understanding the nature of work. The importance of items shifts over time, so the Backlog needs frequent re-prioritization. There are only so many things a person can do well at once, so capping the Doing column keeps you focused and finishing rather than starting. Personal Kanban also works for teams: making work visible lets customers and colleagues see what people are working on, leads to better conversations about priority, and helps surface capacity constraints that can justify new hires. Created by Jim Benson based on lean manufacturing kanban.

**Source.** [personal-kanban](https://gamestorming.com/personal-kanban/)

### Pie Chart Agenda

**Object of play.** Quickly plan an ad hoc meeting by sketching its time as a pie chart, bringing focus to both the order and significance of topics.

**Players.** Small group

**Duration.** 60–90 seconds

**How to play.**
1. Draw a circle on a whiteboard, flip chart, or pad of paper to represent the total time the group has.
2. Write the meeting objective in the middle of the circle (for example, "Brainstorm approaches for dealing with Problem X").
3. As a group, decide what items you want to spend time on and add them around the outside of the circle in a sequence that makes sense.
4. Decide how much time to reserve for each item and slice the pie accordingly, like rough sections of a clock face — "a third on this, the bulk on that, the last five minutes on this."
5. Agree on the plan, start the clock, and begin the meeting.
6. Manage time against the slices as the meeting unfolds, adjusting as needed.

**Strategy.** A clock-shaped agenda does two things a list cannot. As a metaphor, it emphasizes time and expediency, which is vital for ad hoc meetings. As a visual, it represents agenda items as proportional parts of a whole, weighted by importance. Items on a list have a habit of falling off or being skipped entirely; sketched and managed live, a pie chart keeps the time honest and makes trade-offs explicit. What it lacks in formality it makes up for in speed and flexibility. Credited to Sunni Brown.

**Source.** [pie-chart-agenda](https://gamestorming.com/pie-chart-agenda/)

### The Pitch

**Object of play.** A role-playing game that pulls concept work back to reality by forcing participants to sell their idea to a panel of imaginary venture capitalists, focusing on what is feasible, viable, and compelling.

**Players.** 4–12

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1.5 hours

**How to play.**
1. Divide people into small groups, ideally pairs or triads. One group plays venture capitalists; the others are entrepreneurs.
2. Define and agree on the product or service that will be pitched.
3. Each entrepreneur group spends about 10 minutes preparing their pitch — they can write, draw, and rehearse however they like, ideally in separate breakout spaces.
4. Brief everyone that one or two representatives will deliver the pitch verbally, but the whole group will answer questions afterward.
5. Near the end of prep, the VCs give a two-minute warning.
6. Each group pitches within a strict three-minute time limit, and the VCs may ask up to two questions each.
7. Optionally, the VCs pick a winning pitch to add competitive energy.

**Strategy.** Preparing a pitch to a VC obliges participants to focus on the truly important ideas, and the time limit forces concentration on the core proposition. Different groups will emphasize different aspects, producing a range of perspectives on the same concept. The VCs' questions usually expose weak points or clarify ambiguities that can then be discussed by the wider group. The game also captures the natural language people reach for to describe a concept, so don't let participants over-engineer their wording. If players don't know each other, a competitive frame and a small prize bond teams together fast.

### Plus/Delta

**Object of play.** Generate constructive feedback on any activity, idea, work product, or action by separating what worked from what should change.

**Players.** Any

**Duration.** 10–45 minutes

**How to play.**
1. On a whiteboard or flip chart, make two columns. Label the left "Plus" and the right "Delta" (the Greek letter for change).
2. Ask the group to reflect on what was positive or worth repeating about the activity and capture their thoughts under the Plus column.
3. Ask the group to brainstorm what they would change about it and capture those thoughts under the Delta column.
4. Discuss the items, looking for patterns and points where multiple people raised the same plus or delta.
5. Optionally, prioritize the deltas to focus on which changes are worth carrying forward.

**Strategy.** Plus/Delta works because it reframes negative feedback as future change rather than past failure, which lowers defensiveness and surfaces honest assessments alongside concrete improvement ideas. The simple two-column structure removes the awkwardness of giving criticism directly and gives the group a shared artifact to act on. Use it as a recurring retrospective ritual at the end of meetings, sprints, or events — the cumulative effect compounds over time. The earliest known use is at Boeing circa 1980.

### Post The Path

**Object of play.** Quickly diagnose a group's level of understanding of the steps in an existing process and surface where confusion lives.

**Players.** 2–10

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour

**How to play.**
1. Frame the objective: "We're going to create a picture of how we create [x]," where x is the output of the process — a document, a product, an agreement.
2. Write or draw the output of the process on the wall.
3. Establish a common starting point with the group — the trigger that kicks off the process. If the group will struggle, decide it in advance and present it as a best guess on a sticky note.
4. Ask each participant to think about the process from beginning to end and write each step on a separate sticky note.
5. Have participants come to the wall and post their notes, placing their step 1s, step 2s, and so on above and below one another so versions can be compared.
6. Prompt the group to find points of agreement and confusion. Look for terminology problems where people are using different words for the same step.
7. Highlight points where "something magical happens" or no one is clear on the step.

**Strategy.** This exercise will not generally result in a new or better process — it produces a better understanding of the current one, which then naturally flows into a discussion of what to do about the unclear areas. The group draws its own conclusions about what the differences mean. For larger groups, have everyone post simultaneously rather than doing individual readouts. If you sense the group will get stuck in details, cap the number of steps — try 10. Credited to James Macanufo.

### Post-Up

**Object of play.** Generate ideas through silent sticky-note writing, ensuring every voice contributes without interruption.

**Players.** 1–50

**Duration.** 10 minutes to 1 hour

**How to play.**
1. Frame a clear question or topic for the group to brainstorm answers to (for example, "What are possible uses for Product X?").
2. Write the question or topic on a whiteboard so everyone can see it.
3. Ask the group to brainstorm answers individually, silently writing their ideas on separate sticky notes — one idea per note.
4. After a set amount of time, ask everyone to bring their notes to the whiteboard and quickly present each one.
5. If anyone is inspired by what they hear, encourage them to write more notes and add them to the wall after the round of presentations.
6. Optionally apply Harry Brignull's FOG method — mark each note with F (fact), O (opinion), or G (guess) — to add clarity for later decision-making.
7. Move into an organizing or prioritizing step, such as Affinity Mapping or Forced Ranking.

**Strategy.** The silence is the load-bearing element: it lets people think without interruption and prevents the loudest voice from anchoring the group. Putting each idea on a separate note ensures items can later be shuffled, sorted, and grouped as distinct thoughts. Post-Up is an opening move — a first step in a longer flow. From here you typically organize the output by clustering related notes or ranking them. Based on exercises in *Rapid Problem-Solving with Post-it Notes* by David Straker.

**Source.** [post-up](https://gamestorming.com/post-up/)

### Poster Session

**Object of play.** Force experts to boil their ideas down to compelling visual posters and present them gallery-style, replacing long-winded talks with images that can be browsed quickly.

**Players.** 10–100

**Duration.** 20 minutes to develop posters; unlimited time to browse

**How to play.**

3. Set two constraints: each poster must be self-explanatory (understandable without the author present) and visual (text alone is not enough).

### Pre-Mortem

**Object of play.** Open a space at a project's inception to directly name risks, tapping into team experience and intuition before things go wrong rather than after.

**Players.** Any, but small teams typically have the most open dialogue.

**Duration.** Depends on scope; allow up to five minutes per participant.

**How to play.**
1. Schedule the pre-mortem at the project's kickoff, after the goals and plan have been laid out and understood.
2. Bring all key team members together with the project plan visible.
3. Pose a single, simple question: "What will go wrong?" or, more dramatically, "How will this end in disaster?"
4. Give the team space to reflect on their collective experience and name the risks and "elephants in the room" that might otherwise go unspoken.
5. For small teams, run it as discussion. For larger groups, use Post-Up or list generation so every voice is captured.
6. Cluster the surfaced concerns and rank or vote on them to determine priority.
7. Decide what actions need to be taken to address the top risks and bring them up as standing items in ongoing project meetings.

**Strategy.** Pre-mortems are deceptively simple. The challenge is timing: at project kickoff, momentum and enthusiasm are at their peak — exactly the conditions least conducive to talking about failure. By deliberately creating a space to share past learning while it can still influence the work, the team converts the post-mortem's wisdom into prevention. Related to Luke Hohmann's Innovation Game "Remember the Future."

**Source.** [pre-mortem](https://gamestorming.com/pre-mortem/)

### Pro/Con List

**Object of play.** Make a difficult decision by laying out reasons for and against side-by-side so they can be weighed against each other rather than considered one at a time.

**Players.** Any (originally a solo exercise, equally useful in groups)

**Duration.** Can be done in a single session or, as Franklin recommended, over three or four days of consideration.

**How to play.**
1. Divide a sheet of paper into two columns. Label the left "Pro" and the right "Con."
2. Over the course of several days, jot down short hints of the motives that occur to you for and against the decision.
3. Once you have the full list in one view, estimate the relative weight of each item.
4. Where you find a Pro and a Con of equal weight, strike them both out.
5. If a single Pro equals two Cons, strike the three out. If two Cons equal three Pros, strike the five.
6. Continue until you find where the balance lies.
7. After a day or two of further consideration, if nothing important has emerged on either side, come to a determination.

**Strategy.** Franklin called this "Moral or Prudential Algebra." The point is not algebraic precision — the weight of reasons cannot be measured exactly — but rather forcing all the considerations into view at the same time. Difficult decisions are difficult chiefly because we can never hold all the reasons in mind simultaneously; one set surfaces, then another, and our preferences flicker accordingly. Putting everything on a single sheet defeats that flicker. Invented by Benjamin Franklin and described in a 1772 letter to Joseph Priestley.

**Source.** [procon-list](https://gamestorming.com/procon-list/)

### Product Pinocchio

**Object of play.** Personify a product or service as a living character so the team can establish, refine, and evolve its features in ways that are inaccessible when thinking of it as inanimate.

**Players.** 5–20

**Duration.** 1 hour

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, invent four scenes (situations requiring the character to make a decision) and write each on an index card. Examples: "someone tries to steal an old lady's purse" or "a driver encounters a hitchhiker."
2. Prepare five flip charts, each headed with one question: What am I like? What are my values? What is my community? What makes me different? What is my fight?
3. On "What am I like?", draw the product as a character with arms, legs, and head. Have the group call out adjectives, cartoon characters, or celebrities the product reminds them of. Dot vote the top three to five.
4. On "What are my values?", split into small groups, hand each a scene card, and have them discuss what the character would do. Reconvene and surface the underlying values implied by the behaviors.
5. On "What is my community?", explore who the character spends time with, what groups she belongs to, and what her friends have in common.
6. On "What makes me different?", explore strengths, weaknesses, and what would make someone want her on a team.
7. On "What is my fight?", explore her mission, motivations, and obstacles. Optionally toast the character at her wedding or eulogize a competitor.
8. Summarize and discuss what the character traits imply for product features.

**Strategy.** This works only when players suspend disbelief. The drawings and the questions both force "it" into "he" or "she." Naming the character ("Cameron") makes personification easier. Don't discourage outlandish traits — a zany character may surface a real innovation in how customers perceive the product. Let players go far out and only nudge toward consensus as the game closes.

**Source.** [product-pinocchio](https://gamestorming.com/product-pinocchio/)

### Quaker Conversation

**Object of play.** Give every voice in a group a chance to be represented before the natural flow of conversation begins selecting for the loudest, ensuring the widest possible diversity of perspective.

**Players.** Any (works best in groups small enough to circle)

**Duration.** Varies with group size; allow time for each person to speak in full.

**How to play.**
1. Seat the group in a circle so everyone can see each other.
2. Pose a single, well-framed question to the group.
3. Each person answers the question in sequence, usually going around the circle in order.
4. If someone is not yet ready to speak, they can defer their turn until later in the round.
5. Each person answers as fully as they need to in order to feel they have expressed their point of view.
6. Critically, no one responds to, rebuts, or rejoins anyone else's comments — each response is only to the original question.
7. Optionally, capture key ideas on a whiteboard as people speak, to anchor the follow-on conversation.

**Strategy.** Rule four is the hard one to enforce, but it is the heart of the game. Suspending the back-and-forth gives less vocal members space to voice their views, which would otherwise be drowned out. Once the round is complete, the captured ideas become natural launching points for a more focused drill-down on what resonated. Use it as a fire-starter to seed a conversation, not as a stand-alone decision-making process — and set expectations that while every voice deserves to be heard, not all ideas carry equal merit.

**Source.** [knowledge-game-quaker-conversation](https://gamestorming.com/knowledge-game-quaker-conversation/)

### RACI Matrix

**Object of play.** Sort out who does what when responsibilities are unclear, by assigning each task a single Responsible role, a single Accountable role, and the appropriate Consulted and Informed roles.

**Players.** 2–6

**Duration.** 1.5 hours

**How to play.**
1. Build a work breakdown — the items or activities the group shares responsibility for. They should be specific enough to answer "Who does X?"
2. Build a list of roles, not individuals — for example, Project Manager, Business Analyst, Architect — since one person may play multiple roles and one role may have multiple people.
3. Create a matrix with the work breakdown down the vertical axis and the roles across the horizontal axis.
4. For each task, assign letters in the cells: R for Responsible (the doer), A for Accountable (the sign-off — only one per task), C for Consulted (two-way input), and I for Informed (one-way updates).
5. Work through the matrix following the natural progression of the work breakdown from start to finish.
6. Adjust the work breakdown as you go — split items that resist a single Responsible, eliminate items that have no Responsible at all.
7. The matrix is complete when every task has a clear set of responsibilities.

**Strategy.** The golden rule is that only one person can be Accountable for a given task — when more than one is, accountability evaporates. Don't be reluctant to change the work breakdown as the group works through the matrix; difficulty assigning a single Responsible often signals that an item is poorly defined or actually two items. RACI is most valuable when responsibilities have been allowed to blur and morale is suffering as a result.

**Source.** [raci-matrix](https://gamestorming.com/raci-matrix/)

### Random Inputs

**Object of play.** Generate genuinely new thinking around a topic by injecting random nouns that force participants to make associations they have not made before.

**Players.** 5–10

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, generate a list of 50–75 random nouns. Make sure they are NOT contextually related to the players' work.
2. Write each word on an individual slip of paper and put them in a container you can draw from blindly.
3. In a white space visible to everyone, write the topic of the play (for example, "a new ad campaign") and give players plenty of sticky notes.
4. Tell participants the goal is ideas outside of the default thinking around the product or service. Connections can be expansive, even silly.
5. Draw the first word, read it aloud, and draw a picture of it in the white space — even if you can't really draw.
6. Give everyone five minutes to silently write any ideas the word inspires on sticky notes.
7. Have players post all of their ideas in the white space.
8. Repeat with new words until you've generated enough ideas to get traction on the topic.

**Strategy.** This game works because the inputs are random, so scrub your word list for anything people associate with the topic. Watch out for two creative copouts: walking through "a then a+1 then a+2" chains of association back to the product, and rearranging letters to form acronyms. Both retread known paths. You want creative leaps, not methodical bridges. Adapted from Edward de Bono's "Random Input" exercise.

**Source.** [113](https://gamestorming.com/113/)

### Red:Green Cards

**Object of play.** Provide a simple visual channel for a large group to give continuous feedback — approval, disagreement, or confusion — to a presenter or to each other as an event unfolds.

**Players.** Any size, but especially useful in groups of 20 or more.

**Duration.** A simple Red/Green exchange takes only a moment — the length of a question. Discussion may extend it.

**How to play.**
1. Distribute two cards to each participant: one red and one green. Optionally include yellow (neutral) and gray (confusion) cards.
2. Establish the meaning: green for "yes" or approval, red for "no" or disapproval.
3. The presenter or facilitator can call for a vote at any time — for example, "Have we covered this topic sufficiently to move on?" — and participants respond by holding up the appropriate card.
4. Participants can also hold up cards unprompted, nodding along with green or registering an objection with red.
5. When red cards appear, pause to discuss the objection before moving on.
6. When green cards dominate, take that as license to advance.
7. Use yellow and gray cards if you want to surface neutrality or confusion without forcing a binary answer.

**Strategy.** Red:Green Cards solve two problems at once. They eliminate the ritualistic "we all agree" verbal commentary, and they surface participants who would otherwise sit silently fuming over unheard objections. The cards make consent visible without requiring anyone to interrupt, which is what makes them especially valuable at scale. Developed by Jerry Michalski.

**Source.** [redgreen-cards](https://gamestorming.com/redgreen-cards/)

### Scenario Slider

**Object of play.** Get groups to model a business approach against several extreme scenarios using two or three variables, surfacing unexpected answers by playing with the limits.

**Players.** Multiple small groups working in parallel

**Duration.** Varies; typically used after brainstorming or prioritization.

**How to play.**
1. In advance, select two or three variables that would impact how your idea is implemented. For a project these might be money, people, and time.
2. Set context for each variable. Money could range from $10K to unlimited; time from "done in 3 months" to "as much time as you need."
3. Define two or three scenarios by adjusting the sliders to extreme positions — for example, "unlimited funds, all the people you need, must be done in three months." Don't worry whether the scenario is realistic.
4. Working with several groups in parallel, assign one scenario to each group.
5. Ask each group to come up with an approach based on their scenario. Instruct them to take the constraints as given — don't argue that it's impossible, tell us how you would do it.
6. Have each group present their approach, including the tradeoffs and shortcomings forced by the scenario.
7. Debrief as a large group to identify ideas everyone agrees should go forward and which constraints feel non-negotiable.

**Strategy.** Asking groups to produce something tangible — a model, a doodle, an outline — for the report-out crystallizes their thinking and gives the broader group something to react to. The extremes are the point: by setting stakes in the ground you clear the space to discuss how to actually try something rather than spinning on whether it's possible. Different scenarios produce different approaches, and the gaps and overlaps between them are often where the best real-world plan emerges.

**Source.** [knowledge-game-the-scenario-slider](https://gamestorming.com/knowledge-game-the-scenario-slider/)

### Show and Tell

**Object of play.** Get a deeper understanding of stakeholders' perspectives on a topic — a project, a restructuring, a vision shift — by having them bring physical artifacts and explain why each one represents the topic.

**Players.** Any small-to-medium group

**Duration.** 30–60 minutes (varies with group size)

**How to play.**
1. A few days in advance, ask participants to bring an artifact for Show and Tell — something that, from their perspective, represents the topic.
2. Tell them to keep the item hidden until it's their turn.
3. At the meeting, in a white space visible to everyone, write the name of the game and the topic, optionally with a sketch.
4. Ask for volunteers to stand up and show first.
5. Pay close attention to each person's story of why the item represents the topic. Listen for similarities, differences, and emotional language.
6. Capture each contribution in the white space and draw a simple visual of the item next to the comments.
7. Summarize what you've captured and let the group absorb shared themes of excitement, doubt, or concern. Ask follow-up questions to deepen the conversation.

**Strategy.** Show and Tell taps into the power of metaphor to reveal underlying assumptions and associations. A string of items described in fearful or concerned terms is a signal that needs aren't being met. Encourage and applaud honesty, write down every point that seems important to the speaker, and keep the rest of the group quiet during each turn. As facilitator, draw the items even if you can't draw — being visibly vulnerable models the same vulnerability you're asking participants to bring, which matters because Show and Tell can feel exposing, especially for introverts.

### Show Me Your Values

**Object of play.** Surface employee perception of the deep values that underlie an organization, an initiative, or any other topic — including values that are widely felt but rarely spoken aloud.

**Players.** 5–10

**Duration.** 30–45 minutes

**How to play.**
1. Decide the topic in advance. Set up a flat surface where players can write and post images, and write the topic at the top of the area.
2. Provide tape and several magazines of all genres — enough that each player can rifle through three or four.
3. Frame the goal: players will describe in pictures what they perceive to be the values underlying the topic, then share a work-related story indicative of those values. (Example: a turtle image for patience and longevity, with a story about an attractive but high-risk project that wasn't pursued.)
4. Give everyone 10 minutes to cut out one or more images that represent the perceived values.
5. Ask players to tape their images in the designated area and quietly reflect on a story associated with each value.
6. Ask for volunteers to share their images and the stories that go with them.
7. As they speak, write the named values in the space beside the appropriate image.
8. Review the captured values and ask the players to look for overlaps and gaps. Discuss perceptions both shared and unshared.

**Strategy.** Imagery is one step removed from a verbal declaration yet one step deeper than purely intellectual statements, giving players a comfort zone for honest expression — including humor, hyperbole, or hard truths. Encourage truthful representation: it is okay to call out an underlying value as "territoriality" by picking a lion, because beliefs that drive behavior are often unstated but widely repeated in private. Optionally, ask players to also choose images representing what the values are not.

### Spectrum Mapping

**Object of play.** Reveal the diversity of perspectives in a group around a topic and organize them into a meaningful spectrum, letting people express views without having to assert them vocally or claim ownership.

**Players.** 5–15

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour

**How to play.**
1. Before the game, brainstorm topics you want insight from the group on. Write each topic on a sticky note.
2. Introduce the game as a way to illuminate the team's range of perspectives along a continuum that everyone can see.
3. Post the topic sticky notes in a column down the middle of the wall.
4. Ask everyone to silently generate a point-of-view or preference around each topic and write it on a sticky note. Multiple notes per person are welcome.
5. Have players post their notes in a horizontal line on either side of the topic — relationships between notes don't matter yet.
6. Once all notes are posted, work with the group to sort them horizontally. Similar perspectives go next to each other; outliers stand alone and may end up defining the limits of the range.
7. Continue sorting until the group agrees the notes are in their appropriate places, then repeat for any remaining topics.
8. Ask for observations and insights on the lay of the land. If a perspective has been excluded, add it and re-sort.

**Strategy.** Spectrum mapping reveals not just individual ideas but how many members lean which way and where their endpoints sit. After the exercise, players can see whether the group is fiscally conservative, growth-oriented, change-averse, or skewed in some other direction. Knowing the natural inclination — and acknowledging it openly — strengthens future team building, problem-solving, and planning. Reassure players that outlier ideas still belong on the continuum; the game is about mapping, not evaluating.

**Source.** [spectrum-mapping](https://gamestorming.com/spectrum-mapping/)

### Speed Boat

**Object of play.** Speed Boat (also called Sail Boat) is a focused way to surface what is slowing a team, product, or initiative down so the group can identify and prioritize the issues holding them back. The boat metaphor turns abstract complaints into concrete "anchors" that can be discussed and removed. *(Note: the source page returned a Javascript-required error; this digest is reconstructed from the well-known published version of the game.)*

**Players.** Up to 10 (any team that owns the thing being examined).

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Draw a large boat on a wall or whiteboard with a waterline. Below the waterline, draw several anchors hanging from the hull.
2. Write the goal or destination of the "boat" (the product, team, or initiative) above or in front of it, so the direction of travel is obvious.
3. Hand out sticky notes and ask each participant to silently write down anything that is slowing the boat down — one issue per note. Frame anchors as "what is holding us back" rather than "what we don't like."
4. Have players post their anchors on the picture, attaching each note to one of the drawn anchors under the waterline.
5. Cluster similar anchors and discuss as a group. Ask: which anchors are heaviest? Which could be cut now?
6. Optionally, prioritize the top anchors to "cut" and turn each into an action item with an owner.

**Strategy.** Speed Boat works because the visual metaphor depersonalizes complaints — the issue is an anchor on a shared boat rather than a grievance against a person. Use it before retros or planning when the team feels stuck but cannot articulate why. The richer the boat picture (wind, sun, rocks, an island destination), the more dimensions the group can explore.

### Squiggle Birds

**Object of play.** Squiggle Birds is a five-minute warm-up that proves how little drawing is actually needed to convey an idea — the mind is a pattern-making machine and will fill in the rest. It loosens the visual-thinking muscles of people who insist they "can't draw."

**Players.** Any group size.

**Duration.** About 5 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Hand out paper or sticky notes and pens. Ask each participant to draw a page full of random squiggles — loops, scribbles, blobs, anything with no plan.
2. After roughly a minute of squiggling, ask the group to stop.
3. Demonstrate or instruct: with a few simple additions — two dots for eyes, a triangle beak, two stick legs — turn each squiggle into a bird. The squiggle becomes the body and head silhouette.
4. Give the group two or three minutes to convert their own squiggles into birds.
5. Hold up the resulting flocks and discuss what just happened: minimal marks, recognizable creatures.

**Strategy.** The point is the demonstration, not the birds. Run Squiggle Birds at the start of any workshop where you want people to draw, sketch flows, or storyboard. It defuses the "I'm not an artist" objection in a way that lecturing cannot. A nice variation, contributed by readers of the source page, is a second pass where each person adds accessories or a personality to a bird and tells a short story about it — the squiggle prompt produces more original characters than asking someone to "draw a bird" from scratch.

### Stakeholder Analysis

**Object of play.** A stakeholder analysis maps who can significantly impact a project, and who will be impacted by it, so a team can develop a strategy for engaging each group. It is a basic framing exercise that scopes out who has what level of input and interest at the start of any project.

**Players.** Any; ideally key team members who collectively understand all aspects of the project.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour, depending on depth.

**How to play.**
1. Set up a 2x2 matrix on a wall with axes labeled Power (a stakeholder's ability to direct or coerce the project) and Interest (the degree to which they will be affected).
2. Generate the stakeholder list using sticky notes. Prompt with questions: who will be impacted, who is responsible or accountable, who has decision authority, who can support, who can obstruct, and who has been involved in this kind of project before.
3. Place each stakeholder onto the Power/Interest grid based on the group's collective judgment.
4. For each quadrant, develop an engagement strategy: who needs to be informed about what and when, who needs to be consulted, who is responsible for engaging each stakeholder, and how.
5. Share the draft analysis broadly with everyone involved to validate it, fill gaps, and clarify roles.

**Strategy.** Pair stakeholder analysis with a RACI matrix or other "people + project" tools. The 2x2 is the most common cut, but teams can swap or add variables — supportive vs. obstructive, internal vs. external — when circumstances warrant. The exercise traces back to the "Prince Chart" developed by Coplin and O'Leary to predict the outcomes of conflict-laden projects.

**Source.** [stakeholder-analysis](https://gamestorming.com/stakeholder-analysis/)

### Staple Yourself To Something

**Object of play.** This game clarifies a process by following a single object — a product, a trouble ticket, a bill, an idea — through its entire flow from start to finish. The result is a memorable, visual story of the core process that the team can use to spot improvement opportunities or onboard others. It is the right exercise when no one quite knows how the work gets done, or when the documented process is overly complex.

**Players.** 2–10.

**Duration.** 1–2 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Decide in advance on the "bouncing ball" object the group will follow. Familiar examples include "how a bill becomes a law" or how an order moves through fulfillment.
2. Introduce the exercise by drawing the object on the wall. Write the agreed starting point and ending point at opposite ends.
3. Brainstorm the big steps in the process and post them between start and end. Aim for around seven steps for a high-level story.
4. Agree with the group on what information must be captured at each step — people involved, action taken, time consumed.
5. Walk the object through each step, capturing the story visually with stick figures, arrows, and quotes. Use questions like "Who does what here?", "What's next?", and "What's important?".
6. Note branches, loops, and links to other processes off to the side so the main story keeps moving.

**Strategy.** Use the object as a focusing device — anything not directly related to its forward motion gets noted and parked. Add a ticking clock if the object has a deadline; it creates pace. Be explicit about whether you are mapping the current state or a desired future, since participants drift between them. If a process owner is in the room, draw on her expertise but don't let her tell the entire story — letting others narrate is itself a learning experience for her. Credited to James Macanufo.

**Source.** [staple-yourself-to-something](https://gamestorming.com/staple-yourself-to-something/)

### Start, Stop, Continue

**Object of play.** Start, Stop, Continue examines a current situation and develops next steps by sorting actions into three crisp buckets. It is broad enough to work as either an opener or a closer, and it gives groups an immediate, action-oriented frame for messy discussions.

**Players.** 1–10.

**Duration.** 10 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Frame the situation, project, or goal you want to examine and write three column headings on a wall or whiteboard: START, STOP, and CONTINUE.
2. Ask each participant to silently brainstorm on sticky notes, generating ideas in each category: things we should START doing, things we should STOP doing, and things that work and we should CONTINUE.
3. Have individuals post their notes under the appropriate column.
4. Walk through each column with the group, clustering similar notes and discussing patterns.
5. Optionally, dot-vote within each column to surface the highest-priority items, and convert top choices into commitments or action items.

**Strategy.** The format works because it is immediately understandable and balances critique with appreciation — the Continue column protects what is working from being thrown out alongside what isn't. Use it to frame problem-solving meetings, to harvest team feedback, or to brainstorm aspirational steps toward a vision. Variations such as "Toss, Save, Create" map directly onto Stop, Continue, Start. The simplicity of the structure is part of its power; do not over-engineer it.

**Source.** [start-stop-continue](https://gamestorming.com/start-stop-continue/)

### Storyboard

**Object of play.** Storyboard asks players to envision and describe an ideal future as a sequence of images and captions, the way an animator would. Although it is most powerful as a visioning exercise — players literally draw the happy ending they want — the format works for any topic, from describing a project experience to onboarding new employees on a procedure.

**Players.** 8–20.

**Duration.** 45 minutes to 1.5 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, decide the topic of the "ideal future" story (a team, product, organization, customer experience).
2. Divide players into pairs or small groups of three or four. Hand out markers, flip-chart paper, and stands.
3. Explain that each group will visually describe the topic and narrate it. Give them 20–25 minutes to (a) agree on an ideal end state, (b) identify the steps to get there, and (c) draw each step as one large scene per sheet.
4. Give a two-minute warning, then bring the room back together. Ask for a volunteer group to tell its story first.
5. After every group has presented, ask the room what was inspiring, summarize recurring themes, and capture observations and "aha's".

**Strategy.** Many participants panic at the idea of large-scale drawing, so reassure them that the story is the point and that stick figures suffice — they aren't allotted enough time to produce a da Vinci anyway. Captions can carry the meaning the pictures don't. Groups can choose their presentation format: walking a row of posters, flipping pages on a stand, or a "Vanna White" reveal. Storyboarding traces back to Walt Disney's process for animating Steamboat Willie in 1928.

**Source.** [storyboard](https://gamestorming.com/storyboard/)

### SWOT Analysis

**Object of play.** SWOT Analysis is the long-standing technique for evaluating Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats relative to a desired end state. The Gamestorming version turns it into a participatory, sticky-note-driven exercise that helps a group gauge approaching opportunities and dangers and understand the conditions affecting the future they want.

**Players.** 5–20.

**Duration.** 1–2 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting, write "Desired End State" on a flip chart and sketch what it might look like.
2. Build a four-square quadrant on the wall using flip-chart sheets. Label them STRENGTHS (top left), WEAKNESSES (bottom left), OPPORTUNITIES (top right), THREATS (bottom right), with a small icon for each.
3. Walk through the quadrants one at a time. For each, give players 5–10 minutes to silently generate ideas on sticky notes relative to the desired end state.
4. Gather the notes, keep them grouped by category, and cluster each pile by affinity. With small groups, skip clustering and write responses directly while players talk.
5. Name each cluster (for example, "communication") and write the category labels into the quadrant.
6. Have players dot-vote two or three categories per quadrant as the most relevant, then circle the winners.
7. Discuss implications. Reframe weaknesses and threats positively — "what if your competition didn't exist?" — to surface latent strength.

**Strategy.** The analysis is only as good as the candor of the contributions. Frame "weakness" as something improvable and "threat" as a catalyst for performance, so participants don't sandbag the discussion. The most powerful synthesis happens when the group confronts SW against OT in a matrix — that is where strengths get matched to opportunities. Inspired by Albert Humphrey's traditional SWOT.

**Source.** [swot-analysis](https://gamestorming.com/swot-analysis/)

### Synesthesia

**Object of play.** Knowledge work tends to be head-heavy and analytical, filtering out the senses as frivolous even when the output is sensory. Synesthesia role-play forces participants to examine a topic — a product, an interface, a user experience — through a sensory lens, and lets those visceral perceptions inform decisions and designs.

**Players.** 2–5.

**Duration.** 15–45 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Pick the topic. It can be an existing thing ("the interface for our new site") or a concept under exploration ("the user experience").
2. Assign each participant — by choice or randomly — one of the five senses: see, hear, taste, smell, touch. Optionally extend the menu to include temperature, position, and motion.
3. Give players a few quiet minutes to interpret the topic from the perspective of their assigned sense, and to wander into other senses as the imagery prompts them.
4. Have each participant describe to the group what they perceived. Examples: "the interface is warm to the touch and tastes like oranges," "when the app launches I hear an orchestra tuning up but can't see anything," "the user experience smells like a stack of dusty papers."
5. Discuss what these descriptions reveal — overlooked qualities, hidden frustrations, fresh design directions.

**Strategy.** Synesthesia uncovers feelings about an object that purely analytical conversation never surfaces, and it produces vivid, memorable language a team can carry forward. Use it during early concepting when the team needs a more emotional read on something, or during critique when "it just feels off" needs to be unpacked. Source unknown.

**Source.** [synesthesia](https://gamestorming.com/synesthesia/)

### Talking Chips

**Object of play.** Group discussions routinely get dominated by a few voices while quieter participants stay silent. Talking Chips uses physical tokens as a currency for contribution so the group can self-manage the flow of participation without a heavy-handed facilitator.

**Players.** Any small-group discussion (typically 4–10).

**Duration.** Runs alongside whatever conversation it is supporting.

**How to play.**
1. Before the meeting starts, place a stack of chips — poker chips, coins, paper clips, anything tangible — at the center of the table.
2. Each participant draws one chip from the center.
3. To speak, a participant places her chip back into the center. She may speak only when she does this.
4. The round continues until every chip has been placed in the center — meaning every person has spoken once.
5. Once the center pile is full, participants may take their chips back and the cycle repeats.

**Strategy.** The chip is a tangible reminder that every voice has equal claim on airtime. Talking Chips is just as good at drawing out quiet participants — who know they will need to use their chip — as it is at containing dominant ones, who run out of currency and must wait their turn. It works especially well in heated or politically tricky discussions where rank or volume would otherwise determine who gets heard. Developed by Dave Gray, inspired by Byron Reeves's email program Attent.

**Source.** [talking-chips](https://gamestorming.com/talking-chips/)

### Trading Cards

**Object of play.** Trading Cards is an icebreaker that warms a group up by letting people self-define, gives them a "personality" outside the work environment, exposes participants to multiple players in quick snapshots as cards circulate, and produces memorable visuals that become conversation pieces later in the meeting.

**Players.** Unlimited.

**Duration.** 10–15 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Hand out large-format index cards and markers.
2. Ask participants to spend 5–10 minutes creating a personal trading card that includes a self-portrait, a "player" nickname, and one little-known fact about themselves.
3. Have everyone pass their cards around the room in no particular order. Each person reads cards as they land in their hands and holds onto one they would like to ask a question about.
4. Ask for a volunteer to read out the player's name and nickname, then pose a question related to the little-known fact on the card.
5. Let the named player elaborate. They can then either ask the person whose card they are holding a question, or pass to another volunteer.
6. Continue until the group is sufficiently warmed up — but cap the play around 15 minutes.

**Strategy.** Many people grumble about icebreakers, but humans are like cars: we perform better warmed up. Trading Cards integrates participants before the real work begins and surfaces the human side of colleagues who normally only show up as job titles. The cards themselves remain on the table or wall as conversation prompts. Source unknown.

### Understanding Chain

**Object of play.** When there is a lot to say to a lot of different people, the temptation is to explain "everything all at once" and lose the audience. Understanding Chain shifts the group from a content focus to an audience focus and produces a meaningful, linear structure for communication built around the questions the audience actually has.

**Players.** 1–10.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 2 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Break the audience into meaningful groups (the more specific, the better — "the IT folks who fix our laptops" beats "Corporate"). Each audience gets its own chain.
2. Get into the audience's mindset and brainstorm questions in their voice on sticky notes — for example, "Why should I care?", "How does this affect our roadmap?", "What makes this a priority?".
3. Pick a structure for arranging the questions horizontally: Situation–Complication–Resolution (story format), Educate–Differentiate–Stimulate, or a natural conversation arc of Connecting–Focusing–Acting.
4. Sort the question stickies into that left-to-right timeline on the wall, clustering and re-ordering as the structure clarifies.
5. Identify the climax or pivot question — the one that drives the resolution — and stress-test the weak links: which questions are hardest to answer honestly?

**Strategy.** A chain is only as strong as its weakest link. The exercise's real value is exposing the tough questions a presenter would rather skip; groups that have the courage to answer those directly produce communications that land. Developed by Dave Gray as part of XPLANE's consulting approach.

**Source.** [understanding-chain](https://gamestorming.com/understanding-chain/)

### Value Map

**Object of play.** Value Map builds a visual matrix that quickly defines areas of interest in something — a service, product, plan, or website — by forcing participants to choose a limited set of features from a larger collection. The result is presented like a light box: items chosen often glow brightly; items chosen rarely fade; items not chosen stay dark.

**Players.** 5–30.

**Duration.** 15 minutes to 2 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Define the features. Sketch each feature on a card or sticky and group them in a way that makes sense — by category, layer, area of the product. Plot the groups as a table.
2. Constrain the choices. Show participants the full set of cards and ask them to pick a smaller number — a 1:3 ratio works well (10 cards out of 30). Alternatively, give them imaginary money — say £100 — and let them "shop" for features within that budget.
3. Record each participant's selections.
4. Plot the results. Color each feature on the master table according to how many times it was chosen: bright, saturated colors for popular picks, light colors for rare ones, uncolored for items no one chose.
5. Read the matrix as a heat map and discuss what the brightness pattern reveals.

**Strategy.** Value Map is useful when you need to focus effort or settle internal disputes — "consumers really didn't choose any social features, so we don't need to invest in them now." Presenting the matrix as a sequence of slides showing the bright colors first, then the dim ones, makes the priorities land visibly. The forced trade-off (you must leave some things out) is what makes the data meaningful.

**Source.** [value-map](https://gamestorming.com/value-map/)

### Video Card Family Game

**Object of play.** The Video Card Family Game co-creates product or service design insights from collaborative analysis of key frames drawn from ethnographic video field work. Designers and stakeholders sort short video clips into "families" that share a theme, working from observation rather than secondhand reports.

**Players.** 6–12.

**Duration.** 7–8 hours.

**How to play.**
1. In advance: edit ethnographic field video into segments of no more than two minutes each, focused on action rather than talk. Pull a key frame from each clip and print it onto two duplicate index cards with a unique title and ID.
2. Open the session by explaining the rules and the difference between observation (who/what/where/when/how) and interpretation (why).
3. Pair participants up and give each pair duplicate stacks of video cards. Play the corresponding clips and have each player annotate their cards individually, without discussion.
4. After about 30 minutes, each pair discusses what they saw and arranges their cards into themed "families" on a table.
5. Each player picks a favorite family, posts it on a wall poster, and writes a heading for the theme.
6. Reconvene the whole group. Each player presents a family; others propose additions from their stacks. The presenter accepts or rejects additions by replaying the relevant clip and arguing fit.
7. Document each theme: describe it, justify the membership, give two examples, describe the action and its context.

**Strategy.** Video is a rich but slow research source; the card mechanic gives the team a tangible, sortable proxy that makes group sense-making practical. Draws on the children's card game "Happy Families" and Wittgenstein's notion of family resemblance — themes group by likeness, not by strict definition. Originated by Werner Sperschneider; this rendition by Larry Irons.

**Source.** [video-card-family-game](https://gamestorming.com/video-card-family-game/)

### Virtuous Cycle

**Object of play.** Virtuous Cycle reveals opportunities to transform an existing linear process into a more valuable, self-reinforcing one by literally bending the process back on itself. It is most useful when a process is transactional, compartmentalized, or wasteful — or when nobody seems to know what happens after it ends.

**Players.** 3–10.

**Duration.** 1–3 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Bring in a high-level understanding of the current linear process as a starting point.
2. "Black box" the current process: give each step a sticky note on the wall and connect them with arrows, but don't get into internal details.
3. Ask the group what happens before the process: who is involved, what is going on. Capture on stickies and place them before the first step.
4. Repeat for what happens after the process: outcomes, follow-ons, downstream actors.
5. Draw a loop arrow from the end of the linear process back to the start, converting it into a life cycle. Ask: "To get from here back again, what needs to happen? What's missing?".
6. Have players capture ideas along the loop and discuss. Close by listing the questions and opportunities to explore further.

**Strategy.** Pick a process worth repeating — customer experience, knowledge creation, strategic planning. The biggest payoff is often a visceral perspective shift: participants stop thinking only about their internal slice and start thinking about what feeds in and what comes back. Get the right people in the room: enough outside-the-box awareness to fuel the discussion without derailing it. Credited to James Macanufo.

**Source.** [virtuous-cycle](https://gamestorming.com/virtuous-cycle/)

### Visual Agenda

**Object of play.** Most printed agendas get about two seconds of attention. A Visual Agenda — a large, colorful, hand-drawn display of the meeting's outcomes and steps — signals that the meeting matters, raises the room's energy, and helps participants recall later what was discussed. It is worth the extra effort for any meeting that takes serious time and brings people from across disciplines.

**Players.** Any meeting size; the agenda is built by the facilitator beforehand.

**Duration.** Built before the meeting; visible throughout.

**How to play.**
1. Establish the desired outcome(s) for the meeting and craft a flow that will get the group there.
2. Choose a visual framework or metaphor that fits the meeting's tone or theme — a beach scene with footsteps as stages for a vacation rental client, a circuit board for a tech firm, a forest path for an environmental org.
3. Draw the agenda on a large sheet of paper or in presentation software in a non-traditional, creative way. Use the metaphor consistently; let each agenda item live as a distinct visual element.
4. Brand the agenda. If you have copywriting chops, give each stage an interesting phrase. If not, ask an attendee with the right instincts to help.
5. Post the visual agenda where it greets people as they walk in, and refer back to it throughout the meeting to track progress.

**Strategy.** A visual agenda is a gesture: it tells the room you spent time on theirs before they arrived. The investment is small relative to the ROI in attention, energy, and recall. Inspired by The Grove's practice of creating visual agendas before meetings.

**Source.** [visual-agenda](https://gamestorming.com/visual-agenda/)

### Visual Glossary

**Object of play.** Most people would rather pretend they understand an unknown term than admit confusion, which is dangerous in knowledge work where shared meaning is required to move forward. Visual Glossary forces a group to define its tough words — jargon, slang, acronyms, abstract concepts — clearly enough that an image can stand in for the definition.

**Players.** 2–10.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Introduce the exercise as a way to create a common language. Have participants individually brainstorm difficult terms — jargon, technical words, acronyms, internal slang — on sticky notes.
2. Have everyone post their notes in one large pool. Discuss which terms were most frequent and which most need defining.
3. Pick the highest-priority terms and assign each a space on the wall.
4. For the first term, ask the group to describe it in words. The group will often discover that the verbal definition is foggy or contradictory.
5. Now clarify with a picture. Ask: what does this look like? For abstract terms, take a diagrammatic approach — start with the people or things involved and connect them visually.
6. Repeat with the next term until the priority list is covered.

**Strategy.** Don't try to define everything at once; pick the words where misunderstanding does the most damage. A good visual glossary outlives the meeting that produced it — embed the visuals in follow-on materials, training, internal docs, or onboarding. Encourage participants to use the visual elements as shorthand when working with these terms going forward. Credited to James Macanufo.

**Source.** [visual-glossary](https://gamestorming.com/visual-glossary/)

### Welcome To My World

**Object of play.** Most of us assume others see what we see and know what we know — and they don't. Welcome To My World gives players a chance to better understand other players' roles by drawing each other's workflows and comparing those drawings to reality. It chips away at silos and introduces the idea that we may each be operating from a private map of how the work happens.

**Players.** 8–20.

**Duration.** 30 minutes to 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. Provide flip-chart paper, markers, and sticky notes. Ask players to write one of their own job responsibilities on a sticky note (e.g., "create the company newsletter") and stick it to their shirt.
2. Have players wander the room and pair up with someone whose responsibility they are least familiar with or most curious about.
3. In pairs, players take turns drawing how they imagine the other person's workflow around that duty — flowcharts of circles, boxes, and arrows are fine. They cannot interview or ask clarifying questions while drawing. Allow 5–15 minutes of quiet drawing.
4. Each player gets five minutes to share her drawing and explain what it means.
5. Pairs spend another 5–10 minutes clarifying or correcting reality together, marking up the diagrams to show ease, friction, and handoffs to others.
6. Ask volunteers to share their visuals with the larger group and surface insights.

**Strategy.** This game requires diversity in roles to work — if everyone has the same workflow, there is nothing to reveal. It is most powerful between groups that have to collaborate but historically have had little patience for each other's processes. Reassure reluctant drawers that flowchart shapes are familiar from corporate life. Source unknown.

**Source.** [welcome-to-my-world](https://gamestorming.com/welcome-to-my-world/)

### What Could Go Wrong?

**Object of play.** What Could Go Wrong? combines the Pre-Mortem game (imagining failure before it happens) with a customer or user journey map to identify the points along the path where users might abandon a product or service — and what to do about those failure points before they bite. The result is a cyclical user journey overlaid with the risks at each phase and the actions that would mitigate them. *(This is a guest case study by Clifton B. on the Gamestorming site rather than a tightly templated game, so the structure below is reconstructed from the case.)*

**Players.** A small product team (3–6 typical).

**Duration.** Roughly 1–2 hours.

**How to play.**
1. Map the customer or user journey as a cycle. The case used four phases — Awareness, Consideration, Purchase, Renewal — looping Renewal back to Awareness because customer relationships continue past the first sale.
2. For each phase, write short user stories from your personas describing what they want and what concerns them at that stage.
3. Run a Pre-Mortem at each phase: imagine the user has abandoned you here. Why? List every reason this customer could be lost during this phase.
4. For each reason, brainstorm one or more actions you could take to keep the user moving forward to the next phase.
5. Sketch the resulting "projected user journey" showing both the happy path and the abandonment paths at each phase.
6. Step back and decide whether the work to mitigate the risks is justified by the upside — a clear-eyed go/no-go on the feature or initiative.

**Strategy.** The combination is more powerful than either piece alone: the journey gives the Pre-Mortem structure (you imagine failure phase by phase, not in the abstract) and the Pre-Mortem gives the journey teeth (you don't just describe the path, you stress-test it). The case study illustrates the value: after running the exercise, the team chose not to build a premium tier they had been considering, saving themselves significant wasted investment.

**Source.** [what-could-go-wrong](https://gamestorming.com/what-could-go-wrong/)

### WhoDo

**Object of play.** WhoDo identifies stakeholders and clarifies the goals you need each one to act on. Almost any endeavor of substantial impact requires help from others; a WHO + DO list scopes out exactly who needs to do what for the vision to become real.

**Players.** 1–10.

**Duration.** 20–45 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. Start with the vision. Write it out or sketch it at the top of the surface.
2. Draw a two-column matrix below the vision. Label the left column WHO and the right column DO.
3. Fill the WHO column. Ask: who is involved in making this happen? Who is the decision maker? Who has needed resources? Who may be an obstacle? Whose support is required? Capture every individual or group on the list.
4. Fill the DO column. For each WHO, ask what they need to do — or do differently — to move toward the vision. Sharpen each entry into a desired, measurable action.
5. Prioritize: of all the WHO/DO pairs, which matter most? Who needs to act first?

**Strategy.** Bias yourself toward action. The DOs are the harder column because there is a strong pull to settle for "we just want them to understand." When you catch yourself there, ask "what will happen once they understand?" — what you actually want is usually a behavior change, a decision, or a learned action. A natural follow-on is to build an Empathy Map of the most important WHOs. Credited to Dave Gray.

**Source.** [whodo](https://gamestorming.com/whodo/)

### Who/What/When Matrix

**Object of play.** Meetings often end with vague "next steps" discussions where tasks are handed out to unwilling participants with no deadlines. The Who/What/When Matrix replaces that pattern by connecting people to actions they have personally defined and visibly committed to in front of their peers.

**Players.** 1–10.

**Duration.** 15–30 minutes.

**How to play.**
1. On a flip chart or whiteboard, draw three columns: WHO, WHAT, WHEN.
2. Fill the WHO column first. Put every meeting participant's name into the column — start with people, not tasks.
3. Go around the room and ask each participant what concrete next steps they can personally commit to. Place each item in the WHAT column next to the person's name. A given person may have several next steps.
4. For each item, ask the person WHEN they will have it done. Capture the date in the WHEN column.
5. Read the matrix back to the room and confirm commitments. Note any participants who have committed to little or nothing.

**Strategy.** Actions don't take themselves and people don't commit to actions as strongly as they commit to each other. Approaching next steps "people-first" makes accountability visible, leverages peer-pressure credibility, and exposes anyone who didn't volunteer for anything. If someone has an empty row, ask whether they can support someone else's task — or whether their attendance was actually necessary. After the meeting, the facilitator is still on the hook for follow-up; ask participants to email their commitments and circulate the consolidated list. Designed by Dave Gray, inspired by Mike Berman's coaching methods.

**Source.** [whowhatwhen-matrix](https://gamestorming.com/whowhatwhen-matrix/)

### Whole Product Game

**Object of play.** Inspired by Ted Levitt's "Whole Product Strategy," this game categorizes aspects of a product based on customer expectations to help a company find effective forms of differentiation. Products are not just tangible items — they are bundles of benefits, services, and promises — and the goal is to discover ways to set yours apart and go beyond what customers anticipate.

**Players.** 5–8.

**Duration.** 1 hour.

**How to play.**
1. On a large surface, draw four concentric circles with enough room between each to place sticky notes. Don't worry about perfect symmetry.
2. Label the regions: (1) Generic Product — the fundamental thing being sold; (2) Expected Product — the minimum customers expect; (3) Augmented Product — features that go beyond expectations; (4) Potential Product — what could be done to attract and keep customers.
3. Players (the internal team taking the customer perspective, or actual customers) write ideas on sticky notes and place each on the region where it belongs.
4. Remove duplicates and consolidate similar notes with the group's input.
5. Discuss the resulting picture. How can you use this distribution to differentiate? Which moves into the Augmented or Potential ring would matter most to customers?

**Strategy.** The Expected Product attracts customers; the Augmented and Potential Products keep them. Avoid "going in circles" — keep the group focused on actions you can take to exceed customer expectations rather than re-litigating the basics. The game also works at sub-product scope: what makes your customer service unique, for example. An online version is available at innovationgames.com that uses the same four-ring board with light-bulb cards players drag onto rings.

**Source.** [whole-product-game](https://gamestorming.com/whole-product-game/)

### Wizard Of Oz

**Object of play.** In this role-play, two people prototype a machine–human interaction. The "user" interacts with a "machine" played by another person hidden behind a curtain. The technique can stress-test an existing design with a script or improvise a brand-new idea, surfacing what the user actually wants to do and how — independent of what the technology can currently do.

**Players.** 2 (a user and a wizard), plus observers.

**Duration.** 30 minutes or more.

**How to play.**
1. If testing an existing design, prepare a script that defines the machine's possible responses and the inputs it understands. The wizard will use this script — and only this script — to react to the user. For improvisation, skip the script.
2. Visually separate the two players. The "curtain" can be a piece of cardboard or simply having the players turn their backs to each other. The point is to prevent inadvertent cues passing between them.
3. The user initiates a task they want to accomplish using the imagined machine. The wizard responds in the role of the machine.
4. Play through the interaction, watching for friction points, breakdowns, and unexpected moves. The user should challenge the machine; the wizard should stick to what the machine "knows."
5. Observers note where the design fails, where users want capabilities the machine doesn't have, and where the script needs to expand.

**Strategy.** The curtain simultaneously eliminates assumptions about the machine's capabilities and surfaces what the user actually wants to do. The technique was pioneered in the 1970s for early airport kiosks and IBM's "listening typewriter," with a hidden wizard interpreting voice commands and manipulating prototype output. Today it is widely used for any conversational, voice, or AI-driven interface where building the real thing first would be prohibitively expensive.

**Source.** [wizard-of-oz](https://gamestorming.com/wizard-of-oz/)

### World Café

**Object of play.** World Café is a method for improving large-group discussion by borrowing from informal café conversations: round tables, cross-pollinating ideas, and pursuing questions that matter. It scales meaningful dialogue beyond the small groups where most great conversations otherwise stay trapped.

**Players.** 24–30 participants in groups of 4–5 at round tables.

**Duration.** About 1.5 hours.

**How to play.**
1. As leader, develop the "questions that matter" — evocative, simple, immediately tangible questions tied to a real challenge the group faces. One strong question can sustain three rounds; or use a sequence.
2. Set up an inviting environment: round tables (better for conversation than square), markers, flip charts, and paper tablecloths people can draw on directly.
3. Run three 20-minute rounds of small-group discussion at the tables. After each round, one person stays as the "host" of that table; the rest move to other tables as "ambassadors."
4. At each new round, the host briefs the incoming group on the prior conversation, and ambassadors share what they brought from their previous table. Encourage participants to leave evidence — drawings and notes — on the tablecloths so the next group can build on them.
5. By the second and third rounds, encourage groups to look for emerging themes and patterns and capture them visibly toward the middle of the table.
6. Close with a town-hall-style synthesis: revisit the original questions, gather what each table discovered, and connect threads across the room.

**Strategy.** The traveling format is the engine — ideas literally migrate around the world, hybridize, and return enriched. Use a talking stick or button at tables to manage input if dominance becomes a problem. The methodology is maintained by an active community at theworldcafe.com.

**Source.** [world-cafe](https://gamestorming.com/world-cafe/)
