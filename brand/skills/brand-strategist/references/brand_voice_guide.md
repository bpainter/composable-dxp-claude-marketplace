# Brand Voice and Tone Guidelines

## What is Brand Voice?
**Voice** is your brand's consistent personality and values—how you sound across all contexts. It's relatively stable.

**Tone** is how you adapt your voice to specific audiences, situations, and channels. It's flexible and contextual.

Think of voice as your speaking style, and tone as your mood or register.

---

## Voice Dimension Framework

### Personality Traits
Define 4-6 core personality traits that describe your brand voice:

**Examples:**
- Confident + approachable (vs. arrogant or timid)
- Warm + professional (vs. cold or overly casual)
- Direct + empathetic (vs. blunt or vague)
- Playful + credible (vs. flippant or boring)
- Innovative + grounded (vs. trendy or outdated)

### Vocabulary & Language Patterns
What words do you use? What do you avoid?

**Use:**
- Active voice: "We help you..." (not "We can assist with...")
- Concrete nouns: "data-driven campaigns" (not "optimized initiatives")
- Simple words: "use" (not "utilize")
- Short sentences: Vary length for flow

**Avoid:**
- Buzzwords: "cutting-edge," "best-in-class," "world-class" (unless backed by evidence)
- Jargon: Don't assume inside knowledge
- Clichés: "innovative," "synergy," "game-changing" (overused)
- Formal constructions: "We regret to inform you..." (too stiff)

### Tone Variations Across Contexts

#### B2B SaaS (Serious Context)
- Confident, smart, approachable
- Balance professionalism with accessibility
- Avoid: Arrogance, condescension, excessive casualness

Example: "Analytics shouldn't require a math degree. Dovetail translates raw data into clear insights—so your team focuses on what matters."

#### B2C Lifestyle (Casual Context)
- Friendly, relatable, concise
- Reflect your audience's language and values
- Avoid: Tryhard slang, overly casual, inauthentic

Example: "Coffee should be simple. We source direct from farmers, roast in-house, deliver fresh—because great coffee changes mornings."

#### Financial Services (Trust Context)
- Authoritative, transparent, reassuring
- Explain complex concepts clearly
- Avoid: Jargon, false certainty, overpromising

Example: "We believe you deserve to understand how your money works. No hidden fees. No jargon. Just clear, honest investing."

#### Emergency / Error Situations (Supportive Context)
- Empathetic, solution-focused, human
- Acknowledge the problem, show you care, guide to resolution
- Avoid: Blame, robotic language, jokes that feel dismissive

Example: "Your data export failed—sorry about that. We're looking into it. In the meantime, here's an alternative way to pull your data."

## Tone by Channel

### Website / Brand Hub
- Tone: Poised, confident, articulate
- Purpose: Establish credibility and tell your story
- Length: Medium-form (150-500 words typical)
- Examples: About page, value proposition, case studies

### Social Media (LinkedIn, Twitter)
- Tone: Conversational, real, thought-provoking
- Purpose: Build community, spark discussion, share perspective
- Length: Short-form (one-two sentences typical)
- Examples: Insights, behind-the-scenes, industry takes

### Email Marketing
- Tone: Warm, personal, respect-driven
- Purpose: Build relationship, drive action, deliver value
- Length: Scannable, paragraphs 2-3 sentences max
- Examples: Newsletters, product announcements, nurture sequences

### Product UI (Buttons, Tooltips, Error Messages)
- Tone: Helpful, brief, action-oriented
- Purpose: Guide user, reduce friction, maintain flow
- Length: Microcopy (1-2 sentences)
- Examples: "Save draft," "Here's why," "Fix this issue," success confirmations

### Customer Support (Chat, Email, Tickets)
- Tone: Empathetic, solution-focused, human
- Purpose: Resolve issue, rebuild trust, delight
- Length: Conversational, acknowledge → solve → offer proactive help
- Examples: Support tickets, chat responses, escalation messages

### Sales Outreach (Cold Email, Calls)
- Tone: Personalized, consultative, respect-driven
- Purpose: Start conversation, not hard-sell
- Length: Concise (cold email: 50-75 words)
- Examples: Personalized outreach, discovery calls, pitch emails

## Voice Writing Samples

### What NOT to Do: Generic & Lifeless
"Our platform offers best-in-class solutions designed to optimize your business operations and drive results."

**Why it fails:** Buzzwords, no specificity, says nothing

### What TO Do: Clear, Specific, Confident
"Track every interaction—calls, emails, demos—in one place. Stop losing deals to scattered spreadsheets."

**Why it works:** Concrete benefit, acknowledges real problem, direct and confident

---

### What NOT to Do: Overly Clever
"We're not just a tool; we're a vibe. A movement. A lifestyle."

**Why it fails:** Grandiose, not grounded, exhausting

### What TO Do: Authentic & Humble
"We're obsessed with one thing: making project management feel less like a chore and more like flow."

**Why it works:** Human, specific, credible

## Common Voice Archetypes

### The Expert / Educator
- Shares knowledge generously
- Assumes audience wants to learn
- Examples: Hubspot, Moz, OpenAI
- Risk: Can feel preachy or condescending

### The Friend / Confidant
- Approachable, warm, personal
- Uses "you" and "we" language
- Examples: Slack, Duolingo, Basecamp
- Risk: Can feel inauthentic or overly casual

### The Visionary / Inspirer
- Points toward possibility and change
- Aspirational, optimistic
- Examples: Tesla, Apple, TED
- Risk: Can feel grandiose or disconnected from reality

### The Disruptor / Contrarian
- Questions status quo
- Provocative, sometimes irreverent
- Examples: Mailchimp (formerly), Dollar Shave Club, Unbounce
- Risk: Can alienate conservative audiences

### The Trusted Authority
- Credible, reliable, proven
- Speaks with confidence grounded in data
- Examples: AWS, McKinsey, Vanguard
- Risk: Can feel stiff or disconnected from humans

## Creating Your Voice Playbook

1. **Define core personality traits** (4-6 adjectives)
2. **Write 3-5 voice sample paragraphs** across your main use cases (website, email, social, support)
3. **Create tone variations** for different contexts (happy news, bad news, support, social, sales)
4. **Document vocabulary** (use these words, avoid these words)
5. **Provide examples** (do this, not that)
6. **Set guidelines for**: Length, formality, emoji use, slang, humor, storytelling, urgency language
7. **Train your team** with examples and regular feedback

---

## Voice Consistency Over Time

To maintain consistent voice:
- Review social posts, emails, and website copy monthly for drift
- Brief new hires on voice expectations (not just product knowledge)
- Create templates for common formats (email signature, social post structure, support ticket response)
- Share examples of good and bad voice execution
- Give feedback in real-time; voice consistency compounds over time
